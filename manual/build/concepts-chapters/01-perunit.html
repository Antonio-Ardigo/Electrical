1. Per-Unit Thinking

Manual deep-dive: open the full section

The Problem

Our plant lives on two voltage levels at once: SEC delivers at 13.8 kV, and almost everything that actually does work — motors, heaters, lighting — runs at 400/230 V. Between them sit two 1600 kVA transformers. Now try to answer a simple question in ordinary amperes: "how much fault current flows if the LV busbar is short-circuited?" The current on the LV side is about 38 500 A; the same fault, same instant, seen on the MV side, is about 1 115 A. Same event, numbers 34.5 times apart. Every cable, every relay, every calculation on the MV side speaks one numerical language; the LV side speaks another. Before per-unit, engineers carried transformer turns ratios through every line of every fault study, and one missed ratio — one impedance left un-referred across a winding — silently corrupted the whole study.

It got worse with size: a real network has several transformations (transmission, sub-transmission, distribution, LV). Referring every impedance through every ratio by hand was the dominant source of error in protection and fault studies. People tried colour-coded tables of "ohms referred to 13.8 kV" — workable for one substation, hopeless for a network, and meaningless the day the tap-changer moved.

What was at stake is not academic tidiness. A fault calculation that is wrong by a transformer ratio squared (impedance refers with the square of the ratio — a factor of about 1 190 for 13.8 kV/400 V) leads to switchgear bought with the wrong interrupting rating, or relays set to trip never, or always.

Core Principles

Principle 1: Measure everything as a fraction of a chosen "normal"

Statement: instead of volts, amps and ohms, express every quantity as a fraction of a deliberately chosen base value: quantity (pu) = actual value ÷ base value. "pu" means per-unit — per unit of the base. If the base current is 2 309 A and you measure 2 309 A, the current is 1.0 pu: the equipment is at exactly its normal full load. If you measure 38 500 A, the current is 16.7 pu — 16.7 times normal — and the number itself screams "fault".

Example: a transformer at 0.85 pu load is at 85 % of nameplate, whether it is our 1600 kVA unit or a 160 MVA grid transformer. The per-unit number carries the meaning; the base carries the size.

Principle 2: Only two bases are free — the rest follow

Statement: choose a base power S_base (one value for the whole system) and a base voltage V_base (one per voltage level, set equal to the nominal voltage there). Base current and base impedance are then forced by Ohm's law and the power equation:

I_base = S_base / (√3 × V_base) Z_base = V_base² / S_base

You never pick a base current; you derive it. That discipline is what keeps the system self-consistent.

Principle 3: The transformer disappears

Statement: if the base voltages on the two sides of a transformer are chosen in the ratio of its windings (13.8 kV and 0.4 kV for TX-1), then a per-unit impedance has the same numerical value seen from either side. The ratio referral — the historical source of errors — cancels exactly, because the base impedances themselves differ by the ratio squared. The transformer becomes just a series impedance of 0.06 pu in the circuit diagram; the magic 34.5:1 ratio is gone.

Principle 4: Nameplate percent impedance is already per-unit

Statement: the "Z = 6 %" stamped on TX-1's nameplate is a per-unit impedance on the transformer's own rating: 6 % = 0.06 pu on 1600 kVA. Manufacturers worldwide publish in this form precisely so that the number is size-independent and directly usable. (When mixing equipment of different ratings, convert to a common base: Z_new = Z_old × S_base,new / S_base,old.)

Key Innovations

The normalised network (early 20th-century power engineers, formalised ~1930s)

What: redraw the entire multi-voltage network as a single circuit in which every element — grid source, transformer, cable, motor — is one per-unit impedance on a common power base.

Why it mattered: fault studies, load flow and stability studies became single-circuit problems. Every modern power-system program (ETAP, DIgSILENT, SKM) works internally in per-unit; the software you will see study reports from is per-unit thinking, automated.

Example: our plant from grid to LV bus is, in per-unit on 1600 kVA: grid source ≈ 0.0027 pu (a 25 kA source at 13.8 kV is enormous compared to our base — nearly a perfect source) in series with TX-1 at 0.06 pu. The transformer dominates: that single observation, visible at a glance only in per-unit, is why "Isc ≈ FLC ÷ Z%" works so well at LV.

Percent impedance as a universal nameplate language (IEC 60076 practice)

What: standardising that transformer impedance is declared as a percentage on own rating, measured by the short-circuit test.

Why it mattered: a field engineer can estimate the LV fault level of any substation on Earth from two nameplate numbers — rating and Z % — with no other data. That estimate is usually within a few percent of the full study.

Worked Example

Scenario: convert the Rev 3 plant to per-unit on a 1600 kVA base and find the LV bolted-fault current through one transformer. Given: TX-1 = 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Z = 6 %; system 60 Hz; all values indicative.

Step 1 — Choose the bases. Take S_base = 1600 kVA (the transformer's own rating — so its nameplate Z needs no conversion). Base voltages: 13.8 kV on the MV side, 0.4 kV on the LV side (the winding ratio, so the transformer "disappears").

Step 2 — Derive the base currents.

I_base(LV) = S_base / (√3 × V_base) = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) = 2 309 A I_base(MV) = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 13 800 V) = 66.9 A

Why: note these are exactly the Plant Data Card full-load currents — choosing the transformer rating as base makes "1.0 pu current" mean "transformer at full load" on both sides at once.

Step 3 — Express the impedance.

Z_TX = 6 % = 6/100 = 0.06 pu (on 1600 kVA — no conversion needed)

Step 4 — Solve the fault in per-unit. A bolted fault on the LV bus, fed from a strong grid (source impedance ≈ 0.003 pu, neglected here for the field estimate), sees only the transformer:

I_fault = V / Z = 1.0 pu / 0.06 pu = 16.7 pu

Why: the driving voltage is the normal system voltage, i.e. 1.0 pu by construction. The answer reads directly as "16.7 times full load".

Step 5 — Convert back to amperes, on whichever side you care about.

LV side: I_fault = 16.7 × 2 309 A = 38 500 A ≈ 38.5 kA MV side: I_fault = 16.7 × 66.9 A ≈ 1 115 A (same fault, same 16.7 pu)

Sanity check: 38.5 kA matches the Plant Data Card LV fault level, and 1 115 A × (13 800/400) voltage ratio... cross-check: 1 115 A × 34.5 = 38 470 A ≈ 38.5 kA. The two sides agree through the turns ratio, exactly as they must. Including the grid impedance would lower this by only ~4 % — the 6 % transformer dominates.

The field rule this buys you: Isc(LV) ≈ FLC / Z(pu). For any transformer: full-load current divided by per-unit impedance. 2 309 A / 0.06 = 38.5 kA. Two nameplate numbers, one division.

Intuitive Formalization

The whole method is three definitions and one consequence. Each symbol: S_base = chosen reference power (VA), V_base = nominal voltage of the level (V), X(pu) = any quantity X divided by its base.

X(pu) = X_actual / X_base (definition — a pure, unitless ratio) I_base = S_base / (√3 × V_base) (forced by the 3-phase power equation) Z_base = V_base² / S_base (forced by Ohm's law) Z(pu) seen from HV = Z(pu) seen from LV (because Z_base scales by ratio² too)

Physical meaning: per-unit measures stress relative to design. Voltage at 0.95 pu is 5 % undervoltage anywhere in the world. Current at 16.7 pu is a fault, whether the amperes read 38 500 or 38.5. The base values are the "100 % marks" you draw on every gauge; per-unit is reading the gauges in percent instead of raw units. Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws and power equations all keep their familiar forms in per-unit — that is the honest mathematical content: a change of units carefully rigged so the transformer ratio becomes 1.
