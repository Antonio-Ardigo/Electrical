5. Time–Current Selectivity

Manual deep-dive: open the full section

The Problem

A fault on one motor feeder in MCC-1 puts fault current through every device between the fault and the grid: the feeder MCCB, the MCC incomer, the LV-MSB ACB, the transformer's MV breaker, even the SEC supply relay. Each of them sees the same abnormal current at the same instant, and each is perfectly entitled to trip. If they all do, a single welder's damaged cable blacks out the entire plant — the 2 MW process, the control room, everything — and the operators spend an hour discovering which of five open breakers marks the actual fault.

The naive fixes both fail. Make everything trip fast, and any small fault cascades into a total shutdown; spurious trips of the MV incomer become routine and SEC starts asking questions. Make upstream devices insensitive so they "never" trip, and a fault the downstream device fails to clear burns unattended. What the plant actually needs is a hierarchy of patience: the device nearest the fault trips first and fast; each device upstream waits just long enough to give its downstream colleague a fair chance — and no longer, because every extra tenth of a second is paid in I²t damage and arc-flash energy (Ch. 6).

This discipline — selectivity (also called discrimination or coordination) — is what turns a pile of individually excellent breakers into a protection system. It is bought almost entirely with one currency: time.

Core Principles

Principle 1: Trip the smallest possible slice of the network

Statement: for any fault, exactly one device should operate — the nearest one upstream of the fault. Everything above it holds. The measure of a good scheme is not how fast it trips but how little it trips.

Example: motor cable fault → feeder MCCB only; MCC-1 keeps running its other motors; LV-MSB never notices beyond a voltage dip.

Principle 2: Every device is a curve, not a number

Statement: protection devices are characterised on a time–current curve (TCC): operating time versus current, both on log scales. MCCBs have a thermal (long-time) region, sometimes a short-time delay region, and an instantaneous region. Relays have settable curves. Selectivity is a geometric statement: the downstream curve must lie entirely below and to the left of the upstream curve, with a margin, over the whole range of currents both can see.

Principle 3: Grading margin — the honest minimum gap between curves

Statement: the time gap between successive curves is not arbitrary. It must cover, in series: the downstream breaker's clearing time (the upstream relay keeps measuring fault current until the arc actually dies, Ch. 4); upstream relay overshoot (a relay almost at its trip point coasts a little after current stops); timing errors of both devices (IEC accuracy classes); and a safety remainder. Summed honestly this gives the classical 0.3–0.4 s between IDMT relay stages, and ~0.2 s between modern numerical devices with breaker times known.

Principle 4: IDMT — patience proportional to provocation

Statement: an IDMT (Inverse Definite Minimum Time) relay trips faster the larger the current. The IEC standard-inverse curve is t = TMS × 0.14 / (M^0.02 − 1), where M = fault current ÷ pickup setting, and TMS (time-multiplier setting) scales the whole curve up or down. Inverseness mirrors physics: big currents do I²t damage faster, so they deserve less patience — and it lets successive relays grade naturally across a range of fault positions.

Key Innovations

The time-graded ladder (induction-disc relays, early 1900s → numerical relays today)

What: assigning each level of the radial network a deliberate operating time, stepped by the grading margin: in our plant, indicatively — feeder MCCB instantaneous (<0.05 s) → LV-MSB incomer ACB short-time delay 0.3 s → MV transformer-feeder relay ≈0.6 s → SEC upstream above that.

Why it mattered: radial selectivity became a paper (now software) exercise: stack the TCCs, check the daylight between curves. The cost is explicit too — the highest-energy bus (LV-MSB, 38.5 kA) gets the slowest protection, which is the central tension Ch. 6 inherits.

Zone selective interlocking and energy-based tricks (modern trip units)

What: breakers that talk: a downstream device seeing the fault sends a blocking signal upstream; an upstream device that receives no block knows the fault is in its own zone and trips with minimal delay instead of waiting out its grading time. Related: current-limiting cascade at the network edges, and maintenance switches that temporarily collapse the ladder to fast settings while a person works on the board.

Why it mattered: it breaks the cruel trade "selectivity = slow main breakers". With ZSI, a genuine LV-MSB bus fault is cleared in ~0.1 s while feeder faults still grade normally — directly cutting the arc-flash numbers worked in Ch. 6.

Worked Example

Scenario: build the grading margin from first principles, then set the MV IDMT relay above the LV-MSB incomer ACB for an LV bus fault. Given: LV bus fault 38.5 kA (Ch. 2); ACB short-time setting 0.3 s; MV relay IEC standard-inverse, CT-side pickup 80 A primary (≈1.2 × MV FLC 66.9 A); transformer ratio 13.8/0.4 kV. Indicative values.

Step 1 — Assemble the margin budget (why "0.3–0.4 s" is not folklore).

Downstream breaker clearing: ~0.07 s (3–4 cycles at 60 Hz, Ch. 4) Upstream relay overshoot: ~0.05 s Timing tolerances, both devices: ~0.13 s (IEC accuracy + CT errors) Safety remainder: ~0.10 s Total grading margin ≈ 0.07+0.05+0.13+0.10 = 0.35 s

Why: each line is a real, physical delay or uncertainty. Skipping any of them produces a scheme that grades on paper and races in reality.

Step 2 — Refer the LV fault to the MV side. The MV relay sees the LV bus fault through the transformer:

I_MV = 38 500 A × (400 / 13 800) = 38 500 × 0.029 = 1 116 A

Step 3 — Multiple of pickup.

M = I_MV / I_pickup = 1 116 / 80 = 13.95

Step 4 — Relay time at TMS = 1 (IEC standard inverse).

M^0.02 = 13.95^0.02 ≈ 1.054 → M^0.02 − 1 = 0.054 t(TMS=1) = 0.14 / 0.054 ≈ 2.59 s

Why: the exponent 0.02 makes the curve very flat at high multiples — the relay's "patience" barely changes between 10× and 20× pickup, which is exactly what makes grading at high fault currents predictable.

Step 5 — Choose TMS to sit one margin above the ACB. Target time = ACB 0.3 s + margin 0.35 s = 0.65 s:

TMS = t_target / t(TMS=1) = 0.65 / 2.59 ≈ 0.251 → set TMS = 0.25 Actual relay time: t = 0.25 × 2.59 ≈ 0.65 s

Step 6 — Verify the ladder, bottom to top, at the LV bus fault.

Feeder MCCB: instantaneous < 0.05 s (its own zone faults) LV-MSB ACB: 0.30 s (gap to MCCB ≈ 0.25 s ✓ with ZSI/known breaker times) MV relay: 0.65 s (gap to ACB = 0.35 s ✓)

Sanity check: 1 116 A on the MV side ÷ 66.9 A FLC ≈ 16.7 — the same per-unit multiple as the LV side (Ch. 1), confirming the referral. The MV relay also stays below the transformer through-fault damage curve at 0.65 s — the upper boundary every grading study must respect.

The trade made explicit: the plant bought "lose one feeder, not the plant" at the price of a main bus protected at 0.3 s and an MV stage at 0.65 s. That price is paid in arc-flash energy at the LV-MSB — which is Ch. 6's opening line.

Intuitive Formalization

Symbols: M = fault current as a multiple of pickup; TMS = time multiplier; Δt = grading margin; t_n = operating time of level n (counting up from the fault).

IEC SI curve: t = TMS × 0.14 / (M^0.02 − 1) Ladder rule: t_(n+1) ≥ t_n + Δt, with Δt ≈ 0.3–0.4 s (electromech.) or ≈0.2 s (numerical) Margin truth: Δt = t_breaker + t_overshoot + Σerrors + safety

Physical meaning: M is "how sure are we this is a real fault" — the curve gives big, unambiguous faults less patience. TMS slides the whole curve vertically: it is the knob that places a relay on its rung of the ladder. The ladder rule is just queueing etiquette with a stopwatch; the margin truth is the reminder that the stopwatch must include the breaker's physical arc-killing time (Ch. 4), not merely the relay's decision time.
