9. Harmonics & VFDs

Manual deep-dive: open the full section (harmonics are treated with the PFC bank; see also the motors & VFD section)

The Problem

The Rev 3 plant, like every modern process plant, is full of variable frequency drives (tag VFD): pumps, fans and compressors whose speed is controlled electronically. Each drive front-end is a rectifier that does not draw current smoothly from the 400 V / 60 Hz bus — it gulps current in pulses, twice per phase per cycle, only when the incoming sine wave is near its peak. The bus sees a current waveform that is no longer a sine wave at all.

Any distorted but repeating wave can be decomposed into a family of clean sine waves: the 60 Hz fundamental plus harmonics at integer multiples — 300 Hz (5th), 420 Hz (7th), 660 Hz (11th) and so on. These harmonic currents are invisible to a standard ammeter reading "amps", yet they heat transformers and neutrals, make motors growl and run hot in a 50 °C ambient that has no margin to spare, trip breakers mysteriously, and corrupt the voltage wave that every other load on LV-MSB shares.

The lethal twist is the interaction with power factor capacitors. A plain capacitor bank and the 1600 kVA transformer's inductance form a parallel resonant circuit — an electrical bell. If a VFD happens to "ring the bell" at one of its harmonic frequencies, a modest 5th or 7th harmonic current is amplified many times over, and the first casualties are the capacitors themselves: bulging cans, blown fuses, and in the worst case a switchboard fire. Before this was understood, plants installed capacitors and drives independently and watched the capacitors die in months. The fix — the detuned bank — is the centrepiece of this chapter.

Core Principles

Principle 1: A distorted wave is a sum of clean waves (Fourier)

Statement: Any periodic waveform equals a fundamental sine wave plus harmonics at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

At 60 Hz the family is 60, 120, 180, 240, 300 Hz… Think of a violin string: the same string vibrates at its fundamental pitch and simultaneously at overtones, and the mix is the timbre. A VFD input current has a very harsh "timbre". Distortion is quantified by THD (total harmonic distortion): the RMS of all harmonics divided by the fundamental, in percent.

Principle 2: A 6-pulse rectifier produces the h = 6k ± 1 family

Statement: A three-phase bridge rectifier (six diodes — the standard small/medium VFD front-end) draws characteristic harmonics at orders 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19…, with magnitude falling roughly as 1/h.

Why those numbers: the bridge takes six current pulses per cycle, and the symmetry of that pattern cancels the triplen (3rd, 9th) and even harmonics, leaving 6k ± 1. So the dominant pollutants are the 5th (300 Hz, typically ~30 % of fundamental) and the 7th (420 Hz, ~10 %). Define on first use: "order h" simply means h × 60 Hz.

Principle 3: Harmonic currents create harmonic voltages across impedance

Statement: Each harmonic current flowing back through the supply impedance (transformer + grid) drops a harmonic voltage, distorting the bus voltage for every consumer on it.

Ohm's law per frequency: U_h = I_h × Z_h, and inductive impedance grows with frequency (Z_h ≈ h × Z₁). A small high-order current can therefore make a disproportionate voltage mess. IEEE 519 and the SEC Distribution Code limit voltage THD (typically ≤5 % at LV) for exactly this reason.

Principle 4: A capacitor plus an inductance is a resonant circuit

Statement: The PFC bank (capacitive, impedance falling with frequency) in parallel with the transformer (inductive, impedance rising with frequency) has one frequency where the two are equal — parallel resonance — at which harmonic currents are amplified instead of absorbed.

The resonant order is set by the ratio of available fault level to capacitor size: h_res = √(S_sc / Q_c). If that lands on 5 or 7, the bell rings. The worked example below shows it lands uncomfortably close in our plant.

Key Innovations

The detuned reactor (1970s, European LV practice)

What: A series reactor added to each capacitor step, sized so that reactor reactance is a fixed percentage p (here 7 %) of the capacitor reactance. The branch becomes a series L-C circuit tuned to f_tune = f₁ / √p — 227 Hz at 60 Hz — deliberately below the lowest characteristic harmonic (300 Hz).

Why it mattered: Above its tuning frequency a series L-C branch looks inductive. So at 300 Hz and everything above, the "capacitor" bank is no longer a capacitor at all — parallel resonance with the transformer can no longer occur at any harmonic the VFDs produce. The bank still delivers its kvar at 60 Hz almost unchanged. One cheap reactor converts a fire hazard into a mildly helpful harmonic sink.

Multi-pulse and active front ends (1980s–2000s)

What: 12-pulse rectifiers (two 6-pulse bridges fed by a phase-shifting transformer) cancel the 5th and 7th, leaving 11th and 13th; active front ends (AFE) use switched transistors to draw a near-sinusoidal current (THD-i < 5 %).

Why it mattered: Treats the disease at the source instead of the symptom at the bus — used for the largest drives where one machine dominates the harmonic budget.

The active harmonic filter (1990s onward)

What: A power-electronic device that measures the distorted bus current in real time and injects the exact anti-phase harmonic current — noise-cancelling headphones for the switchboard.

Why it mattered: Retrofittable, adaptive, and the standard remedy when a plant grows more drives than its original harmonic study assumed.

Worked Example: Resonance check — plain bank vs 7 % detuned bank on LV-MSB

Given: LV bus 400 V / 60 Hz fed by one TX (1600 kVA, Z = 6 %), short-circuit current Isc ≈ 38.5 kA; PFC bank Q_c ≈ 580 kvar (Chapter 8). All values indicative.

Step 1 — Short-circuit (stiffness) level of the bus.

S_sc = √3 × U × I_sc = 1.732 × 400 V × 38 500 A ≈ 26.7 MVA

Why: S_sc measures how "stiff" the bus is — the inductance behind it. It is the L of the resonant circuit. (Cross-check: S_sc ≈ S_tx / Z = 1600 kVA / 0.06 = 26.7 MVA. Consistent.)

Step 2 — Resonant order with a PLAIN (no reactor) bank.

h_res = √(S_sc / Q_c) = √(26 700 / 580) = √46.0 = 6.8

f_res = 6.8 × 60 Hz ≈ 407 Hz

Why: 407 Hz sits almost exactly on the 7th harmonic (420 Hz) — one of the two frequencies every 6-pulse VFD in the plant pumps out. The 7th-harmonic currents would be amplified by the resonance, circulate between transformer and capacitors, and cook the bank. Note: the result also moves with switching state — close the bus-tie (Isc ≈ 77 kA, S_sc ≈ 53.3 MVA) and h_res shifts to √(53 300/580) = 9.6 (≈575 Hz). You cannot "tune around" a resonance that moves; you must remove it.

Step 3 — Tuning frequency of the 7 % DETUNED bank. Detuning factor p = X_L/X_C = 0.07.

f_tune = f₁ / √p = 60 / √0.07 = 60 / 0.2646 = 227 Hz  (order n = 3.78)

Why: The series reactor and capacitor resonate (series) at 227 Hz. Above 227 Hz the branch impedance is net inductive.

Step 4 — Verdict at the harmonic frequencies.

5th = 300 Hz > 227 Hz → branch inductive → no parallel resonance possible

7th = 420 Hz > 227 Hz → inductive → safe;  same for 11th, 13th…

Sanity check: 227 Hz lies below 300 Hz with ~25 % margin, but above the 3rd (180 Hz) — triplens are scarce on a balanced three-wire system, so the bank cannot be overloaded by them either. The 7 % reactor raises the capacitor terminal voltage by roughly 1/(1−p) − 1 ≈ 7.5 %, which is why the cans are 480 V-class units on a 400 V bus. Result: plain bank = resonance at ≈407 Hz, on top of the 7th harmonic — reject. Detuned 7 % bank = tuned to 227 Hz, inductive at all VFD harmonics — accept.

Intuitive Formalization

Three short formulas carry the whole chapter. First, frequencies: harmonic order h means frequency f_h = h × 60 Hz, and a 6-pulse rectifier emits h = 6k ± 1 (k = 1, 2, 3…) with current magnitude roughly I_h ≈ I₁ / h. Second, the danger condition for a plain capacitor bank:

h_res = √(S_sc / Q_c)

where S_sc (MVA) is the bus short-circuit level — physically, the strength of the transformer/grid inductance behind the bus — and Q_c (Mvar) is the connected capacitor power. The square root appears because resonance happens where inductive and capacitive reactances cross, and each scales linearly (one up, one down) with frequency. If h_res lands near 5, 7, 11 or 13, expect amplification. Third, the cure:

f_tune = f₁ / √p

where p is the reactor's reactance as a fraction of the capacitor's (p = 0.07 → 227 Hz at 60 Hz, n = 3.78). Physical meaning: below f_tune the branch behaves as the capacitor you wanted; above f_tune the reactor wins and the branch is an inductor — and an inductor cannot parallel-resonate with another inductor. Everything a field engineer needs is: compute h_res, see that it threatens, specify p = 7 %, verify f_tune < 300 Hz.
