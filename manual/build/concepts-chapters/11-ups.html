11. The UPS

The Problem

When SEC supply fails, Chapter 12 shows the plant goes dark for roughly 12 seconds before the diesel generator picks up. Twelve seconds is nothing to a motor — and an eternity to a computer. The DCS controllers, the safety (ESD) system, the protection relays' auxiliary supplies, telecoms and the operator stations will all reboot, and a process plant whose control system reboots mid-process does not pause — it trips, vents, and takes hours to restart. Worse, electronics do not even need 12 seconds to die: a voltage dip of a few cycles — 50 milliseconds, the kind caused by a remote 13.8 kV fault or a big motor start — is enough to crash an unprotected PLC.

The historical attempts are instructive. Plants first tried "essential" feeders from two transformers — but both share the same upstream dips. Then rotary sets (motor-generator with a flywheel) — robust, but maintenance-heavy and only seconds of ride-through. What the control room actually needs is power that is continuously manufactured on site from stored energy, with the mains relegated to the role of keeping the store topped up.

That is the UPS — uninterruptible power supply. The Rev 3 plant carries a 100 kVA double-conversion UPS with 30 minutes of battery autonomy (tag UPS), feeding the DCS, ESD, critical instruments and operator interfaces. Thirty minutes is not arbitrary: it covers the DG start (12 s) hundreds of times over, and if the DG fails to start, it gives operators time to shut the process down in an orderly, safe sequence rather than wherever the lights went out.

Core Principles

Principle 1: Double conversion — the load never sees the mains

Statement: Incoming AC is rectified to a DC bus, the battery floats on that DC bus, and an inverter manufactures a brand-new sine wave for the load — always, not just during outages.

This is the "online double-conversion" topology (VFI — voltage and frequency independent, per IEC 62040-3). The load is fed from the inverter 24/7; mains dips, harmonics and frequency wobbles are absorbed at the rectifier and never reach the DCS. When the mains dies, nothing switches — the battery simply stops being charged and starts being discharged. Transfer time to battery: zero milliseconds, by construction.

Principle 2: The battery is an energy tank sized in kWh

Statement: Autonomy is energy: battery kWh = load kW × backup hours, grossed up for inverter losses, battery aging and design margin.

Power (kVA/kW) sizes the electronics; energy (kWh) sizes the battery. The two are independent purchases — the same 100 kVA UPS can carry 10 minutes or 2 hours depending on how many battery blocks you bolt on. The worked example sizes ours.

Principle 3: The static bypass is the escape route

Statement: A thyristor switch can connect the load directly to raw mains in under a quarter-cycle if the inverter is overloaded, fails, or must clear a downstream fault.

It seems paradoxical — a device whose whole point is to isolate the load from the mains keeps a direct mains path on hot standby. But the inverter is a polite, current-limited source (Principle 4); when brute force is needed, the bypass supplies it. The bypass only works if inverter output and mains are kept synchronised, which the UPS does continuously whenever mains is healthy.

Principle 4: An inverter is a current-limited source — fault clearing changes character

Statement: Transistors cannot survive the 10–20 × overcurrents a transformer delivers happily; an inverter electronically limits fault current to about 2–3 × In.

Downstream protection philosophy must be rethought: a breaker that relies on a big magnetic-trip current to open instantly may simply never see it. This single fact drives the UPS distribution board design (small breaker ratings, careful grading, bypass-assisted fault clearing) and is computed explicitly below.

Key Innovations

The static transfer switch (1960s–70s)

What: Anti-parallel thyristor pairs that transfer the load between inverter and mains in < 5 ms — faster than any electromechanical device, faster than electronics can notice.

Why it mattered: It made the double-conversion topology trustworthy: the UPS can fail, overload or clear faults without the load ever rebooting. It also enables maintenance bypass — the whole UPS can be wrapped out of the circuit for service while the load runs on raw mains.

VRLA batteries, and now Li-ion (1980s / 2010s)

What: Valve-regulated lead-acid blocks — sealed, rack-mounted, no acid handling, no battery room ventilation in the classic sense. Li-ion arrives with twice the life, half the footprint and far better tolerance of the 25 °C+ battery-room temperatures that plague Saudi installations (VRLA life halves for every ~10 °C above 20–25 °C).

Why it mattered: The battery is the UPS's mortal component. Chemistry and room temperature, not electronics, decide whether the promised 30 minutes exists on the day it is needed.

Eco/hybrid modes — and why we decline them (2000s)

What: Running the load on bypass (raw mains) with the inverter idling, achieving ~99 % efficiency instead of ~94 %, and transferring to inverter only when the mains misbehaves.

Why it mattered: A genuine saving in data centres — but the load briefly tastes the raw mains disturbance before transfer. For a process plant ESD/DCS at 50 °C in a weak-grid corner of the network, the few-percent efficiency is not worth the exposure; Rev 3 specifies true double conversion.

Worked Example: Battery energy for 100 kVA / 30 min — and the selectivity trap

Given: UPS 100 kVA, load power factor 0.8 → 80 kW; autonomy 30 min (0.5 h); inverter efficiency η_inv = 0.94; end-of-life aging factor 1.25 (battery is replaced when capacity falls to 80 %); design margin 1.1 (temperature, tolerance, future load creep); battery string 432 V DC nominal (216 lead-acid cells × 2 V). All values indicative.

Step 1 — Power the battery must deliver. The inverter sits between battery and load, so battery power is grossed up for its losses:

P_dc = P_load / η_inv = 80 / 0.94 = 85.1 kW

Step 2 — Raw energy for 30 minutes.

E_raw = P_dc × t = 85.1 kW × 0.5 h = 42.6 kWh

Why: energy = power × time. Note it is the DC-side 85.1 kW, not the 80 kW load, that drains the tank.

Step 3 — Aging and design margin.

E_design = 42.6 × 1.25 × 1.1 = 58.5 kWh

Why: the 30 minutes must exist on the battery's last good day, not its first, and at the real room temperature, not the datasheet's 20 °C.

Step 4 — Convert to amp-hours at the string voltage.

C = E_design / U_dc = 58 500 Wh / 432 V ≈ 135 Ah → select 150 Ah blocks

Note: real selection uses the manufacturer's constant-power discharge tables (a 30 min discharge extracts less than the 10 h-rate Ah), so vendors typically land on 150–180 Ah for this duty — our arithmetic puts us in the right aisle of the catalogue.

Sanity check: 58.5 kWh is about 1.5 hours of an average Saudi villa's summer consumption packed into two battery cabinets — believable for ~10 m² of plant room.

Step 5 — The selectivity trap. UPS full-load current and fault current:

In = 100 000 / (√3 × 400) = 144 A

I_fault,inverter ≈ 2 to 3 × In = 290–430 A  (for ~100–200 ms)

Now take a 32 A C-curve MCB on the UPS distribution board: its instantaneous (magnetic) trip needs 5–10 × 32 A = 160–320 A. The inverter's 290–430 A may just reach it — or may not, leaving the fault to a slow thermal trip while the whole UPS bus sags and every other critical load browns out.

Verdict and fixes: keep downstream breakers small relative to UPS size (rule of thumb ≤ 20–25 % of In per outgoing way, here ≤ ~32 A, with B-curves preferred), use fast fuses where selectivity is critical, and rely on the static bypass: a healthy mains delivers thousands of amps through the bypass for the few cycles needed to blow the faulty way's protection cleanly, then the load returns to the inverter.

Intuitive Formalization

Two small equations size the machine; one inequality governs its protection. Sizing:

E_batt = (P_load / η_inv) × t × k_age × k_margin

Symbols and their physical meaning: P_load (kW) — the real power the critical loads draw (kVA × their power factor); η_inv — the inverter's efficiency, i.e. the toll the electronics charge on every watt-hour leaving the tank; t (h) — the autonomy promise; k_age ≈ 1.25 — the battery you will own in year 4, not the one you bought; k_margin ≈ 1.1 — humility about temperature and load growth. Then C (Ah) = E_batt / U_dc converts energy to the catalogue quantity, with U_dc the DC string voltage.

Protection: the inverter can deliver at most I_f ≈ (2–3) × In, so any downstream device that must open instantly on inverter power alone needs:

I_magnetic-trip < 2 × In,UPS  (with margin)

If that cannot be met, the fault-clearing muscle must come from the static bypass — design the board assuming it.
