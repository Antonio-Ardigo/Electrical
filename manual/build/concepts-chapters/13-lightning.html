13. Lightning & Surge Protection

Manual deep-dive: open the full section

The Problem

It is tempting to dismiss lightning in Saudi Arabia. The interior plateau sees perhaps 5–15 thunderstorm days a year — a tenth of the tropics — and months can pass without a cloud. But the plant's exposure is not measured in storms per year; it is measured in consequences per strike. A single discharge onto or near the incoming 13.8 kV overhead line injects tens of kilo-amps with a rise time of microseconds, and the travelling voltage wave it launches arrives at MV-SWGR at the speed of light in cable. Spring storms over the escarpments and the Red Sea coast are real, violent, and statistically certain over a 25-year plant life.

And lightning is only the loudest member of the surge family. Every capacitor-bank step switching (Chapter 8, several times a day), every transformer energisation, every fault clearance on the SEC network generates switching surges — smaller than lightning but vastly more frequent. The victims are always the same: insulation, and above all electronics. A 400 V variable frequency drive or a DCS input card has insulation designed to withstand perhaps 1.5–2.5 kV for microseconds; a surge of 6 kV at the main board, left untreated, is a dead drive, and dead drives across a plant are a production outage with a six-week lead time.

Before coordinated protection, plants relied on a lightning rod and hope. The rod protects the building from fire; it does nothing for the electronics, because the surge problem is conducted, not structural. The modern answer has two halves: capture the strike where it can do no harm (air terminals placed by the rolling-sphere method, down-conductors, earthing), and then clamp what still gets onto the wires with a cascade of surge protective devices (SPDs) whose let-through voltage steps down, board by board, below what each category of equipment can survive.

Core Principles

Principle 1: A surge is a travelling wave, fast beyond intuition

Statement: Standard test waves codify reality: 8/20 µs (current reaches crest in 8 µs, decays to half in 20 µs) for induced/switching surges, 10/350 µs for direct strike currents carrying far more charge and energy.

For scale: an entire 8/20 surge is over in less time than the 400 V mains takes to move one degree of its 60 Hz cycle. No breaker, no relay, nothing mechanical can react — only physics already sitting in the circuit (spark gaps, metal-oxide varistors) can act on this timescale.

Principle 2: Equipment withstand is graded in categories (IEC 60664-1)

Statement: On a 230/400 V system, insulation coordination assigns rated impulse withstand by location: Category IV 6 kV (origin of installation — metering, main incomer), Category III 4 kV (distribution boards, MCCs), Category II 2.5 kV (appliances, drives), Category I 1.5 kV (sensitive electronics — DCS cards, network gear).

This ladder is the design contract: the protection scheme's only job is to guarantee that the surge voltage actually arriving at each location stays below that location's number.

Principle 3: An SPD is a voltage-controlled short circuit

Statement: A surge protective device is invisible (megohms) at 230 V but collapses to a near-short within nanoseconds when voltage rises, diverting surge current to earth and "clamping" the voltage at its protection level Up.

Two technologies split the work: spark gaps (Type 1) swallow the brutal 10/350 direct-strike energy; metal-oxide varistors (MOVs) (Type 2/3) clamp lower and faster but with less energy capacity. Jargon defined: Up = the voltage the SPD lets through at its rated discharge current — the single most important number on its nameplate.

Principle 4: The lead length is part of the SPD

Statement: The voltage seen by downstream equipment is Up plus the inductive drop along the SPD's connection conductors: U = Up + L·di/dt, and at surge di/dt even half a metre of wire matters.

A perfect SPD on lazy 1 m tails is a mediocre SPD. The installation rule — total connection length ≤ 0.5 m, or use the V-wiring (through-terminal) connection — comes straight from this principle, and the worked example puts numbers on it.

Key Innovations

The rolling-sphere method (electro-geometric model, 1960s–70s)

What: Imagine a sphere — radius 20 m for the strictest protection level LPL I, 30/45/60 m for LPL II–IV (IEC 62305) — rolled over every surface of the plant. Anywhere the sphere can touch, lightning can strike; air terminals are placed so the sphere rests only on them.

Why it mattered: It replaced guesswork cones with a physical model of the final leader jump, and it reveals the unintuitive: tall tank edges, pipe-rack corners and roof-mounted coolers — not just the highest mast — are strike points needing terminals.

The coordinated SPD cascade (IEC 61643 / 62305, 1990s)

What: Type 1 (spark-gap, 10/350-rated) at the service entrance / LV-MSB; Type 2 (MOV, 8/20-rated) at each distribution board; Type 3 at sensitive terminal equipment within 10 m of the load. Each stage absorbs energy and hands a smaller remnant to the next; decoupling inductance (≥10 m of cable, or coils) ensures they fire in the right order.

Why it mattered: No single device can both swallow 25–100 kA of strike energy and clamp below 1.5 kV. The cascade divides the labour, exactly like the worked example shows.

Lightning protection zones and bonding (IEC 62305-4)

What: The plant is mapped into nested zones LPZ 0 (exposed) → LPZ 1 (inside building) → LPZ 2 (inside cabinet); every conductive service crossing a boundary is bonded or surge-protected at the crossing, and all earths (lightning, protective, instrument, NER) meet a single earthing system.

Why it mattered: Surges enter on any metal — armour, screens, pipes, fences. Zoning turns "protect everything" into a checkable boundary discipline, and a single bonded earth prevents the lethal potential differences that multiple "clean earths" create during a strike.

Worked Example: A 10 kA 8/20 µs surge through the Type 1/2 cascade

Given: Surge 10 kA, 8/20 µs waveform, arriving on the 400 V system at LV-MSB. Type 1 SPD at LV-MSB: Up = 2.5 kV at its rated discharge current. Type 2 SPD at distribution board DB: Up = 1.5 kV. SPD connection leads at the MSB: 0.5 m phase tail + 0.5 m earth tail = 1.0 m total, wire inductance ≈ 1 µH/m. Equipment withstand per IEC 60664-1 (230/400 V): Cat III = 4 kV at boards, Cat II = 2.5 kV at drives, Cat I = 1.5 kV at DCS electronics. All values indicative.

Step 1 — Rate of current rise. The 8/20 wave crests at 10 kA in 8 µs:

di/dt = 10 000 A / 8 µs = 1.25 kA/µs = 1.25 × 10⁹ A/s

Why: inductive voltage depends on the steepness, not the size, of the current — this number drives everything that follows.

Step 2 — Inductive voltage on the SPD leads.

U_lead = L × di/dt = (1.0 m × 1 µH/m) × 1.25 × 10⁹ A/s = 1.0 × 10⁻⁶ × 1.25 × 10⁹ = 1250 V

Note: one metre of ordinary wire "adds" 1.25 kV to a 2.5 kV protector — half the SPD's own rating. This is Principle 4 in cash terms, and why the ≤0.5 m total-lead rule exists.

Step 3 — Let-through voltage at the main board.

U_MSB = Up + U_lead = 2500 + 1250 = 3750 V ≈ 3.75 kV

Step 4 — Compare to withstand categories.

3.75 kV < 4 kV (Cat III) → boards/MCC equipment at the MSB: protected, slim margin

3.75 kV > 2.5 kV (Cat II) → drives, and > 1.5 kV (Cat I) → DCS: NOT protected by Type 1 alone

Why: the Type 1 did its job — it absorbed the energy and brought 10 kA down to a 3.75 kV remnant — but that remnant still kills electronics. The cascade must continue.

Step 5 — Type 2 stage at the DB. At least 10 m of cable (inductance ≈ 10 µH) separates MSB from DB, attenuating the surge front and ensuring coordination; the Type 2 clamps the remnant:

U_DB ≈ Up(Type 2) + lead term = 1500 + (0.5 m × 1 µH/m × reduced di/dt ≈ 0.3 kA/µs) ≈ 1500 + 150 ≈ 1.65 kV

1.65 kV < 2.5 kV (Cat II) → drives protected; for Cat I (1.5 kV) DCS cards: add Type 3 within 10 m

Sanity check: each stage roughly halves the threat (≈3.75 kV → ≈1.65 kV → <1.2 kV at Type 3), and every location ends below its IEC 60664 category with margin. Reverse check: without any SPD, even a modest induced surge of 6 kV exceeds every category below the service entrance — consistent with the field experience that unprotected VFDs die in the first spring storm. Result: Type 1 (MSB) + Type 2 (DBs) + Type 3 (DCS cabinets), short leads, one bonded earth — every equipment class sits below its withstand.

Intuitive Formalization

Surge protection is governed by one additive equation per location and one inequality per equipment class:

U_equipment = Up + L_lead × di/dt   and require   U_equipment < U_withstand(category)

Symbols and their physics: Up (kV) — the SPD's let-through voltage, the residual the clamp cannot remove (a MOV is a resistor whose value collapses with voltage, never to zero); L_lead (µH) — connection inductance, about 1 µH per metre of any practical wire, an irreducible property of geometry; di/dt (kA/µs) — the surge steepness, set by nature (8/20, 10/350) not by us; U_withstand — the IEC 60664-1 ladder (6 / 4 / 2.5 / 1.5 kV at 230/400 V). The cascade exists because no single device satisfies the inequality everywhere: energy capacity and low clamping are opposing virtues, so the Type 1 trades clamping for energy, the Type 3 trades energy for clamping, and the cable between them decouples the stages. For strike capture, the geometric rule is the rolling sphere: a strike terminates wherever a sphere of radius R (20 m at LPL I) can touch — protection means it touches only air terminals.
