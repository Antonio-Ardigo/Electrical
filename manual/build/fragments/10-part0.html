The SPOF Assessment Campaign

You are one of two Electrical Engineers on a four-month campaign to deliver an Electrical Single Point of Failure (SPOF) Assessment across 21 groundwater desalination plants in the Central & Eastern sector — 19 in Riyadh Province and 2 in the Eastern Province (no plants in Madinah or Qassim), 16 sites, ~1.66 M m³/day total. The core team is:

Electrical Technician Optional Non-intrusive field surveys and asset inventory support only — not part of the core team.

What you are asked to do per plant

For each of the 21 plants you are expected to work autonomously through the following:

  • Desk study: review the existing single-line diagrams, equipment schedules and prior studies.
  • Plant calculation package: produce load-flow (verifying bus voltages and cable loadings under normal and N-1 conditions), short-circuit (prospective fault current at each bus), protection coordination (relay grading), and earthing & lightning assessments with power-system software. Arc-flash per IEEE 1584 (incident energy at each working position) and RAM/RBD — Reliability, Availability, Maintainability / Reliability Block Diagram availability modelling (Part 3) — are advantageous.
  • SPOF assessment: inventory and classify electrical single points of failure; assess criticality using FMECA — Failure Mode, Effects and Criticality Analysis per IEC 60812 (Part 3) — which ranks each failure mode by Risk Priority Number (RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection, max 125 on the 1–5 scales used here).
  • SLD verification: red-line the single-line diagrams against the as-found plant during site visits.
  • Concept modifications: for each recommended change, prepare a marked-up SLD, equipment outline specification, scheme description, sequence of operation and high-level bill of materials. Concept level only — not detailed design, procurement or construction.
  • Assessment forms: complete the four per-plant deliverable forms (see Section — The Four Deliverable Forms).
Note — indicative figures: All ratings, capacities and figures in this manual are indicative and tentative. They illustrate method and typical practice. Confirm every value against the plant's actual load list, short-circuit study, utility connection agreement and governing specifications before acting on them.

The Saudi Electrical Context

Where you are in the workflow: before the first desk study, understand who the utility is, what the connection looks like, and which rulebooks govern every plant in this campaign.

Every plant you will assess is connected to the Saudi grid and operates under the Saudi regulatory framework. Even though the engineering inside the fence is recognisably "IEC-style" MV/LV practice, the surrounding context — the utility, the connection voltage, the codes, the certification regime — is specific to the Kingdom. This section gives you the working map. As everywhere in this manual, regulatory details evolve; treat the descriptions below as indicative and confirm the current editions of all codes and the actual connection agreement per plant.

SEC — the utility you connect to

The Saudi Electricity Company (SEC) is the dominant transmission and distribution utility in the Kingdom and the supply authority for the plants in this campaign. From your point of view as an assessment engineer, SEC matters in four practical ways:

  • Connection agreement: each plant has (or should have) a connection agreement with SEC stating the connection voltage, the agreed supply capacity, the metering arrangement and the network fault-level data. This document belongs in your Document Inventory (form SF-01) for every plant — chase it during the desk study.
  • Network data: the utility fault level at the point of connection is an SEC-supplied figure. In this manual we assume a 25 kA design value at 13.8 kV (SEC networks are commonly quoted in the 25–40 kA range, indicative) — confirm per plant before relying on any short-circuit result.
  • Metering and tariffs: revenue metering (MV-MET) is on the SEC side of the ownership boundary; tariff structure and any power-factor or maximum-demand clauses come from the SEC supply contract.
  • Compliance codes: SEC publishes the SEC Distribution Code, which — together with the national Saudi Grid Code — sets the technical conditions a connected customer must meet (see below).
Note — who regulates whom. SEC is the utility, not the regulator. The sector regulator (the Water & Electricity Regulatory Authority, WERA — formerly ECRA) licenses utilities and approves the codes; SEC applies them at the connection point. For your assessments the practical interface is SEC: the connection agreement, the Distribution Code and the network data all come through the utility.

Connection at 13.8 kV distribution

A ~2 MW industrial plant in Saudi Arabia is normally connected at the 13.8 kV SEC distribution level — too large for an LV service, far too small for a transmission connection. The typical arrangement, and the one assumed throughout this manual, is:

  • SEC feeder(s) terminate in a utility metering cubicle (MV-MET) at the plant boundary — this is the ownership and metering demarcation point;
  • the customer's 13.8 kV metal-clad switchgear (MV-SWGR, 17.5 kV class, indicative 31.5 kA / 3 s rating) distributes to the two 13.8/0.4 kV, 1600 kVA transformers (TX-1/TX-2, Dyn11, Z = 6 %, MV FLC ≈ 66.9 A each, indicative);
  • everything downstream of MV-MET is the customer's responsibility — which is exactly the scope of your SPOF assessment.

Other SEC distribution voltages exist (33 kV and legacy levels in some areas), and a few plants may have non-standard arrangements. Always confirm the actual connection voltage and the demarcation point from the connection agreement and the as-found installation — a red-line on the SLD at the boundary is one of the most common site-visit findings.

Saudi Grid Code essentials for an industrial customer

The Saudi Grid Code and the SEC Distribution Code define the technical envelope of the supply and the customer's obligations. For an industrial customer of this size the essentials are (all values indicative — verify against the current code edition and the connection agreement):

  • Frequency: the national grid operates at 60 Hz. Normal operation is held within a narrow band around 60 Hz (roughly ±0.5 %, i.e. ~59.7–60.3 Hz under normal conditions, indicative), with wider excursions permitted briefly during disturbances. Your underfrequency/overfrequency protection and any generator controls must coordinate with these bands.
  • Voltage: distribution-level supply voltage is typically maintained within about ±5 % of nominal at the connection point under normal conditions (indicative); the customer must keep its own disturbances (flicker, harmonics, unbalance) within code limits.
  • Power factor: a contractual expectation of power factor ≥ 0.9 (lagging) at the connection point is the typical basis (indicative — the exact threshold and any penalty mechanism are tariff/contract matters). This is the commercial driver behind the PFC bank: this plant's design targets 0.95, i.e. ≈ 580 kvar of detuned capacitors, comfortably inside the expectation.
  • Harmonics and power quality: emission limits at the point of connection follow the codes (aligned with the IEC 61000 series); VFD-heavy plants like these deserve attention here — hence the 7 % detuning reactors on the PFC bank.
  • Protection coordination at the boundary: the customer's incoming protection must grade with the SEC feeder protection; setting changes at the incomer normally require utility agreement.
Tip — what to actually check on a plant. You are not auditing Grid Code compliance in detail. But three quick checks belong in every assessment: (1) does the metered power factor honour the contractual basis, (2) do the incomer relay settings match the SEC-agreed settings record, and (3) is the declared fault level in the short-circuit study consistent with the SEC network data? A mismatch on any of these is a finding.

SASO and the adoption of IEC standards

The Saudi Standards, Metrology and Quality Organization (SASO) is the national standards body. Rather than writing electrical standards from scratch, SASO largely adopts IEC standards, issuing them as Saudi national standards (typically referenced as SASO IEC xxxxx), sometimes with national deviations (notably the 60 Hz frequency, 230/400 V utilisation voltage and the 50 °C climatic context). The practical consequences for you:

  • your IEC knowledge transfers almost directly — switchgear is still IEC 62271-series equipment, transformers are still IEC 60076 machines, LV assemblies still follow IEC 61439;
  • when citing a standard in a deliverable, cite the SASO-adopted edition where one exists, alongside the SEC codes and SBC 401;
  • watch for the national deviations: ratings stated for 60 Hz or 40 °C ambient in a generic IEC catalogue sheet do not automatically hold at 60 Hz and 50 °C (see Designing for 50 °C and Dust).

SABER — the conformity certification platform

SABER is the Saudi online conformity-assessment platform through which regulated products imported into or sold in the Kingdom obtain their Product Certificate of Conformity and per-consignment Shipment Certificate, issued via SASO-approved certification bodies. Many categories of electrical equipment are regulated products. Why an assessment engineer should care:

  • when your Recommendations Register leads to a concept modification with new equipment, the procurement phase will require SABER certification for regulated items — flag this in the outline specification so it is not discovered at customs;
  • vendor "typical" data sheets are not evidence of Saudi acceptance — ask for the certified data sheet and SASO/SABER status (see the catalogue disclaimer convention in Part 6);
  • lead times for certification are real schedule items in any modification plan.

SBC 401 — the Saudi Building Code, Electrical

SBC 401 is the electrical part of the Saudi Building Code. It governs electrical installations in buildings and facilities in the Kingdom and is grounded in the IEC 60364 installation rules (with national adaptations for 230/400 V, 60 Hz and the Saudi climate). For the plants in this campaign, SBC 401 is most relevant to:

  • LV installation practice: earthing arrangement (TN-S in this manual's basis), protection against electric shock, RCD requirements for final circuits, cable installation and identification;
  • Buildings on the plant: switchrooms, control rooms, admin buildings — their wiring, lighting, socket circuits and emergency systems fall squarely under SBC 401;
  • Modification packages: any concept modification touching an LV installation should cite SBC 401 alongside the SASO-adopted IEC standards in its outline specification.
Note — how the three layers stack. Think of the regulatory stack in three layers: SASO-adopted IEC standards define what good equipment and installations are; SBC 401 defines how installations in buildings must be designed and erected; the SEC Distribution Code / Saudi Grid Code define the rules at the connection point with the utility. A compliant plant satisfies all three simultaneously. In your deliverables, cite them together.
Key takeaway: SEC supplies at 13.8 kV / 60 Hz under the SEC Distribution Code and the Saudi Grid Code (power factor ≥ 0.9 typical contractual basis, indicative); SASO adopts the IEC standards you already know; SABER certifies the products you specify; SBC 401 governs the building installations. Cite all of them together, and confirm current editions and the per-plant connection agreement before issuing any deliverable.

Designing for 50 °C and Dust

Where you are in the workflow: calibrate your engineering instincts to the Saudi environment before you size, check or inspect anything — the climate quietly invalidates many "standard" ratings.

Most IEC equipment ratings are written for a 40 °C maximum ambient (often 35 °C average). The plants in this campaign live in a different world: 50 °C design ambient, intense solar radiation, wind-blown dust and sand, and — at coastal and high-water-table sites — aggressive humidity and salinity. Almost every reliability and sizing judgement you make must pass through this filter. All figures below are indicative; site climatic data and manufacturer derating curves govern.

50 °C ambient — the dominant design driver

Heat is the single most important environmental variable for electrical equipment, because almost every failure mechanism — insulation ageing, contact deterioration, electronics drift, battery decay — accelerates with temperature. A useful rule of thumb: every 8–10 °C of additional operating temperature roughly halves the life of organic insulation (the classic Arrhenius/Montsinger behaviour). Running gear designed for 40 °C at a true 50 °C ambient is therefore not a marginal note — it can halve insulation life and erode every thermal margin in the plant.

What it means in practice on these plants:

  • Transformers: an IEC 60076 transformer's rated temperature rise assumes a standard ambient; at 50 °C it must either be purchased with a suitably reduced rise (the usual Saudi practice) or operated below nameplate. Check the nameplate ambient on site — a "standard ambient" unit at full load in a Saudi summer is a finding.
  • Switchgear and busbars: continuous current ratings are ambient-referenced; a board rated 2500 A at 40 °C may carry meaningfully less at 50 °C switchroom temperature. The LV main board carrying ≈ 2309 A per transformer (indicative) has little slack to waste.
  • The diesel generator: engine and alternator outputs fall with intake air temperature and altitude — which is exactly why this plant's standby set is specified as 1250 kVA ESP at 50 °C (indicative), not at the brochure's reference conditions.
  • Electronics (VFDs, relays, UPS): most drives derate above 40 °C (typically a few percent of output current per °C, indicative) and their cooling fans become life-limiting components.
Warning — nameplate ≠ usable rating. On site, never accept a nameplate rating at face value. Ask: at what ambient was this rating declared, and what is the real ambient where the equipment lives? A piece of equipment that is "adequately rated" on paper but thermally over-driven every summer afternoon is a slow-motion failure — and a legitimate FMECA entry.

Solar gain on outdoor enclosures

Air temperature is only part of the story. Direct solar radiation on an outdoor enclosure adds heat the ambient figure does not capture: an unshaded metal kiosk, feeder pillar or marshalling box in the Saudi sun commonly runs 10–15 °C above the surrounding air internally (indicative) — so a 50 °C day can mean 60–65 °C inside the box, beyond the working range of much electronics and battery equipment.

  • Prefer shaded or indoor locations for anything electronic; a simple sunshade canopy over an outdoor panel is one of the cheapest reliability upgrades available.
  • Specify light-coloured / reflective finishes and, where applicable, double-skin (ventilated) roofs on outdoor enclosures.
  • On site, note the orientation of outdoor panels: a west-facing door in afternoon sun is the worst case. Touch-test (back of hand, carefully) and record suspiciously hot enclosures in the Site-Visit Report.

Dust and sand — ingress, filters and creepage

Wind-blown dust and occasional sandstorms are routine in the Central sector. Dust attacks electrical equipment in three distinct ways, each with its own countermeasure:

  • Ingress: dust inside enclosures blankets components (worsening heat), abrades moving parts and contaminates contacts. Specify minimum IP54 for outdoor enclosures (IP65 for instruments and small boxes is common practice, indicative) per IEC 60529, and keep switchroom doors and gland plates properly sealed. On site, dust drifts inside a panel mean the IP integrity is already lost — record it.
  • Ventilation and filters: any forced-ventilated equipment (switchroom HVAC, generator radiator and intake, VFD cooling paths) needs sand-trap louvres and serviceable filters — and a maintenance regime that actually changes them. A clogged filter quietly converts a ventilation system into a heat trap; ask to see the filter-change records.
  • Creepage and tracking: dust layers on insulators, combined with the morning condensation events of desert climates, create conductive films that promote surface tracking — especially at MV. Outdoor MV insulation in dusty/saline areas is normally specified with increased creepage distance (heavy pollution class per IEC/TS 60815, indicative), and periodic insulator cleaning belongs in the maintenance plan.

Humidity and corrosion — coastal C4/C5 conditions

The Eastern Province sites (and any site near sabkha/salt flats or with saline groundwater — common around groundwater desalination plants by definition) combine heat with chloride-laden humidity. In ISO 12944 atmospheric-corrosivity terms these environments are typically category C4 (high) to C5 (very high) — indicative; classify per site.

  • Specify C4/C5 paint systems (or hot-dip galvanising plus duplex coating) for outdoor steelwork, cable trays, ladder racks and enclosures; stainless fasteners and hinges.
  • Inside panels, look for green/white corrosion bloom on copper and aluminium terminations — a classic precursor to hot joints. Anti-condensation heaters must actually be working (a failed panel heater is a frequent and easy site finding).
  • Beware dissimilar-metal joints (aluminium lug on copper bar) in humid salty air — galvanic corrosion is accelerated; bimetallic connectors and inhibitor compound are the standard defence.

Cable derating at 50 °C — the ≈ 0.82 factor

Published current-carrying capacities for XLPE cables are tabulated at a reference ambient (30 °C in air for many IEC 60364-5-52 tables). At a 50 °C air ambient, the correction factor for 90 °C XLPE insulation is approximately 0.82 (indicative) — a cable "good for" 100 A in the table is good for about 82 A here, before any grouping or solar-exposure corrections, which stack multiplicatively on top.

Quick feel — what 0.82 does to a feeder

I_permissible = I_table × k_ambient = 100 A × 0.82 = 82 A

Stack a grouping factor of, say, 0.8 for several touching cables on a tray and the same cable is down to ≈ 66 A — two-thirds of its table rating. This is why cables sized "by the table" in a temperate-climate design routinely turn out undersized in the Kingdom. Full method and worked examples in Part 2 — Cable Selection.

On site, treat any cable route in direct sun, any heavily-bundled tray, and any duct bank in hot ground as derating hot-spots, and check whether the plant's cable schedule states the ambient on which its sizing was based.

Batteries — life halves every 8–10 °C above 20 °C

Stationary batteries (the UPS bank and the 110 V DC DCDB system that trips the MV switchgear) are the plant components most punished by heat. The design life of a VRLA battery is quoted at 20–25 °C; as a rule of thumb, service life halves for every 8–10 °C above 20 °C (indicative). A "10-year" VRLA block living at 35 °C is realistically a 2–3 year battery; at 40 °C+, worse.

  • Battery rooms/areas must be air-conditioned and held near 20–25 °C; verify the actual room temperature on every site visit, not just the set-point.
  • Check battery installation date vs design life corrected for the real room temperature — an aged battery in a hot room is a high-criticality FMECA entry, because the DC system is the classic hidden SPOF: when it fails, MV protection cannot trip (see Part 3).
  • Where rooms cannot be reliably cooled, consider NiCd chemistry (more temperature-tolerant, indicative) in the Recommendations Register.

HVAC as a reliability-critical system

Put the threads together and an uncomfortable conclusion emerges: in this climate the switchroom air-conditioning is part of the electrical protection system. If the HVAC fails on a summer afternoon, room temperature can climb past equipment limits within hours — VFDs trip or derate, batteries cook, relays and UPS electronics drift toward failure. A single non-redundant air-conditioning unit serving a critical switchroom is a genuine single point of failure and must appear in your SPOF inventory like any breaker or transformer.

  • Look for N+1 redundancy (two units, each able to carry the room) on critical switchrooms and battery rooms; record the as-found arrangement.
  • Check for a high-temperature alarm in the room, routed to a place where somebody actually responds — an unalarmed HVAC failure is found only when equipment starts tripping.
  • Ask what happens on power failure: is the HVAC on the essential (DG-backed) supply? A diesel-backed plant whose switchroom cooling is on the non-essential bus can ride through the outage and then overheat during it.
Tip — the afternoon test. Whenever practicable, time part of your site visit for the early afternoon. Equipment, rooms and enclosures show their true thermal behaviour at 14:00 in July, not at 08:00 in January. A thermal camera (where permitted, non-intrusively) earns its keep in this hour.

Field checklist — environment

  • Record switchroom and battery-room temperatures (actual, not set-point) and the HVAC arrangement (single / N+1, essential-supply backed, high-temperature alarm).
  • Check nameplate ambient ratings of transformers, switchgear and the DG against the 50 °C design ambient; flag any standard-ambient equipment running near full load.
  • Inspect outdoor enclosures for solar exposure, IP integrity (seals, gland plates, dust drifts inside) and condition of sand-trap louvres / filters.
  • Inspect for corrosion: coating condition (C4/C5 environments), terminal bloom inside panels, working anti-condensation heaters, dissimilar-metal joints.
  • Note cable routes in direct sun or heavy bundling; confirm the cable schedule states its sizing ambient (expect ≈ 0.82 air derating at 50 °C).
  • Verify battery installation dates against temperature-corrected life expectancy; record room temperature next to the battery age.
Key takeaway: 50 °C ambient is the dominant design driver — it derates cables (≈ 0.82), transformers, switchgear, drives and the DG; solar gain adds another 10–15 °C inside outdoor enclosures; dust demands IP54+, filters and creepage margin; coastal salt demands C4/C5 protection; heat halves battery life every 8–10 °C above 20 °C. Treat the switchroom HVAC as reliability-critical — in this climate, cooling failures are electrical failures. All figures indicative; site data and manufacturer curves govern.
