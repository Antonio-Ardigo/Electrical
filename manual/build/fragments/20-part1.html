Worked example — Transformer sizing from demand

Given ~2 MW demand at 0.85 power factor, size the transformer bank:

S = P / pf = 2000 kW / 0.85 ≈ 2353 kVA

Apply a design and diversity margin of +20–25 %:

S_design = 2353 × 1.25 ≈ 2941 kVA

Split across two transformers (N-1 split-bus), select next standard size:

S_each = 2941 / 2 ≈ 1471 kVA → select 2 × 1600 kVA

Result: 2 × 1600 kVA, Dyn11, 13.8 kV / 0.4 kV (tags TX-1 and TX-2). Values are indicative.

Power architecture — the master SLD

The master single-line diagram below shows the complete reference power architecture. Study it before proceeding — every section of Part 1 refers to items on this drawing.

The architecture has three tiers:

  1. MV (13.8 kV) tier: Utility feed → metering (MV-MET) → MV switchgear (MV-SWGR) with protection IEDs (MV-PROT) and neutral earthing resistor (MV-NER).
  2. Transformation tier: Two independent transformer feeders supply TX-1 and TX-2, each stepping 13.8 kV down to 400 V.
  3. LV (400 V) tier: Main switchboard (LV-MSB) with a split bus (A and B) joined by a normally-open bus-tie (BT); Motor Control Centres (MCC-1, MCC-2), PFC bank, distribution boards, essential board (EDB), diesel generator (DG), ATS, UPS and DC battery system (DCDB).
Key takeaway: The ~2 MW reference plant is the template for everything in this manual. Two 1600 kVA transformers on a split LV bus with a normally-open bus-tie give partial N-1 resilience — understanding why this architecture is used, and where it is still vulnerable, is the core of the SPOF assessment.

How to Read an SLD

Where you are in the workflow: before the site visit, you must be comfortable reading any MV/LV SLD you are handed, not just the reference drawing.

Power-flow direction and voltage levels

Read an SLD top to bottom — energy flows from the utility source at the top to the loads (motors, boards) at the bottom. On the reference drawing there are two clearly separated horizontal zones:

  • MV zone (upper band): 13.8 kV — from the utility intake down to and including the transformer primaries. MV busbars are drawn in firebrick red on colour drawings.
  • LV zone (lower band): 400 V — from the transformer secondaries to all distribution equipment and loads. LV busbars are drawn in navy blue.

The two transformers (TX-1, TX-2) are the physical and electrical boundary between the MV and LV zones.

Busbar convention and the split bus

The LV main switchboard (LV-MSB) is drawn as a split bus: Bus A (left, fed by TX-1) and Bus B (right, fed by TX-2) are two physically separate copper bars in the one board. They are joined only through the normally-open bus-tie (BT). In normal operation each transformer supplies its own half of the plant independently. The split-bus arrangement:

  • limits the fault current that a single transformer must interrupt,
  • contains the blast zone of any bus fault to one section, and
  • allows the bus-tie to close automatically on loss of one incomer, maintaining supply to both buses from the surviving transformer.

Red-lining on site

On a site visit, you compare the SLD against the as-found installation and mark discrepancies in red on a working print or a PDF annotation tool. Common discrepancies to look for:

  • Breaker labelling or device number differs from drawing.
  • An extra or missing feeder — a circuit on site not shown on the drawing.
  • Bus-tie in a different normal position (open or closed) from the drawing.
  • Equipment substituted (e.g. RMU instead of metal-clad switchgear).
  • Protection relay type or settings different from what is listed.
  • Cables or transformers replaced with a different rating.
Key takeaway: Read SLDs top-to-bottom (source to load), know the IEC 60617 symbols, and understand the split-bus concept. On site, every discrepancy between drawing and reality is a red-line — and potentially a SPOF waiting to be found.

PCC vs MCC

Where you are in the workflow: this distinction is fundamental — confusing the two is one of the most common errors on assessment forms.

Both the PCC and the MCC are LV assemblies built to IEC 61439-1/-2, and they can look similar in a switchroom. They do very different jobs:

Note — vocabulary variation: In different organisations, the PCC is also called the Main LV Switchboard (MLVSB), Main Distribution Board (MDB), or simply the Main Board. The MCC may be called a Motor Control Panel (MCP) or a starter board. The function — bulk distribution vs. individual motor control — is the reliable distinguishing criterion, regardless of what the nameplate says.
Tip — SPOF implication: Because the PCC is the single point through which all transformer power passes before reaching the MCCs, a fault or loss of the PCC is a plant-level SPOF. The split bus with bus-tie is specifically designed to limit this vulnerability. When assessing a plant, check whether the PCC is truly split-bus or is effectively a single-bus board with cosmetic segregation.
Key takeaway: PCC (LV-MSB) = bulk distribution hub, fed by transformers, feeds MCCs. MCC = motor starter assembly, fed by PCC, feeds individual motors. The PCC feeds the MCCs; the MCCs feed the loads.

Power-Flow Walk-Through

Where you are in the workflow: tracing the three power paths is what you do mentally at every site visit — it reveals where the architecture is robust and where it is not.

Keep the master SLD (Fig 1-1) open alongside this section. There are three distinct paths through the plant — trace each one on the drawing as you read.

Path 1 — Main (normal) path: utility to process motor

  1. Utility 13.8 kV feed arrives at the site and passes through the revenue metering cubicle (MV-MET) where energy is metered for tariff billing.
  2. The supply enters the MV switchgear (MV-SWGR) via incomer breaker [52-I]. The protection IEDs (MV-PROT) watch this incomer and the transformer feeders for faults. The 13.8 kV neutral is earthed through the resistor (MV-NER).
  3. Two transformer feeder breakers [52-T1] and [52-T2] supply the two transformers (TX-1 and TX-2), which step 13.8 kV down to 400 V.
  4. Each transformer secondary feeds its LV incomer ACB: TX-1 → ACB-A → Bus A, TX-2 → ACB-B → Bus B. The bus-tie (BT) between them is normally open, so the two bus sections are independent.
  5. Outgoing MCCB feeders from Bus A and Bus B supply the Motor Control Centres (MCC-1, MCC-2), the PFC bank (PFC), distribution boards (DB) and the essential board (EDB).
  6. Inside an MCC, the appropriate starter (DOL, soft-starter or a VFD) energises the process motor — pump, fan, conveyor or mixer.
Tip — tracing on a real SLD: On a site SLD that may be larger or more complex, follow the same top-to-bottom discipline and identify: (a) the metering point, (b) the MV incomer and switchgear, (c) the transformer(s), (d) the LV main board and its bus arrangement, (e) the MCC feeders, and (f) the standby and DC paths. This sequence works on any MV/LV plant drawing.

Path 2 — Standby path: on utility loss

When the utility supply is lost (or falls outside acceptable voltage/frequency limits):

  1. The ATS detects utility loss and issues an auto-start signal to the diesel generator (DG).
  2. The DG starts, ramps to rated speed, and the AVR brings the voltage to 400 V ± tolerance.
  3. The ATS transfers the essential bus from the utility feed to the DG output — the transfer is mechanically interlocked to ensure only one source feeds the EDB at a time.
  4. The essential distribution board (EDB) remains energised — escape lighting, fire systems and critical process auxiliaries stay live.
  5. Within the LV-MSB, if one incomer ACB trips (loss of one transformer), the bus-tie (BT) auto-closes so the surviving transformer can supply both Bus A and Bus B (at reduced capacity — load shedding may be needed).
Note — ATS changeover time: The reference plant assumes an open-transition ATS with a total changeover (diesel start + transfer) of ≤ 10 s (indicative); older or poorly maintained sets may take 10–30 s. During this window, only the UPS-backed loads remain energised. The EDB is de-energised for this interval unless the ATS uses a static (electronic) transfer or the EDB has its own small UPS. Verify the actual changeover time on site and confirm it is acceptable for the essential loads.

Path 3 — DC / control path: always alive

The control and protection path is designed to be available at all times — even during a complete AC blackout:

  1. The DCDB (DC battery and charger) is float-charged from the LV supply and holds its battery at full charge continuously. On loss of AC, the battery sustains the DC bus.
  2. The 110 V DC bus from the DCDB feeds the trip and close coils of the MV switchgear breakers and the MV protection relay IEDs. The protection system remains operative — it can still detect faults and trip breakers — even with no AC available.
  3. The UPS is float-charged from the LV AC bus (typically Bus B). On loss of AC it switches seamlessly to its own battery (static changeover, no break). It supplies the DCS, PLC, SCADA and instrumentation at clean regulated AC.
  4. Together, the DCDB and UPS ensure that both protection (MV DC) and control (AC to DCS/instruments) remain available for the full battery autonomy period, giving the operator visibility and the ability to operate the MV switchgear during a blackout.
Tip — SPOF on the DC path: A common finding is that both the UPS and the DCDB charger are supplied from the same LV bus section or even the same feeder, removing their independence. On site, verify that the UPS and DCDB have segregated sources. Also check the battery condition and last test date — an aged or untested battery is a hidden SPOF that does not show up on the SLD.
Key takeaway: Three paths — normal (utility to motors), standby (DG via ATS to essential loads), and DC/control (DCDB to MV switchgear; UPS to DCS). Trace all three on every SLD you review; the points where paths converge or where independence is lost are your SPOF candidates.

Question 1

Which two tags mark the boundary between the 13.8 kV and the 400 V parts of the plant? State what each one does.

Show answer

Question 2

What is the difference between LV-MSB and MCC-1? Which one feeds which?

Show answer

LV-MSB is the PCC (Power Control Centre) / Main LV Switchboard — it receives the transformer outputs and distributes bulk 400 V power. Think "the LV power hub."

MCC-1 is a Motor Control Centre — an assembly of motor starters (DOL, soft-starter, VFD) that control and protect individual process motors. Think "where the motors are started."

The LV-MSB feeds MCC-1 (via an outgoing MCCB feeder from Bus A). MCC-1 feeds the individual motors.

Question 3

The bus-tie BT is normally open. What event causes it to close automatically, and why is that desirable?

Show answer

The bus-tie closes automatically on the trip or loss of either LV incomer ACB (i.e. loss of one transformer or its MV feeder). The auto-close logic detects the incomer trip and closes BT so the surviving transformer can carry both Bus A and Bus B loads.

This is desirable because it prevents loss of supply to half the plant whenever one transformer is lost — it is the primary N-1 resilience feature of the LV distribution. Without it, loss of TX-1 would immediately shed all Bus A loads (MCC-1, PFC, DB-A and any Bus A feeders).

Question 4

Name the three tags on the standby/essential power path and state the order in which energy flows through them on a utility outage.

Show answer

DG → ATS → EDB.

On a utility outage: the DG starts (auto-start signal from the ATS), ramps to rated voltage and frequency; the ATS transfers the essential bus from the utility source to the DG output; the EDB (essential distribution board) remains energised, supplying escape lighting, fire systems and critical process auxiliaries.

Question 5

Why does the plant need a separate DCDB when it already has a UPS? What does each supply?

Show answer

They serve two different and independently critical supply circuits:

  • UPS supplies AC power (400 V or 230 V) to the DCS, PLC, SCADA and instrumentation systems. These are AC-powered electronics that need a clean, regulated, uninterrupted supply.
  • DCDB supplies 110 V DC to the trip and close coils of the MV switchgear breakers and to the MV protection relay IEDs. These must operate even when the AC system is completely dead — a DC battery is the only supply that can guarantee this.

If you replaced the DCDB with a UPS, the MV switchgear would lose its ability to trip or close once the UPS itself lost its input feed. The DCDB's battery is the last line of protection operability during a full AC blackout.

Question 6

What is the purpose of MV-NER, and what would happen if it were removed (neutral left floating)?

Show answer

MV-NER (the Neutral Earthing Resistor) earths the 13.8 kV system neutral through a resistor to limit earth-fault current to approximately 300–400 A (indicative). This value is high enough for earth-fault protection relays to detect the fault reliably, but low enough to limit damage to equipment and reduce the step/touch voltage hazard at the fault location.

If the NER were removed and the neutral left floating (isolated neutral system):

  • An earth fault would not produce a reliable fault current — the protection relay could not detect it dependably, meaning the fault might persist and worsen.
  • The healthy phases would rise to line-to-line voltage above earth, stressing cable insulation and equipment rated for line-to-earth voltage.
  • Transient overvoltages (ferro-resonance) could develop on the unearthed network, potentially damaging voltage transformers and other equipment.

Why 60 Hz Changes Things

Where you are in the workflow: this Saudi (SEC) edition of the manual is written for a 60 Hz grid. If your previous experience is on 50 Hz plants — Europe, most of Asia and Africa, the rest of the GCC — this section tells you exactly which of your instincts still hold and which must be recalibrated before you touch a calculation or a nameplate.

What frequency physically means

System frequency is the number of complete alternating-current cycles per second, in hertz. On the SEC grid the voltage on every conductor swings from positive peak, through zero, to negative peak and back 60 times every second — a full cycle takes 1/60 s ≈ 16.7 ms (compared with 20 ms on a 50 Hz grid).

Frequency is the heartbeat of the grid. It is set by the rotational speed of the synchronous generators feeding the network, and — unlike voltage, which differs from busbar to busbar — frequency is the same everywhere on an interconnected AC system at any instant. Every generator in the Kingdom spins in lock-step; every transformer core magnetises and demagnetises 60 times a second; every induction motor chases a magnetic field rotating at a speed fixed by that same 60 Hz. When load exceeds generation the whole grid slows together (frequency droops); when generation exceeds load it speeds up. That is why frequency is the universal balance indicator used by the grid operator — and why nothing inside your plant can choose its own frequency (except behind a VFD or a UPS inverter, which synthesise their own).

Note — voltage is local, frequency is global. A voltage problem (sag, drop, unbalance) can exist on one feeder of your plant only. A frequency excursion is a system-wide event affecting the entire SEC grid — if you see 58 Hz on a plant meter, either the grid is in serious trouble or (far more likely) you are looking at the output of an islanded generator or a drive. This distinction matters when diagnosing power-quality complaints on site.

Because frequency enters directly into the physics of rotating machines, magnetic cores and reactive components, moving a design from 50 Hz to 60 Hz changes real numbers — speeds, fluxes, reactances, kvar. The changes are systematic and predictable. The table in the next sub-section is the master list; the sub-sections after it explain the three consequences that cause real trouble in the field: motor shaft power, transformer flux, and capacitor-bank tuning.

Motors: +20 % speed can mean +73 % power

An induction motor's speed is locked to frequency by the synchronous-speed formula n = 120 × f / p (p = number of poles). A 4-pole machine therefore runs at 1500 rpm synchronous on a 50 Hz grid and 1800 rpm on the 60 Hz SEC grid (actual shaft speed is 2–3 % lower because of slip — typically ~1470 rpm vs ~1775 rpm). The motor itself does not mind: a 60 Hz-wound motor is designed for that speed.

The trap is the driven machine. Centrifugal pumps and fans obey the affinity laws at fixed impeller/blade geometry:

  • Flow ∝ speed — at +20 % speed the pump moves +20 % flow;
  • Head (pressure) ∝ speed² — (1.2)² = 1.44, i.e. +44 % head;
  • Shaft power ∝ speed³ — (1.2)³ = 1.728, i.e. up to +73 % power.

Read that last line again, because it surprises everyone the first time: a "modest" 20 % speed increase nearly doubles the power a centrifugal load demands. (The exact increase depends on where the new operating point lands on the system curve — the cube law is the worst case at fixed system resistance — but +50 to +73 % is realistic.) This is why a pump set engineered for a 50 Hz country can never simply be shipped to a 60 Hz site: either the impeller is trimmed for the higher speed, a different (larger) motor is fitted, or a VFD is installed to run the pump back down to its design speed.

Worked comparison — the same pump at 50 Hz and 60 Hz

A centrifugal pump absorbs 70 kW at 1470 rpm on a 50 Hz grid, driven by a 75 kW 4-pole motor. The skid is relocated to a 60 Hz plant without modification. Estimate the new shaft power.

New speed: n₂ ≈ n₁ × (60/50) = 1470 × 1.2 ≈ 1764 rpm Affinity (power): P₂ = P₁ × (n₂/n₁)³ = 70 × (1.2)³ = 70 × 1.728 ≈ 121 kW

The 75 kW motor is now asked for ≈121 kW — an overload of about 61 %:

Loading = 121 / 75 ≈ 161 % of motor rating

Result: the thermal overload relay trips within minutes — or, if someone has "solved" the nuisance tripping by raising the overload setting, the winding insulation cooks instead. Sanity check: flow is +20 % and head +44 %, so the process side also receives far more than design duty — the hydraulic numbers confirm the electrical ones. All values indicative.

Tip — what to look for on site: on any pump or fan skid, compare the motor nameplate frequency and speed (e.g. "60 Hz / 1775 rpm") against the driven-machine data plate. A 50 Hz pump curve pinned in the file with a 60 Hz motor on the skid is a finding even if nothing has tripped yet — it usually means the margins have been silently consumed.

Transformers: flux, and the one-way compatibility rule

A transformer core's peak magnetic flux is set by the voltage-per-cycle it must absorb: Φ_max = V / (4.44 × f × N) (N = turns). Voltage in the numerator, frequency in the denominator. Hold the voltage constant and raise the frequency, and the flux falls; lower the frequency and the flux rises. This gives a simple, asymmetric compatibility rule:

  • 50 Hz-designed unit operated at 60 Hz, same voltage: flux drops to 50/60 ≈ 83 % of design — the core runs further from saturation. Magnetically safe (verify accessories: fans, tap-changer motors, instrumentation rated for 60 Hz).
  • 60 Hz-designed unit operated at 50 Hz, same voltage: flux rises to 60/50 = 120 % of design. Transformer cores are deliberately designed close to the knee of the magnetising curve to save iron, so +20 % flux drives the core into saturation: the magnetising current jumps from ~1 % to tens of percent, it becomes sharply distorted, the core overheats and hums loudly, and the unit can fail in hours. A 60 Hz-designed transformer must never be used on a 50 Hz system at the same voltage.
Warning — second-hand and relocated equipment. The dangerous direction (60 Hz design → 50 Hz grid) is exactly the direction equipment travels when a Saudi plant is decommissioned and its transformers are resold into 50 Hz markets — or when a "spare" sourced from a 60 Hz fleet is fitted in a 50 Hz sister plant. Within the Kingdom you are safe; across borders, the nameplate frequency is a hard constraint. For our plant, TX-1 / TX-2 are specified as 60 Hz units (13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z = 6 %) per the transformer catalogue.

Reactance, capacitors and the detuned PFC bank

Inductive reactance is directly proportional to frequency — X = 2πfL — so every reactance expressed in ohms is 20 % higher at 60 Hz for the same physical inductance: cable reactance, current-limiting reactors, transformer leakage reactance in ohms. (Percent impedances quoted on nameplates, like the Z = 6 % of TX-1/TX-2, are already stated on the equipment's own rated-frequency base and carry through unchanged into per-unit fault calculations.) Capacitors mirror this: reactive output Q = 2πfCV² is +20 % at 60 Hz for the same capacitance and voltage, and so is the capacitor current.

Where both effects meet is the detuned power-factor-correction bank (PFC, ≈580 kvar indicative). Each capacitor step has a series reactor sized at 7 % of the capacitor reactance, creating a series-resonant circuit tuned below the lowest characteristic harmonic so the bank cannot resonate with VFD harmonics:

f_res = f_supply / √0.07 → 50 Hz system: 189 Hz | 60 Hz system: 227 Hz

On the 60 Hz SEC grid the tuning point is ≈227 Hz — comfortably below the 5th harmonic at 300 Hz, exactly as 189 Hz sits below 250 Hz on a 50 Hz grid. But this only works when the reactor and capacitors are engineered as a 60 Hz set: pairing a 50 Hz reactor with 60 Hz capacitors (or vice versa) shifts the tuning point unpredictably and can park it near a real harmonic, turning the protection device into an amplifier. Order PFC equipment explicitly for 13.8 kV-network/400 V, 60 Hz service and check the step nameplates on site.

Why Saudi Arabia is a 60 Hz country

The Kingdom's 60 Hz standard is a legacy of who built the first big power systems. Electrification at scale began in the Eastern Province with the oil industry from the late 1930s onward, engineered to North American practice — 60 Hz. As regional utilities grew and were later consolidated into SEC, the Kingdom standardised nationally on 60 Hz with 230/400 V utilisation (the voltage was unified by royal decision in the 1990s, with conversion of older 127/220 V areas phased over many years). This makes Saudi Arabia — together with North America, parts of Japan and a few other states — one of the minority of 60 Hz countries, and notably different from the rest of the GCC, which is 50 Hz. That is why the GCC Interconnection grid ties into the Saudi system through back-to-back HVDC converters (at Al Fadhili): direct AC interconnection between a 50 Hz and a 60 Hz system is impossible. All historical details indicative — interesting context, not assessment input.

The SEC Network Upstream

Where you are in the workflow: your assessment boundary starts at the 13.8 kV intake, but the design numbers at that boundary — voltage, fault level, tariff, reliability — are all dictated by what the Saudi Electricity Company (SEC) operates upstream. This section explains where the power comes from, so the numbers in the connection agreement make sense.

Where the energy comes from — generation mix

The Saudi grid is supplied predominantly by thermal generation burning natural gas and liquid fuels — gas turbines, combined-cycle blocks and steam plant, much of it co-located with desalination on the coasts — with a rapidly growing fleet of utility-scale solar PV and the first large wind farms. National programmes target a far higher renewable share (an indicative aspiration of ~50 % of generation) by 2030, with gas covering most of the remainder as liquid fuels are displaced. All figures indicative — the mix changes year on year and does not alter how you assess the plant.

What the mix does change is grid behaviour at the connection point. Synchronous thermal machines provide the rotating inertia that holds 60 Hz steady and the fault current your protection relies on; inverter-based solar provides neither in the same way. As the mix shifts, utilities tighten grid codes — ride-through requirements, power-factor windows, harmonic limits — which is why this manual cites the Saudi Grid Code and SEC Distribution Code alongside SASO-adopted IEC standards, and why any on-site generation or large drive installation needs checking against the current code revision.

The 13.8 kV feeder(s) to our plant

From the primary substation, the plant is served by one or more 13.8 kV feeders — usually XLPE underground cable in industrial cities, sometimes overhead line in remote areas. Three arrangements matter for your reliability assessment:

  • Single radial feeder: one cable, one path. Cheapest, and a plain single point of failure at the utility level — any feeder fault is a full plant outage for the duration of the repair or switching time.
  • Ring / loop connection (RMU): the plant tees into a ring that can be fed from either direction. A cable fault is isolated by switching and supply is restored in minutes-to-an-hour rather than repair time. Common in SEC industrial-city networks.
  • Dual dedicated feeders: two independent feeders onto the two sections of MV-SWGR with the bus-section breaker [52-BS] — the premium arrangement, giving genuine utility-side N-1. Reserved for larger or more critical plants.

The reference plant assumes a single utility intake (with the [52-BS] giving internal sectionalising only) — which is precisely why the standby path (DG → ATS → EDB) exists. On site, establish which arrangement you actually have: it is the first row of the utility-side SPOF table in Part 3.

The declared fault level: 25–40 kA, design for 25 kA minimum

When SEC connects a customer at 13.8 kV it declares a design fault level at the point of connection — the prospective short-circuit current the customer's equipment must withstand and interrupt. For SEC 13.8 kV networks this declaration falls in the range 25–40 kA (indicative; the connection agreement states the binding figure). Two things about this number routinely confuse newcomers:

  • It is a planning ceiling, not today's measurement. The actual fault current available today might be 12 or 15 kA. But the utility will add transformers, parallel feeders and generation over the equipment's 30-year life, and every addition raises the fault level. The declared value is the level the network is allowed to grow to without the customer having to replace switchgear.
  • The customer must design to at least the declared minimum — 25 kA — even if local measurements suggest less. Under-rated switchgear that "has always been fine" is a latent bomb: the first fault after a network reinforcement can exceed its interrupting capacity, and a breaker that fails to clear a 13.8 kV fault fails catastrophically.

This is why the reference MV-SWGR is specified at 31.5 kA / 3 s (17.5 kV class): it covers the 25 kA SEC design fault level with margin inside the declared range, at the standard IEC rating step above it. See the MV switchgear catalogue for typical product families, and Part 2 for how the MV fault level cascades into the LV figures (38.5 kA at one transformer, ≈77 kA prospective tie-closed — all indicative).

Warning — never infer the fault level from old single-line diagrams. SLDs show what was declared at design time, decades ago. Always obtain the current declared fault level in writing from SEC (or from the latest connection-agreement revision) before judging switchgear adequacy. A plant connected in 1995 against 20 kA may now sit on a 31.5 kA network.

Metering and tariff basics — why MV-MET matters commercially

The revenue meter in MV-MET is the commercial boundary: everything the plant consumes — including the losses inside TX-1/TX-2, since metering is at MV — is billed by SEC against it. The elements of an industrial bill, indicatively:

  • Energy charge (SAR/kWh): the bulk of the bill, at the industrial tariff category rate. For larger customers SEC applies time-of-use (TOU) structures — higher rates in the summer afternoon system peak (air-conditioning driven), lower off-peak. Indicative: shifting deferrable load (pumping to storage, batch processes) out of the peak window is often the cheapest energy saving available.
  • Demand-related charges: linked to the contracted/recorded maximum demand (kVA or kW). Exceeding contracted capacity triggers penalties and may require a formal connection upgrade.
  • Power-factor clause: SEC requires industrial customers to maintain power factor above a threshold (indicatively 0.9 lagging at the metering point), with a surcharge proportional to the shortfall below it. This clause is the commercial justification for the PFC bank: correcting from 0.85 to a target of 0.95 keeps the plant clear of the penalty band with margin, and simultaneously frees transformer and cable capacity (≈580 kvar, indicative — sizing worked through in Part 2).
Tip — read twelve months of bills. During the desk study, request a year of SEC invoices. They reveal, at zero site effort: the real maximum demand vs the 2353 kVA design figure, the load's seasonality, whether power-factor surcharges are being paid (a failed or undersized PFC bank shows up in money before it shows up in measurements), and any demand-limit exceedances. Bills are the cheapest condition-monitoring data you will ever get.

Outage escalation — what failure upstream looks like

Utility-side disturbances arrive in escalating sizes, and the plant's defence ladder (Part 1, power-flow walk-through) is built to match:

  • Voltage dips and momentary interruptions (cycles to ~1 s): remote faults cleared by utility protection, auto-reclosing on overhead sections. The plant rides through on drive DC-bus inertia and the UPS; the classic casualty is contactor drop-out in the MCCs tripping motors that the process then needs restarted in sequence.
  • Sustained feeder outage (minutes to hours): cable fault, feeder breaker lockout. The ATS starts the DG and transfers the essential bus (≤ 10 s, indicative); non-essential load is shed by design. Ring-connected plants may be back-fed within the hour by SEC switching.
  • Primary substation / sub-transmission outage (hours): multiple feeders lost simultaneously; no back-feed available. The plant runs on the DG for the duration — which is why DG fuel autonomy (day-tank plus bulk storage) belongs in the assessment.
  • Regional / system event (rare, potentially long): grid-scale disturbance. The DG, the UPS (30 min, indicative) and the DCDB battery are the plant's entire world; orderly process shutdown is the success criterion.

Operationally, every plant should hold a current SEC escalation contact list (emergency/outage number, account manager for the connection agreement) and an outage log. The log matters technically as well as commercially: a pattern of dips clustered at certain hours often identifies a network cause SEC can fix — and it tells you which power-quality mitigations (ride-through relays, delayed-dropout contactors) would actually pay back.

Derating: the 50 °C Tax

Where you are in the workflow: every rating you will read on a nameplate or in a catalogue was promised at a reference temperature — usually 30 or 40 °C. Our design ambient is 50 °C. The gap between those two numbers is a tax levied on the capacity of nearly every item in the plant, and this section teaches you to compute it before Part 2 asks you to.

One physical idea behind every derating factor

All electrical equipment is ultimately limited by insulation temperature. A cable, a winding, a breaker contact or a capacitor can carry current only until its hottest point reaches the temperature its insulation system can survive for its design life — 90 °C for XLPE cable, 155 °C (class F) for typical motor and dry-type transformer insulation, and so on. The operating temperature is always the same sum:

hot-spot temperature = ambient temperature + temperature rise caused by load

The insulation limit is fixed by chemistry. So when the ambient term grows — from the 30–40 °C assumed by international standards to the 50 °C Saudi design ambient (see the environment section in Part 0) — the allowable rise, and therefore the allowable load, must shrink. That is the whole theory of derating. Each equipment class merely converts the lost rise into a capacity factor in its own way, which is why the factors below differ in size but never in direction. Two general rules before the specifics:

  • Derating compounds. A cable that loses 18 % to ambient and 20 % to grouping keeps 0.82 × 0.80 ≈ 66 % of its table rating — the factors multiply, they do not add.
  • The reference temperature is part of the rating. "400 A" means nothing until you know "400 A at what ambient". Two data sheets quoting the same current at different reference temperatures are quoting different cables.
Note — heat also arrives from outside. At 50 °C air temperature, direct solar gain on outdoor enclosures and cable trays adds further heating on top of ambient (sun-exposed steel surfaces can far exceed air temperature). Shading, ventilation and light-coloured enclosures are not cosmetic — they are part of the thermal design, alongside the dust/IP54 requirements of the same environment.

Cables: the ≈0.82 factor

IEC current-carrying-capacity tables (IEC 60364-5-52 / IEC 60287 basis) for cables in air are referenced to a 30 °C ambient. The correction factor for another ambient follows directly from the insulation-temperature logic above:

k = √( (θ_conductor − θ_ambient) / (θ_conductor − θ_reference) )

For 90 °C XLPE insulation in 50 °C air instead of 30 °C:

k = √( (90 − 50) / (90 − 30) ) = √(40/60) = √0.667 ≈ 0.82

So every XLPE cable in this plant delivers, as a rule of thumb, about 82 % of its table rating before any other factor is applied. PVC-insulated cable (70 °C conductor) fares worse — k ≈ √(20/40) ≈ 0.71 — one reason XLPE dominates in the Kingdom. Buried cables use a different reference (soil temperature, indicatively 25–35 °C here) and are further punished by dry desert sand, whose thermal resistivity can be several times the standard 2.5 K·m/W assumption; specify thermally stabilised backfill or derate hard. The full sizing procedure, with grouping and voltage-drop checks, is worked in Part 2 — cable sizing.

Worked example — the "400 A" cable that is really a 328 A cable

A single-core XLPE copper cable is tabulated at 400 A in free air (30 °C reference). It is installed on a cable ladder at the 50 °C design ambient. What may it actually carry?

I_z = I_table × k_ambient = 400 A × 0.82 = 328 A

If it also runs bunched with two other loaded circuits on the same ladder (grouping factor ≈ 0.80, indicative):

I_z = 400 × 0.82 × 0.80 ≈ 262 A

Result: the "400 A" cable is a ~328 A cable at 50 °C — and only a ~262 A cable when grouped. Sanity check: the protective device and the design current must both sit at or below the derated capacity (I_b ≤ I_n ≤ I_z = 328 A), not the table figure; a 400 A breaker on this cable at 50 °C would be a finding. Transformers: insulation-class margin and the fan (AF) boost

IEC 60076 rates a transformer for an ambient of 40 °C maximum (30 °C monthly average, 20 °C yearly average). At a 50 °C site, the same logic as cables applies to the oil or winding temperature rise: roughly, each kelvin of ambient above the standard basis costs about 1 % of continuous loading capability (indicative), so a standard-design 1600 kVA unit would only be good for ~1440–1480 kVA here. Three standard remedies, usually combined at specification stage rather than bolted on later:

  • Specify the 50 °C ambient on the order — the manufacturer designs the unit with reduced guaranteed temperature rises so the nameplate kVA is honest at site conditions. This is the approach assumed for TX-1/TX-2 (1600 kVA each at 50 °C, indicative).
  • Insulation-class margin (dry-type): build with class F or H insulation (155 / 180 °C capability) but limit the design rise to a lower class's values — the unused thermal headroom absorbs the extra 10 K of ambient instead of being spent on load.
  • Forced-air (AF/ONAF) boost: fans on a cast-resin AN unit or radiator fans on an ONAN oil unit typically add +25–40 % capacity (indicative). Useful as contingency margin (e.g. for the bus-tie-closed, one-transformer-carries-all condition) — but note the fans, their supply and their controller become reliability items in their own right for the FMECA.
Tip — read the rise, not just the kVA. On the transformer nameplate, check the guaranteed temperature-rise figures and the stated design ambient. A unit stamped "1600 kVA, 40 °C ambient" installed at a 50 °C site is not a 1600 kVA unit here — that single line on the nameplate is a desk-study finding before you ever measure anything. LV breakers in boards: 0.8–0.9

An ACB or MCCB is rated (IEC 60947-2) at a reference air temperature — typically 40 °C around the device. Installed inside a Form 4b switchboard in a 50 °C switchroom, the air around the breaker is the room's 50 °C plus the board's internal rise (heat from busbars, other devices and the breaker itself), easily reaching 55–65 °C at the device. Manufacturers publish derating tables for exactly this; the working rule is that a breaker inside a board at our ambient delivers 80–90 % of its frame/trip rating (indicative). Consequences for the reference plant:

  • The LV-MSB incomer ACBs must carry the transformer LV FLC of 2309 A continuously. A 2500 A frame at a 0.85 in-board factor is only ~2125 A — not enough; the specification therefore moves to a 3200 A frame (3200 × 0.85 ≈ 2720 A ≥ 2309 A, indicative) or relies on the manufacturer's certified temperature-derating curve for the actual board. This is why the schedule shows a ~2500–3200 A bus.
  • Electronic trip units are set to the derated continuous current, and the board's ventilation (or air conditioning of the room) is part of the breaker's rating — a failed switchroom HVAC silently converts adequate breakers into overloaded ones.

Typical ACB/MCCB families and their published derating data are referenced in the LV breaker catalogue.

VFDs and UPS: 1–1.5 %/°C above 40 °C

Power-electronic equipment derates fastest, because its limiting components — IGBTs, DC-bus electrolytic capacitors — are the most temperature-sensitive items in the plant. Typical manufacturer rule for VFDs: full rating up to 40 °C, then 1–1.5 % output current per °C above, i.e. 10–15 % lost at 50 °C (indicative); many vendors simply cap operation at 50 °C entirely. UPS units behave similarly, and their batteries are a separate, harsher story (below). Practical responses: select drives one frame size up, and treat the switchroom air-conditioning as process-critical equipment — for drive rooms, the HVAC is the derating mitigation, and its failure mode belongs in the SPOF analysis of Part 3.

Diesel generators: 0.5–1 %/°C above 40 °C

Genset site rating (ISO 8528) falls with intake-air temperature — hot, thin air means less oxygen per stroke and weaker alternator cooling — by an indicative 0.5–1 % per °C above 40 °C, plus altitude derating where applicable. A set quoted "1250 kVA" at ISO standard conditions delivers perhaps 1130–1190 kVA at a 50 °C site. The reference DG avoids this trap by being specified as 1250 kVA ESP at 50 °C ambient — the derating is already inside the number. On site, always determine whether a genset nameplate states standard-condition or site-condition output, and check radiator sizing (a "tropical" high-ambient radiator is a distinct option). Batteries: life halves every 8–10 °C

Batteries are the exception that proves the rule: heat barely reduces their capacity (it slightly increases it) — instead it destroys their life. Internal corrosion and grid degradation are chemical reactions that follow Arrhenius behaviour: reaction rate, and therefore ageing, roughly doubles for every 8–10 °C above the reference temperature (20–25 °C for VRLA). Indicatively, a "10-year design life" VRLA block achieves:

  • ~10 years at 20–25 °C (air-conditioned battery room),
  • ~5 years at ~30–33 °C,
  • ~2–3 years at ~40 °C,
  • little more than a year in an unconditioned 50 °C enclosure.

This applies equally to the UPS battery (the 30 min autonomy bridging the DG start — see the power-flow walk-through) and the DCDB battery (the last line of MV protection operability). A dead battery room air-conditioner is therefore a hidden plant-level SPOF: nothing trips, nothing alarms, the batteries simply age out years early and fail on the first real outage. On site: check the battery room temperature log, the A/C condition, and the date and result of the last discharge test. Typical UPS systems in the UPS catalogue.

50 °C field checklist

  • Record switchroom, drive-room and battery-room temperatures; compare against the design assumptions.
  • Verify the cable schedule states its ambient basis and that 50 °C correction (≈0.82) plus grouping factors were applied.
  • Check transformer nameplates for the stated design ambient and guaranteed temperature rises; confirm AF fans (if fitted) run on demand.
  • Compare LV-MSB incomer ACB frame/trip settings against the 2309 A continuous duty with the in-board derating (0.8–0.9) applied.
  • Confirm VFD and UPS room A/C is duty/standby or alarmed; note any drive already running above 40 °C cabinet inlet.
  • Check whether the DG nameplate output is ISO standard-condition or 50 °C site-rated, and whether a high-ambient radiator is fitted.
  • Obtain the battery-room temperature log and the date/result of the last UPS and DCDB battery discharge tests.
  • Inspect outdoor enclosures and cable trays for direct solar exposure; note missing sunshades on sun-facing equipment.
Key takeaway: every rating is a promise made at a reference temperature, and at the 50 °C Saudi design ambient (see Part 0 — environment) that promise is taxed: cables × 0.82, breakers-in-boards × 0.8–0.9, drives and UPS −10–15 %, gensets −5–10 %, transformers fixed only if ordered for 50 °C, and battery life halved per 8–10 °C. The factors multiply, the deratings are invisible on the SLD, and the air-conditioning that holds them all together is itself a single point of failure.
