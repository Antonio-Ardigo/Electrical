The MV/LV Plant at a Glance

Where you are in the workflow: before you can assess a plant, you must be able to read its single-line diagram and name every item on it. Part 1 builds that foundation.

Design basis of the reference plant

Throughout this manual, calculations and examples are illustrated using a representative ~2 MW industrial process plant (water / desalination service, typical of the sites you will assess). The design basis is summarised below; all figures are indicative and tentative.

Worked example — Transformer sizing from demand

Given ~2 MW demand at 0.85 power factor, size the transformer bank:

S = P / pf = 2000 kW / 0.85 ≈ 2353 kVA

Apply a design and diversity margin of +20–25 %:

S_design = 2353 × 1.25 ≈ 2941 kVA

Split across two transformers (N-1 split-bus), select next standard size:

S_each = 2941 / 2 ≈ 1471 kVA → select 2 × 1600 kVA

Result: 2 × 1600 kVA, Dyn11, 11 kV / 0.4 kV (tags TX-1 and TX-2). Values are indicative.

Power architecture — the master SLD

The master single-line diagram below shows the complete reference power architecture. Study it before proceeding — every section of Part 1 refers to items on this drawing.

The architecture has three tiers:

  1. MV (11 kV) tier: Utility feed → metering (MV-MET) → MV switchgear (MV-SWGR) with protection IEDs (MV-PROT) and neutral earthing resistor (MV-NER).
  2. Transformation tier: Two independent transformer feeders supply TX-1 and TX-2, each stepping 11 kV down to 400 V.
  3. LV (400 V) tier: Main switchboard (LV-MSB) with a split bus (A and B) joined by a normally-open bus-tie (BT); Motor Control Centres (MCC-1, MCC-2), PFC bank, distribution boards, essential board (EDB), diesel generator (DG), ATS, UPS and DC battery system (DCDB).
Key takeaway: The ~2 MW reference plant is the template for everything in this manual. Two 1600 kVA transformers on a split LV bus with a normally-open bus-tie give partial N-1 resilience — understanding why this architecture is used, and where it is still vulnerable, is the core of the SPOF assessment.

How to Read an SLD

Where you are in the workflow: before the site visit, you must be comfortable reading any MV/LV SLD you are handed, not just the reference drawing.

Power-flow direction and voltage levels

Read an SLD top to bottom — energy flows from the utility source at the top to the loads (motors, boards) at the bottom. On the reference drawing there are two clearly separated horizontal zones:

  • MV zone (upper band): 11 kV — from the utility intake down to and including the transformer primaries. MV busbars are drawn in firebrick red on colour drawings.
  • LV zone (lower band): 400 V — from the transformer secondaries to all distribution equipment and loads. LV busbars are drawn in navy blue.

The two transformers (TX-1, TX-2) are the physical and electrical boundary between the MV and LV zones.

IEC 60617 symbols used on the reference SLD

Note — IEC 60617: This is the international standard defining graphical symbols for diagrams. The symbols used on the reference SLD are drawn in accordance with IEC 60617 series. If you encounter a drawing using ANSI/IEEE symbols (common in North America), the layout is the same but some symbols differ — the most common example is the circuit-breaker symbol (IEC: a solid square; ANSI: two diverging lines). Always check the drawing legend before interpreting unfamiliar symbols.

Busbar convention and the split bus

The LV main switchboard (LV-MSB) is drawn as a split bus: Bus A (left, fed by TX-1) and Bus B (right, fed by TX-2) are two physically separate copper bars in the one board. They are joined only through the normally-open bus-tie (BT). In normal operation each transformer supplies its own half of the plant independently. The split-bus arrangement:

  • limits the fault current that a single transformer must interrupt,
  • contains the blast zone of any bus fault to one section, and
  • allows the bus-tie to close automatically on loss of one incomer, maintaining supply to both buses from the surviving transformer.

Red-lining on site

On a site visit, you compare the SLD against the as-found installation and mark discrepancies in red on a working print or a PDF annotation tool. Common discrepancies to look for:

  • Breaker labelling or device number differs from drawing.
  • An extra or missing feeder — a circuit on site not shown on the drawing.
  • Bus-tie in a different normal position (open or closed) from the drawing.
  • Equipment substituted (e.g. RMU instead of metal-clad switchgear).
  • Protection relay type or settings different from what is listed.
  • Cables or transformers replaced with a different rating.
Key takeaway: Read SLDs top-to-bottom (source to load), know the IEC 60617 symbols, and understand the split-bus concept. On site, every discrepancy between drawing and reality is a red-line — and potentially a SPOF waiting to be found.

PCC vs MCC

Where you are in the workflow: this distinction is fundamental — confusing the two is one of the most common errors on assessment forms.

Both the PCC and the MCC are LV assemblies built to IEC 61439-1/-2, and they can look similar in a switchroom. They do very different jobs:

Feature PCC (Power Control Centre) MCC (Motor Control Centre)
Tag in reference plant LV-MSB MCC-1, MCC-2
Primary function Receives transformer output(s); distributes bulk LV power to downstream boards and feeders. The LV power hub. Houses individual motor starters — DOL, star-delta, soft-starter, VFD — that control and protect specific process motors.
Incomers Air Circuit Breakers (ACBs) from transformers; motorised bus-tie ACB. Single MCCB incomer from the PCC (LV-MSB) outgoing feeder.
Outgoing feeders MCCBs feeding MCCs, distribution boards, PFC bank, large individual loads. Per-motor buckets: MCCB or fuse-contactor, contactors, overload relay, local/remote control, ammeter, run/trip indication.
Bus rating (indicative) ~2500–3200 A; 50–65 kA fault withstand. ~1000–2000 A; fault withstand matched to upstream board.
Form of segregation (IEC 61439) Form 4b — busbars separated from functional units; terminals separated from other functional units. Form 3b/4b — functional units (starters) separated from each other.
Where on the SLD The central LV switchboard spanning Bus A and Bus B — immediately below the transformers. On outgoing MCCB feeders from the LV-MSB Bus A/B; one MCC per bus section in the reference plant.
One-line description "The LV power hub — it feeds the MCCs." "Where the motors are started — it feeds the loads."
Note — vocabulary variation: In different organisations, the PCC is also called the Main LV Switchboard (MLVSB), Main Distribution Board (MDB), or simply the Main Board. The MCC may be called a Motor Control Panel (MCP) or a starter board. The function — bulk distribution vs. individual motor control — is the reliable distinguishing criterion, regardless of what the nameplate says.
Tip — SPOF implication: Because the PCC is the single point through which all transformer power passes before reaching the MCCs, a fault or loss of the PCC is a plant-level SPOF. The split bus with bus-tie is specifically designed to limit this vulnerability. When assessing a plant, check whether the PCC is truly split-bus or is effectively a single-bus board with cosmetic segregation.
Key takeaway: PCC (LV-MSB) = bulk distribution hub, fed by transformers, feeds MCCs. MCC = motor starter assembly, fed by PCC, feeds individual motors. The PCC feeds the MCCs; the MCCs feed the loads.

Key takeaway: Eighteen tags, two voltage levels, three functional layers — generation of the supply (MV), distribution (LV-MSB), and control/protection (DCDB/UPS/DG/ATS). Memorise the function of each tag in one sentence; you will need that knowledge to populate the FMECA without hesitation.

Power-Flow Walk-Through

Where you are in the workflow: tracing the three power paths is what you do mentally at every site visit — it reveals where the architecture is robust and where it is not.

Keep the master SLD (Fig 1-1) open alongside this section. There are three distinct paths through the plant — trace each one on the drawing as you read.

Path 1 — Main (normal) path: utility to process motor

  1. Utility 11 kV feed arrives at the site and passes through the revenue metering cubicle (MV-MET) where energy is metered for tariff billing.
  2. The supply enters the MV switchgear (MV-SWGR) via incomer breaker [52-I]. The protection IEDs (MV-PROT) watch this incomer and the transformer feeders for faults. The 11 kV neutral is earthed through the resistor (MV-NER).
  3. Two transformer feeder breakers [52-T1] and [52-T2] supply the two transformers (TX-1 and TX-2), which step 11 kV down to 400 V.
  4. Each transformer secondary feeds its LV incomer ACB: TX-1 → ACB-A → Bus A, TX-2 → ACB-B → Bus B. The bus-tie (BT) between them is normally open, so the two bus sections are independent.
  5. Outgoing MCCB feeders from Bus A and Bus B supply the Motor Control Centres (MCC-1, MCC-2), the PFC bank (PFC), distribution boards (DB) and the essential board (EDB).
  6. Inside an MCC, the appropriate starter (DOL, soft-starter or a VFD) energises the process motor — pump, fan, conveyor or mixer.
Tip — tracing on a real SLD: On a site SLD that may be larger or more complex, follow the same top-to-bottom discipline and identify: (a) the metering point, (b) the MV incomer and switchgear, (c) the transformer(s), (d) the LV main board and its bus arrangement, (e) the MCC feeders, and (f) the standby and DC paths. This sequence works on any MV/LV plant drawing.

Path 2 — Standby path: on utility loss

When the utility supply is lost (or falls outside acceptable voltage/frequency limits):

  1. The ATS detects utility loss and issues an auto-start signal to the diesel generator (DG).
  2. The DG starts, ramps to rated speed, and the AVR brings the voltage to 400 V ± tolerance.
  3. The ATS transfers the essential bus from the utility feed to the DG output — the transfer is mechanically interlocked to ensure only one source feeds the EDB at a time.
  4. The essential distribution board (EDB) remains energised — escape lighting, fire systems and critical process auxiliaries stay live.
  5. Within the LV-MSB, if one incomer ACB trips (loss of one transformer), the bus-tie (BT) auto-closes so the surviving transformer can supply both Bus A and Bus B (at reduced capacity — load shedding may be needed).
Note — ATS changeover time: A diesel generator takes typically 10–30 s to start, synchronise and accept load (indicative). During this window, only the UPS-backed loads remain energised. The EDB is de-energised for this interval unless the ATS uses a static (electronic) transfer or the EDB has its own small UPS. Verify the actual changeover time on site and confirm it is acceptable for the essential loads.

Path 3 — DC / control path: always alive

The control and protection path is designed to be available at all times — even during a complete AC blackout:

  1. The DCDB (DC battery and charger) is float-charged from the LV supply and holds its battery at full charge continuously. On loss of AC, the battery sustains the DC bus.
  2. The 110 V DC bus from the DCDB feeds the trip and close coils of the MV switchgear breakers and the MV protection relay IEDs. The protection system remains operative — it can still detect faults and trip breakers — even with no AC available.
  3. The UPS is float-charged from the LV AC bus (typically Bus B). On loss of AC it switches seamlessly to its own battery (static changeover, no break). It supplies the DCS, PLC, SCADA and instrumentation at clean regulated AC.
  4. Together, the DCDB and UPS ensure that both protection (MV DC) and control (AC to DCS/instruments) remain available for the full battery autonomy period, giving the operator visibility and the ability to operate the MV switchgear during a blackout.
Tip — SPOF on the DC path: A common finding is that both the UPS and the DCDB charger are supplied from the same LV bus section or even the same feeder, removing their independence. On site, verify that the UPS and DCDB have segregated sources. Also check the battery condition and last test date — an aged or untested battery is a hidden SPOF that does not show up on the SLD.
Key takeaway: Three paths — normal (utility to motors), standby (DG via ATS to essential loads), and DC/control (DCDB to MV switchgear; UPS to DCS). Trace all three on every SLD you review; the points where paths converge or where independence is lost are your SPOF candidates.

Question 1

Which two tags mark the boundary between the 11 kV and the 400 V parts of the plant? State what each one does.

Show answer

TX-1 and TX-2 — the two MV/LV power transformers (1600 kVA each, indicative; 11 kV / 0.4 kV, Dyn11). They step the 11 kV utility supply down to 400 V for the LV distribution system. TX-1 feeds Bus A and TX-2 feeds Bus B of the LV-MSB. Everything above them is MV (11 kV); everything below them is LV (400 V).

Question 2

What is the difference between LV-MSB and MCC-1? Which one feeds which?

Show answer

LV-MSB is the PCC (Power Control Centre) / Main LV Switchboard — it receives the transformer outputs and distributes bulk 400 V power. Think "the LV power hub."

MCC-1 is a Motor Control Centre — an assembly of motor starters (DOL, soft-starter, VFD) that control and protect individual process motors. Think "where the motors are started."

The LV-MSB feeds MCC-1 (via an outgoing MCCB feeder from Bus A). MCC-1 feeds the individual motors.

Question 3

The bus-tie BT is normally open. What event causes it to close automatically, and why is that desirable?

Show answer

The bus-tie closes automatically on the trip or loss of either LV incomer ACB (i.e. loss of one transformer or its MV feeder). The auto-close logic detects the incomer trip and closes BT so the surviving transformer can carry both Bus A and Bus B loads.

This is desirable because it prevents loss of supply to half the plant whenever one transformer is lost — it is the primary N-1 resilience feature of the LV distribution. Without it, loss of TX-1 would immediately shed all Bus A loads (MCC-1, PFC, DB-A and any Bus A feeders).

Question 4

Name the three tags on the standby/essential power path and state the order in which energy flows through them on a utility outage.

Show answer

DG → ATS → EDB.

On a utility outage: the DG starts (auto-start signal from the ATS), ramps to rated voltage and frequency; the ATS transfers the essential bus from the utility source to the DG output; the EDB (essential distribution board) remains energised, supplying escape lighting, fire systems and critical process auxiliaries.

Question 5

Why does the plant need a separate DCDB when it already has a UPS? What does each supply?

Show answer

They serve two different and independently critical supply circuits:

  • UPS supplies AC power (400 V or 230 V) to the DCS, PLC, SCADA and instrumentation systems. These are AC-powered electronics that need a clean, regulated, uninterrupted supply.
  • DCDB supplies 110 V DC to the trip and close coils of the MV switchgear breakers and to the MV protection relay IEDs. These must operate even when the AC system is completely dead — a DC battery is the only supply that can guarantee this.

If you replaced the DCDB with a UPS, the MV switchgear would lose its ability to trip or close once the UPS itself lost its input feed. The DCDB's battery is the last line of protection operability during a full AC blackout.

Question 6

What is the purpose of MV-NER, and what would happen if it were removed (neutral left floating)?

Show answer

MV-NER (the Neutral Earthing Resistor) earths the 11 kV system neutral through a resistor to limit earth-fault current to approximately 300–400 A (indicative). This value is high enough for earth-fault protection relays to detect the fault reliably, but low enough to limit damage to equipment and reduce the step/touch voltage hazard at the fault location.

If the NER were removed and the neutral left floating (isolated neutral system):

  • An earth fault would not produce a reliable fault current — the protection relay could not detect it dependably, meaning the fault might persist and worsen.
  • The healthy phases would rise to line-to-line voltage above earth, stressing cable insulation and equipment rated for line-to-earth voltage.
  • Transient overvoltages (ferro-resonance) could develop on the unearthed network, potentially damaging voltage transformers and other equipment.
