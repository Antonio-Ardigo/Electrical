Part 2 — Calculations

Where you are in the workflow: you have identified the equipment and read the SLD (Part 1); now you run the numbers that tell you whether the existing equipment is correctly rated before you write a single recommendation.

Assessment calculations are verification, not green-field design. Your job in the field is to ask: "Is this transformer / breaker / cable / genset still adequate for what the plant is actually doing today, in this climate, on this grid?" — and to back the answer with arithmetic, not opinion.

Why Calculations Matter in an Assessment

Every rating you see on the SLD — a transformer kVA, a breaker breaking capacity, a busbar bracing figure, a cable cross-section, a capacitor bank, a genset — is the output of a calculation somebody performed years ago. That calculation is the audit trail that justifies the rating. In an assessment you work in the opposite direction: you start from the nameplate, reverse-engineer the assumption that was made, and check whether reality still matches it.

Calculations are not a one-time design activity. They appear at four distinct moments in the life of a plant, and a field engineer must recognise which moment they are standing in:

Moment in plant lifeQuestion the calculation answersTypical trigger
1. Design "What rating do we buy?" — sizing from a load list that does not yet exist as physical equipment. New plant, major expansion. Owned by the design consultant; produces the original studies.
2. Verification (this manual's focus) "Is what was bought still adequate for what the plant actually does?" — the same arithmetic run backwards against nameplates and meter readings. Condition assessment, due diligence, insurance survey, SPOF/FMECA study.
3. Modification "Can we add this 250 kW compressor without re-equipping the substation?" — delta calculations layered on the as-found base case. Debottlenecking, new packages, PFC retrofit, DG addition. Requires the management-of-change file to carry the revised studies.
4. Incident investigation "Why did the breaker explode / the cable burn / the relay not trip?" — forensic re-calculation of fault duty, loading and settings as they were at the moment of failure. After a trip, fire, arc-flash event or unexplained outage. The most unforgiving audience your arithmetic will ever have.

The two as-found failure modes you are hunting in moment 2:

  • Under-rating — cables and busbars that overheat (fire risk), switchgear that cannot interrupt a fault (catastrophic failure — see short-circuit withstand), protection that mis-coordinates or nuisance-trips (coordination), motors that stall, process voltage too low to run.
  • Over-rating — capital tied up in oversized transformers, switchgear frames, cables and gensets the plant will never use; this matters most when the client is deciding whether to add load or replace equipment.

The Saudi (SEC) Basis — What Changes and Why It Matters

This Rev 3 edition localises every calculation to a plant connected to the Saudi Electricity Company (SEC) distribution network. Three things change relative to the generic IEC textbook case, and each one flows through the arithmetic of every later section:

  • Voltage levels: 13.8 kV MV, 400/230 V LV. SEC's standard distribution voltage is 13.8 kV (a North-American-heritage level adopted across the Kingdom), not the 11 kV common in 50 Hz countries. The LV utilisation voltage is 400/230 V three-phase TN-S. Both levels appear inside every √3 × V denominator in Part 2 — get the voltage wrong and every current is wrong by 20–25 %.
  • Frequency: 60 Hz. The Saudi grid runs at 60 Hz. Consequences: a 4-pole motor runs at 1800 rpm (not 1500), reactances are 20 % higher for the same inductance (X = 2πfL), and imported 50 Hz equipment must be explicitly verified for 60 Hz duty. Frequency does not appear in the FLC formula — but it silently sits inside every impedance, every motor speed and every capacitor rating.
  • Climate: 50 °C design ambient, dust and sand. The "derating culture" is the defining habit of good Saudi electrical engineering. Cable tables, busbar ratings, transformer ONAN ratings and DG outputs are all published at 30–40 °C reference ambients; at a 50 °C design ambient an XLPE cable in air keeps only about 0.82 of its tabulated ampacity, and a diesel genset is bought against its ESP rating at 50 °C, not its 25 °C brochure figure. Enclosures outdoors are minimum IP54 against dust. Any rating you read off a generic table and do not derate is a finding waiting to happen.
Standards basis (cite together): SASO-adopted IEC standards (Saudi Standards, Metrology and Quality Organization adopts IEC with national deviations), the SEC Distribution Code and Saudi Grid Code for connection, power factor and fault-level requirements, and SBC 401 (Saudi Building Code — Electrical) for installation practice. Where this manual cites "IEC 60909" or "IEC 60364", read the SASO-adopted edition.

1 — Load & Demand Estimate Core

Goal: confirm the ~2 MW plant demand and convert it to apparent power (kVA) — the anchor point for every sizing calculation that follows.

From Load List to Maximum Demand

Why this exists

No plant ever draws the sum of its nameplates. Motors are oversized for their pumps, standby drives sit idle, heaters cycle, and not everything runs at once. The load estimate exists to bridge the gap between two very different numbers: the connected load (sum of every nameplate — here ≈ 2.7 MW) and the maximum demand (the highest simultaneous draw the supply must actually deliver — here ≈ 2.0 MW). The bridge is built from two factors applied per feeder or per group:

  • Demand factor — the ratio of a group's actual peak draw to its connected nameplate total (captures partial loading and idle standby units).
  • Diversity factor — recognition that different groups do not peak at the same instant, so the system peak is less than the sum of group peaks.

Everything downstream hangs off the result: transformer kVA (§2), busbar and breaker frames via FLC (§3), cable sizes (§6), the PFC bank (§7) and the SEC connection capacity itself. The SEC Distribution Code requires a declared maximum demand at the point of connection — this calculation is where that declaration comes from.

What goes wrong without it

Two opposite disasters, both common:

  • Sizing on nameplates ("everything on, all the time"): the plant buys 2 × 2000 kVA transformers, a 3200 A switchboard and an SEC connection it never uses. Capital is wasted, transformers idle at 40 % loading for twenty years burning no-load losses, and the over-stiff system masks creeping load growth until it surprises someone.
  • Sizing on optimism (or never re-checking): the original 2005 load list said 1.6 MW. Fifteen years of "small" additions — a 90 kW air compressor here, a chiller there — and the true peak is now 2.3 MW. Nobody re-ran the estimate. The first symptom is a transformer winding-temperature alarm during the August peak, at 50 °C ambient, when cooling margin is already at its thinnest. The second symptom is an incomer trip during the most profitable production week of the year.

In the Saudi climate the second failure mode has a vicious twist: plant demand peaks (HVAC and chilled-water load) at exactly the moment equipment capability is at its lowest (50 °C ambient derating). The load estimate and the derating culture meet head-on in mid-August.

Feel for the numbers

Anchors for 2.0 MW, computed honestly:

  • ≈ 900 household kettles boiling at once (2 000 kW / 2.2 kW per kettle = 909).
  • ≈ 2 700 horsepower (2 000 kW / 0.746 kW per hp = 2 681 hp) — roughly twenty family cars at full throttle, running continuously.
  • ≈ 130 large Saudi villas at summer peak (taking ≈ 15 kW per villa with every AC unit running: 2 000 / 15 ≈ 133).
  • Energy per day at peak: 2 MW × 24 h = 48 MWh/day — at an indicative industrial tariff of 0.18 SAR/kWh that is ≈ 8 600 SAR per day, ≈ 3.2 million SAR per year. Demand estimation errors are measured in millions of riyals, in both capex and opex directions.

Equipment settings

A load estimate has no relay pickup of its own, but it sets numbers on other people's equipment, and you should verify each one against the table above:

  • Maximum-demand declaration in the SEC connection agreement (and the tariff's demand charge) — should match the calculated ≈ 2.0 MW / 2 353 kVA within reason.
  • Demand alarm thresholds in the power-management system (PMS) — typically alarm at 90 % of declared demand.
  • Load-shedding priority table — the essential / non-essential split used in the N-1 scheme (§2) and DG sizing is drawn directly from this load table's rows.
  • Incomer trip-unit long-time setting (Ir) — must sit above the per-transformer share of this demand (breaker deep-dive).

Equipment technology

The data behind a believable load estimate comes from hardware:

  • Tariff metering at the SEC intake — class 0.2S/0.5S CT-operated meters recording kW, kvar and maximum demand (typically 15- or 30-minute integrating windows). The MD register is the single most trustworthy demand number on site.
  • Multifunction power meters on the LV-MSB incomers and major feeders — modern digital meters log demand profiles to memory or to the PMS.
  • MCC ammeters and motor protection relays — per-feeder currents for building a bottom-up profile where no meter exists.
  • Portable power-quality analysers — a 7-day logging campaign on the incomers is the standard assessment move when historical data is missing.

Control

In a well-instrumented plant the PMS/SCADA continuously trends kW, kvar and kVA per incomer and per major feeder, time-stamped, with the demand window aligned to the tariff meter. The assessment question is whether anyone looks at it: ask for the 12-month demand trend and the all-time peak. If the PMS exists but trends are not archived, raise it — demand growth is invisible without a trend, and the load-shedding controller (which acts on these same measurements) cannot be validated either.

Sizing method

  1. Obtain (or build) the load list: every feeder with nameplate kW, duty (continuous / intermittent / standby), and pf.
  2. Group by MCC / distribution board; apply a demand factor per group from duty knowledge (continuous process 0.7–0.9; intermittent 0.3–0.6; standby 0).
  3. Apply diversity between groups if their peaks demonstrably do not coincide (be conservative: in a Saudi summer, HVAC and process peaks usually do coincide).
  4. Sum to maximum demand P (kW); divide by pf for S (kVA); add 20–25 % growth margin for the installed transformer base.
  5. Cross-check top-down against 12 months of tariff-meter MD readings — the bottom-up table and the meter should agree within ≈ 10 %; investigate any larger gap before using either number.
Field tip — what to capture: obtain the latest load list or motor schedule from the client. If none exists, read each MCC ammeter at peak shift and back-calculate kW per feeder as sqrt(3) × V × I × pf. Sum the branch demands to build your own diversity estimate, then compare with the SEC tariff meter's maximum-demand register — the cheapest cross-check on site.

3 — Full-Load Current (LV & MV) Core

Goal: convert every kVA rating on the SLD into the ampere figure the conductors actually carry — the 400 V full-load current (FLC) of a 1600 kVA transformer, its 13.8 kV primary current, and the total plant LV current — because amperes, not kVA, are what breakers, busbars, cables and CTs are rated in.

From kVA to Amperes

Why this exists

Power equipment is bought in kVA and MW, but it fails in amperes. Heat in a conductor is I²R; mechanical force between busbars is proportional to I²; a breaker contact welds or survives according to the current through it. The full-load current calculation is the universal translator between the power world of the load estimate (§1) and the current world of every physical conductor on site. It is the single most-used formula in this manual:

I = S / (sqrt(3) × V) [A, with S in VA and V in volts, line-to-line]

The sqrt(3) appears because in a balanced three-phase system the power is shared across three phases whose line voltage is sqrt(3) times the phase voltage. You do not need to re-derive it in the field; you do need to never forget it — omitting the 1.732 makes every answer 73 % too high.

FLC matters twice over on this plant because the same kVA produces two wildly different currents on the two sides of each transformer: at 13.8 kV a 1600 kVA unit draws a modest 66.9 A; at 400 V the same power becomes 2 309 A — a 34.5 : 1 ratio (13 800 / 400), which is exactly the turns ratio. This is why MV switchgear (catalogue) is physically small and LV switchboards are rooms full of copper.

What goes wrong without it

A real-world composite, seen on more than one assessment: a plant adds load over the years and the LV incomer trip unit starts nuisance-tripping in summer. The electrician's "fix" is to raise the long-time pickup Ir — first to 100 % of the 2500 A sensor rating, then someone defeats it further. Nobody recalculates FLC. The transformer is now permitted to run above its 2 309 A rating indefinitely; at 50 °C ambient its insulation life halves for roughly every 6–8 K of extra winding temperature. Two summers later the winding-temperature trip operates during the August peak — or worse, the unit fails internally and the plant discovers what an unplanned 12-week transformer lead time costs.

The mirror-image error sits on the MV side: a CT ratio chosen without an FLC calculation. A 200/1 CT on a feeder that carries 66.9 A runs at 33 % of rated primary current — accuracy and relay sensitivity suffer, and the earth-fault pickup, set as a percentage of CT primary, may be too coarse to detect a high-resistance fault. Wrong FLC → wrong CT → blind protection (§10).

Feel for the numbers

  • 2 309 A is an enormous current by everyday standards — about 175 domestic 13 A socket circuits drawing flat-out simultaneously, flowing in each of three phases. At 400 V it is carried by parallel busbars roughly the cross-section of a hardcover book per phase.
  • 66.9 A on the MV side is, by contrast, less current than a domestic cooker circuit carries — but at 13.8 kV. Same power, 34.5 times the voltage, 1/34.5 of the current. High voltage exists precisely to make current (and therefore copper and I²R loss) small.
  • Heat check: the LV incomer connection has resistance of the order of tens of micro-ohms. Even at 20 µΩ, 2 309 A produces I²R ≈ 2 309² × 20e-6 ≈ 107 W in a single bolted joint — a soldering-iron's worth of heat in one connection, continuously. This is why thermography of LV incomers (one loose bolt → hundreds of watts → discoloured, cooked joint) is a standard assessment activity.
  • Rule of thumb worth memorising for 400 V: FLC ≈ 1.44 A per kVA (1000 / (1.732 × 400) = 1.443). For 13.8 kV: FLC ≈ 0.0418 A per kVA, or ≈ 42 A per MVA.

Worked calculation

Worked example — FLC set for the Rev 3 plant (indicative)

(a) MV FLC of one 1600 kVA transformer at 13.8 kV:

I_MV = S / (sqrt(3) × V) = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 13 800 V) I_MV = 1 600 000 / 23 902 = 66.9 A

This sizes the MV transformer-feeder breakers [52-T1] / [52-T2] in MV-SWGR (a 630 A panel is bored at 66.9 A — normal and fine for MV) and points to a CT primary of 75–100 A on the feeder protection.

(b) LV FLC of the same transformer at 400 V:

I_LV = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) = 1 600 000 / 692.8 = 2 309 A

This sets the LV-MSB incomer ACB frame (next standard frame ≥ 2 500 A, often 3 200 A for margin) and the incomer busbar/riser rating.

(c) Total LV load current at plant maximum demand (2 000 kW / 0.85 pf = 2 353 kVA, from §1):

I_total = 2 352 941 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) = 2 352 941 / 692.8 = 3 396 A

Shared across two incomers with the bus-tie open: ≈ 1 698 A per transformer — that is 1 698 / 2 309 = 73.5 % loading, matching the N-1 check (§2).

Sanity checks:

  • Turns-ratio check: I_LV / I_MV = 2 309 / 66.9 = 34.5 = 13 800 / 400. ✓ The two answers are mutually consistent — if your LV and MV FLCs do not divide to the voltage ratio, one of them is wrong.
  • Rule-of-thumb check: 1 600 kVA × 1.44 A/kVA ≈ 2 304 A ≈ 2 309 A. ✓
  • Order of magnitude: total plant current (3 396 A) exceeds one transformer's FLC (2 309 A) — confirming that one transformer alone cannot carry the whole plant without load shedding, which is the entire premise of the N-1 scheme.

All values indicative — confirm nameplate kVA and measured voltage on site.

Equipment settings

FLC is not itself a setting — it is the reference quantity that almost every setting on the plant is expressed against. On an assessment, walk the chain and verify each one:

  • LV incomer ACB long-time pickup Ir — a pickup modestly above transformer FLC (up to ≈ 1.05 × FLC) is normal practice: it gives close overload protection while absorbing metering tolerance and load variation without nuisance tripping. The manual's reference setting study (the breakers chapter) sets Ir = 2 375 A (0.95 × a 2 500 A sensor), i.e. 1.03 × FLC. What the assessor should flag is Ir above ≈ 1.1 × FLC (≈ 2 540 A) or above the cable/busbar ampacity — that genuinely permits sustained transformer overload (see the breaker deep-dive).
  • MV feeder relay overcurrent pickup — typically 1.1–1.25 × MV FLC, i.e. ≈ 74–84 A primary on the 66.9 A feeder, graded with the LV incomer (§10).
  • CT ratios — MV feeder ≈ 75/1 or 100/1 (FLC at 67–89 % of primary rating: healthy); LV incomer 2 500/5 or 3 200/5.
  • Metering and alarm thresholds — PMS high-current alarms typically at 90 % of FLC per incomer; demand alarms per §1.
  • Motor feeder settings are referenced to motor FLC, not transformer FLC — same formula with motor kW, efficiency and pf (§5).

Equipment technology

Where the FLC numbers physically live, and what carries them:

  • Nameplates — the transformer nameplate (catalogue) states rated kVA per cooling stage and usually prints rated current per winding directly: read it, do not trust drawings. The breaker rating plug, busbar label and cable schedule are the other primary sources.
  • Busbars and risers — at 2 309 A the LV incomer is hard-drawn copper bar, typically 2–3 laminations per phase, sized for both continuous current (at 50 °C ambient inside an IP-rated board — derate!) and short-circuit bracing (§4).
  • Current transformers — ring or bar-primary CTs scale thousands of amperes down to 1 A or 5 A for relays and meters; separate cores for metering (accuracy class 0.2S/0.5S, saturates early to protect instruments) and protection (class 5P/10P, stays linear into fault current).
  • Trip units and digital relays — measure current continuously via internal or external CTs; modern units display true-RMS per-phase current, which is your most accessible on-site FLC reading.

Control

Current is the most-monitored quantity on the plant. The LV trip units and MV relays stream per-phase amperes to the PMS/SCADA over the substation network; the tariff meter integrates it into demand. Assessment use: pull the 12-month per-incomer current trend and overlay the 2 309 A line — sustained operation above ≈ 0.85 pu (≈ 1 960 A) at 50 °C ambient deserves a finding even before the FLC line is crossed, because the derating culture says published ratings assume kinder ambients. Where no trending exists, a clamp-meter reading at peak shift, divided by 2 309 A, gives instant per-unit loading.

Sizing method

  1. Take rated S (kVA) from the nameplate — not the drawing.
  2. Take actual line voltage V — measure it; SEC LV systems sit at 400 V nominal but the as-found voltage matters for current.
  3. Compute I = S × 1000 / (1.732 × V).
  4. Select the next standard conductor/frame rating above FLC, then apply the 50 °C / grouping / enclosure derating factors (§6) and re-check that the derated rating still exceeds FLC.
  5. Cross-check: LV FLC / MV FLC must equal the voltage ratio; FLC ≈ 1.44 A/kVA at 400 V; measured current / calculated FLC = per-unit loading.

9 — Load Flow & Voltage Profile Reference

Goal: understand what a load-flow (power-flow) study is, follow the voltage from the SEC intake down to a motor terminal by hand, know what the software adds beyond hand arithmetic, and recognise when to demand a study.

What a Load Flow Actually Is

A load flow answers one deceptively simple question: with every load drawing its normal power, what is the voltage at every bus and the current in every branch? It is the "steady-state weather map" of the plant — no faults, no starting transients, just the plant running. Think of the network as a water system: SEC is the reservoir at fixed pressure (13.8 kV), every cable and transformer is a pipe with friction (impedance), and every load is a tap drawing flow. Open all the taps and the pressure at the far end of the system is inevitably lower than at the reservoir. The load flow computes exactly how much lower, everywhere, simultaneously.

Why it cannot be fully done by hand: each voltage drop depends on the current, but each load's current depends on its voltage (a constant-power motor draws more current as voltage falls). The equations are circular, so the solution is found by iteration — guess voltages, compute currents, recompute voltages, repeat until the numbers stop moving. For a radial two-bus system one iteration by hand is plenty (below); for a real plant model with dozens of buses, motors and the PFC bank, the iteration is what the software is for.

Three outputs matter to an assessor:

  • Bus voltages — every bus within limits (typically ± 5 % at utilisation points, per SASO-IEC 60364 and the SEC Distribution Code at the point of connection)?
  • Branch loadings — every transformer, cable and busbar below its (derated) continuous rating in both normal and N-1 configurations?
  • Reactive flows and losses — power factor at the SEC intake ≥ 0.95 with the PFC bank in service (§7), and losses not flagging an inefficient operating split?

The pattern to internalise: voltage only ever falls as you move toward the load (capacitors and taps are the two deliberate exceptions), and the design game is to spend the ± 5 % budget so that the last motor on the worst feeder at the summer peak still sees ≥ 0.95 pu. The off-load tap changer is the coarse correction (set once, at shutdown); the PFC bank is the dynamic one — reactive current is what drags voltage down through the predominantly reactive transformer impedance, so correcting pf at the bus also props up the voltage.

A Two-Bus Load Flow by Hand

The simplest possible load flow — source bus, one impedance, load bus — is exactly the SEC-to-LV-MSB path, and it is worth doing by hand because it is the hand check you will run against any software study you are handed.

Worked example — Voltage drop SEC bus → LV-MSB (indicative)

System: stiff 13.8 kV source → TX-1 (1600 kVA, Z = 6 %) → LV-MSB bus. Load: ≈ 73 % of transformer rating (1 176 kVA) at pf 0.90 with the PFC bank in service (cos φ = 0.90, sin φ = 0.436).

Step 1 — Split the transformer impedance. Z = 6 % is mostly reactance; take R ≈ 1.2 %, X ≈ 5.9 % (so that sqrt(1.2² + 5.9²) ≈ 6.0 — typical for a 1600 kVA distribution unit; nameplate load loss gives the exact R).

Step 2 — Apply the regulation formula (valid for small drops):

dV% ≈ load_pu × (R% × cos φ + X% × sin φ) dV% ≈ 0.73 × (1.2 × 0.90 + 5.9 × 0.436) dV% ≈ 0.73 × (1.08 + 2.57) = 0.73 × 3.65 = 2.7 %

Step 3 — Place the result on the voltage profile. With the off-load tap at +2.5 % the no-load secondary sits at ≈ 410 V; subtracting the 2.7 % drop (≈ 11 V) lands the LV-MSB bus at ≈ 399–400 V at full normal load — on nominal. Adding the worst downstream feeder drop of ≈ 2.4 % (§6) puts the farthest motor terminal at ≈ 0.97 pu — inside the ± 5 % limit. ✓

Sanity checks: (1) at zero load the drop is zero, so the bus would sit at the tapped 410 V — lightly-loaded night-shift voltage visibly higher than day-shift voltage is normal and confirms the model. (2) In N-1 (one transformer at ≈ 100 %+ with load shed): dV% ≈ 1.0 × 3.65 ≈ 3.7 % — the surviving bus dips a further ≈ 1 %, still workable. (3) Worsen the pf to 0.85 (PFC out of service): sin φ = 0.527, dV% ≈ 0.73 × (1.02 + 3.11) ≈ 3.0 % — the PFC bank is quietly worth ≈ 0.3–0.5 % of bus voltage. All values indicative.

Loss check, same hand level: copper loss scales with loading². A 1600 kVA unit with ≈ 17 kW full-load copper loss dissipates ≈ 0.73² × 17 ≈ 9 kW at normal load, plus ≈ 2–3 kW constant iron loss — per transformer, continuously, which is why "two units at 73 %" versus "one unit at 100 % + one energised idle" is a real operating-cost question the load flow can answer.

What the Software Adds

Commercial power-system tools (ETAP, DIgSILENT PowerFactory and similar — named here generically, with no endorsement) do nothing conceptually beyond Step 2 above. What they add is scale and rigour:

  • Simultaneity — they solve every bus at once by iteration (Newton–Raphson and variants), correctly capturing the circular voltage-depends-on-current-depends-on-voltage coupling that hand methods linearise away.
  • Load models — constant-power motors, constant-impedance heaters and lighting, and the PFC bank's voltage-squared sensitivity are modelled distinctly; at depressed voltage these behave very differently.
  • Scenario sweeps — normal / N-1 / tie-closed / DG-island / minimum and maximum grid voltage, each at several tap positions, in minutes. The tap-selection table in a good study report is the direct product.
  • A validated network model — the same model then feeds the short-circuit (§4), motor-starting (§5), protection-coordination (§10) and arc-flash studies. The load flow is the foundation study: if its input data are wrong, every downstream study inherits the error.
Garbage in, garbage out. A software study is only as good as the cable lengths, transformer test-sheet impedances and load data typed into it. Your hand check above should land within ≈ 1 % of the software's transformer drop; if it does not, question the model's inputs before questioning your arithmetic.

When to Demand a Study (and How to Read One)

Recommend (or require) a fresh load-flow study whenever any of these is true:

  • No study exists, or it predates the as-built plant — the plant then has no formal basis for its tap settings, cable ratings or transformer loading limits. Absence is itself a finding.
  • Load has grown > ≈ 10 % since the study revision, or any feeder ≥ ~150 kW has been added (management-of-change trigger).
  • Voltage complaints — flicker, motors running hot, drives tripping on undervoltage, contactors dropping out on big-motor starts.
  • Configuration changes — PFC retrofit, DG addition or re-rating, proposal to operate tie-closed, or a new SEC connection agreement.
  • As-found measurements disagree with the study — measured bus voltage or incomer current more than a few % from the study's normal case.

15 — Per-Unit System & the MVA Method Core

Goal: learn the per-unit (pu) system — the universal bookkeeping trick behind every impedance percentage on every nameplate — and its field-friendly cousin the MVA method, then use both to reproduce the plant's headline LV fault level (≈ 38.5 kA) from nothing but the transformer nameplate.

The Per-Unit Idea, From Scratch

Why this exists

A power system spans wildly different voltage levels — here 13.8 kV and 400 V, joined by a transformer. Working in ohms across that boundary is miserable: every impedance must be converted by the square of the turns ratio (34.5² ≈ 1 190) each time it crosses the transformer, and one slipped conversion ruins the whole study.

The per-unit system removes the problem by expressing every quantity as a fraction of a chosen base: pick one base power S_base for the whole system and the nominal voltage of each level as V_base, and then voltage, current, power and impedance all become dimensionless ratios. The magic: a transformer's impedance has the same per-unit value seen from either winding — the 34.5² conversion is baked into the bases and disappears from the arithmetic. The network becomes one flat circuit with no voltage levels in it.

You already use per-unit daily without naming it: the transformer nameplate "Z = 6 %" is a per-unit impedance (0.06 pu on the transformer's own kVA and voltage base); "the motor draws 6 × FLC at start" is per-unit current; "bus voltage 0.97 pu" is per-unit voltage. The system has only four rules:

quantity_pu = actual value / base value I_base = S_base / (sqrt(3) × V_base) [per voltage level] Z_base = V_base² / S_base Z_pu(new base) = Z_pu(old) × (S_base,new / S_base,old) [same voltage]

The last rule is the one used constantly in the field: nameplate impedances come on each machine's own rating, and you must move them all to one common base before adding them.

What goes wrong without it

The classic, expensive error: an engineer estimates the LV fault level for a switchboard extension by adding the transformer impedance in ohms to the upstream grid impedance in ohms — but takes the grid impedance at 13.8 kV without referring it through the transformer. The grid ohms are ≈ 1 190 times too large; the computed fault current collapses; the new board is bought at 25 kA where the true duty is ≈ 38.5 kA single-transformer (and ≈ 77 kA if anyone ever closes the tie). The error sleeps until the first real bolted fault, at which point an underrated breaker may fail to interrupt — the most violent failure mode an LV board has (§4).

The quieter failures: mixing impedances quoted on different kVA bases without the base-change rule (a 6 % on 1600 kVA is not the same animal as 6 % on 2000 kVA), or comparing relay settings across voltage levels in amperes instead of per-unit, which makes coordination reviews (§10) needlessly error-prone. Per-unit is not optional sophistication; it is the error-proofing that makes multi-voltage arithmetic survivable on a clipboard.

Feel for the numbers

  • Z = 6 % means exactly this: with the LV winding bolted shut, only 6 % of rated primary voltage is needed to push full-load current through the transformer. Flip it round: apply full voltage to that short and you get 1 / 0.06 ≈ 16.7 × full-load current. The fault level of a transformer-fed bus is hiding in plain sight on the nameplate.
  • 1.0 pu is always "rated, nominal, comfortable". Per-unit turns every quantity into a universal gauge: 0.97 pu voltage = slightly low anywhere from 400 V to 400 kV; 6 pu current = motor starting; 16.7 pu current = bolted fault. Your intuition transfers across the whole industry.
  • The MVA method makes "strength" additive-ish: every element is described by the short-circuit MVA it can pass (S_sc = S_rating / Z_pu). Sources in parallel: add. Elements in series: combine like parallel resistors (product over sum). The SEC grid here is a ≈ 600 MVA "reservoir"; the 1600 kVA transformer is a ≈ 27 MVA "pipe" — and a 27 MVA pipe fed from a 600 MVA reservoir passes ≈ 26 MVA: the skinniest pipe dominates. That single sentence is most of short-circuit engineering.

Worked calculation

Worked example — LV fault level by per-unit and by MVA method (indicative)

Chosen bases (stated explicitly, always): S_base = 1600 kVA (the transformer's own rating — the convenient choice here); V_base = 13.8 kV on the MV side and 400 V on the LV side.

Step 1 — Base currents (one per voltage level):

I_base,MV = 1 600 000 / (1.732 × 13 800) = 66.9 A I_base,LV = 1 600 000 / (1.732 × 400) = 2 309 A

Note these are exactly the FLCs from §3 — choosing the transformer's own rating as base makes 1.0 pu current = FLC, which is why it is the convenient choice.

Step 2 — Transformer impedance in per-unit: nameplate Z = 6 % on its own 1600 kVA base → Z_tx = 0.06 pu. No base conversion needed (we deliberately chose its base). Had we chosen S_base = 2 MVA instead: Z_tx = 0.06 × (2000 / 1600) = 0.075 pu — different number, same physics, identical final answer.

Step 3 — LV fault current, infinite-bus assumption (grid impedance ≈ 0, the standard conservative first pass):

I_fault,pu = V_pu / Z_pu = 1.0 / 0.06 = 16.7 pu I_sc = 16.7 × I_base,LV = 16.7 × 2 309 A ≈ 38 500 A ≈ 38.5 kA

Step 4 — Same answer by the MVA method (no currents until the very end):

S_sc,tx = S_rating / Z_pu = 1 600 kVA / 0.06 = 26 667 kVA ≈ 26.7 MVA I_sc = S_sc / (sqrt(3) × V) = 26 700 000 / (1.732 × 400) ≈ 38 500 A ≈ 38.5 kA

Step 5 — How much does the finite grid actually shave off? SEC design fault level 25 kA at 13.8 kV (SEC range 25–40 kA):

S_sc,grid = sqrt(3) × 13.8 kV × 25 kA ≈ 597 MVA

Grid and transformer in series — combine product-over-sum:

S_sc,LV = (597 × 26.7) / (597 + 26.7) ≈ 25.6 MVA → I_sc ≈ 36.9 kA

The infinite-bus 38.5 kA is ≈ 4 % conservative — which is exactly the right direction to err when checking breaker ratings.

Sanity checks: (1) direct method: I_sc = FLC / Z_pu = 2 309 / 0.06 = 38.5 kA ✓ — three independent routes, one answer. (2) 16.7 × FLC "feels" right for a 6 % transformer (1/0.06). (3) Two transformers tie-closed: double the source → ≈ 77 kA, the Rev 3 tie-normally-open rationale (§4). All values indicative; motor contribution adds a further increment in a formal IEC 60909 study.

Where Per-Unit Numbers Live on Real Equipment

Equipment settings

Per-unit is not just a paper convention — most of the settings you will dial into real devices are entered in per-unit of something, and knowing the base is half the setting:

  • Transformer nameplate impedance is always per-unit (percent) on the transformer's own rating: Z = 6 % means 0.06 pu on 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV. Change the base, and the number changes — the physics does not.
  • Relay pickups are set in multiples of nominal (a 51 element pickup of "1.1 × In" is a per-unit statement), and CT secondary quantities are effectively per-unit on the CT ratio (§10).
  • LV trip units express L/S/I pickups as multiples of the frame or plug rating — "L = 0.95 × In, S = 4 × In" is per-unit language (§14).
  • Voltage bands in the Saudi Grid Code / SEC Distribution Code are stated as ± percent of nominal — per-unit voltage by another name.

Per-unit on the nameplate

Equipment technology

The "equipment" of per-unit is the data that feeds it. The numbers come from three physical places: nameplates (transformer Z%, rated kVA and voltages — stamped after the factory short-circuit test per SASO-adopted IEC 60076), type-test certificates (switchgear ratings expressed against rated values), and the utility intake declaration (SEC fault level in kA or MVA at 13.8 kV — ask for the design value in writing; the range is typically 25–40 kA). Treat all three as controlled documents: a per-unit study is only as good as the bases and impedances it was fed.

Per-unit in relays and studies

Control

Power-system software (load flow, short circuit, arc flash) works internally in per-unit; what you control is the model data discipline. The plant single-line in the software must carry the same bases, the same nameplate impedances and the same SEC intake fault level as the controlled documents — and every modification (new transformer, tie philosophy change, SEC reinforcement) triggers a model update and re-run. The PMS and metering supply the measured MW / Mvar / voltage values you compare against the model's per-unit predictions: persistent disagreement means the model, not the plant, is wrong.

Choosing the base values

Sizing method

The per-unit / MVA recipe you will reuse for the rest of Part 2 — pin it up:

  1. Choose bases and say so out loud: one S_base for the whole study (the transformer rating, or a round 10/100 MVA); V_base = nominal voltage of each level (13.8 kV, 400 V). Base currents follow: I_base = S_base / (sqrt(3) × V_base).
  2. Convert every impedance to the common base: Z_new = Z_nameplate × (S_base / S_nameplate). Grid: Z_grid,pu = S_base / S_sc,grid.
  3. Combine: series impedances add; parallel sources add their short-circuit MVA (or combine impedances product-over-sum).
  4. Read off the answers: I_sc = (V_pu / Z_pu) × I_base at the bus of interest; voltage drops and loadings fall out in percent directly.
  5. Sanity-check against the direct method (FLC / Z_pu for a single transformer) and against the previous study revision before trusting any new number.
Per-unit is the dialect the whole industry speaks: nameplates, relays, trip units and grid codes all quote per-unit numbers. Master the base conversion once and every fault, dip and loading question in this manual becomes one or two lines of arithmetic.
