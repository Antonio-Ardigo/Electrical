Part 2 — Calculations

Where you are in the workflow: you have identified the equipment and read the SLD (Part 1); now you run the numbers that tell you whether the existing equipment is correctly rated before you write a single recommendation.

Assessment calculations are verification, not green-field design. Your job in the field is to ask: "Is this transformer/breaker/cable/genset still adequate for what the plant is actually doing today?" — and to back the answer with arithmetic, not opinion.

Why Calculations Matter in an Assessment

Every rating you see on the SLD — a transformer kVA, a breaker breaking capacity, a busbar bracing figure, a cable cross-section, a capacitor bank, a genset — is the output of a calculation. That calculation is the audit trail that justifies the rating. In an assessment you work in the opposite direction: you start from the nameplate, reverse-engineer the assumption that was made, and check whether reality still matches it.

The two failure modes you are looking for:

  • Under-rating — cables and busbars that overheat (fire risk), switchgear that cannot interrupt a fault (catastrophic failure), protection that mis-coordinates or nuisance-trips, motors that stall, process voltage too low to run.
  • Over-rating — wasted capital tied up in oversized transformers, switchgear frames, cable and gensets the plant will never use; this matters most when the client is evaluating whether to add load or replace equipment.

1 — Load & Demand Estimate

Goal: confirm the ~2 MW plant demand and convert it to apparent power (MVA) — the anchor point for every sizing calculation that follows.

Method

Assumptions (indicative): sum of connected loads (nameplate) ≈ 2.7 MW; overall demand/diversity factor ≈ 0.75; overall power factor before correction = 0.85.

Worked example — Demand and apparent power

Step 1 — Active demand:

Pdemand = Σ Pconnected × demand factor

Pdemand = 2.7 MW × 0.75 = 2.025 MW ≈ 2.0 MW

Step 2 — Apparent power:

S = P / pf

S = 2.0 MW / 0.85 = 2.35 MVA

Step 3 — Installed transformer base (with diversity/spare margin):

Sinstalled = 2.35 × 1.20 to 1.25 ≈ 2.8–3.0 MVA

This drives the 2 × 1600 kVA selection (3.2 MVA installed).

Result: maximum demand ≈ 2.0 MW / 2.35 MVA at 0.85 pf. All values indicative.

Field tip — What to capture: obtain the latest load list or motor schedule from the client. If none exists, read each MCC ammeter at peak shift and multiply by √3 × V × pf to back-calculate kW per feeder. Sum the branch demands to build your own diversity estimate.

Assessment interpretation

If as-found…Likely meaningAction
Demand > transformer capacity Plant has grown since original design Flag overload risk; recommend load-flow study
Demand < 50 % capacity for years Oversized original design or load reduction Check transformer no-load losses; note in report
No load list available Documentation gap Raise as finding; capture ammeter readings
The demand estimate sets the size of the entire electrical plant. Every other calculation in Part 2 is downstream of this number. If the load list is wrong or absent, all downstream ratings are unverifiable.

2 — Transformer Sizing & N-1 Check

Goal: verify the 2 × 1600 kVA selection and confirm that one transformer (TX-1 or TX-2) can carry the essential load via the bus-tie (BT) when the other is lost.

Normal loading

Worked example — Normal per-unit loading

With 2.35 MVA shared equally across both transformers:

Sper tx = 2.35 MVA / 2 = 1.175 MVA

Loadingnormal = 1.175 / 1.6 = 0.73 pu (≈ 73 %)

Each transformer runs at roughly 73 % of nameplate — a comfortable margin.

N-1 check (one transformer lost)

Worked example — N-1 loading with essential-load shedding

Worst case (no shedding):

LoadingN-1, full = 2.35 / 1.6 = 1.47 pu (147 %) — not acceptable for continuous operation.

With non-essential load shed (essential fraction ≈ 55 %):

Sessential = 0.55 × 2.35 = 1.29 MVA

LoadingN-1, essential = 1.29 / 1.6 = 0.81 pu (≈ 81 %) — within continuous rating. ✓

Result: the 2 × 1600 kVA + bus-tie design is N-1 secure for essential load, provided non-essential load is shed automatically. This is the load-shedding requirement on LV-MSB.

Assessment check: confirm the bus-tie (BT) auto-transfer scheme is functional and the load-shedding sequence is current. A bus-tie that cannot close automatically, or load shedding that has never been tested, makes the N-1 claim invalid.

What to check on site

  • Transformer nameplates: confirm kVA, voltage ratio, vector group, Z %, off-load tap positions and current tap setting.
  • Transformer loading (incomer ammeter or SCADA trend): compare against 73 % normal / 81 % N-1 limits.
  • Bus-tie (BT): confirm auto-transfer is enabled and last test date.
  • Load-shedding relay/PMS: confirm essential/non-essential split list is documented and relays are active.
  • Transformer cooling: ONAN or ONAF? Are fans/pumps serviceable?
On the forms →
(a) Document inventory: populate the Transformer record row — enter nameplate kVA, Z %, vector group, tap position, and calculate loading % from the incomer ammeter reading.
(b) AS-FOUND site data required: transformer nameplate (kVA, Z %, voltage ratio, vector group, tap range and current position); incomer ACB ammeter reading (A) or SCADA kVA trend at peak load; bus-tie status (normally-open confirmed Y/N); load-shedding relay type and last-test date; cooling type (ONAN/ONAF) and fan status.
Two transformers are only as good as their bus-tie and load-shedding scheme. Verify all three on site before claiming N-1 security.

3 — Full-Load Current (LV & MV)

Goal: calculate the 400 V full-load current (FLC) of a 1600 kVA transformer — the basis for the incomer ACB frame size and busbar rating — and the corresponding MV primary current.

Formula

Ifl = S / (√3 × V)

Worked example — LV FLC (1600 kVA at 400 V)

Ifl = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V)

Ifl = 1 600 000 / 692.8 = 2 310 A

This sets the LV-MSB incomer ACB frame (≥ 2 500 A) and the ≈ 2 500–3 200 A busbar rating.

Worked example — MV FLC (1600 kVA at 11 kV)

Ifl,MV = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 11 000 V)

Ifl,MV = 1 600 000 / 19 052 = 84 A

This sizes the MV transformer-feeder breakers [52-T1] / [52-T2] in MV-SWGR and the CT primary ratio for the feeder protection relays.

Quick sanity check on site: read the incomer ammeter or clamp-meter on the LV bus incomer cable. Divide by 2 310 A to get per-unit loading. Values consistently above 0.85 pu should be investigated.

General 3-phase FLC formula

For any three-phase equipment rated S (kVA) at line voltage V (V):

4 — Short-Circuit Current & Switchgear Withstand

Goal: calculate the LV prospective short-circuit current from the transformer impedance, check the bus-tie parallel case, and verify that the switchgear breaking capacity and busbar bracing are adequate.

Formula

Isc = Ifl / Z(pu)

Assumption: Z = 6 %; infinite upstream MV source (conservative — ignores MV source impedance, so the real value is slightly lower).

Worked example — Single-transformer fault contribution at 400 V

Isc = 2 310 A / 0.06 = 38 500 A ≈ 38.5 kA

This is the prospective symmetrical short-circuit current at the LV-MSB busbars from one transformer alone.

Worked example — Both transformers in parallel (bus-tie closed)

Isc,parallel ≈ 2 × 38.5 kA = 77 kA (upper bound; ignores source and cable impedance)

This is the bounding case when the bus-tie is closed and both transformers feed the same bus simultaneously.

Why the bus-tie is normally open

Operating the bus-tie (BT) normally-open keeps each bus half fed from only one transformer, limiting the fault duty on each section to ≈ 38.5 kA. Selecting the LV-MSB and MCC bus at 50–65 kA covers this single-transformer fault with margin. If the bus-tie is closed continuously (or accidentally left closed), the fault duty doubles to ≈ 77 kA and may exceed the board rating — a safety-critical finding.

Critical assessment check: if you find the bus-tie (BT) closed under normal running conditions, verify the switchboard fault rating immediately. A 50 kA-rated board exposed to 77 kA cannot safely interrupt a fault — this is a Category A finding requiring immediate action.

Site verification checklist

  • Confirm BT is normally-open and electrically interlocked (both incomers cannot close simultaneously onto the same bus).
  • Read ACB and MCCB nameplates: confirm rated breaking capacity (Icu) ≥ 38.5 kA (single-tx scenario).
  • Check busbar bracing rating on LV-MSB label or type-test certificate: should be ≥ 50 kA / 1 s (or equivalent peak).
  • Confirm a short-circuit/fault-level study exists and is dated within ≤ 5 years (or after any network change).
  • Check that MCCs fed from LV-MSB are rated for the same fault level.
On the forms →
(a) Document inventory / recommendations register: populate the Short-circuit Assessment row — record calculated Isc (kA), rated Icu on each ACB/MCCB (kA from nameplate), busbar bracing (kA from label), and the result (adequate / marginal / inadequate).
(b) AS-FOUND site data required: transformer nameplate Z % (each unit); ACB and MCCB breaking capacity (Icu) from nameplate or door label (kA); LV-MSB busbar short-time withstand rating (kA / duration) from board label or type-test certificate; bus-tie status and interlock scheme description; date of last formal fault-level study.
38.5 kA from one transformer; up to 77 kA with both in parallel. The normally-open bus-tie is a protective operating rule, not just a design convention — verify the interlock is active and the board rating has not been quietly exceeded.

5 — Motor FLC, Starting Methods & LV/MV Feed Threshold

Goal: calculate the full-load current and starting current of a representative LV motor, explain why large motors use soft-start or VFD, and identify the LV-to-MV feed decision threshold.

Full-load current formula (motor)

Ifl = P / (√3 × V × pf × η)

Worked example — 110 kW LV motor FLC and DOL starting current

Given: P = 110 kW, V = 400 V, pf = 0.86, η = 0.95.

FLC:

Ifl = 110 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.86 × 0.95)

Ifl = 110 000 / 566 = 194 A

DOL starting current (≈ 6.5 × FLC):

Istart = 6.5 × 194 = 1 260 A

A 1 260 A inrush causes a voltage dip on the bus, mechanical/electrical stress on the motor windings, and possible nuisance trips on other feeders. A soft-starter limits this to ≈ 3 × FLC (≈ 580 A); a VFD limits it to near FLC and provides speed control.

Starting method selection guide

Motor size (LV) Typical starting method Approx. start current Notes
Up to ≈ 7.5 kW Direct-on-line (DOL) ≈ 6–7 × FLC Bus impact negligible at this size
≈ 7.5–55 kW DOL / star-delta / soft-starter Star-delta: ≈ 2 × FLC Star-delta suits constant-torque loads; soft-starter preferred for pumps
≈ 55–200 kW Soft-starter or VFD Soft-starter: ≈ 3 × FLC Soft-start/VFD reduces bus dip and extends motor life
Speed-controlled loads VFD (any size) ≈ 1 × FLC Energy saving on pumps/fans (affinity laws)

LV-to-MV feed threshold

Individual motors larger than ≈ 150–200 kW are often more economically fed at MV (3.3 / 6.6 kV) via a dedicated MV motor starter rather than the 400 V MCC. At 200 kW, the LV current reaches ≈ 350 A — cable and starter costs rise steeply. For a ≈ 2 MW plant the largest drives may sit near this boundary; check the motor schedule.

Assessment tip: if you find a large motor (> 150 kW) on the LV MCC with no soft-start or VFD, check the voltage-dip impact on adjacent motors and instruments. Also check whether the MCC starter contactor and cable are correctly rated — the starting current can be 6–7 times the FLC nameplate.
For a 110 kW motor at 400 V: FLC ≈ 194 A, DOL inrush ≈ 1 260 A. Motors above ≈ 55 kW should have soft-start or VFD; above ≈ 150–200 kW, consider MV feed.

6 — Cable Sizing & Voltage Drop

Goal: size a motor feeder cable and verify the voltage drop is within acceptable limits (typically ≤ 5 % for motor feeders).

Four-step method

Worked example — 110 kW motor feeder (from §c-motors)

Step 1 — Design (base) current:

IB = motor FLC = 194 A

Step 2 — Derating factors (IEC 60364):

kgrouping = 0.80 (cables grouped on tray)

ktemp = 0.94 (40 °C ambient)

ktotal = 0.80 × 0.94 = 0.752

Iz(req) = IB / ktotal = 194 / 0.752 = 258 A

Step 3 — Cable selection:

Select 95 mm² Cu XLPE with tabulated rating ≈ 264–300 A on tray (per manufacturer / IEC tables). Iz ≥ 258 A. ✓

Step 4 — Voltage-drop check:

Run length L = 120 m; for 95 mm² Cu: R ≈ 0.23 Ω/km, X ≈ 0.08 Ω/km; pf = 0.86 → cosφ = 0.86, sinφ = 0.51.

ΔV = √3 × I × (R·cosφ + X·sinφ) × L

ΔV = 1.732 × 194 × (0.23 × 0.86 + 0.08 × 0.51) × 0.120

ΔV = 1.732 × 194 × (0.1978 + 0.0408) × 0.120

ΔV = 1.732 × 194 × 0.2386 × 0.120 = 9.6 V

%ΔV = 9.6 / 400 × 100 = 2.4 %

Within the ≤ 5 % limit (and ≤ 3 % is comfortable). Selection accepted.

Assessment guidance

In the field you cannot always measure cable resistance directly. Use the cable size stamped on the gland tag or tray label, the feeder length from the cable schedule, and the above formula to estimate voltage drop. Then compare with the measured voltage at the motor terminal (meter at the motor DB) versus the LV-MSB bus voltage.

Undersized cable signs: discoloured insulation, hot gland or termination, cable tray showing signs of heat distortion, motor terminal voltage significantly below bus voltage. Any of these should be flagged immediately.

7 — Power-Factor Correction (PFC)

Goal: size the automatic capacitor bank (PFC) to raise the plant power factor from 0.85 to 0.95, and understand why detuning reactors are mandatory when VFDs are present.

Formula

Qc = P × (tanφ1 − tanφ2)

Worked example — PFC from 0.85 to 0.95 (P = 2.0 MW)

pf1 = 0.85 → φ1 = 31.8°, tanφ1 = 0.620

pf2 = 0.95 → φ2 = 18.2°, tanφ2 = 0.329

Qc = 2 000 kW × (0.620 − 0.329) = 2 000 × 0.291 = 582 kVAr

Select a standard ≈ 600 kVAr automatic bank in steps (e.g. 6 × 100 kVAr).

Correcting to 0.95 also reduces apparent power: S = 2.0 / 0.95 = 2.11 MVA (from 2.35 MVA), relieving transformer and cable loading by ≈ 10 %.

Detuning reactors

VFDs and non-linear loads inject harmonic currents (5th, 7th, 11th… harmonics). An un-detuned capacitor bank presents a very low impedance at harmonic frequencies and can resonate with the supply inductance, causing catastrophic capacitor failure and high harmonic distortion. For this plant, with multiple VFDs, the bank must be fitted with 7 % detuning reactors (series inductors that raise the bank's resonant frequency above the 5th harmonic).

Assessment check: if you find a capacitor bank without detuning reactors on a plant with VFDs, this is a reliability finding. Check for capacitor over-temperature, bulging, or blown fuses in the bank — signs that resonance has already caused damage.

What to record on site

  • Confirm PFC bank kVAr rating matches ≈ 600 kVAr (± 20 % acceptable).
  • Confirm detuning reactors (7 % or 14 %) are present and intact.
  • Read power-factor meter at LV-MSB (or from the SCADA historian trend): should be ≥ 0.93 with bank in service.
  • Check power-factor penalty charges on the utility invoice — evidence of consistently low pf even with the bank in service suggests under-sizing or damaged capacitors.
  • Note the automatic controller step count and switching threshold settings.
~580 kVAr of correction raises pf from 0.85 to 0.95, reducing apparent power by ≈ 10 %. On a VFD-heavy plant, detuning reactors are not optional — they are the difference between a working bank and an exploding one.

8 — Standby Generator Sizing Rationale

Goal: justify the indicative DG rating (≈ 1 000 kVA) on an essential-load basis and explain the constraints that really drive the size.

Sizing calculation

Worked example — Essential-load generator sizing

Assumptions (indicative): the DG covers essential loads only (not full 2 MW plant); essential active load ≈ 0.65 MW at pf 0.8.

Running (steady) apparent demand:

Sessential = P / pf = 0.65 MW / 0.8 = 0.81 MVA

Step-load / motor-start allowance (the largest essential motor starting against the generator's limited transient capacity):

SDG ≥ Sessential × 1.25 = 0.81 × 1.25 = 1.01 MVA

Result: select a ≈ 1 000 kVA standby DG — matching the design basis. All values indicative.

The governing constraint: voltage dip on motor start

For a generator, the largest single motor start is often the governing constraint, not the steady running load. A generator has a much higher source impedance than the utility, so a large DOL motor start causes a severe transient voltage dip that can stall other motors already running. The remedy is:

  • Soft-start or VFD all large essential motors (reduces starting kVA to ≈ 1.0–1.5 × FLC).
  • Sequence essential motor starts after DG reaches rated voltage/frequency (ATS timer).
  • Size the DG AVR for fast voltage recovery (< 2 s to within ± 3 %).
De-rating reminders: genset ratings are typically at ISO conditions (25 °C, sea level). For hot climates or elevated sites, apply the manufacturer's de-rating factor (commonly −1 % per 5 °C above 25 °C, −1 % per 100 m above sea level). A 1 000 kVA unit at 45 °C and 300 m may deliver only ≈ 940 kVA — check the site data sheet.

Site assessment checklist

  • Confirm DG nameplate kVA rating and compare with essential-load estimate.
  • Check de-rating data sheet for site altitude and ambient temperature.
  • Confirm ATS (ATS) transfer time setting and load-shed sequence at DG start.
  • Check fuel day-tank level and fuel consumption log (evidence of recent tests).
  • Review last monthly/annual test run report: confirm the DG accepted the full essential load at rated voltage and frequency.
  • Confirm AVR and governor are within calibration.
  • Check battery/charger for engine starting: voltage, last replacement date.
The genset is not sized for full plant load — only the essential fraction (≈ 55 %, ≈ 1 000 kVA). The motor-start transient is usually the binding constraint, not the steady-state load. A genset that has never been tested under full essential load cannot be claimed as reliable backup.

9 — Reading & Validating a Load-Flow Study

Goal: confirm the voltage profile, power flows and losses at peak load — that every bus stays within ± 5 % of nominal and no branch is thermally overloaded.

Transformer voltage regulation (hand check)

Worked example — Voltage regulation at 73 % loading

Split Z = 6 % into R ≈ 1.2 %, X ≈ 5.9 %. At normal ≈ 73 % loading (§c-transformer), pf ≈ 0.90 (cosφ = 0.90, sinφ = 0.436):

ΔV% ≈ loadpu × (R%·cosφ + X%·sinφ)

ΔV% ≈ 0.73 × (1.2 × 0.90 + 5.9 × 0.436)

ΔV% ≈ 0.73 × (1.08 + 2.57) = 0.73 × 3.65 = 2.7 %

With a +2.5 % off-load tap, the LV-MSB bus sits at ≈ 400 V at full load. Adding the worst feeder drop of 2.4 % (§c-cables) puts the motor terminal at ≈ 97–98 % of nominal — inside the ± 5 % limit. ✓

Indicative transformer losses

Transformer copper (load) loss scales with loading2. For a unit with full-load copper loss ≈ 17 kW at 73 % loading:

PCu = 0.732 × 17 kW = 0.533 × 17 ≈ 9 kW per transformer at normal load.

Iron (no-load) loss is constant; for a 1 600 kVA unit, typically ≈ 2–3 kW.

If no load-flow study exists: this is itself a finding. A plant with no documented power-system study has no formal basis for its tap settings, cable ratings, or transformer loading limits. Recommend a load-flow study as part of your remediation programme.
On the forms →

A load-flow study validates the sizing from §1–§8 under realistic load conditions. Without it you cannot formally claim that voltages, flows, and taps are correct. Its absence is a finding, not just a gap.

10 — Protection Coordination & Selectivity

Goal: set protective devices so the device closest to the fault trips first, isolating the smallest possible zone (selectivity/discrimination), while still clearing every fault within the required time.

The protection chain

Tracing upstream from a fault on a motor feeder:

Motor MCCB → MCC busbar → LV outgoing MCCB → LV incomer ACB (LV-MSB) → MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T] (MV-SWGR) → MV incomer relay [52-I]

Time grading

Worked example — Time-graded relay settings (indicative)

Between two graded relays, leave a margin Δt for breaker opening time + relay overshoot + CT error + safety ≈ 0.3 s:

tupstream = tdownstream + Δt

Indicative ladder (from feeder to incomer):

Device / relaySetting (indicative)Notes
Motor feeder MCCB ≈ 0.05 s (instantaneous/short-time) Closest to fault; fastest clear
LV incomer ACB short-time delay t = 0.20 s 0.05 + Δt ≈ 0.20 s
MV feeder relay [52-T] (IDMT 51) t = 0.50 s 0.20 + 0.30 = 0.50 s
MV incomer relay [52-I] t = 0.80 s 0.50 + 0.30 = 0.80 s

Worked example — MV relay pickup setting (CT 100/1 on 84 A primary FLC)

Ipickup = 1.25 × 84 A = 105 A → 1.05 × CT (set ≈ 1.05 on a 100/1 CT)

Transformer internal faults are cleared instantaneously by the 87T differential relay, independent of the time grading above.

Assessment: what to verify

  • Request the coordination study / time-current coordination curves (TCC). If none exists, raise as a finding.
  • Confirm relay settings in the IED configuration files match the approved coordination study values.
  • Confirm each protective device (ACB, MCCB, relay) has been tested within the required period (typically every 1–3 years for MV relays; 5 years for LV trip units).
  • Check that the 87T differential relay (if fitted) is in service and its CTs are correctly wired and matched.
  • Confirm IDMT curve type and time-multiplier setting are consistent across all upstream relays.
  • Verify zone-selective interlocking (ZSI) or bus-differential is active (if the design relies on it for arc-flash mitigation).
Loss of selectivity means an upstream breaker trips for a downstream fault, blacking out a much larger zone than the faulty feeder. This is a high-impact finding — it multiplies the production loss from any fault. It is also invisible until a fault occurs unless the coordination curves are checked.
On the forms →
(a) Document inventory / recommendations register: record whether a coordination study (TCC) exists and its date; note the LV incomer ACB short-time setting (s) and MV feeder relay time-dial / time-multiplier setting; flag any device whose as-found setting does not match the study.
(b) AS-FOUND site data required: relay settings reports or IED configuration printouts for each MV feeder relay, MV incomer relay, and transformer differential relay; ACB trip-unit settings (Long-time, Short-time, Instantaneous bands and delays in amps and seconds); CT ratios on each MV feeder; last relay/trip-unit test date and result.
A coordination study tells you that relay A trips before relay B for a fault between them. Without documented, verified settings, you cannot make that claim — and the next fault will reveal the gap.

11 — Arc-Flash / Incident Energy (IEEE 1584) & the Clearing-Time Lever

Goal: estimate the incident energy and arc-flash boundary at the LV-MSB, label the gear and select PPE — and show why clearing time is the dominant lever for reducing arc-flash hazard.

Arc-flash is a life-safety calculation. The indicative values below illustrate method only. Actual PPE selection, labelling, and arc mitigation must be based on a full IEEE 1584-2018 study with site-specific data. Never rely on these figures for live-work decisions.

Inputs for the LV-MSB

System voltage400 VBasis of design

Worked example — Indicative arc-flash calculation at LV-MSB (400 V)

Step 1 — Arcing current (LV arcing/bolted ratio ≈ 0.58 per IEEE 1584):

Ia ≈ 0.58 × 38.5 kA ≈ 22 kA

Step 2 — Incident energy at D = 600 mm, t = 0.20 s (indicative from IEEE 1584-2018 empirical model):

E ≈ 10 cal/cm²

(This is an illustration only — the IEEE 1584-2018 model uses six parameters and electrode configuration corrections that cannot be reduced to a simple formula here.)

Step 3 — Arc-flash boundary (distance where E falls to EB = 1.2 cal/cm², inverse-square scaling):

AFB ≈ D × √(E / EB)

AFB ≈ 0.6 m × √(10 / 1.2) = 0.6 × 2.89 ≈ 1.7 m

Result: ≈ 10 cal/cm² at 600 mm working distance. Arc-rated PPE must exceed this (e.g. a 12–25 cal/cm² arc-rated kit); arc-flash boundary ≈ 1.7 m.

The clearing-time lever

Incident energy is approximately proportional to clearing time. Halving the clearing time roughly halves the energy. This is the cheapest and most effective arc-mitigation tool:

Clearing time (s) Indicative E (cal/cm²) Mechanism
0.20≈ 10Normal LV incomer short-time delay
0.05≈ 2.5 Arc-reduction maintenance switch / ZSI / arc-flash relay
0.02≈ 1.0 High-speed arc-flash detector (optical) — near threshold level

Adding an arc-flash reduction maintenance switch, zone-selective interlocking (ZSI), or an optical arc-flash relay to achieve t ≈ 0.05 s reduces E to ≈ 2.5 cal/cm² and shrinks the boundary substantially — at some tension with the selectivity grading of §c-protection, which is why bus differential / ZSI is the preferred solution (it achieves fast clearing without losing selectivity).

Assessment: what to check

  • Confirm an IEEE 1584-compliant arc-flash study exists and is dated (≤ 5 years or since last network change).
  • Check that arc-flash labels are posted on all switchgear panels including LV-MSB, MCCs, and MV-SWGR.
  • Confirm PPE kits available on site match or exceed the arc-flash incident energy class shown on the labels.
  • Verify the maintenance switch (if fitted) is functional and its activation procedure is documented in the site safety rules.
  • Check whether ZSI or bus-differential is active and tested.
On the forms →
(a) Document inventory / recommendations register: record whether an IEEE 1584 arc-flash study exists and its date; note incident energy (cal/cm²) and arc-flash boundary from the study for each assessed panel; record PPE category required; flag missing or outdated labels.
(b) AS-FOUND site data required: bolted fault current (from short-circuit study, §c-shortcircuit); upstream device clearing time (relay setting or ACB short-time delay in seconds); electrode/enclosure configuration (from drawings or physical inspection); working distance (measure or per IEEE 1584 default); arc-flash label (photograph it — capture cal/cm², boundary, and PPE category); PPE available on site (category rating on kit label).
Arc-flash hazard is driven by fault current and clearing time. The clearing time is the most cost-effective lever. A maintenance switch or ZSI that cuts clearing from 0.20 s to 0.05 s reduces incident energy by 75 % — without replacing any switchgear.

12 — Earthing & Lightning-Protection Assessment

Goal: verify the MV neutral earthing resistor (MV-NER), the LV earth system, the earth-conductor cross-sections, and the lightning-protection classification (IEC 62305).

MV neutral earthing resistor (NER)

The MV system is resistance-earthed to limit earth-fault current to ≈ 300 A, so faults are detectable but arc energy and damage are limited.

Worked example — NER resistance (11 kV, 300 A earth fault)

RNER = (VLL / √3) / Ief

RNER = (11 000 / 1.732) / 300 = 6 351 / 300 ≈ 21 Ω

The NER must be rated for its short-time dissipation: P = I2 × R = 3002 × 21 = 90 000 × 21 = 1.89 MW briefly (for the fault-clearing duration, e.g. 10 s).

LV earthing (TN-S)

The 400 V system is TN-S, solidly earthed. An LV earth fault is effectively a line fault and is cleared quickly by the feeder protection. No NER is used on the LV side.

Earth-conductor sizing (adiabatic)

Worked example — Minimum earth-conductor cross-section (LV fault)

Formula (IEC 60364-5-54, adiabatic): A = I × √t / k

I = 38.5 kA (LV fault from §c-shortcircuit); t = 0.2 s (LV incomer clearing time from §c-protection); k = 143 (Cu conductor with PVC/XLPE insulation per IEC 60364).

A = 38 500 × √0.2 / 143 = 38 500 × 0.447 / 143 ≈ 120 mm² Cu

Earth conductors at the LV-MSB and main equipment earthing conductors must be ≥ 120 mm² Cu (or equivalent aluminium per the standard).

Earth grid & lightning protection

Earth grid: target resistance typically ≤ 1 Ω for the substation (confirm against soil resistivity ρ and IEEE 80 touch/step voltage calculations for the specific site). The earth-grid design must ensure touch voltages < 50 V (step voltages lower) during the maximum earth-fault condition.

Lightning protection (IEC 62305): the lightning-protection level (LPL I to IV) is set by a risk assessment per IEC 62305-2, considering structure dimensions, location, use, and annual flash density. LPL I (most stringent) applies to process plants with flammable/explosive content; LPL III–IV for standard industrial buildings. The LPS comprises air terminals (rods/mesh), down-conductors, and earth termination, all bonded to the main earth grid.

Site assessment checklist

  • Confirm MV-NER nameplate resistance (Ω) and short-time rating — compare with calculated 21 Ω / 1.89 MW (indicative).
  • Check the NER neutral CT is wired to the earth-fault relay and the relay is in service.
  • Confirm main earth-conductor sizes at LV-MSB busbars ≥ 120 mm² Cu (or aluminium equivalent).
  • Measure (or request certificate for) earth-grid resistance: target ≤ 1 Ω (or as specified).
  • Confirm all MV and LV equipment bodies, cable armouring, and cable trays are bonded to the earth grid.
  • Check the IEC 62305 lightning-protection risk assessment and LPS installation certificate are available.
  • Inspect air terminals and down-conductors for corrosion, mechanical damage, or missing/loose connections.
On the forms →
(a) Document inventory / recommendations register: record NER nameplate resistance (Ω) and short-time rating; main earth-conductor sizes (mm²); earth-grid resistance measurement (date and value in Ω); LPS certificate date and LPL class; note any earth-bonding deficiencies found.
(b) AS-FOUND site data required: NER nameplate (Ω, short-time kW / kVA, rated duration); neutral CT ratio and earth-fault relay trip threshold; measured main earth-conductor size (mm²) from gland tags or cable schedule; earth-grid resistance test certificate; soil resistivity test data (if available); IEC 62305 risk assessment and LPS installation drawings/certificate; photographs of air-terminal and down-conductor condition.
Earthing is the last line of defence against electrocution, equipment damage, and lightning. An NER at the wrong resistance, an under-sized earth conductor, or an unbonded cable tray can all be lethal. Verify with measurements, not just drawings.

Where the money is

The calculations that dominate capital cost are load/transformer sizing (§1–§3) and short-circuit rating (§4): between them they fix ACB frame sizes, breaking capacities, busbar bracing, cable cross-sections, and the genset — the most expensive items on the plant. By contrast, PFC (§7) and voltage-drop/cable losses (§6) mainly affect operating cost and utility penalties rather than headline capital. Motor starting (§5) sits in between. The power-system studies (§9–§12) are mostly engineering and settings plus modest hardware, but their value is measured in avoided injuries, equipment loss, and unplanned downtime.

Prioritise verification of §1–§4 first in any assessment: if the transformer/breaker/ busbar ratings are wrong, everything downstream is at risk. Then verify §11–§12 for life-safety compliance. All others follow.

Self-Check Problems

Work each problem before opening the answer. All values are indicative; use √3 = 1.732.

Problems

  1. A second LV motor is rated 75 kW, 400 V, pf 0.85, η 0.94. Find its FLC and its DOL starting current (use 6.5×).

    Show answer

    FLC = 75 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.85 × 0.94) = 75 000 / 553 = 136 A

    DOL = 6.5 × 136 = 882 A

  2. A 1 250 kVA transformer has Z = 6 %. Find its 400 V FLC and secondary short-circuit current.

    Show answer

    FLC = 1 250 000 / (1.732 × 400) = 1 250 000 / 692.8 = 1 804 A

    Isc = 1 804 / 0.06 = 30.1 kA

  3. Re-correct the plant from 0.85 → 0.93 (instead of 0.95). How much kVAr is needed (P = 2.0 MW)?

    Show answer

    pf2 = 0.93 → φ2 = 21.6°, tanφ2 = 0.396; tanφ1 = 0.620.

    Qc = 2 000 × (0.620 − 0.396) = 2 000 × 0.224 = 448 kVAr (≈ 450 kVAr bank).

  4. With one transformer out, the bus-tie carries 1.3 MVA of essential load on a 1 600 kVA unit. What is the per-unit loading — is it acceptable?

    Show answer

    Loading = 1.30 / 1.60 = 0.81 pu (81 %).

    Acceptable for the essential-load N-1 case (non-essential load shed). Within continuous rating. ✓

  5. A feeder carries IB = 160 A with grouping derating kg = 0.85 and temperature derating kt = 0.91. What tabulated cable rating Iz is required?

    Show answer

    Iz(req) = 160 / (0.85 × 0.91) = 160 / 0.7735 = 207 A.

    Select a cable with tabulated rating ≥ 207 A (e.g. 70 mm² Cu XLPE on tray ≈ 220–240 A, confirm against manufacturer table).

  6. The MV system is resistance-earthed to limit earth-fault current to 400 A at 11 kV. What NER resistance is required?

    Show answer

    RNER = (11 000 / 1.732) / 400 = 6 351 / 400 = 15.9 Ω

  7. An arc-flash study gives 6 cal/cm² at a 600 mm working distance. Estimate the arc-flash boundary using EB = 1.2 cal/cm² and inverse-square scaling.

    Show answer

    AFB = 0.6 m × √(6 / 1.2) = 0.6 × √5 = 0.6 × 2.236 = 1.34 m

  8. A cable feeder is 150 m long, carries 220 A at pf 0.90 (sinφ = 0.436). The cable is 120 mm² Cu XLPE: R = 0.196 Ω/km, X = 0.079 Ω/km (on tray). Calculate the voltage drop and state whether it is within the 5 % motor-feeder limit at 400 V.

    Show answer

    ΔV = 1.732 × 220 × (0.196 × 0.90 + 0.079 × 0.436) × 0.150

    ΔV = 1.732 × 220 × (0.1764 + 0.0344) × 0.150

    ΔV = 1.732 × 220 × 0.2108 × 0.150 = 12.1 V

    %ΔV = 12.1 / 400 × 100 = 3.0 % — within the 5 % limit. ✓

  9. The upstream MV relay [52-T] trips at t = 0.50 s. The MV incomer relay [52-I] must grade above it with a 0.3 s margin. What is the minimum MV incomer relay trip time?

    Show answer

    t52-I = t52-T + Δt = 0.50 + 0.30 = 0.80 s

  10. A DG set is rated 1 000 kVA at ISO conditions. The site is at 400 m altitude and 42 °C ambient. Apply de-rating: −1 % per 100 m above sea level; −1 % per 5 °C above 25 °C. What is the effective site rating?

    Show answer

    Altitude de-rating: −1 % × (400 / 100) = −4 %

    Temperature de-rating: −1 % × (42 − 25) / 5 = −1 % × 3.4 = −3.4 %

    Total de-rating = 4 % + 3.4 % = 7.4 %

    Site rating = 1 000 × (1 − 0.074) = ≈ 926 kVA (indicative; confirm with manufacturer data).
