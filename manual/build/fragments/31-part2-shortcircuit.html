4 — Short-Circuit Current & Switchgear Withstand Core

Goal: understand why the prospective short-circuit current is the single most consequential number in the plant, calculate it from the SEC utility connection down to the 400 V busbars (including the IEC 60909 refinements — voltage factor, X/R, peak factor, motor contribution), and verify that every switchboard, breaker and busbar between the 13.8 kV intake and the last MCC can both survive and interrupt the worst fault it can ever see.

This section feeds almost every other calculation in Part 2: the breaker rating checks in §14 — Circuit Breakers, the grading study in §10 — Protection Coordination, the incident-energy calculation in §11 — Arc-Flash, and the cable thermal withstand check in §6 — Cable Sizing. Get this number wrong and every one of those studies inherits the error.

Indicative data. Every numeric value in this section is indicative and chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis (SEC 13.8 kV, 60 Hz, design utility fault level 25 kA within the SEC-stated 25–40 kA range; 2 × 1600 kVA, Z = 6 % transformers; 400/230 V TN-S LV system; 50 °C design ambient). The plant's formal short-circuit study to the SASO-adopted edition of IEC 60909, performed with the fault level confirmed in writing by SEC, governs. Standards are cited as SASO-adopted IEC together with the SEC Distribution Code / Saudi Grid Code and SBC 401.

The One Number Every Rating Answers To

Why this exists

Walk through the plant and read the nameplates. The LV-MSB incomer ACB says something like Icu 65 kA. The MV switchgear label says 31.5 kA / 3 s. The busbar type-test certificate quotes a peak withstand in kA. The MCC door label, the fuse cartridges in the PFC panel, even the little MCBs in the lighting distribution board — every one of them carries a fault rating in kiloamps. None of those numbers describes anything the equipment does on a normal day. They all answer one question, and it is the question this section exists to ask: if a bolted short circuit occurs right here, how much current will the network push through this spot before something opens?

The answer is called the prospective short-circuit current (Isc, or Ik" in IEC 60909 language). "Prospective" is doing important work in that name: it is the current that would flow if the fault were a perfect zero-impedance connection and nothing intervened. It is a property of the network, not of the fault or of any protective device. You cannot reduce it by setting a relay differently, and you cannot wish it smaller — you can only make sure every piece of equipment in its path is rated for it.

The physics is humbling. Under normal load the network behaves like a polite negotiation: the load's impedance limits the current to what the process needs — roughly 2 309 A through each transformer at full load. A bolted fault removes the load from the circuit entirely. What remains between the SEC generators and the fault is only the series impedance of the supply path: the grid, the transformer, a few metres of busbar. On the Rev 3 plant that residual impedance is about 6 % on the transformer base — which means the fault current is about 1 / 0.06 ≈ 17 times the transformer's full-load current. Seventeen times. Every conductor, joint, support and contact in the path must carry that current — and the breakers must interrupt it — without converting the switchroom into the failure story told in the next sub-section.

Two distinct duties flow from the one number, and the distinction matters all through this section:

  • Withstand — every component the fault current flows through (busbars, cable, CTs, isolators, the closed contacts of breakers that are not tripping) must survive the thermal and magnetic punishment for as long as the fault persists. This is a passive, mechanical duty: it begins at the instant of the fault, milliseconds before any relay has even measured anything.
  • Interruption — the one device assigned to clear the fault must be able to break that current: stretch, cool and extinguish an arc carrying tens of kiloamps. This is the breaking-capacity duty (Icu at LV, rated short-circuit breaking current at MV) covered in detail in §14.

Both duties are sized from the prospective short-circuit current. That is why this calculation comes so early in Part 2 and why it is flagged Core: transformer sizing (§3) tells you what the plant needs; the short-circuit study tells you what the plant must survive.

The prospective short-circuit current is a property of the network, not of any device. It defines two duties at every point in the system: components in the fault path must withstand it, and the clearing device must interrupt it. Every kA rating on every nameplate in the plant is an answer to this one number.

A Failure Story: the Board That Was Never Asked

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional, but every element of it is assembled from failure modes documented across the industry, and every number is consistent with the Rev 3 plant basis. Read it as a rehearsal of what the checklist at the end of this section is protecting you from.

A process plant much like this one had grown in stages. The original 400 V switchboard was bought when the site had a single 1000 kVA transformer; the board was honestly rated 25 kA, comfortably above the 21 kA the old transformer could deliver, and the type-test certificate said so. Years later the site re-powered: two 1600 kVA, Z = 6 % units, a new bus-tie arrangement, a doubling of demand. The new transformers could each drive about 38.5 kA into a bolted fault on the board — and with the tie closed, towards 77 kA. Nobody re-ran the short-circuit study. The old board "had always been fine". The fault level had been raised; the board was never asked whether it could cope.

For six years nothing happened, because nothing happened: prospective fault current is invisible until there is a fault. Then, during a maintenance window, a forgotten bonding lead was left across two phases behind the incomer compartment, and the incomer was closed onto it. A bolted phase-to-phase fault, dead short, full network behind it.

What followed took less than a quarter of a second. Within the first half-cycle — 8.3 ms at 60 Hz — the current rose to its fully offset peak, close to 88 kA on this network (the worked calculation below shows where that number comes from). The electromagnetic force between parallel conductors scales with the square of the instantaneous current — F ∝ I² — so a board braced for a 25 kA duty (peak around 53 kA) suddenly experienced forces nearly three times what its supports were type-tested for. The busbars did what unbraced conductors carrying anti-parallel currents always do: they tried to fly apart. One support insulator cracked; a bar deflected; the phase-to-phase clearance collapsed; and the bolted fault became a three-phase arcing fault in open air inside the enclosure.

Now the second failure mode took over. An arc at this current level burns at temperatures of the order of 10 000–20 000 K — hotter than the surface of the sun — and it is not stationary: magnetic forces drive it along the bars (away from the source) like a blowtorch on rails. The enclosure was ordinary folded sheet steel, never designed for internal arc containment. The arc burnt through the rear panel in roughly 100 ms, vented plasma and vaporised copper into the cable trench behind the board, and ignited cable insulation. The upstream MV protection — graded slow precisely so the LV devices could act first, as explained in §10 — finally cleared the fault from the 13.8 kV side after several hundred milliseconds. The LV incomer breaker never got the chance: an early instant of the fault had already exceeded its 25 kA interrupting rating, its contacts welded on the first peak, and from then on it was just another conductor in the fault path.

Total damage: the switchboard destroyed, the cable trench fire-damaged, the plant down for weeks awaiting a replacement board on long lead time. The two electricians in the room survived because they happened to be standing beside, not in front of, the cubicle — the arc-flash consequences of exactly this scenario are quantified in §11. The investigation's finding fitted in one sentence: the installed fault level had been raised above the switchboard's rating, and no process existed to catch it.

Notice the chain of cause and effect, because it is general:

  • Underrated withstand fails first and fails mechanically — bracing, supports, clearances — within the first peak, before any protection can act. No relay setting can prevent it.
  • Underrated interruption fails next — a breaker asked to break more than its Icu may weld, restrike or rupture, converting the protective device itself into the failure.
  • Containment fails last — a non-arc-rated enclosure lets the arc out, escalating an electrical fault into a fire and a personnel hazard.
The trap of the quiet upgrade. Fault level rises whenever the network gets "stronger": a bigger or lower-impedance transformer, a second transformer paralleled, a closed bus-tie, a new SEC infeed, on-site generation paralleled to the bus. None of these changes look dangerous — they all improve supply. Every one of them must trigger a re-check of the short-circuit study against every downstream rating. The bus-tie case is the most common offender and has its own sub-section below.

How Violent Is 38.5 kA?

Feel for the numbers

Numbers in kiloamps slide past the eye. Before the formal calculation, let us honestly estimate two things: the energy released if the 400 V bus faults and arcs for 0.3 s, and the mechanical force the busbars feel at the first peak. Both follow from arithmetic you can redo on a phone.

Energy. The power the network can push into a three-phase bolted fault at the LV-MSB is:

Estimate — energy in a 0.3 s arcing fault at the LV-MSB

Apparent power feeding a bolted three-phase fault:

S_fault = sqrt(3) × V × Isc = 1.732 × 400 V × 38 500 A ≈ 26.7 MVA

For a bolted fault nearly all of that energy is dissipated out in the network impedance (mostly inside the transformer). The dangerous case is the arcing fault, where the arc itself is a resistor sitting at the fault point. Maximum power transfer theory says a resistive arc dissipates the most energy locally when its impedance matches the source impedance — at which point roughly half the bolted-fault power burns in the arc:

P_arc (upper estimate) ≈ 0.5 × 26.7 MW ≈ 13.3 MW

If protection clears the fault in 0.3 s (a realistic time if the incomer's short-time delay is involved — see §14):

E_arc ≈ 13.3 MW × 0.3 s ≈ 4.0 MJ

Sanity check: this is the crude upper-bound estimate; the IEEE 1584 method in §11 computes the realistic arcing current and the fraction of energy reaching a worker. But as an order of magnitude it is honest — and it is the right order of magnitude to be frightened of.

Now translate 4.0 MJ into everyday quantities, computed honestly:

  • TNT: TNT releases ≈ 4.6 MJ/kg, so 4.0 MJ ≈ 0.9 kg of TNT — a stick-of-dynamite-class energy release inside a sheet-steel cabinet at chest height. (The comparison is about energy, not blast dynamics — an arc releases it over 0.3 s rather than microseconds — but the pressure wave from an arc in an enclosure is still enough to blow doors off non-arc-rated boards.)
  • Car crash: a 1 200 kg car at 100 km/h (27.8 m/s) carries KE = 0.5 × 1 200 × 27.8² ≈ 0.46 MJ ≈ 0.5 MJ. The arc releases the kinetic energy of about eight highway-speed cars, in a third of a second, in a box.
  • Kettle: a 2 kW kettle would take 4 000 000 / 2 000 = 2 000 s ≈ 33 minutes to release the same energy that the arc releases in 0.3 s. The arc is roughly 6 600 kettles running at once.

Force. Parallel conductors carrying current exert forces on each other — anti-parallel currents (as between two phases at the moment of a phase-to-phase fault) repel. The force per metre between two long parallel bars is:

Estimate — magnetic force between LV busbars at the first peak

F/L = 2 × 10^-7 × i1 × i2 / d (N/m, currents in A, spacing d in m)

Take the first-peak current with DC offset, ip ≈ 88 kA (derived in the worked calculation below from kappa ≈ 1.61 at the transformer terminals), and a typical 100 mm phase spacing:

F/L = 2 × 10^-7 × (88 000)^2 / 0.1 = 2 × 10^-7 × 7.74 × 10^9 / 0.1 ≈ 15 500 N/m

That is ≈ 15.5 kN per metre ≈ 1.6 tonne-force on every metre of busbar — as if a family car were hung off each metre of bar, applied sideways, arriving in 8.3 ms. A busbar span between supports 0.5 m apart carries a transverse load of ≈ 7.7 kN (≈ 790 kg).

Now the tie-closed case, Isc ≈ 77 kA, ip ≈ 175 kA:

F/L = 2 × 10^-7 × (175 000)^2 / 0.1 ≈ 61 000 N/m ≈ 6.2 tonne-force per metre

Sanity check — the I-squared law: doubling the current (38.5 → 77 kA) quadrupled the force (15.5 → 61 kN/m), exactly as F ∝ I² demands. This is why a board rated 50 kA cannot be "probably OK" at 77 kA: it is not 54 % overstressed, the bracing forces are (77/50)² ≈ 2.4 times its tested duty.

Hold these two pictures — a kilogram of TNT of energy, and a car's weight of sideways force per metre of bar — while reading the rest of the section. They are what the words "breaking capacity" and "short-time withstand" actually mean.

A 0.3 s arcing fault at the 400 V bus releases of the order of 4 MJ — about 0.9 kg of TNT, or eight highway-speed car crashes — and the first current peak loads the busbars with about 1.6 tonne-force per metre. Force scales with I²: closing the bus-tie doesn't add 100 % to the mechanical duty, it adds 300 %.

From SEC Intake to the 400 V Bus, Step by Step

Worked calculation

We now compute the prospective short-circuit current at the two places where it matters most — the 13.8 kV switchgear bus and the 400 V LV-MSB — and then refine the raw numbers with the three IEC 60909 corrections a field engineer should be able to reproduce: the voltage factor c, the peak factor κ (DC offset), and the motor contribution. All arithmetic is doable on a phone. The per-unit machinery behind these shortcuts is developed in §2 — Per-Unit System; the transformer data come from §3 — Transformer Sizing.

Step (a) — fault level at the 13.8 kV intake. SEC does not hand you an impedance; it hands you (on request, in writing) a design fault level at the point of connection. For this plant the Rev 3 design basis is 25 kA at 13.8 kV, and SEC's stated planning range for this class of network is 25–40 kA — which is why the MV switchgear is bought at 31.5 kA (next sub-section) rather than barely above 25 kA. Convert the current into a short-circuit apparent power, because MVA is the most convenient currency for the next step:

Worked calculation — MV fault level as short-circuit MVA

Formula (three-phase apparent power at the fault):

S"k = sqrt(3) × U × Ik"

Substitution, with U = 13.8 kV and Ik" = 25 kA:

S"k = 1.732 × 13 800 V × 25 000 A = 597.6 MVA ≈ 598 MVA

It is also worth expressing the grid "strength" as an impedance on the transformer base, because that tells us whether the grid impedance matters at the 400 V bus. On the 1600 kVA base of one transformer:

Z_grid (pu, 1600 kVA base) = S_base / S"k = 1.6 MVA / 597.6 MVA ≈ 0.0027 pu ≈ 0.27 %

Sanity check: the grid contributes about 0.27 % of impedance in series with the transformer's 6 % — under one twentieth. The 400 V fault level is therefore dominated by the transformer, and treating the SEC grid as an "infinite bus" overstates the LV fault current by only a few percent — an error in the safe direction for rating equipment. At the upper SEC value of 40 kA (956 MVA) the grid term shrinks further, to ≈ 0.17 %. Indicative values; SEC's written confirmation and the formal IEC 60909 study govern.

Field habit. The MV fault level is an SEC planning number and can rise over the life of the plant as the SEC network is reinforced. Treat the 25 kA figure as today's floor, not a constant of nature: that is exactly why the MV board is specified at 31.5 kA, inside SEC's stated 25–40 kA range, and why the re-study triggers at the end of this section include "SEC notifies a change of fault level".

Step (b) — fault current at the 400 V bus through one transformer. The most useful shortcut in all of power engineering: for a fault at the LV terminals of a transformer fed from a strong grid, the fault current is simply the transformer's full-load current divided by its per-unit impedance. The logic is one sentence long — the impedance voltage Z % is, by definition, the fraction of rated voltage needed to push rated current through the short-circuited transformer, so applying full voltage to a short circuit pushes 1/Z times rated current.

Worked calculation — LV bus fault current, one 1600 kVA transformer

Transformer full-load current at 400 V (from §3):

I_FLC = S / (sqrt(3) × U) = 1 600 000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) = 2 309 A

Infinite-bus fault current with Z = 6 % = 0.06 pu:

Isc = I_FLC / Z = 2 309 A / 0.06 ≈ 38 500 A = 38.5 kA

Refinement — include the SEC grid impedance from step (a) in series:

Isc = 2 309 / (0.06 + 0.0027) = 2 309 / 0.0627 ≈ 36.8 kA

Sanity check: the grid-included value is only ≈ 4 % lower than the infinite-bus value, confirming step (a)'s conclusion that the transformer dominates. For rating equipment we keep the conservative 38.5 kA; for protection settings the study value matters. Cross-check against the "17 times" intuition from the start of this section: 1/0.06 = 16.7, and 16.7 × 2 309 A ≈ 38.5 kA. Consistent.

The bus-tie case. The LV-MSB has two bus sections, one per transformer, with a normally-open bus-tie. Close the tie with both transformers energised and a fault anywhere on the bus now sees two 6 % impedances in parallel — i.e. 3 % — between itself and the (effectively infinite) grid:

Worked calculation — LV bus fault current, bus-tie closed

Z_parallel = 0.06 / 2 = 0.03 pu (two identical transformers in parallel) Isc = 2 309 A / 0.03 ≈ 77 000 A = 77 kA

Equivalently: each transformer independently drives its own 38.5 kA into the same fault, and the contributions add:

Isc = 38.5 kA + 38.5 kA = 77 kA

Sanity check: doubling the sources exactly doubles the fault current (the small grid term spoils this by only a couple of percent). But recall from "Feel for the numbers" that busbar force scales with current squared — the mechanical duty quadruples, from ≈ 15.5 kN/m to ≈ 61 kN/m.

This single calculation is the entire justification for the plant's tie-normally-open operating philosophy. With the tie open, each bus section sees at most ≈ 38.5 kA plus motor contribution (step (d)), comfortably inside a 50–65 kA board rating. With the tie closed and both incomers shut, the prospective current approaches 77 kA plus motor contribution — at or beyond the top of the board's rating. The tie exists for maintenance transfer: the switching sequence is close tie → open one incomer, so that both incomers and the tie are never all closed except for the few seconds of a make-before-break transfer, under a documented procedure (and, in many plants, an electrical interlock that enforces "two of three" — see §14).

One transformer: Isc ≈ 2 309 A / 0.06 ≈ 38.5 kA at the 400 V bus. Tie closed, both transformers: ≈ 77 kA — fault current doubles, busbar forces quadruple. That is why the bus-tie is normally open and why closing it is a controlled, interlocked, short-duration operation.

Step (c) — the IEC 60909 refinements. The raw 1/Z arithmetic above is the right first estimate, but the SASO-adopted IEC 60909 method adds three corrections that change purchasing decisions. Each is small to compute and large in consequence.

(c-1) The voltage factor c. Real buses do not sit at exactly nominal voltage; transformers have taps; the standard wants the maximum credible fault current for rating purposes. IEC 60909 therefore multiplies the nominal voltage by a factor c before calculating: for maximum fault currents, cmax = 1.10 at MV, and 1.05 at LV systems with the usual +6 % supply tolerance (1.10 where +10 % applies). A minimum set (cmin = 0.95–1.00) is used when you need the smallest fault current — for checking that protection still picks up at the far end of a long cable (§10). Applied to our LV bus:

Worked calculation — applying the voltage factor at the 400 V bus

Ik" = c × Isc(raw) = 1.05 × 38.5 kA ≈ 40.4 kA (one transformer) Ik" = 1.05 × 77 kA ≈ 80.9 kA (tie closed)

Sanity check: a 5 % voltage uplift gives a 5 % current uplift — fault current is linear in driving voltage. Note what this does at the margins: a board rated exactly 38.5 kA would already be non-compliant on the IEC 60909 maximum case. This is one reason the LV-MSB is specified at 50–65 kA.

(c-2) The peak factor κ and the DC offset. Everything so far is an RMS, steady-state-symmetrical number. But a fault that strikes at the wrong point on the voltage wave does not start symmetrically: the inductive network cannot change its current instantaneously, so a decaying DC component is superimposed on the AC fault current, and the very first peak can approach twice the symmetrical peak. How close it gets depends on how slowly the DC component decays — i.e. on the network's X/R ratio (decay time constant τ = X/(ωR) = L/R). High X/R = inductive, slow decay, high first peak. IEC 60909 captures this with the empirical peak factor:

Worked calculation — peak current ip at the 13.8 kV bus

Formula (IEC 60909 peak factor, then peak current):

kappa = 1.02 + 0.98 × e^(-3 / (X/R)) ip = kappa × sqrt(2) × Ik"

Substitution with a typical MV network X/R ≈ 15 and Ik" = 25 kA:

kappa = 1.02 + 0.98 × e^(-3/15) = 1.02 + 0.98 × e^(-0.2) = 1.02 + 0.98 × 0.819 ≈ 1.82 ip = 1.82 × 1.414 × 25 kA ≈ 64.4 kA peak

Sanity check: the theoretical ceiling is kappa = 2.0 (purely inductive, no decay), giving ip = 2 × 1.414 × 25 = 70.7 kA; a purely resistive network gives kappa = 1.02, ip ≈ 36 kA. Our 64.4 kA sits sensibly near, but below, the ceiling, as expected for X/R = 15. The MV switchgear's rated peak withstand is 2.6 × 31.5 kA ≈ 82 kA (the 2.6 factor is the 60 Hz standard ratio built into the IEC rating structure), so 64.4 kA passes with margin. At the 400 V transformer terminals the X/R is lower (≈ 6 for a 1600 kVA unit), giving kappa ≈ 1.61 and the ip ≈ 88 kA already used in "Feel for the numbers": 1.61 × 1.414 × 38.5 ≈ 87.7 kA.

(c-3) What the DC offset means in time. At 60 Hz one half-cycle lasts 1 / (2 × 60) = 8.33 ms — the fully offset first peak arrives about 8.3 ms after fault inception, long before any relay-plus-breaker chain can act. So the peak duty is unavoidable: busbar bracing and breaker making capacity must be rated for ip with no help from protection. Thereafter the DC component decays with τ = (X/R)/ω — for X/R = 15 at 60 Hz, τ = 15/377 ≈ 40 ms — so a breaker that opens its contacts at, say, 50–80 ms still interrupts a current with significant remaining asymmetry. This is why breaker ratings distinguish the symmetrical breaking current from the asymmetrical duty (expressed via the rated DC component percentage at contact separation), and why fast-tripping MV breakers on high-X/R networks must be checked for DC component, not just RMS amperes — taken up in §14.

Step (d) — motor contribution. Here is the refinement most often forgotten in hand calculations: during a fault, the plant's own running motors stop being loads and briefly become generators. A spinning induction motor has kinetic energy in its rotor and trapped flux in its magnetic circuit; when the bus voltage collapses, that flux drives current out of the motor and into the fault. The motor feeds the fault through its own subtransient impedance — which is essentially its locked-rotor impedance — so each running motor contributes roughly its starting current, 4–6 × FLC, decaying away over a few cycles as the trapped flux dies (induction motors have no excitation to sustain it).

Worked calculation — contribution of 500 kW of running LV motors

Assume an indicative 500 kW of LV motors running on one bus section at the moment of the fault (efficiency ≈ 0.94, pf ≈ 0.85). Aggregate full-load current:

I_FLC(motors) = 500 000 W / (1.732 × 400 V × 0.85 × 0.94) ≈ 900 A

Initial fault contribution at 4–6 × FLC (use 4 × as the typical aggregate figure — large motors near 6, small motors and those behind long cables nearer 4 or below):

I_motor ≈ 4 × 900 A ≈ 3 600 A ≈ 3.5–4 kA (up to ≈ 5.4 kA at 6×)

Total initial symmetrical fault current at the bus, one transformer:

Ik"(total) ≈ 38.5 kA + 4 kA ≈ 42–43 kA

Sanity check: the motor add-on is ≈ 10 % of the transformer contribution — small, but exactly the kind of margin that decides whether a 40 kA-rated device passes or fails. Note the direction of flow: motor current arrives at the fault from the load side, so it stresses outgoing feeder breakers "backwards" and inflates the first-cycle peak duty even when the upstream source is modest. IEC 60909 includes motor groups whenever their combined contribution exceeds about 5 % of the fault current — which it does here.

Pulling it together — the indicative Rev 3 fault-current picture (formal IEC 60909 study governs):

Rate equipment for the sum, not for the transformer alone.

Turning the Fault Table into Nameplate Ratings

Equipment settings

Strictly, the short-circuit study produces ratings to buy more than knobs to set — the settings themselves (pickups, time delays, curves) belong to the grading study in §10 and the breaker section in §14. But every rating selection below is a decision made against the fault table from the worked calculation, and the field engineer should be able to audit each one:

  • MV switchgear short-time withstand — 31.5 kA / 3 s. The bus, CTs, isolating contacts and cable terminations of the 13.8 kV board must carry the full fault current for the rated short time while upstream/downstream grading plays out. 31.5 kA > the 25 kA design level with margin inside SEC's 25–40 kA planning range; the 3 s duration covers the slowest credible backup clearing time. The paired peak (making) withstand, ≈ 2.6 × 31.5 ≈ 82 kA, covers the computed ip ≈ 64 kA. If SEC ever confirms a level above 31.5 kA, the board — not a setting — is what must change.
  • MV breaker rated short-circuit breaking current. Selected equal to the board's 31.5 kA class, with the rated DC component percentage checked against the network X/R ≈ 15 (τ ≈ 40 ms at 60 Hz) at minimum contact-parting time.
  • LV-MSB rated short-time/withstand — 50–65 kA class. The board must beat the worst single-source case including motors (≈ 42–43 kA) with margin; it is not rated for the tie-closed 77 kA+ case, which is why the operating philosophy (tie normally open, two-of-three interlock, make-before-break transfer only) is itself a "setting" — an administrative and interlock configuration that holds the duty inside the rating.
  • LV ACB/MCCB breaking capacities — Icu vs Ics. Icu (ultimate breaking capacity) is the maximum fault the breaker can interrupt once, after which it may need overhaul; Ics (service breaking capacity, quoted as a % of Icu) is what it can interrupt and remain in service. Select Icu ≥ the prospective fault at the point of installation (incomer ACBs: ≥ 50 kA at 400 V; feeder MCCBs further out may legitimately use lower Icu where cable impedance has reduced the fault level — proven by calculation, never by assumption). Prefer Ics = 100 % Icu at the incomers, where a re-closeable board after a fault is worth the price. Catalogue families in Part 6 — LV ACB/MCCB catalogue.
  • Icw of the LV incomers (short-time withstand of the breaker itself). An incomer ACB that must stay closed while a downstream device clears needs its own 1 s withstand ≥ the through-fault current — the basis of time-graded selectivity in §10.
  • Minimum fault check. The same study, run with cmin and maximum impedance, yields the smallest fault current at the far end of each feeder — the number protection pickups must sit below. Both edges of the study are used: maximum for ratings, minimum for settings.
Never rate to the bare transformer number. 38.5 kA is the transformer's contribution alone. The rating decision is made against c × (transformer + grid refinement) + motor contribution at the relevant bus, on the worst credible switching configuration. All values here are indicative; the certified IEC 60909 study fixes the final figures.

How Hardware Survives: Bracing and Containment

Equipment technology

Two pieces of physical engineering stand between the numbers above and the failure story at the start of this section: busbar bracing (surviving the magnetic forces) and arc-resistant construction (containing the worst case when, despite everything, an arc occurs inside the enclosure). The breaker arc-interruption technologies — vacuum at MV, air-chute at LV — are covered in §14; here we deal with the passive structure.

Busbar bracing. "Feel for the numbers" computed the load: F/L = 2 × 10-7 × ip² / d — note that the structural duty is set by the peak current ip, not the RMS value, because F ∝ ip² and the worst blow lands on the very first offset peak at 8.3 ms. A switchboard manufacturer meets this duty with:

  • Support spacing. Each busbar span behaves as a beam under a distributed magnetic load; bending stress grows with the square of the span. Halving the distance between insulator supports quarters the stress — which is why a 65 kA board visibly has supports every 300–400 mm where a 25 kA board might have them every metre. This is the cheapest kA upgrade lever a manufacturer has, and it is why withstand rating is baked into the mechanical design and cannot be retrofitted in the field.
  • Support strength and bar stiffness. Moulded or cast-resin insulator supports rated for the computed cantilever load; bars oriented edge-on to the force where possible; phase spacing d chosen as a compromise (wider spacing cuts the force linearly but lengthens the board).
  • Resonance check. The magnetic force on a bar carrying a 60 Hz current pulsates at 120 Hz (force ∝ i² rectifies the waveform). The bar/support system's natural frequency must sit away from 120 Hz, or the fault shakes the structure at resonance and the type-tested margin evaporates.
  • Proof by type test, not by calculation. The Icw / ip withstand of an assembled board is demonstrated by an actual short-circuit type test on the design (IEC 61439 series at LV, IEC 62271-200 at MV). The certificate is design-specific: change the bar size, spacing or supports and the certificate no longer applies.

Arc-resistant enclosures. Withstand ratings assume the fault current flows in the bars. The nastier case is the internal arcing fault — a tool, a rodent, tracking across polluted insulation (a real consideration at 50 °C with wind-blown dust). For MV switchgear, IEC 62271-200 defines an Internal Arc Classification (IAC): the assembly is type-tested with a deliberately initiated internal arc at rated current (for this plant's class, an IAC rating of 31.5 kA / 1 s would match the board) and must satisfy criteria that amount to: doors stay shut, no fragments ejected, no burn-through of accessible surfaces, and cotton indicators placed where personnel would stand do not ignite. The classification letters state which sides are protected — A for authorised-personnel access, with F/L/R (front/lateral/rear) coverage. Construction features that achieve it: reinforced doors and hinges, pressure-relief flaps that vent the arc blast upward into a duct or plenum away from the operator, compartment segregation (busbar / breaker / cable boxes) so an arc in one compartment cannot involve the next, and in premium designs ultra-fast arc-detection that collapses or clears the arc in tens of milliseconds (§11). Equivalent LV practice uses arc-fault-contained assemblies tested to the IEC/TR guidance under the IEC 61439 umbrella. Catalogue families in Part 6 — MV switchgear catalogue.

Field check. The IAC rating, like Icw, is on the nameplate and in the type-test certificate — and it is only valid with the enclosure as tested: doors closed and latched, pressure flaps unobstructed, gland plates fitted, venting path clear. A propped-open door or a blocked relief duct quietly deletes the arc rating without changing a single nameplate.

Seeing the Fault: Records, Oscillography and the PMS

Control

Prospective fault current cannot be "controlled" in the feedback sense — it is set by the network. What the control and monitoring layer does is three things: configure the network so the duty stays inside ratings, record every fault that actually happens, and turn those records into verification of the study this whole section is built on.

  • Configuration enforcement. The plant's power management system (PMS) and the switchgear interlocking together hold the fault level down: the two-of-three incomer/tie interlock at the LV-MSB prevents the 77 kA parallel configuration except during a deliberate transfer, and the PMS mimic shows at a glance whether the tie is closed. The DG (which adds its own subtransient contribution when paralleled) is connected through the ATS in open transition, so it never feeds a bus in parallel with SEC. Any future closed-transition or parallel-running modification is, by definition, a fault-level modification.
  • Oscillography in relays and trip units. Every modern MV protection IED, and the electronic trip units of the LV incomer ACBs, capture a disturbance record on every trip: sampled current and voltage waveforms (typically COMTRADE format at MV), pre-fault and post-fault, plus an event log time-stamped to the station clock. On that record you can see everything this section calculated — the symmetrical RMS level, the DC offset and its decay (hence the real X/R), the first peak, the motor contribution as an initial bump that decays over the first few cycles, and the exact clearing time.
  • PMS event recording. The PMS aggregates relay events, breaker status changes and analogue snapshots across the plant onto one synchronised timeline, so the sequence "feeder relay picked up → feeder breaker opened in 65 ms → incomer never tripped" can be reconstructed without walking from panel to panel. Accurate time synchronisation (GPS/NTP to the relays) is what makes the sequence trustworthy.
  • Using fault records after an incident. The post-incident drill: (1) freeze and download the records from every device that saw the fault, before they are overwritten; (2) read the actual fault magnitude off the oscillography and compare it with the study's predicted value for that location — a real 30 kA fault where the study predicted 20 kA means the model (or the SEC fault level) is wrong, and every rating verified against the study is now in question; (3) verify the protection operated per the grading plan (§10); (4) check the cleared duty against the interrupting device's rating and its cumulative-duty maintenance counter — breakers earn inspection after heavy interruptions; (5) file the record with the study, so the next revision starts from measured reality.
Every real fault is a free, full-scale validation test of the short-circuit study. A plant that downloads, reads and archives its relay oscillography knows its true fault levels; a plant that just resets the flag and re-closes is operating on an unverified model.

The Bus-by-Bus Fault Table: How a Study Is Actually Built

Sizing method

"Sizing" for short-circuit means establishing the prospective fault duty at every bus, then proving every device on that bus survives and (where required) interrupts it. The procedure, step by step:

  1. Fix the sources. Get the SEC intake fault level in writing (design value here: 25 kA at 13.8 kV; SEC range 25–40 kA — use the declared maximum for ratings). Add on-site sources: DG subtransient contribution when it can be connected, and running-motor contribution at each LV bus.
  2. Model the impedances. Grid, transformers (Z = 6 % on 1600 kVA), significant cable runs. Convert to a common base (per-unit / MVA method).
  3. Compute per bus, worst credible configuration. For each bus: initial symmetrical Ik", peak ip (κ·sqrt(2)·Ik" with the X/R of that bus), and thermal equivalent for the protection clearing time. The Rev 3 headline figures: MV board ≈ 25 kA, LV-MSB ≈ 38.5 kA one transformer, ≈ 77 kA tie-closed — which is exactly why the tie is normally open and interlocked.
  4. Build the fault table (bus → Ik", ip, duty time) and check every device against it: switchgear Icw (MV: 31.5 kA / 3 s) and peak withstand, breaker Icu/Ics (§14), busbar bracing, cable adiabatic limits (§7), and the protection settings that assume these currents (§10).
  5. Margin and document. Select the next standard rating above the computed duty (here 50–65 kA class at the LV-MSB), state the configuration assumptions on the study cover sheet, and put the study under document control.
  6. Re-study triggers: any new source (DG paralleling, solar/BESS), any transformer change, any tie-philosophy change, or an SEC network reinforcement letter. A certified IEC 60909 (SASO-adopted) study by a competent engineer governs over the hand numbers in this manual — all values here are indicative.
