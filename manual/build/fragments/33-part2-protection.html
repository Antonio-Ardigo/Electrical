10 — Protection Coordination & Selectivity Core

Goal: understand protection as the plant's nervous system — detect, decide, disconnect — and learn to set it so that the device closest to the fault always trips first (selectivity), while every fault is still cleared fast enough to limit damage. The centrepiece is a complete IDMT grading study across three levels of the Rev 3 plant: an MCC feeder MCCB, the LV-MSB incomer ACB, and the MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T] in MV-SWGR.

This section supplies the brains side of the brains-and-muscle contract introduced in §14 — Circuit Breakers. The fault currents we grade against come from §4 — Short-Circuit Current & Switchgear Withstand; the consequence of slow clearing on personnel safety is quantified in §11 — Arc-Flash; the relay hardware itself is catalogued in Part 6 — Protection Relays.

Indicative data. Every numeric value in this section — pickups, time multipliers, fault currents, clearing times — is indicative, chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis (SEC 13.8 kV, 60 Hz; 2 × 1600 kVA Dyn11 transformers; 400/230 V TN-S; 50 °C design ambient). The plant's formal protection coordination study governs. Standards cited are the SASO-adopted editions of IEC 60255 (measuring relays), IEC 61869 (instrument transformers) and IEC 60909 (fault calculation), applied with SBC 401, the SEC Distribution Code and the Saudi Grid Code.

The Plant's Nervous System: Detect, Decide, Disconnect

Why this exists

Touch a hot pan and your hand pulls back before you have consciously registered pain. Nerve endings detect, the spinal cord decides, muscles disconnect your hand from the hazard — all in a few tens of milliseconds, without involving the brain's slow deliberate reasoning at all. A power system needs exactly the same reflex, for exactly the same reason: by the time a human could react to a short circuit, the damage would be long done. Protection is that reflex, and like the body's, it has three distinct jobs that are easy to conflate and vital to keep separate:

  • Detect. Current transformers (CTs) and voltage transformers (VTs) are the nerve endings. They continuously report a scaled-down, safe replica of what is happening on the primary conductors — 1 A or 5 A secondary images of currents that may reach tens of kiloamps.
  • Decide. The protection relay is the spinal cord. It compares the measured quantities against settings — is this current larger than anything normal operation can produce? For how long has it been there? — and when the answer crosses a threshold, it closes an output contact. The decision itself is physically trivial: milliamps through a contact, microjoules of energy.
  • Disconnect. The circuit breaker is the muscle (§14). It receives the trip command on the 110 V DC trip circuit and physically interrupts the fault current — the only violent, energetic step in the whole chain.

If detection-decision-disconnection were the whole story, protection would be easy: put one enormous relay at the SEC intake, set it sensitive and fast, and let it trip for everything. The plant would be perfectly protected — and perfectly useless, because every cable nick on a 5.5 kW pump feeder would black out two megawatts of process. The hard part of protection engineering is not making it trip; it is making the right device trip, and only that device.

This property is called selectivity (or discrimination): for any fault, the protective device immediately upstream of the fault clears it, and every device further upstream sees the disturbance pass and stands down. The faulted branch dies; everything else lives. In the Rev 3 plant the chain from a process motor up to the utility looks like this:

Motor starter / MCCB (in MCC-1) → MCC incomer → LV outgoing MCCB (LV-MSB) → LV incomer ACB (LV-MSB) → MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T] (MV-SWGR) → MV incomer relay [52-I] → SEC network protection

Seven layers of protection stand between a motor terminal fault and the SEC grid. Selectivity means a fault at the motor is cleared by layer one and seen — but not acted on — by layers two through seven. Each upstream layer is the backup for the one below it: if the motor MCCB jams, the MCC incomer clears the fault a few tenths of a second later. Backup costs you a wider outage and more fault damage, which is exactly why it must be slower — it should act only when the primary protection has demonstrably failed.

So protection design is a permanent three-way negotiation:

  • Sensitivity — pick up for every genuine fault, even a high-resistance earth fault limited to a few tens of amps by the NER, yet ride through every legitimate event: full load at 50 °C, motor starting at six times rated current, transformer inrush at ten times, PFC capacitor switching transients.
  • Speed — clear faults before conductors anneal, busbars buckle and an arc becomes an explosion (§11).
  • Selectivity — lose the smallest possible zone, which requires deliberately slowing down upstream devices so downstream ones win the race.

Speed and selectivity pull in opposite directions, and the rest of this section is essentially the engineering of that compromise: how much time to give each layer, where to take the time back with unit schemes like the 87T transformer differential that don't need to wait, and how to prove on paper — before the fault — that the race will be won by the right runner.

Protection has one purpose with two halves: clear every fault (dependability) and clear it with the nearest device (selectivity). A scheme that trips too much is almost as costly as one that trips too little — and both failures are invisible until the day of the fault, which is why grading is calculated and tested, never assumed.

A Failure Story: One Motor Fault, Whole Plant Black

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional but assembled from real coordination failures reported across the industry. Every number is consistent with the Rev 3 plant basis. Notice that nothing in this story is broken in the usual sense — every relay measures correctly, every breaker operates perfectly. The only defect is in the settings.

The setup. Two years ago, during commissioning, the MV relay settings were left at values copied from a "similar project". The MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T] got a sensitive pickup and a low time multiplier — fast, on the theory that fast is safe. The LV incomer ACB trip unit was left at factory defaults, with a generous short-time delay "to avoid nuisance trips". Nobody ran a coordination study; the commissioning schedule was tight, the plant started, and for two years every relay sat silently confirming nothing — because there was no fault to reveal the error.

The fault. On a Wednesday morning a 90 kW process-water pump motor fed from MCC-1 fails: a winding-to-winding short evolves within a cycle into a three-phase fault at the motor terminals. Through the motor cable, the fault pulls roughly 20 kA at the MCC — well above every pickup in the chain. Now four devices see the same event simultaneously and start their private races:

DeviceWhat it seesWhat its (bad) settings tell it
Motor feeder MCCB (MCC-1) ≈ 20 kA — far above its instantaneous pickup Trip now. It begins to operate; contacts will be parting within ≈ 20 ms, fully clear in ≈ 50–70 ms.
LV incomer ACB (LV-MSB) ≈ 20 kA flowing through it toward the MCC Short-time delay 0.4 s — it starts timing, comfortably slow. (This one, ironically, is graded fine.)
MV feeder relay [52-T] 20 kA at 400 V referred to 13.8 kV ≈ 0.58 kA on the MV side — about 8.7 × transformer FLC Pickup 80 A, time multiplier 0.05: its standard-inverse curve says operate in ≈ 0.15 s.
MV incomer relay [52-I] The same 0.58 kA Copied settings, slightly slower: ≈ 0.4 s. Still timing.

The race. The motor MCCB needs about 60 ms in total. The MV relay [52-T] needs about 150 ms. On paper the MCCB wins — but watch what actually happens. The MCCB unlatches at 20 ms and begins stretching the arc. During those same milliseconds the MV relay's induction-disc emulation has been integrating; numerical relays do not reset instantly when current disappears, and this one has overshoot: having reached 90 % of its travel, it coasts the rest of the way even as the current collapses. At t ≈ 160 ms, with the motor fault already dead and buried, relay [52-T] closes its trip contact. The MV breaker for TX-1 opens flawlessly 60 ms later.

The result. Half the LV board goes dark. The bus-tie BT is open (as designed), so LV-MSB section A is dead: half the MCCs, half the process. The DG starts for the essential bus, the UPS carries the control system — and the operators, seeing half the plant down and an MV breaker open, reasonably conclude there has been a transformer fault. They spend four hours testing a perfectly healthy TX-1 (§3) before anyone thinks to check the trip flags on a motor feeder in MCC-1. Total production loss: one shift. Actual faulted equipment: one 90 kW motor worth perhaps an hour of changeover work.

The post-mortem. Three lessons, all general:

  • Both relays saw the fault; the upstream one was faster. This is the textbook definition of a grading failure. Selectivity is not a property a relay has — it is a relationship between relays, and it only exists if someone calculated it. Each relay here did exactly what its settings demanded.
  • Overshoot ate the apparent margin. Even though the MCCB technically cleared first, 150 ms minus 60 ms was nowhere near enough separation. The grading margin (0.3–0.4 s, derived in the next sub-section) exists precisely to absorb breaker clearing time, relay overshoot and measurement errors. "Faster on paper" is not selectivity.
  • Mis-grading multiplies every fault. The plant did not lose a pump; it lost half a switchboard, plus four hours of misdirected troubleshooting, because the trip pattern lied about where the fault was. A correct grading scheme is also a diagnostic tool: when only the motor MCCB trips, the fault location is announced by the trip itself.
The most dangerous property of a mis-graded plant is that it works perfectly until the first fault. No alarm, no symptom, no degraded performance — settings errors are invisible in normal operation. The only ways to find them are (a) a coordination study checked against as-found settings, or (b) the hard way, as above. Note also the inverse failure: if [52-T] had been set too slow and the motor MCCB had failed to clear, the fault would have burned for seconds. Grading errors in either direction are findings.

Milliseconds and Megajoules

Feel for the numbers

Protection lives on two scales humans have no intuition for: time measured in milliseconds and energy measured in megajoules. Before setting a single relay, calibrate your gut on both.

The time scale. The plant's heartbeat is the 60 Hz cycle: one cycle = 1/60 s = 16.7 ms — about half the duration of a camera flash, or one-twentieth of an eye blink (≈ 300 ms). Everything in protection is conveniently counted in these cycles:

EventTypical durationIn 60 Hz cyclesEveryday anchor

Circuit breaker opens and clears≈ 50–80 ms3–5 Springs release, contacts part, arc extinguished at a current zero (§14)
Fastest realistic fault clearing (relay + breaker) ≈ 80–100 ms5–6 One-third of an eye blink — and the fault still lived through 5 peaks of current
One grading margin0.3–0.4 s18–24 A long syllable of speech
Backup clearing two levels up≈ 0.7–1.0 s40–60 A full spoken word — an eternity; busbars are being damaged

Where the 0.3–0.4 s grading margin comes from. The margin between two graded time-overcurrent devices is not a tradition — it is a budget, and every line item is a physical delay or error that must be paid for:

Component of marginIndicative allowanceWhy
Downstream breaker total clearing time0.05–0.08 s The upstream relay keeps seeing fault current until the downstream breaker has actually interrupted — not merely been told to.
Upstream relay overshoot0.03–0.05 s Timing elements coast briefly after current vanishes (disc inertia in electromechanical relays; algorithm reset time in numerical IEDs).
Relay timing errors (both relays)≈ 0.05–0.10 s IEC 60255 allows class errors on operating time; CT errors shift the measured current and hence the point on the curve.
Safety margin≈ 0.10 s For everything unmodelled: setting tolerances, drift, temperature.
Total≈ 0.25–0.33 s → use 0.3–0.4 s 0.3 s is acceptable between numerical IEDs; use 0.4 s where an electromechanical relay is upstream (more overshoot, wider errors).

The energy scale. Why fight for every tenth of a second? Because fault damage is governed by I²t — energy delivered grows linearly with time at a given fault current, and the current is enormous. Two honest comparisons on the Rev 3 numbers:

Feel-for-it calculation — what one extra second of arcing costs

(a) Arc energy at the LV-MSB. A bus fault arcs at roughly Iarc ≈ 0.58 × 38.5 ≈ 22 kA (§11), with a total arc-voltage drop of very roughly 300 V (the round number used in §11):

P_arc ≈ V_arc × I_arc = 300 V × 22 000 A ≈ 6.6 MW Cleared in 0.1 s: E = 6.6 MW × 0.1 s ≈ 0.66 MJ Cleared in 1.0 s: E = 6.6 MW × 1.0 s ≈ 6.6 MJ

0.66 MJ is a 2 kW kettle running for about five minutes — released inside a cubicle in a tenth of a second. It wrecks the compartment. 6.6 MJ is roughly the energy of 1.4 kg of TNT (≈ 4.6 MJ/kg), or a 1.5-tonne car striking a wall at about 340 km/h. It does not wreck a compartment; it removes the switchboard from the plant's asset register and endangers everyone in the room. Same fault, same current — the only difference is the clearing time your settings chose.

(b) Cable withstand (adiabatic I²t). A 300 mm² XLPE copper cable can absorb, adiabatically, k²S² with k ≈ 143:

I²t_max = (k × S)² = (143 × 300)² = (42 900)² ≈ 1.84 × 10&sup9; A²s At 38.5 kA: t_max = 1.84×10&sup9; / (38 500)² = 1.84×10&sup9; / 1.48×10&sup9; ≈ 1.24 s

Clearing in 0.1 s uses about 8 % of the cable's thermal life-budget for that event; clearing in 1.0 s uses about 80 % — one breaker hesitation away from cooked insulation along the entire route. Sanity check: this is the same k²S² check used for earth conductors in §12, and the 1.24 s figure is comfortably above our slowest intended backup clearing (≈ 0.7 s, next sub-section) — the coordination plan and the cable thermal limit are consistent.

Hold these two scales together and the whole philosophy of grading falls out: the first device gets to be fast (50–100 ms) because selectivity costs it nothing. Every backup layer pays one grading margin (≈ 0.3 s) for the privilege of being selective, and the energy table above is the price list that tells you why you cannot afford many layers — by the third, you are at 0.7–1.0 s and flirting with the cable's thermal limit and intolerable arc energy. This is exactly why unit protections (87T differential) and zone-selective interlocking exist: they buy back speed without giving up selectivity, by knowing where the fault is instead of waiting to infer it.

One 60 Hz cycle is 16.7 ms; relay plus breaker can clear in ≈ 5 cycles; each layer of time-graded backup adds ≈ 0.3–0.4 s; and fault energy is proportional to clearing time. Grading is therefore the art of spending milliseconds like money — and the budget at the LV bus runs out at roughly one second.

Worked IDMT Grading Study — MCC Feeder to MV Relay

Worked calculation

We now grade three levels of the Rev 3 plant for phase faults, bottom-up, exactly as a protection engineer would. The chain (bus-tie BT open, one transformer per bus section):

Level 1: LV-MSB outgoing MCCB feeding MCC-1 (indicative 400 A frame, electronic trip) → Level 2: LV-MSB incomer ACB from TX-1 (electronic trip unit, IEC standard-inverse mode) → Level 3: MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T] in MV-SWGR (numerical IED, ANSI 51, CT 100/1).

The timing engine for Levels 2 and 3 is the IEC 60255 standard-inverse (SI) IDMT curve — "inverse definite minimum time": the bigger the current, the faster the trip, scaled by a time-multiplier setting (TMS):

t = TMS × 0.14 / ((I/Is)^0.02 − 1)   [s]   where Is = pickup current, I = fault current

Two settings per device, then: the pickup Is (where the curve starts — chosen from load current) and the TMS (which slides the whole curve up or down in time — chosen to create the grading margins).

The common reference fault for grading Levels 2→3 is a three-phase fault on the LV-MSB busbar: 38.5 kA at 400 V. The MV relay sees this same fault through the transformer, scaled by the voltage ratio:

I_MV = I_LV × (V_LV / V_MV) = 38 500 × (400 / 13 800) = 38 500 × 0.029 = 1 116 A ≈ 1.12 kA

Sanity check: a fault limited only by the transformer's 6 % impedance draws 1/0.06 = 16.7 times FLC: 16.7 × 66.9 A = 1 115 A on the MV side. Same answer by two independent routes — the referral is right. Note how small 1.12 kA is at MV: barely 17 × transformer full load, and only 4.5 % of the 25 kA the MV bus can deliver. The transformer impedance hides LV faults from the MV relay — this single fact shapes the whole MV setting.

Step 1 — Level 1: MCC-1 feeder MCCB (the fast end of the chain)

The device nearest the fault zone needs no upward grading below it (the MCC's own starters grade beneath it, but they are current-limited and fast). Indicative settings for a 400 A electronic-trip MCCB:

Long-time pickup: Ir = 400 A (matches MCC-1 group demand, cable rating governs) Short-time pickup: Isd = 8 × Ir = 3 200 A, delay 0.05 s Total clearing at 20 kA fault: 0.05 s delay + ≈ 0.02 s interruption ≈ 0.07 s

Sanity check: the largest motor in MCC-1 (≈ 90 kW, FLC ≈ 160 A) starts DOL at ≈ 6 × FLC ≈ 960 A — well below the 3 200 A short-time pickup, so motor starting cannot trip the feeder. A fault at the MCC bus (≈ 20 kA) is 6 times the short-time pickup — detection is certain. Both ends verified: rides through the biggest legitimate current, sees the smallest fault.

Step 2 — Level 2: LV incomer ACB, pickup then TMS

(a) Choose the pickup Is. The incomer must carry one transformer's full output continuously (2 309 A) without nuisance tripping, with a little headroom for measurement error and brief excursions, while staying far below the smallest fault it must detect:

Is = 1.1 × FLC = 1.1 × 2 309 A = 2 540 A

Sanity check (sensitivity): the minimum realistic bus fault — a phase-to-phase fault — is ≈ 0.87 × 38.5 = 33.5 kA, which is 13 times the pickup. Detection is never in doubt. Sanity check (stability): 2 540 A > 2 309 A maximum continuous current — no trip at full load, even at 50 °C with the trip unit reading 5 % high. Note that this is an alternative, IDMT-based illustration: the manual's reference LSIG setting study for the same incomer (the breakers chapter) sets Ir = 2 375 A — the pickups differ because the schemes differ, not because one is wrong.

(b) Compute the curve time at the reference fault, TMS = 1. At the LV bus fault, I = 38.5 kA through the incomer:

I/Is = 38 500 / 2 540 = 15.2 (I/Is)^0.02 = 15.2^0.02 = 1.056  →  denominator = 1.056 − 1 = 0.056 t(TMS=1) = 0.14 / 0.056 = 2.50 s

(c) Choose TMS for a 0.3 s margin over Level 1. The feeder MCCB below is fully clear in ≈ 0.07 s, so the incomer must not operate before 0.07 + 0.30 = 0.37 s at any current both devices can see:

TMS_required = t_target / t(TMS=1) = 0.37 / 2.50 = 0.148  →  set TMS = 0.15 Operating time: t = 0.15 × 2.50 = 0.38 s at 38.5 kA

Sanity check: we graded at 38.5 kA — the worst case, because the inverse curve is fastest at the highest current. For the more realistic fault at the MCC bus (20 kA), I/Is = 7.9, t(TMS=1) = 0.14/(7.9^0.02 − 1) = 0.14/0.042 = 3.32 s, so t = 0.15 × 3.32 = 0.50 s — margin over the feeder grows to 0.43 s. If the margin holds at maximum current, it holds everywhere below. Also check load-side stability: at twice pickup (a brief reacceleration surge, 5 080 A), t = 0.15 × 0.14/(2^0.02 − 1) = 0.15 × 10.0 = 1.5 s — long enough to ride through transient overloads.

Step 3 — Level 3: MV transformer-feeder relay [52-T], pickup then TMS

(a) Choose the pickup Is. Transformer MV full-load current is 66.9 A. Allow 20 % margin for sustained full output plus measurement error, and express it on the 100/1 CT:

Is = 1.2 × FLC = 1.2 × 66.9 A = 80.3 A  →  set 80 A primary = 0.80 × In on the 100/1 CT

Sanity check (sensitivity): the smallest fault this relay must back up is the LV bus fault seen through the transformer: 1 120 A = 14 times pickup — comfortably detected (a common rule asks for at least 1.3 × pickup at the minimum fault; we have ten times that). Sanity check (stability): transformer energisation inrush peaks around 8–12 × FLC (≈ 540–800 A) but decays within a few cycles; on the SI curve at 669 A (I/Is = 8.4) the relay would need 0.28 × 0.14/(8.4^0.02−1) ≈ 0.9 s of sustained current to trip — inrush is gone long before that. The IDMT curve itself rides through inrush.

(b) Compute the curve time at the reference fault, TMS = 1. The common fault is the LV bus fault = 1.12 kA seen at MV:

I/Is = 1 120 / 80 = 14.0 (I/Is)^0.02 = 14.0^0.02 = 1.054  →  denominator = 1.054 − 1 = 0.054 t(TMS=1) = 0.14 / 0.054 = 2.58 s

(c) Choose TMS for a 0.3 s margin over Level 2. The LV incomer operates in 0.38 s at this same fault, so [52-T] must wait until at least 0.38 + 0.30 = 0.68 s:

TMS_required = 0.68 / 2.58 = 0.264  →  set TMS = 0.28 (next available step, with headroom) Operating time: t = 0.28 × 2.58 = 0.72 s at 1.12 kA  →  margin = 0.72 − 0.38 = 0.34 s ≥ 0.3 s ✓

Sanity check (equipment withstand): if the LV incomer ever fails and [52-T] does the clearing, the fault persists 0.72 s + MV breaker ≈ 0.06 s ≈ 0.78 s. The transformer's through-fault withstand (IEC 60076-5) is 2 s at full through-fault current — OK. The LV cable I²t limit computed earlier was 1.24 s at 38.5 kA — OK, with margin. The grading plan fits inside every thermal limit it relies on.

(d) Add the high-set instantaneous (ANSI 50) — speed where selectivity is free. Here is the elegant trick: the MV relay can never see more than 1.12 kA through the transformer, because the 6 % impedance caps it. Any current above that must be a fault on the MV side — the cable or the transformer primary itself, squarely in [52-T]'s own zone. So an instantaneous element set above the maximum through-fault needs no grading at all:

I>> = 1.3 × 1 120 A ≈ 1 460 A  →  set 1 500 A primary (15 × In CT), t = 0.00 s (instantaneous)

An MV cable fault (≈ 25 kA, far above 1.5 kA) now clears in relay ≈ 0.03 s + breaker ≈ 0.06 s ≈ 0.1 s instead of riding the 51 curve. Sanity check (inrush): 1 500 A is roughly twice the worst inrush (≈ 800 A), and a numerical IED additionally filters the DC offset and harmonics of inrush — the 50 element will not false-trip on energisation. The 1.3 factor covers asymmetry and CT transient error at the through-fault boundary.

What sits above Level 3. The MV incomer relay [52-I] backs up [52-T] and must grade 0.3 s above the slowest [52-T] response it can witness — indicatively ≈ 1.0 s at the through-fault level — and above that sits SEC's own network protection. This is where the time budget meets its ceiling: the Saudi Grid Code and the SEC Distribution Code set expectations for fault clearance at the intake, and SEC reviews and approves the consumer's incomer settings at connection. If the plant's ladder is too slow at the top, the cure is not a faster top relay — it is removing a layer from the ladder, or replacing time grading with unit schemes (87T, bus differential, zone-selective interlocking) that clear in ≈ 0.1 s without any margin at all. For the arc-flash consequences of these choices see §11.

The whole grading study is two decisions per device, made bottom-up: pickup from load (above the maximum legitimate current, far below the minimum fault) and TMS from the margin (0.3 s above the device below, at the maximum fault both can see). Then verify the margins hold across the current range, and verify the slowest backup clearing still fits inside every cable and transformer withstand limit. Indicative here; the plant's formal TCC study governs.

Definite-time or IDMT? Every timed element offers both shapes, and the choice is not cosmetic:

  • Definite time (DT) — trips a fixed delay after pickup, regardless of how large the current is. Predictable, easy to grade by simple ladders (0.1 / 0.4 / 0.7 s), ideal where fault current barely varies with location — which is exactly the case for NER-limited earth faults, where every fault draws roughly the same current wherever it is.
  • IDMT — faster at higher current, following the IEC 60255 curve families (standard inverse as used above; very inverse 13.5/(M − 1) and extremely inverse 80/(M² − 1) where heavier discrimination near the source or fuse coordination is needed). Ideal for phase faults, where current falls with distance from the source: nearby (high-current) faults clear fast, remote faults — which the downstream device should be clearing anyway — get more time automatically.

Earth-fault settings and the NER. The 13.8 kV system neutral is earthed through the NER — 7.97 kV, 400 A for 10 s (19.9 Ω) — so no MV earth fault anywhere in the plant can exceed ≈ 400 A (§12). That changes the settings logic completely:

  • Pickup: phase-CT residual connections suffer from spill current (CT mismatch during through-faults and inrush), so earth-fault pickups sit at 10–20 % of the NER limit: 40–80 A primary. On the 100/1 feeder CTs that is 0.4–0.8 × In residually, or lower with a core-balance CT, which measures the true sum flux and allows pickups down to a few amps.
  • Curve: definite time, because the NER makes every earth fault look the same size — IDMT would gain nothing. Indicative ladder: feeder 51N ≈ 0.3 s, incomer 51N ≈ 0.6 s.
  • Standby earth fault (51G): a CT in the NER neutral connection itself sees every earth fault on the system — the last resort. Set ≈ 0.9–1.0 s (above the incomer 51N), and verify the whole ladder clears well inside the NER's 10 s thermal rating. Sanity check: slowest clearing ≈ 1.0 s + breaker ≈ 1.1 s ≪ 10 s ✓ — the resistor survives even a full backup sequence with a roughly ninefold margin.

The 87T question at 1600 kVA. Transformer differential compares current entering the MV side with current leaving the LV side, scaled and vector-corrected for the Dyn11 group; any imbalance above the bias characteristic means an in-zone fault, tripped instantaneously on both sides with no grading margin needed — unit protection at its purest. The honest engineering position for this plant:

  • When it is justified: 87T is standard above ≈ 5 MVA. At 1600 kVA it is a judgement call — justified where the transformer is critical to production (N-1 only works if the failed unit is repairable), where MV cable runs are included in the zone, or where the arc-flash study wants faster TX-zone clearing than the 51 ladder can give. Otherwise the 50/51 + 49 package, plus the transformer's own devices (Buchholz/pressure for oil units, winding temperature for cast resin — §3), is a defensible scheme at this size.
  • Stabilisation against inrush: energising the transformer draws inrush on the MV side with nothing leaving the LV side — to a naive differential that is a perfect internal fault. The classic cure: inrush current is rich in second harmonic (typically 15–40 %), which genuine fault current is not, so the relay restrains when the 2nd-harmonic content exceeds ≈ 15–20 % of fundamental. Modern IEDs add waveform-gap recognition and CT-saturation discriminators on top.
  • The supporting cast: a percentage-bias slope (to tolerate CT errors and off-nominal tap positions), an unrestrained high-set differential for massive internal faults, and matched CT performance on both sides (next sub-section). An 87T operation always drives the 86 lockout.

The SEC interface. At the intake, settings stop being a private matter. The SEC Distribution Code and Saudi Grid Code require the consumer's protection to clear plant faults within defined times so they never stress the utility network, and SEC reviews the incomer relay settings (50/51, 50N/51N, 27/59, 81U/O) at connection approval and after changes. Practically: the [52-I] settings and the grading study behind them are submittal documents, the revenue-metering CTs/VTs at MV-MET are sealed SEC property, and any re-grading that slows the top of the ladder needs SEC concurrence. Treat the utility boundary as a fixed ceiling the whole ladder must fit under.

Reading a setting file cold? Translate it first: every line is one device number + pickup + curve + time setting. Rebuild the ladder on paper (pickup and operating time at the common fault, level by level, exactly as in the worked study) before judging any single value. A pickup is never right or wrong alone — only relative to the load below it and the relay above it.

From Spinning Discs to IEDs — and the CTs That Feed Them

Equipment technology

Three generations of "decide". The relay's job has never changed; the physics doing the deciding has been revolutionised twice:

  • Electromechanical (1900s–1980s). The IDMT curve was originally a physical object: an aluminium induction disc, spun by the torque of fault current through a coil, against a spring, toward a trip contact. More current, more torque, faster travel — the inverse curve is literally the mechanics of the disc, and the constants of the IEC standard-inverse formula were chosen to fit it. The time-multiplier dial physically moved the contact closer. Rugged and trusted, but: one function per case, accuracy drifting with friction and age, overshoot from disc inertia (hence the fatter 0.4 s margins of that era), and no memory of what happened. Plenty are still in service worldwide; treat their settings and test records with respect and suspicion in equal measure.
  • Static / solid-state (1970s–1990s). The disc replaced by analogue electronics — comparators, RC timing circuits. Faster, no moving parts, tighter tolerances, but still one box per function and famously sensitive to heat — a real concern at 50 °C switchroom design ambient.
  • Numerical IEDs (1990s–today). The current generation, and what MV-PROT specifies: a processor samples the CT/VT waveforms (typically 16–32 samples per cycle), filters them digitally, and runs every protection function of this section — 50/51, 50N/51N, 87T, 27/59, 81, 49, 50BF — as software in one box, an Intelligent Electronic Device. With them come the game-changers: settings in files (versionable, checkable against the study), event records and oscillography (the fault's flight recorder), self-supervision (a failed relay announces itself instead of being discovered at the next fault), multiple setting groups (one command to switch the grading when BT closes and fault levels double), and communications (Control, below). The relay families in use are catalogued in Part 6.

The nerve endings: CTs and VTs (SASO-adopted IEC 61869). The finest IED is blind without honest instrument transformers, and they are specified by four numbers every field engineer should be able to parse. Take the [52-T] feeder CT, 100/1 A, 5P20, 5 VA:

  • Ratio 100/1: 100 A primary becomes 1 A secondary. Chosen just above the circuit's load (66.9 A FLC → 67 % of ratio — healthy; a CT loafing at 10 % of ratio measures poorly, one at 120 % overheats).
  • Class 5P20: a protection class CT — 5 % maximum composite error, guaranteed up to 20 times rated current (the accuracy limit factor, ALF), at rated burden. Protection CTs are deliberately built to stay roughly linear far beyond rating; metering CTs (class 0.5, as in MV-MET) do the opposite — they saturate early to protect the meters. Never interchange them.
  • Burden 5 VA: the load (relay + lead resistance) the CT can drive at rated current while honouring its class.
  • Knee point (for class PX / differential duty): the point on the magnetising curve where the core gives up — 10 % more voltage buys 50 % more magnetising current. Differential schemes specify CTs by knee-point voltage (Vk ≥ K × If,sec × (Rct + 2Rlead + Rrelay)) so both sides of the 87T saturate equally — or not at all — during through-faults.

Worked mini-calculation — is the 100/1, 5P20, 5 VA CT good enough?

Step 1 — actual burden. A numerical IED is a feather-light load (≈ 0.1 VA). The leads dominate: say 30 m of 2.5 mm² copper each way (ρ ≈ 0.0175 Ω·mm²/m):

R_leads = 0.0175 × (2 × 30) / 2.5 = 0.42 Ω → at 1 A rated: 0.42 VA Actual burden ≈ 0.42 + 0.1 ≈ 0.5 VA (vs 5 VA rated)

Step 2 — effective accuracy limit. A CT running below rated burden stays linear to a higher multiple than its nameplate ALF. With CT internal resistance Rct ≈ 0.4 Ω (= 0.4 VA at 1 A):

ALF_eff ≈ ALF × (S_rated + S_internal) / (S_actual + S_internal) = 20 × (5 + 0.4) / (0.5 + 0.4) = 20 × 6.0 = 120 CT stays accurate up to ≈ 120 × 100 A = 12 kA primary

Step 3 — compare with duty. The 51 element's maximum measured current is the through-fault, 1.12 kA = 11.2 × In — the CT is linear with a factor-of-ten margin ✓. But an MV-side fault reaches ≈ 25 kA = 250 × In, double the effective limit — the CT saturates for the very faults the 50 element exists to clear.

Result and sanity check: the CT is comfortably adequate for the graded 51/51N duty (the duty that actually needs timing accuracy), and the saturation at 25 kA is acceptable only because the 50 element is set at 15 × In: the CT reproduces the rising edge faithfully for the first few milliseconds before saturating, and a numerical 50 element latches its decision in that window. This is the standard outcome for feeder CTs on a stiff bus — adequate by calculated margin, not by nameplate alone — and exactly the check the formal study must document.

Why CT saturation matters so much for instantaneous elements. When the core saturates, the secondary current collapses into narrow spikes — the relay may see a fraction of the true RMS value. Three practical consequences. First, an instantaneous element set high (for grading reasons) might be slowed or, with an old electromechanical relay, fail to operate at all on a heavily saturated waveform — modern IEDs use peak detection and saturation detectors to ride through. Second, the DC offset of an asymmetrical fault (§4) drives the core toward saturation far faster than the symmetric value suggests — transient performance, not just steady-state ALF, decides. Third, unequal saturation between the two CT sets of an 87T creates false differential current during external through-faults — the precise reason differential CTs are specified by knee point and the relay carries a bias slope. A protection study that never checked its CTs has only studied half the chain.

VTs in brief. Voltage transformers (or low-power capacitive dividers in modern switchgear) feed the 27/59/81 elements and any directional functions: 13 800/√3 : 110/√3 V typical, protection class 3P, with fuses or MCBs on the secondary whose failure must alarm — a dead VT looks like a dead bus to a 27 element, and can start an unwanted DG transfer sequence.

The relay can only be as honest as its CT. Check the chain in this order: ratio vs load, class vs function (P-class for protection; metering class never), actual burden vs rated, effective ALF vs maximum fault — and knee-point matching for any differential. A twenty-minute CT calculation prevents the ugliest protection failures: the ones where the relay was right and its eyes were lying.

The Trip Circuit, the DC Rail, and the Control Room

Control

A protection decision is worthless until it physically reaches a trip coil, and the path it travels is a system in its own right — with its own failure modes and its own supervision.

The 110 V DC backbone. Every trip in MV-SWGR runs on the 110 V DC system from the DCDB battery and charger — never on AC. The logic is brutal: the moment protection is needed most is a bolted fault, which is precisely the moment the AC voltage collapses. The battery makes the trip supply independent of the fault. Consequences worth internalising: the DCDB is arguably the most safety-critical auxiliary in the plant (a dead battery silently disarms every MV protection at once); chargers, battery autonomy and DC earth-fault monitoring deserve the same inspection rigour as the relays themselves; and the IEDs, trip coils, closing coils and 86 lockouts draw from segregated, fused DC circuits so one wiring fault cannot disarm a whole board.

Trip-circuit supervision (TCS). The trip wire from relay contact to breaker coil can be broken — a loose terminal, a corroded breaker auxiliary switch, a blown DC fuse — and nothing visible changes until the relay one day trips into an open circuit. TCS fixes the invisibility: a few milliamps are passed continuously through the trip-coil path (in both breaker positions, in the two-resistor scheme), and any interruption raises an alarm today instead of a failure-to-trip on fault day. On a plant audit, the "trip circuit healthy" indications and their alarm routing are a two-minute check with outsized value.

Breaker failure (50BF) — backup without the time penalty. Classic time grading backs up a stuck breaker by waiting a full grading margin at the next level up. The modern alternative is explicit: when a relay issues a trip, a 50BF timer starts; if current is still flowing after ≈ 150–200 ms (the trip should have cleared it in ≈ 80 ms), the scheme declares the breaker failed and direct-trips the adjacent breakers — for [52-T] that means tripping [52-I] — without riding any IDMT curve. It is faster than the graded backup, and it distinguishes "breaker stuck" from "relay graded slow", which the time ladder alone cannot.

The IED as a station citizen. Numerical relays are also the substation's data layer:

  • SCADA / PMS integration: breaker states, measurements, alarms and trip flags stream to the plant power-management system over IEC 61850 (station bus; GOOSE peer-to-peer messages can replace hardwired interlocking and 50BF wiring) or, in simpler installations, Modbus to the DCS. Remote open/close commands pass the other way, under interlock.
  • Event records and oscillography: every pickup, trip and reset is time-stamped to the millisecond; disturbance records capture the actual current and voltage waveforms through the fault. After any trip, the first investigative act is pulling these records — they distinguish a genuine fault from a mis-grading in minutes (recall the four lost hours in the failure story). Time synchronisation (SNTP / IRIG-B) across IEDs is what makes multi-relay records line up into one story.
  • Setting management: the as-running configuration file is the legal twin of the coordination study. Version it, and diff it after every vendor visit.

Testing — proving the reflex still works. Protection is the plant's least-exercised system: it may wait years between operations, and every part of it is a dormant failure in waiting. The discipline is secondary injection: a test set injects synthetic currents and voltages into the relay's terminals (CT circuits isolated via the test block), and the tester verifies pickup values, curve timing at several multiples — does the SI curve at TMS 0.28 really give 0.72 s at 14 × pickup? — and, crucially, the whole chain through to the breaker mechanism actually tripping. Primary injection (real current through the CT primary) confirms ratios and wiring at commissioning. Indicative intervals: MV numerical relays every 2–3 years (self-supervision watches the electronics; the test watches what self-supervision cannot — settings, wiring, the breaker); LV trip units ≈ every 5 years; always after any setting change.

A protection scheme has a silent dependency chain: battery → DC distribution → trip wiring → trip coil → mechanism — and the relay supervises only part of it. The three cheap defences are TCS (watches the wiring), battery supervision (watches the energy), and periodic injection testing with a real breaker trip (watches everything at once). A plant that skips all three has protection on paper only.

Running a Protection Study, Step by Step

Sizing method

"Sizing" protection means producing — or auditing — a coordination study. The procedure below is the standard sequence; the worked study earlier in this section is steps 5–7 executed on one slice of the plant.

  1. Assemble the data. Single-line diagram with every protective device; equipment ratings and impedances (transformers, cables, motors); CT/VT ratios and classes; existing settings; SEC intake data — fault level (25 kA design; state the SEC 25–40 kA range), upstream protection settings and required clearance times at the boundary.
  2. Run the fault study. Maximum and minimum short-circuit currents at every bus per §4 (IEC 60909): maximum (e.g. 77 kA with BT closed) tests withstand and sets the tight grading point; minimum (one TX, phase-to-phase, remote faults) tests that every pickup is still reached. Earth-fault levels per the NER.
  3. Select the schemes per equipment class. Which device numbers protect what — the scheme table below is the template. Decide here whether unit protections (87T, bus differential, ZSI) are justified, because they remove layers from the time ladder.
  4. Size the measuring chain. CT ratios from load, classes from function, burden/ALF checks as in the mini-calculation, knee-point specifications for differential sets; VT ratios and classes. Do this before the settings — settings promised on saturating CTs are fiction.
  5. Calculate settings bottom-up. For each level: pickup from maximum load (with a minimum-fault sensitivity check), curve type, then TMS/delay for the margin over the level below at the maximum common fault — exactly the worked study's Steps 1–3. Add instantaneous elements wherever an impedance (a transformer) guarantees zone separation for free.
  6. Verify by TCC overlay. Plot every device of each chain on one log-log time-current diagram, LV and MV referred to a common voltage base. Check: margins ≥ 0.3 s at every shared current; no curve crossings; every damage curve (cable I²t, transformer through-fault per IEC 60076-5, motor stall) sits above its protecting device's curve; every inrush and starting point sits below. Repeat for the abnormal configurations: bus-tie closed (77 kA — a second setting group?), DG island (fault level a small fraction of SEC's — do the pickups still see faults?).
  7. Implement and prove on site. Load the setting files; secondary-injection test against the study values (not against the previous settings); primary-inject the CT circuits at commissioning; trip the real breakers; file the test records with the study revision. Submit the intake settings to SEC where required.

Re-study triggers. A coordination study is a snapshot of one network configuration. Re-verify whenever: SEC advises a changed fault level or changed upstream settings; a transformer or generator is added or replaced (fault levels move); the bus-tie operating philosophy changes; a major feeder, MCC or large motor/VFD is added; any protective device is replaced by a different model; or an unexplained or unselective trip occurs. And on a fixed clock — every 5 years is a common contractual and insurance expectation — because undocumented plant drift is itself a trigger that nobody reports.

Assessment: What to Verify, and Self-Check

On a site assessment, protection coordination is mostly a documents and evidence exercise — the hardware tells you almost nothing by inspection:

  • Request the protection coordination study and its TCC overlays. If none exists, or it predates the last network change, raise as a high-priority finding.
  • Export the as-running IED setting files and diff them against the study values — every mismatch is a finding (recall: both relays in the failure story were "healthy").
  • Verify pickup logic on a sample: LV incomer Is above the 2 309 A FLC; MV [52-T] pickup above 66.9 A FLC and far below the 1.12 kA through-fault; margins ≥ 0.3 s at the maximum common fault.
  • Check earth-fault settings against the NER (400 A / 10 s): pickups in the 40–80 A range, ladder clearing well inside 10 s, standby earth-fault (51G) present on the neutral CT.
  • Confirm CT ratios, classes and burdens match the study (nameplates vs setting files), and that any 87T has its knee-point / CT-matching calculation on record.
  • Confirm the 87T (if fitted) is in service, 2nd-harmonic restraint enabled, and its 86 lockout functional, with resets logged.
  • Sight the secondary-injection test records: MV relays within 2–3 years, LV trip units within 5 years, and a retest after every setting change.
  • Verify DCDB health (battery test records, charger alarms, DC earth-fault monitoring) and that trip-circuit supervision is fitted and its alarms reach a manned location.
  • Confirm the bus-tie interlock / setting-group arrangement covers the 77 kA tie-closed case, and that DG-island sensitivity was checked.
  • Confirm the SEC intake settings on record match the SEC-approved submittal.

Self-check questions — everything needed is in this section and §4:

  • State the three jobs of a protection system and which physical component performs each. Why must the "decide" step be deliberately slowed at upstream levels?
  • A grading margin of 0.3 s is specified between two numerical relays. List the four budget items it pays for, with rough magnitudes.
  • Recompute the LV incomer operating time at a 10 kA fault (Is = 2 540 A, TMS = 0.15, SI curve) and confirm it still grades over a feeder clearing in 0.07 s. (Expected: I/Is ≈ 3.9, t ≈ 0.76 s — the margin grows as current falls.)
  • Why can [52-T]'s instantaneous element be set with no grading margin at all? What plant parameter creates that freedom, and what is the numeric boundary here?
  • Why are the MV earth-fault elements graded with definite time rather than IDMT in this plant? Which single component makes every earth fault "the same size"?
  • The 87T must not trip on transformer energisation. Name the phenomenon, its electrical signature, and the relay feature that exploits it.
  • Your 100/1, 5P20, 5 VA CT now feeds 80 m of 1.5 mm² leads instead of 30 m of 2.5 mm². Rework the burden and effective ALF — is the 50 element's decision window still defensible?
  • The plant closes BT permanently "for reliability". List every line of the grading study that must be revisited — and name the withstand check that comes before any of them.

A coordination study is the only proof that, for a fault between any two devices, the downstream one wins the race — by a margin that survives breaker time, overshoot and measurement error. Without that study, verified against as-found settings and backed by test records, selectivity is a hope, not a property. The next fault will audit the plant whether or not anyone else has.
