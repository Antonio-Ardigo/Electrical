11 — Arc-Flash / Incident Energy (IEEE 1584) & the Clearing-Time Lever Reference

Goal: understand what an arcing fault physically is, estimate the incident thermal energy a worker would receive at the LV-MSB, map that energy to arc-rated PPE and an arc-flash boundary — and demonstrate, with arithmetic you can redo on a phone, why clearing time is the single most powerful lever for reducing the hazard.

Arc-flash is a life-safety calculation. Every number in this section is indicative and exists to teach the method and the physics. Actual PPE selection, equipment labelling, energised-work permits, and arc-mitigation design must be based on a full, certified IEEE 1584-2018 study using site-specific as-built data, performed in recognised power-system software and reviewed by a competent engineer. The hazard nobody can see on the SLD

Why this exists

Most electrical safety training is built around shock: current passing through the body because the body became part of the circuit. Shock is a contact hazard — if you do not touch the conductor (directly, or through a tool), you are safe. Arc flash is fundamentally different. Arc flash is a radiant and blast hazard: it injures people who never touch anything. A worker standing half a metre from a switchboard, hands at their sides, doing everything "right" by shock-protection logic, can receive third-degree burns over their face, chest and arms in a third of a second. The two hazards have different physics, different protective measures, and different calculations — and confusing them is one of the most common and most dangerous gaps in industrial electrical safety.

What actually happens in an arcing fault? Air is normally an excellent insulator. But if a conductive bridge forms between phases or phase and earth — a dropped spanner, a misaligned racking cluster, a sagging busbar support, a forgotten test lead, even a rodent — current begins to flow through that bridge, vaporises it, and strikes an electric arc: a column of plasma, the fourth state of matter, in which the air itself has been torn into free electrons and ions and become a conductor. Once struck, the arc no longer needs the original bridge. It sustains itself as long as the source keeps feeding it current.

The numbers involved are not "industrial" — they are astrophysical:

  • Temperature. The arc core reaches roughly 20 000 °C — about four times the surface temperature of the sun (≈ 5 500 °C). No material on earth withstands this; everything in the arc path melts or vaporises.
  • Copper expansion. When solid copper busbar flashes to vapour it expands roughly 67 000 times in volume — almost instantly. A few cubic centimetres of busbar become several cubic metres of superheated copper vapour inside a closed steel box. That is, functionally, an explosion.
  • Pressure wave. The expanding plasma and metal vapour produce a blast front that blows open panel doors, throws workers across the room, and can rupture eardrums. Pressure on a worker's chest at close range can reach thousands of kilograms-force.
  • Shrapnel and droplets. Molten copper and steel droplets are sprayed outward at high velocity, together with fragments of the enclosure itself.
  • Incident thermal energy. A large fraction of the arc's power is radiated as intense thermal energy onto everything facing it — including the worker. This radiant dose, measured in cal/cm² on the skin, is the quantity this section teaches you to estimate.

And this is why a calculation — not merely a PPE habit — is both physically and legally necessary. Physically: the hazard varies by more than an order of magnitude from one panel to the next, depending on fault current, protective-device clearing time, enclosure geometry and working distance. A glove-and-visor habit that is adequate at a small distribution board can be lethally inadequate two panels away at the main switchboard, fed by the same transformer. You cannot see the difference; only arithmetic reveals it. Legally: NFPA 70E (used in Saudi industrial practice alongside SASO-adopted IEC standards and the SEC/owner safety rules) requires an arc-flash risk assessment with documented incident energy or PPE category for each piece of equipment before energised work, and warning labels on the gear. "We always wear the heavy suit" is not a risk assessment — it is a guess, and on a 50 °C Saudi site it is a guess that creates its own heat-stress hazard (see Sizing method).

Shock protection asks: "could current pass through the body?" Arc-flash protection asks: "how much thermal energy would land on the body, at working distance, before the upstream device clears the fault?" The second question has a numeric answer, in cal/cm², and every energised-work decision hangs on it.

A morning at the LV-MSB

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional but assembled from the common features of real incident reports. Every step of it is ordinary — that is the point.

A technician is asked to return a 400 A feeder air-circuit-breaker to service on the LV-MSB — a 400 V main switchboard fed by a 1600 kVA transformer (indicative bolted fault level ≈ 38.5 kA, see §c-shortcircuit). Production is running; nobody wants to ask for a shutdown to rack in one breaker, and the site has no arc-flash study and no labels, so the job is treated as routine: ordinary coveralls, leather gloves, safety glasses. He opens the cubicle door and begins racking the breaker in on the energised bus — pushing the moving contacts toward bars carrying the full prospective fault current of the transformer.

His torque tool slips and falls across the cluster contacts just as they approach the bus. The metal bridges two phases. What happens next is faster than any human reflex:

  • t = 0 ms. The tool vaporises; an arc strikes between phases. Arcing current is about 22 kA — less than the 38.5 kA bolted value, because the arc itself has impedance (we calculate this properly in the worked calculation). Arc power is several megawatts. The flash is brighter than staring at the sun; his pupils have no time to close.
  • t ≈ 5–10 ms. Copper vapour and plasma blast out of the open cubicle. The pressure wave hits his chest and throws him backwards; the sound — well over 140 dB — injures both eardrums.
  • t = 0 to 300 ms. Here is the quiet villain: the upstream incomer breaker's short-time delay is set at 0.3 s — set high years ago, to make sure feeder breakers always trip first (selectivity, see §c-protection). Its instantaneous element was set above the arcing current. So for 18 cycles at 60 Hz, the arc burns, pumping radiant energy onto the technician from less than half a metre.
  • t = 300 ms. The incomer finally trips. The board is destroyed. The technician is alive — most arc-flash victims are — but his injuries were decided in those 18 cycles.

Now the injury physics. The medical threshold used in arc-flash engineering is the onset of a second-degree (just-curable) burn: 1.2 cal/cm² — about the heat your fingertip receives if you hold it over a cigarette-lighter flame for one second. As the worked calculation below shows, an arc like this one delivers on the order of 20+ cal/cm² at his working distance — roughly twenty times the second-degree threshold. At that dose, exposed skin receives deep partial- and full-thickness burns; worse, his untreated cotton-polyester coverall ignites and continues burning after the arc is gone, often the dominant cause of fatal burn area in real events. Survival probability for burn victims falls steeply with burned body-surface area and age; weeks of intensive care and skin grafts follow even in the "lucky" cases.

Trace the causes backwards and notice that none of them is exotic:

  • No study → nobody knew this panel was a ≈ 20 cal/cm² location; it looked like every other grey box.
  • No label → no trigger for a permit, PPE, or de-energisation decision at the point of work.
  • Settings chosen for selectivity only → 0.3 s of arc burn-through time, because no one had ever asked the protection engineer the other question: "and what does that delay do to incident energy?"
  • No maintenance switch / ZSI / arc relay → no fast mode available even for planned energised work.
The single most dangerous routine task in LV/MV operations is racking breakers on an energised bus. It puts a person at minimum distance, with the panel open, performing a mechanical action that can itself initiate the fault. The hierarchy is: de-energise first; if not feasible, rack remotely or with the door closed; energised manual racking is the last resort and only ever inside the limits of a current arc-flash study and permit.

How big is an arc, really?

Feel for the numbers

Before any standard or empirical model, get an honest physical feel with arithmetic a field engineer can do on a phone. An arc dissipates power like any resistive element: power = voltage across it × current through it. In a 400 V board, the arc voltage (the sum of the anode and cathode drops plus the plasma column) is typically a few hundred volts — take Varc ≈ 300 V as a round indicative figure. The arcing current from the worked example below is Ia ≈ 22 kA.

Back-of-envelope — arc power and energy at the LV-MSB

P_arc = V_arc x I_a = 300 V x 22 000 A = 6 600 000 W = 6.6 MW

Six point six megawatts — more than three times the entire 2.0 MW demand of the plant — dissipated inside one cubicle, in a plasma ball a few centimetres across. Now multiply by the clearing time of 0.3 s:

W = P_arc x t = 6.6 MW x 0.3 s = 1.98 MJ ~ 2.0 MJ

Convert to TNT equivalent (TNT releases ≈ 4.6 MJ/kg):

m_TNT = 2.0 MJ / 4.6 MJ/kg ~ 0.43 kg of TNT

Sanity check on the comparison: the energy is comparable to nearly half a kilogram of TNT, though an arc releases it as heat, light and a slower pressure rise rather than a true detonation — devastating in an enclosure, but not identical blast physics. The comparison is for scale, and the scale is right.

Put 2 MJ next to everyday quantities:

  • Kettles: a 2 kW kettle would take 1 000 seconds — about 17 minutes — to deliver 2 MJ. The arc delivers it in 0.3 s, i.e. at 3 300 kettles' worth of power.
  • Car crash: a 1.5-tonne car at 100 km/h (27.8 m/s) carries kinetic energy ½ × 1500 × 27.8² ≈ 0.58 MJ. The arc event releases the energy of three to four such crashes, a hand-span from the worker's chest.
  • Dynamite: a standard ≈ 200 g stick of dynamite carries roughly 1 MJ — the arc is about two sticks.

One more honest estimate, because it previews the formal result. Suppose (crudely) the 2 MJ radiated uniformly in all directions from a point. At the LV working distance of 455 mm, the energy is spread over a sphere of area:

Back-of-envelope — energy density at working distance

A = 4 x pi x r^2 = 4 x 3.1416 x 0.455^2 = 2.6 m^2 = 26 000 cm^2 E = 2 000 000 J / 26 000 cm^2 ~ 77 J/cm^2 ~ 18 cal/cm^2 (1 cal = 4.184 J)

Sanity check: in reality only part of the arc energy is radiant (much goes into melting metal, heating gas, sound and blast), which pushes the dose down; but a switchboard enclosure focuses the energy out of the open front toward the worker rather than spherically, which pushes it back up. The two effects roughly trade off, and indeed the formal IEEE 1584-style result below lands at ≈ 23 cal/cm² — same order of magnitude. When a ten-line phone estimate and an empirical standard agree within a factor of ~1.3, you can trust you understand the physics.

Finally — and this is the central lesson of the whole section — look at which levers you actually control:

  • Current? Barely. Ia is fixed by the transformer impedance and the source (§c-shortcircuit). You cannot set it; you can only know it.
  • Distance? Helps (energy falls off roughly with distance to the power ~1.5–2 in an enclosure), but a human with a tool in a cubicle is at ≈ 455 mm and physically cannot be much further.
  • Time? Energy is directly proportional to time (E ∝ t) — and time is a setting on a device. Cut the clearing time from 0.3 s to 0.1 s and the 2 MJ becomes 0.66 MJ. Same fault, same board, one-third of the dose. No other variable in the problem is this cheap to change.
Arc energy = (megawatts you cannot choose) × (seconds you can choose). The protection engineer's time settings are, quite literally, a dial that sets how badly a worker gets burned. That is why this calculation lives in Part 2 next to protection coordination, not in a safety appendix.

The real IEEE 1584-2018 model is a set of empirical polynomial equations in six-plus parameters (voltage, Ibf, gap, electrode configuration, enclosure dimensions, distance), with interpolation between test voltages and a mandatory second pass at a reduced arcing current. Incident energy at the LV-MSB, step by step

Worked calculation

Step 1 — Arcing current Ia from bolted current Ibf.

The 38.5 kA from §c-shortcircuit is the bolted value: a zero-impedance metallic short. A real arc is not a bolted short — the plasma column has substantial resistance and a ~300 V drop, which at 400 V is a large fraction of the driving voltage. So the arcing current is markedly lower than the bolted current. At LV the reduction factor is roughly 0.6 (IEEE 1584 empirical; at MV the arc voltage is a small fraction of system voltage, so Ia ≈ Ibf there):

I_a ~ 0.6 x I_bf = 0.6 x 38.5 kA = 23.1 kA --> use ~22 kA (model value)

The 2018 standard does not use a flat 0.6 — it computes Ia from a polynomial in lg Ibf, voltage, gap and electrode configuration, precisely because this number matters twice: it sets the arc power and it is the current at which you must read the upstream device's trip time. We carry Ia ≈ 22 kA.

Sanity check: 22 kA is 57 % of 38.5 kA — within the 50–70 % band expected for 400 V arcs. If your software ever reports LV arcing current equal to bolted current, something is wrong.

Step 2 — Clearing time at the arcing current.

From the upstream incomer ACB's time–current curve (§c-protection) read the total clearing time at 22 kA, not at 38.5 kA. The instantaneous pickup is set above 22 kA (for selectivity), so the short-time element governs:

t = t_relay + t_breaker = 0.26 s + 0.04 s ~ 0.3 s (18 cycles at 60 Hz)

This trap is worth repeating: a fault current lower than bolted can be more dangerous, because it may drop below an instantaneous pickup and be cleared on a delayed element instead. Less current, more time, more energy.

Step 3 — Normalised incident energy En (2002-form, normalised to t = 0.2 s and D = 610 mm; constants for a grounded system in a metal box, gap G = 32 mm):

lg E_n = K1 + K2 + 1.081 x lg(I_a) + 0.0011 x G lg E_n = (-0.555) + (-0.113) + 1.081 x lg(22) + 0.0011 x 32 lg(22) = 1.342 --> 1.081 x 1.342 = 1.451 ; 0.0011 x 32 = 0.035 lg E_n = -0.555 - 0.113 + 1.451 + 0.035 = 0.818 E_n = 10^0.818 ~ 6.6 cal/cm^2 (at 0.2 s and 610 mm)

Step 4 — Scale to the real time and distance. Energy scales linearly with time, and with distance via an empirical exponent x ≈ 1.5 for LV switchgear enclosures (between 1/D for a focused beam and 1/D² for a point source); Cf = 1.5 is the LV correction factor:

E = C_f x E_n x (t / 0.2) x (610 / D)^1.5 E = 1.5 x 6.6 x (0.3 / 0.2) x (610 / 455)^1.5 (610/455) = 1.34 ; 1.34^1.5 = 1.34 x sqrt(1.34) = 1.34 x 1.158 = 1.55 E = 1.5 x 6.6 x 1.5 x 1.55 = 9.9 x 1.5 x 1.55 ~ 23 cal/cm^2

Result (as-found): E ≈ 23 cal/cm² at 455 mm.

Sanity checks: (a) the back-of-envelope radiant estimate in Feel for the numbers gave ≈ 18 cal/cm² — same order, good. (b) 23 cal/cm² is ~19 times the 1.2 cal/cm² second-degree threshold — consistent with the severe-burn narrative. (c) Typical published values for 400–480 V boards with delayed clearing run 8–40 cal/cm²; we are inside the band.

Step 5 — Arc-flash boundary AFB (the distance at which E falls to EB = 1.2 cal/cm²; an unprotected person must stay outside it). Inverting the same distance law:

AFB = D x (E / E_B)^(1/1.5) = 0.455 x (23 / 1.2)^(2/3) 23 / 1.2 = 19.2 ; 19.2^(1/3) = 2.68 ; 2.68^2 = 7.2 AFB = 0.455 x 7.2 ~ 3.3 m

(A naive inverse-square assumption would give 0.455 × √19.2 ≈ 2.0 m — note how the gentler enclosure exponent makes the true boundary larger. When approximating boundaries, err large.)

Step 6 — Recompute with the maintenance switch ON (t = 0.1 s). Only the time term changes — everything else is identical:

E = 1.5 x 6.6 x (0.1 / 0.2) x 1.55 = 9.9 x 0.5 x 1.55 ~ 7.7 cal/cm^2 AFB = 0.455 x (7.7 / 1.2)^(2/3) = 0.455 x 6.4^(2/3) = 0.455 x 3.45 ~ 1.6 m

Result (maintenance mode): E ≈ 7.7 cal/cm², AFB ≈ 1.6 m.

Sanity check: 23 / 7.7 = 3.0 — exactly the 0.3/0.1 time ratio, as it must be since E ∝ t. One switch position cut the dose to a third.

Step 7 — Map to PPE and decisions. Against the NFPA 70E arc-rated PPE categories (CAT 1: 4 / CAT 2: 8 / CAT 3: 25 / CAT 4: 40 cal/cm² — full table in the assessment section):

As-found: E ~ 23 cal/cm^2 > 8 --> CAT 3 suit territory (25 cal/cm^2 rated) Maintenance: E ~ 7.7 cal/cm^2 <= 8 --> CAT 2 daily-wear arc-rated clothing

The practical meaning: as found, any energised work at this board demands a full 25 cal/cm² arc-flash suit with hood — brutal at 50 °C ambient — and a 3.3 m clear zone. With a 0.1 s maintenance mode, the same task is a CAT 2 job in arc-rated daily workwear with a balaclava and face shield, boundary 1.6 m. Nothing about the board changed; only the time setting did.

Side calculation — and what about the MV switchgear?

The same logic applies at the 13.8 kV MV-SWGR, with three changes of character. First, at MV the ~300 V arc-column drop is a small fraction of the system voltage, so the arcing current is nearly the full bolted value (Ia ≈ Ibf; with the SEC source assumed 25 kA design, Ia ≈ 24–25 kA — no comfortable 0.6 reduction). Second, the arc power is far higher, because the same kA now burns across a much larger voltage. A crude scale check, taking an MV arc voltage of a few kV across the larger (~150 mm) gaps:

P_arc(MV) ~ 3 000 V x 25 000 A = 75 MW (vs 6.6 MW at the LV-MSB)

Third, the standard working distance is larger — 914 mm instead of 455 mm — which claws back roughly a factor of (914/455)^1.5 ≈ 2.8 in incident energy. The net result at typical MV relay times (0.3–0.5 s of graded overcurrent) is still routinely severe, which is why MV practice leans on fast bus differential protection, arc-resistant construction and closed-door operation rather than PPE (see Equipment technology). Sanity check: 75 MW × 0.4 s = 30 MJ — fifteen times the LV event of Step 2. Nobody "suits up" for that; the controls must be engineering controls.

These are training simplifications. The flat 0.6 arcing-current factor, the rounded 2002-form constants, the single x = 1.5 exponent, and the single operating mode are all simplifications, stated openly. A real study must also run: tie-closed (≈ 77 kA), DG-only and reduced-source modes; the IEEE 1584-2018 reduced arcing current case (a lower Ia with its own — usually longer — clearing time, taking the worst result); and the actual electrode configurations. The certified study governs labels, PPE and permits.

The clearing-time lever

Equipment settings

Everything in this sub-section follows from one identity:

t_clear = t_device (relay or trip-unit decision time, incl. intentional delay) + t_breaker (mechanical opening + arc extinction)

The breaker mechanical time is fixed by hardware: an LV ACB opens in roughly 40–60 ms (≈ 2.5–3.5 cycles at 60 Hz); an MV vacuum breaker in 3–5 cycles (50–83 ms). Everything else is settings — and settings are where incident energy is won or lost. On a modern LV electronic trip unit (LSIG — Long-time, Short-time, Instantaneous, Ground-fault; see §c-breaker) the relevant knobs are:

  • Instantaneous (I) pickup. If I is set below the arcing current (e.g. pickup 15 kA with Ia = 22 kA), the fault clears with no intentional delay — t ≈ 50–80 ms total and incident energy collapses. If I is set above Ia (or switched off, as is common on incomers for selectivity), the fault falls through to the S-band and burns for the full short-time delay. Always check where Ia — not Ibf — lands on the TCC.
  • Short-time (S) delay. Each step of intentional S-delay (0.1 → 0.2 → 0.3 → 0.4 s) buys selectivity with downstream breakers and pays for it linearly in cal/cm². Our worked example's 23 cal/cm² is mostly purchased S-delay. Settings reviews should treat every 0.1 s of S-delay as "+≈ 7.7 cal/cm² on this bus" (at our example's current and distance) and demand justification.
  • Arc-flash reduction maintenance switch (ERMS). A dedicated mode — selector switch, padlockable, usually with a blue pilot lamp — that temporarily enables a low, no-delay instantaneous element (e.g. pickup 2.5–4 kA, t ≈ 0 + breaker time) while a person is at the gear. Selectivity is deliberately sacrificed for the duration of the task only. This is the "0.1 s" of Step 6: the cheapest 3× energy reduction in the industry.
  • Zone-selective interlocking (ZSI). Downstream breakers that see the fault send a restraint signal upstream; an upstream breaker that sees fault current but no restraint knows the fault is on its own bus and trips at its minimum time instead of waiting out its S-delay. ZSI gives near-instantaneous bus-fault clearing without losing selectivity for through-faults — permanently, not just in maintenance mode. (Bus differential protection achieves the same at MV.)

The trade-off, stated plainly: selectivity (§c-protection) wants upstream devices slow, so the smallest downstream device always clears first and the plant stays up. Arc-flash safety wants upstream devices fast, because the upstream device is what protects the person standing at the bus. These two legitimate goals pull the same setting in opposite directions. The resolution hierarchy is:

  1. ZSI or bus differential — fast on in-zone faults, selective on through-faults; the engineering "have both" answer.
  2. ERMS — selective normally, fast while humans are exposed; the operational compromise.
  3. Permanently faster settings — accept some selectivity loss where the energy numbers are unacceptable and neither of the above is available.

Indicative effect of the available mechanisms at our LV-MSB (Ia ≈ 22 kA, D = 455 mm, scaling the worked result linearly with time):

How to read the clearing time off a TCC — the exact procedure. Field engineers get this wrong more often than any other step, so spell it out:

  1. Plot (or pull up) the time–current curve of the upstream device — the one that must clear a fault on the bus where the person works. For a fault on the LV-MSB bus that is the incomer ACB (or, if the incomer itself is the damaged item, the transformer's MV-side protection — check both).
  2. Enter the horizontal axis at the arcing current Ia = 22 kA — never at the bolted 38.5 kA. The curve is read where the fault actually sits.
  3. Read up to the curve's total clearing band (top of the band, not the nominal line): that includes relay/trip-unit tolerance.
  4. Add the breaker operating time if the plotted curve is a relay curve rather than a total-clearing curve (separate relays at MV: add 3–5 cycles; integrated LV trip units usually plot total clearing — check the legend).
  5. Repeat at the IEEE 1584-2018 reduced arcing current: a smaller current sits further left on an inverse curve, i.e. higher on the time axis. Keep whichever (Ia, t) pair yields the higher energy.
During any protection settings review, put a column for "incident energy at each main bus" next to the selectivity check. A grading study that has never been cross-checked against an arc-flash study is only half a study — and re-issuing labels after a settings change is mandatory, not optional (see Control).

Hardware that fights the arc

Equipment technology

Settings reduce the arc's duration. A second family of measures attacks the arc with hardware — containing it, detecting it faster than any current-based relay can, extinguishing it, or moving the human out of range altogether.

Arc-resistant ("internal arc classified") switchgear. MV switchgear can be type-tested to withstand an internal arc per IEC 62271-200 (the IAC classification). A rating such as IAC AFLR 31.5 kA 1 s reads: tested for personnel Accessibility on the Front, Lateral and Rear sides, at 31.5 kA arc current, for 1 second — matching, for our plant, the indicative 17.5 kV-class MV gear with 31.5 kA / 3 s bus withstand (see §cat-mv-swgr). Construction features: reinforced doors and hinges, pressure-relief flaps on top of each compartment, and exhaust plenums / ducts that channel the plasma and hot gases up and out of the room (or to a safe outdoor discharge) instead of out of the front of the panel into the operator's face. Cotton indicators placed around the gear during the type test must not ignite. Two cautions: the rating holds only with doors closed and latched — it protects nobody during open-door work — and it limits external consequences; the gear internals are still destroyed.

Optical arc-detection relays. An arc announces itself with an immense flash of light long before a current element can time out. Arc-flash relays (see §cat-relays) use point light sensors or a bare fibre-optic loop run through the busbar, cable and breaker compartments, and trip in < 2.5 ms from light detection. To avoid spurious trips from camera flashes or door-light reflections, the trip is normally supervised by a simultaneous overcurrent check (light AND current). Total clearing then becomes essentially the breaker time alone: ≈ 2.5 ms + ~50 ms = 50–80 ms — the ≈ 4 cal/cm² row in the table above. Retrofittable into existing gear, and one of the highest safety returns per riyal in brownfield plants.

Arc quenching / arc elimination devices. The fastest class of mitigation: triggered by an optical relay, a quenching device creates a deliberate, contained three-phase bolted short (or transfers the arc into a containment chamber) within ≈ 4 ms. Because a bolted metallic path has far lower impedance — and far lower voltage drop — than the plasma column, the arc collapses into the device and the energy release at the original fault location essentially stops; the upstream breaker then clears the bolted current normally. Incident energy at the worker can fall below 1.2 cal/cm². Cost and complexity reserve these for high-value or high-exposure boards.

Remote racking and remote switching. The most elegant fix for the racking scenario of our failure narrative: a motorised racking drive operated from a pendant or pedestal outside the arc-flash boundary (recall: 3.3 m as-found), or switching done from the PMS/ control room. The energy at the equipment is unchanged — but no body is inside the boundary to receive it. Risk = hazard × exposure; remote operation zeroes the exposure term.

IR inspection windows. Crystal or polymer windows rated for the panel, through which thermographic surveys of joints and terminations are done with covers closed. They remove one of the most common routine reasons for opening energised panels — and a loose, heating joint found early is an arc that never happens. In Saudi dust and 50 °C ambients, periodic IR scanning of LV and MV terminations is standard practice; windows make it a zero-exposure task.

Hierarchy of controls, arc-flash edition: eliminate (de-energise, remote racking) > engineering controls (fast settings, ZSI, arc relays, quenchers, arc-resistant gear, IR windows) > administrative (permits, boundaries, labels) > PPE — the last line, which assumes the arc happens and merely argues with it about burn depth.

Control, monitoring and keeping the study alive

Control

Where arc-flash devices sit in the protection scheme. An optical arc-flash relay is wired as an independent, parallel tripping path — it does not replace the overcurrent scheme of §c-protection, it bypasses its time grading when light is detected. Typical architecture at our plant: light sensors in each LV-MSB and MV-SWGR compartment → arc relay (current-supervised from the incomer CTs) → direct trip of the incomer (and, for an MV bus event, transfer-trip of the upstream feeder breaker so the bus is cleared even if the local breaker is the damaged item). The trip path is hard-wired; the PMS is informed, never relied upon, for tripping.

PMS / SCADA integration. The power-management system should carry, as a minimum: arc-relay trip and sensor-failure alarms (the relays self-monitor fibre continuity); ERMS position as a status point — with an alarm if the maintenance switch is left ON after the work permit closes, since a plant left permanently in maintenance mode silently loses its selectivity; breaker trip events time-stamped for post-incident reconstruction; and trip-unit setting read-back where supported, so an unauthorised settings change is detectable. Event records (waveforms, sequence-of-events) are the evidence base for any post-arc investigation.

Label data management. Each arc-flash label (anatomy in the assessment section) is the visible tip of a dataset: bus name, operating mode, Ibf, Ia, clearing device and time, working distance, E, AFB, PPE requirement, study revision and date. Keep the dataset as a controlled register (the study software's database, exported to the maintenance system), with label IDs tied to equipment tags. A label whose pedigree cannot be traced to a study revision is a decoration, not a control.

Re-study triggers. The study is a snapshot of the network and its settings on the study date. It must be revalidated when — not if — any of the following occurs:

  • Any protective setting change (relay, trip unit, fuse size) — the worked example showed 0.1 s of S-delay is worth ≈ 7.7 cal/cm² at the LV-MSB;
  • Any source change — SEC fault-level advice moving within the 25–40 kA range, transformer replacement or impedance change, adding/re-rating the 1250 kVA DG, UPS bypass arrangements;
  • Topology or mode changes — operating with the bus-tie closed (≈ 77 kA instead of 38.5 kA), new feeders, new MCCs;
  • Equipment changes — different breaker (different clearing time), retrofit of ZSI / arc relays / ERMS (the labels should then show the improved maintenance-mode energy);
  • Calendar — NFPA 70E practice: review at intervals not exceeding 5 years even if "nothing changed", because something always has.
An arc-flash study is not a document, it is a configuration item: it is valid only while the network, sources and settings it modelled remain frozen. Management-of-change procedures must list "re-validate arc-flash study and labels" as a mandatory step for every protection or source modification.

The arc-flash study, start to finish

Sizing method

"Sizing" here means sizing the hazard — and from it the PPE, boundaries and mitigation. The study procedure, in the order the work is actually done:

  1. Data collection. As-built one-line diagrams; transformer nameplates (kVA, Z%, vector group — for us 2 × 1600 kVA, Dyn11, Z = 6 %); SEC source fault level in writing (assume 25 kA design, state the 25–40 kA range); cable types, sizes and lengths; every protective device with its model and as-left settings; enclosure types, bus gaps and working distances per panel; DG and UPS data. Data quality decides study quality — this step is 60 % of the effort.
  2. Short-circuit study. Compute three-phase bolted fault current at every bus (§c-shortcircuit) for every credible operating mode: tie open / tie closed / DG / minimum source. Crucially, arc-flash needs the minimum credible fault current as well as the maximum — lower current can mean slower clearing and higher energy.
  3. Arcing currents. For each bus and mode, compute Ia (and the IEEE 1584-2018 reduced Ia,min) from Ibf, voltage, gap and electrode configuration.
  4. Clearing times. On each TCC (§c-protection), read the upstream device's total clearing time at Ia (and at Ia,min), adding breaker mechanical time. Cap exposure duration where justified (a worker exposed > 2 s is assumed to have escaped or been blasted clear — apply such caps with documented engineering judgement only).
  5. Incident energy and boundaries. Compute E at the standard working distance and the arc-flash boundary for every bus, every mode; report the worst case per bus.
  6. Mitigation pass. Wherever E exceeds the site target (commonly "get every routinely-operated panel to ≤ 8 cal/cm², i.e. CAT 2"), iterate: settings changes, ERMS, ZSI, arc relays, remote racking — re-running steps 4–5 each time. This loop is where the engineering value lives.
  7. Labels, PPE table, boundaries. Issue labels per panel (worst mode), the site PPE category table, and boundary distances; mark boundaries physically where practical for fixed high-energy locations.
  8. Handover and training. Brief operations and maintenance crews; align the permit-to-work forms; file the study as a controlled document with its re-study triggers (Control).

Saudi practice notes (Rev 3).

  • Heat stress is a co-equal hazard. At a 50 °C design ambient, a CAT 3/CAT 4 arc-flash suit is itself dangerous: core temperature rises fast, and heat exhaustion inside a sealed hood degrades exactly the fine judgement the task needs. Saudi practice therefore leans hard on the engineering side: drive energy down to CAT 2 (≤ 8 cal/cm²) so daily-wear arc-rated clothing suffices; schedule energised diagnostics for early morning; impose suit-time limits with hydration breaks; prefer remote racking and IR windows over suited open-panel work. A study that "solves" a 35 cal/cm² bus with a 40 cal suit has traded a burn hazard for a heat-stroke hazard — drive the energy down instead.
  • Permit culture. SEC- and Aramco-influenced sites run a strict permit-to-work regime: energised electrical work requires a dedicated permit with the incident energy / PPE category stated on the permit face, a documented justification for not de-energising, isolation certificates, and a standby person. The arc-flash label is what the permit issuer reads — another reason labels must be current.
  • Standards stack. There is no single Saudi arc-flash code; practice combines NFPA 70E / IEEE 1584-2018 (risk assessment method and calculation) with SASO-adopted IEC standards — IEC 62271-200 IAC for switchgear, IEC 61482 for arc-rated clothing — under SBC 401 and the SEC Distribution Code / Saudi Grid Code for the installation itself. State all of them in the study basis.

Above 40 cal/cm² no category exists: the blast (pressure, shrapnel) — which PPE does not stop — dominates. The only acceptable controls there are de-energisation or re-engineering the clearing time. Note also that arc-rated PPE addresses the thermal dose only; hearing, eyes and lungs need their own protection inside any boundary.

Boundaries — keep the two families separate in your head. The arc-flash boundary (3.3 m as-found at our LV-MSB; 1.6 m with ERMS) is the energy line: cross it during energised work and you need arc-rated PPE matched to the bus energy, because beyond it an unprotected person would receive less than 1.2 cal/cm². The shock approach boundaries (limited and restricted, set by voltage from the site shock-protection tables) are the contact line: they control who may come how close to exposed live conductors, with what qualification and insulation. The two are computed from different physics and one can be larger than the other — at high-energy LV boards the arc-flash boundary is usually the outer one. The label states both; the permit enforces both; the standby person polices both.

Field reality check. When you walk the plant with this section in hand, the assessment is not "is there PPE in the cabinet?" but a chain of evidence: study → settings as-left match the study → labels match the study revision → PPE on site matches the labels → permits quote the labels → ERMS/ZSI/arc relays actually work when secondary-injection tested. Any broken link breaks the whole chain, and the most commonly broken link is the second one: someone changed a setting after the study.

Self-check — work these without looking back, then verify:

  • Explain to a non-engineer why a worker who never touches a conductor can still be fatally injured at a switchboard — and why the hazard differs panel to panel.
  • Why is the LV arcing current (≈ 22 kA) lower than the bolted current (38.5 kA), and why must clearing time be read from the TCC at the arcing current? What is the "less current, more energy" trap?
  • Recompute the worked example for t = 0.2 s and confirm E ≈ 15 cal/cm² (linear scaling from 23 at 0.3 s). Which PPE category does that demand?
  • State the approximate energy released by a 6.6 MW arc burning for 0.3 s, in MJ and in kg of TNT, and name two everyday comparisons.
  • List the four PPE categories with their cal/cm² thresholds, and explain why "> 40" has no category.
  • Describe how ZSI clears a bus fault quickly without losing feeder selectivity, and contrast it with the ERMS approach.
  • Name at least four events that invalidate an existing arc-flash study and its labels.
  • Explain the Saudi-specific tension between arc rating and heat stress at 50 °C, and the engineering strategy that resolves it.
  • Verify on site: study exists and is ≤ 5 years old; labels posted on LV-MSB, MCCs and MV-SWGR; PPE on site matches label categories; ERMS functional and procedure documented; ZSI / bus differential / arc relays tested.
Incident energy = arc power × clearing time, spread over distance. You cannot choose the megawatts and you can barely choose the distance — but the seconds are a setting. At the LV-MSB, the same fault delivers ≈ 23 cal/cm² at 0.3 s and ≈ 7.7 cal/cm² at 0.1 s: one maintenance-switch position is the difference between a CAT 3 flash suit and CAT 2 workwear. Calculate, label, and make the time short — and remember every figure here is indicative; the certified IEEE 1584-2018 study governs.
