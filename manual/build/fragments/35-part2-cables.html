6 — Cable Sizing, Voltage Drop & Withstand Core

Goal: understand why every cable-sizing rule in the book is really a rule about insulation temperature; run the three independent checks — ampacity with the full Saudi derating chain, voltage drop at 60 Hz, and adiabatic short-circuit withstand — on two real connections of this plant: the TX-1-to-LV-MSB link (2 309 A over ~25 m) and a 160 kW pump feeder (≈ 280 A over 120 m); and finish with a complete step-by-step sizing procedure for the Rev 3 basis: 400/230 V, 60 Hz, TN-S, 50 °C design air ambient, 35 °C soil, dust and sand everywhere.

The load currents used here come from §2 — Full-Load Currents, the fault duty (38.5 kA at the LV bus, one transformer) from §4 — Short-Circuit Current & Switchgear Withstand, and the device that must protect every cable we size is dissected in §14 — Circuit Breakers.

Indicative data. Every numeric value in this section — base ratings, derating factors, resistances, reactances, k-constants — is indicative and chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis. Tabulated ampacities vary between table editions and manufacturers; the project cable schedule and the formal sizing study govern. Standards cited are the SASO-adopted editions of IEC 60364-5-52 (current-carrying capacity and installation methods) and IEC 60502-1/-2 (power cables 0.6/1 kV and up to 30 kV), applied together with SBC 401 and the SEC Distribution Code.

A Cable Is a Thermal Device

Why this exists

Ask a layperson what a cable is and they will say "a conductor of electricity". Ask a cable engineer and you will get a different answer: a cable is a thermal device that happens to conduct electricity. The copper or aluminium in the middle does not care how hot it gets — copper melts at 1 085 °C and is perfectly happy at 200 °C. What cares, intensely, is the few millimetres of polymer wrapped around it. Cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) is rated for a continuous conductor temperature of 90 °C; PVC for 70 °C. Exceed those numbers and the insulation does not fail today — it ages faster, quietly, chemically, until one summer afternoon years from now it fails for you.

Once you accept that a cable is a thermal device, every sizing rule in every standard suddenly makes sense as one of three ways the insulation can be overheated:

  • Slow heating — the ampacity check. Load current flowing through the conductor resistance generates heat continuously (I²R). That heat must escape through the insulation, sheath, and surrounding air or soil. The steady-state balance between heat generated and heat removed sets the conductor temperature. The "current-carrying capacity" in the tables is simply the current at which that balance lands exactly on 90 °C (XLPE) with the table's assumed surroundings. Change the surroundings — hotter air, more neighbouring cables, dry sand instead of moist soil — and the same current produces a higher temperature, which is why we derate.
  • Fast heating — the short-circuit withstand check. During a fault the cable may carry tens of kiloamps for tenths of a second. There is no time for heat to escape: the energy dumps adiabatically into the copper, and the conductor temperature rockets from 90 °C towards the insulation's short-time limit (250 °C for XLPE). The adiabatic equation tells us the minimum cross-section that can absorb the fault energy without crossing that line before the breaker clears.
  • Heating somebody else's equipment — the voltage-drop check. This one is not about the cable's own temperature but about what the cable does to the load at its far end. Resistance and reactance steal voltage along the run; a motor fed at 370 V instead of 400 V draws more current for the same power, runs hotter, and produces less starting torque. The voltage-drop limit protects the load's thermal life and the stability of everything downstream.

These three checks are independent, and that word matters. They test different physics over different timescales — years, milliseconds, and continuously — and a cable must pass all three. A short run at huge current passes voltage drop trivially but is governed by ampacity. A long run at modest current passes ampacity easily but is governed by voltage drop. A short, lightly loaded cable on a stiff bus can pass both and still be destroyed by a fault it was never sized to survive — governed by the adiabatic check. You cannot tell in advance which check will bite, so you always run all three. The worked calculations below are arranged to show each check governing in turn.

On this plant the thermal problem is harder than almost anywhere else on Earth. The IEC base tables assume 30 °C air and 20 °C soil. The Rev 3 design basis is 50 °C air and 35 °C soil, with dry sand whose thermal resistivity can be two or three times that of the moist soil the tables assume. An XLPE cable that carries 100 A in Europe carries about 82 A here before its insulation reaches the same 90 °C. Saudi cable sizing is, at its heart, the discipline of never forgetting that the tables were written for somewhere cooler.

A cable is a thermal device: every sizing rule exists to keep insulation temperature inside its rated limit — slowly (ampacity and derating), suddenly (adiabatic short-circuit withstand), or at the far end of the run (voltage drop). The three checks are independent and all must pass; any one of them can govern, and on the Saudi basis the thermal margins the tables silently assume are simply not there.

A Failure Story: the Trench That Cooked a Cable

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional but assembled from failure modes seen repeatedly in hot-climate plants. Every number is consistent with the Rev 3 basis. Notice that nothing in this story breaks — until the very end. That is what makes thermal failures so dangerous: the plant gives almost no warning.

Year 0 — the shortcut. During construction, a 120 m feeder to a process pump is sized from a manufacturer's quick-reference card: 280 A load, 185 mm² rated "around 430 A in air" — nearly 50 % margin, signed off in five minutes. Nobody applies the derating chain. The card's rating assumed 30 °C air and a single cable lying alone on a tray. This cable will actually run in a covered outdoor cable trench at the edge of the plant, touching five other loaded power cables, in air that the design basis says reaches 50 °C — and inside a covered trench in direct sun, locally hotter still. Apply the honest factors — ≈ 0.82 for 50 °C and ≈ 0.75 for six grouped circuits — and the real capacity is 430 × 0.82 × 0.75 ≈ 264 A. The cable is undersized by about 6 % before the plant has even started. Nothing happens. 280 A flows; the conductor settles not at 90 °C but at roughly 95–100 °C every summer afternoon. XLPE does not melt at 100 °C. It just begins to die.

Years 1–8 — Arrhenius does its quiet work. Insulation ageing is a chemical reaction, and like most chemical reactions it follows the Arrhenius law: the reaction rate roughly doubles for every 8–10 °C rise in temperature. The flip side is the rule every cable engineer carries in their head: every 8–10 °C of sustained over-temperature halves the insulation's life. XLPE run at its rated 90 °C is good for a design life of, say, 30–40 years. Run at 100 °C, that becomes 15–20. The trench cover traps heat; wind-blown sand fills the gaps between cables and blocks the convective paths the tables assumed; a later project lays two more cables in the same trench, worsening the grouping factor that was never applied in the first place. Each effect alone is a few degrees. Together they push the hottest metre of the run — the section under the sun-baked cover, in the middle of the group — to perhaps 105–110 °C on peak afternoons. The polymer hardens, micro-cracks, and loses dielectric strength, fastest exactly where it is hottest. The plant sees nothing: the pump runs, the breaker holds, the panel meter reads 280 A as it always has.

Year 9 — a Tuesday in August. 48 °C in the shade, plant at full output, the grid voltage a few percent low so every motor is drawing a few percent more current — the cable's worst hour of the year, repeated daily for weeks. In the hottest metre, the embrittled insulation finally cracks through to the screen. Earth-fault, then within a cycle phase-to-phase, fed at the full bus fault level. The feeder breaker clears it — that part of the protection works — but the pump is gone from the process at peak production, the trench cover comes off to reveal a charred section and five neighbouring cables with heat-damaged sheaths, and the repair is a cable replacement through a live, congested trench in August. The failure investigation finds no defective product, no operator error, no protection mis-setting. It finds a derating chain that was never applied, nine years earlier, by someone saving five minutes.

The signature of thermal under-sizing is that everything works — for years. Protection does not detect over-temperature; an overload relay set at the cable's paper rating happily passes a current the derated cable cannot stand. The defences are: applying the derating chain at design time, setting the overload pickup to the derated ampacity (not the nameplate one — see §14), and catching hot spots with thermography before the chemistry finishes its work. In the field, look for discoloured or hardened sheath, hot glands, deformed tray plastic, and sand-packed trenches — each is a chapter of this story in progress.

Feel for the Numbers

Feel for the numbers

Before sizing anything, calibrate your intuition. How much heat does a big cable actually make? Take a 240 mm² copper conductor — a serious cable, about as thick as a garden hose — carrying 400 A. Its AC resistance at operating temperature is about 0.0754 mΩ per metre. The heat generated in each metre of one conductor is:

Quick estimate — heat per metre of a loaded 240 mm² cable

P = I² × R = 400² × 0.0000754 Ω/m = 160 000 × 0.0000754 = 12.1 W per metre per conductor

A three-phase circuit has three loaded conductors, so each metre of the circuit dissipates:

P_3ph = 3 × 12.1 ≈ 36 W per metre of route

Sanity check: ~30–40 W is a small soldering iron. Picture a soldering iron strapped to every single metre of the run, switched on permanently — over a 120 m route, that is ≈ 4.3 kW of continuous heating, two domestic space heaters' worth, buried in a trench in a country where the air is already at 50 °C. The entire ampacity problem is the question: can each metre of surroundings carry one soldering iron's worth of heat away, forever, without the insulation passing 90 °C?

That image explains nearly everything about derating at a glance:

  • Why ambient temperature matters so much. Heat only flows downhill, from hot to cold. The driving force is the temperature difference between the 90 °C conductor and the surroundings. At the tables' 30 °C air there is a 60 K hill for the heat to roll down; at our 50 °C there is only a 40 K hill — two-thirds the driving force, so only √(40/60) ≈ 0.82 of the current (heat goes with I², hence the square root). One line of algebra, and it is the single most important number in Saudi cable engineering.
  • Why XLPE beats PVC here. PVC's 70 °C limit leaves only a 20 K hill above 50 °C air — its derating factor is √((70−50)/(70−30)) = √0.5 ≈ 0.71, against XLPE's 0.82. Worse, PVC's short-circuit limit is 160 °C against XLPE's 250 °C, so its adiabatic k-constant is lower too (115 vs 143 for copper). In a 50 °C country, XLPE is not a luxury; PVC power cable is close to unusable for anything heavily loaded. This plant standardises on XLPE (90 °C) throughout.
  • Why grouping factors exist. Six soldering irons in one trench must share the same cooling air and the same patch of soil. Each cable's "ambient" is raised by its neighbours' heat, so each can carry less. The grouping factor is just mutual heating given a number.
  • Why dry sand is the enemy. Moist soil conducts heat about as well as brick (thermal resistivity ≈ 1.0 K·m/W — the tables' assumption). Dry desert sand is an insulator by comparison, 2.5–3.0 K·m/W: it wraps the cable in a thermal blanket. A direct-buried cable in unimproved dry sand can lose 20–30 % of its tabulated rating from soil resistivity alone, which is why buried runs here get imported thermal backfill (stabilised sand-cement mix) rather than native dune sand.

Two more 60 Hz-specific intuitions, because this is a 60 Hz plant and most cable tables in circulation are written for 50 Hz:

  • Skin effect is slightly worse at 60 Hz. Alternating current crowds toward the conductor surface; the skin depth in copper is ≈ 9.3 mm at 50 Hz and ≈ 8.5 mm at 60 Hz. Small cables (≤ 95 mm², radius well under one skin depth) barely notice. Large single-core conductors — the 630 mm² cables in our transformer link, radius ≈ 15 mm — do: their AC resistance at 60 Hz runs a few percent (indicatively 2–4 %) above the 50 Hz table value. Real designs use 60 Hz manufacturer data; for hand checks, adding ~3 % to R for very large cables is honest enough.
  • Reactance scales directly with frequency. X = 2πfL, so the same cable geometry has 1.2× the reactance at 60 Hz that the 50 Hz tables show. For small cables, where resistance dominates voltage drop, this hardly matters. For large cables — where X can exceed R several times over — the reactive term dominates the voltage drop, and forgetting the 1.2 factor understates the drop on exactly the runs where it matters most. The worked calculation below shows both numbers side by side.
Carry three numbers in your head for this plant: 0.82 (XLPE air derating at 50 °C — the cost of the climate), ~12 W per metre per conductor for a heavily loaded large cable (a soldering iron per metre of circuit), and 1.2× on every 50 Hz tabulated reactance. With those three, you can sanity-check almost any cable decision on site with a phone calculator.

Worked Calculation — the TX Link and a Pump Feeder, All Three Checks

Worked calculation

We now size two very different connections completely, running each through the full set of checks. The contrast is deliberate: the transformer link is short and enormous (ampacity governs), the motor feeder is long and modest (voltage drop nearly governs), and the adiabatic check sits in the background of both, waiting to govern anything small. All cables are copper, XLPE (90 °C), 0.6/1 kV to IEC 60502-1, on the Rev 3 environment: 50 °C air, 35 °C soil, 60 Hz.

Case A — TX-1 to LV-MSB link: 2 309 A, route ≈ 25 m, on ladder rack in air

Step A1 — Design current. The LV full-load current of one 1 600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV transformer (§2):

I_B = S / (√3 × U) = 1 600 000 / (1.732 × 400) = 2 309 A

The link must carry this continuously — a transformer incomer is sized for the transformer's capability, not today's load, so no diversity is taken.

Step A2 — Installation method and base rating. No practical multicore cable carries 2 309 A; this duty is met with parallel single-core cables per phase. Choose 630 mm² Cu/XLPE single-core, trefoil groups on ladder rack in free air (IEC 60364-5-52 method F). Indicative tabulated rating per cable, trefoil in free air at the table's 30 °C reference:

I_table ≈ 870 A (630 mm² Cu XLPE single-core, trefoil, free air, 30 °C — indicative)

Step A3 — Derating chain. Two factors apply (no soil factors — this run is in air):

k_ambient = √((θ_cond − θ_amb) / (θ_cond − 30)) = √((90 − 50)/(90 − 30)) = √0.667 = 0.82 k_grouping ≈ 0.90 (four trefoil groups on one ladder, spaced ≥ one cable diameter apart — indicative) I_z(per cable) = 870 × 0.82 × 0.90 = 642 A

Step A4 — Number of parallel runs.

n ≥ I_B / I_z = 2 309 / 642 = 3.6 → n = 4 runs per phase Check: 4 × 642 = 2 568 A ≥ 2 309 A ✓ (margin 11 %)

So the link is 4 × (3 × 1c 630 mm² Cu/XLPE) — twelve single-core power cables in four trefoils, plus the neutral and PE runs. Each parallel run carries 2 309 / 4 ≈ 577 A, i.e. 90 % of its derated capacity. Sanity check: at 577 A a 630 mm² conductor (R ≈ 0.037 mΩ/m) dissipates 577² × 0.000037 ≈ 12 W/m — one soldering iron per metre per conductor, exactly the intuition from above; twelve conductors ≈ 150 W per metre of ladder, which is why these ladders are run in open air with spacing, never sealed into a duct.

Parallel runs only share current equally if they are identical: same size, same length, same formation, same phase arrangement (transposed or symmetrically grouped). A lazy phase layout at 60 Hz unbalances the reactances and one run quietly carries 20 % more than its sisters. At this current level many plants use busduct instead of cables precisely to avoid this problem — the cable solution is shown here because it teaches the method.

Step A5 — Voltage drop at 60 Hz. Per-cable impedance (90 °C, trefoil, indicative): R ≈ 0.0367 Ω/km; tabulated 60 Hz reactance X50 ≈ 0.130 Ω/km. Convert the reactance to 60 Hz, then divide both by the 4 parallel runs:

X_60 = 1.2 × X_50 = 1.2 × 0.130 = 0.156 Ω/km R_eff = 0.0367 / 4 = 0.00918 Ω/km ; X_eff = 0.156 / 4 = 0.0390 Ω/km

At plant power factor 0.85 (cosφ = 0.85, sinφ = 0.527), route length L = 0.025 km:

ΔU = √3 × I × L × (R·cosφ + X·sinφ) ΔU = 1.732 × 2 309 × 0.025 × (0.00918 × 0.85 + 0.0390 × 0.527) ΔU = 100.0 × (0.00780 + 0.02055) = 100.0 × 0.02835 = 2.8 V → 2.8 / 400 = 0.71 % ✓

For comparison, the same calculation with the raw 60 Hz reactance gives 100.0 × (0.00780 + 0.01713) = 2.5 V (0.62 %). On this short link the 60 Hz correction is harmless — but notice that the reactive term is already 2.6× the resistive term. On large cables, voltage drop is a reactance problem, and reactance is the term that grows 20 % when you move from a 60 Hz table to a 60 Hz plant.

Step A6 — Adiabatic short-circuit check. Worst case: a fault on the link cleared by upstream protection in t = 0.3 s (a deliberately slow, conservative teaching value — transformer-secondary faults are cleared by the MV side or the incomer ACB short-time stage, see §14) at the one-transformer fault level of 38.5 kA (§4). Minimum copper/XLPE cross-section, with k = 143:

S_min = I × √t / k = 38 500 × √0.3 / 143 = 38 500 × 0.548 / 143 = 21 090 / 143 = 147 mm² Installed per phase: 4 × 630 = 2 520 mm² ≫ 147 mm² ✓ (margin > 17×)

Sanity check and verdict: for the transformer link, ampacity governs (it set the 4 × 630 mm² answer); voltage drop and adiabatic withstand pass with huge margins. That ordering is typical of short, heavy connections.

Case B — 160 kW process pump feeder: ≈ 280 A, route 120 m, multicore on perforated tray

Step B1 — Design current. Motor FLC by the §2 method (400 V, 60 Hz, 4-pole 1 800 rpm pump motor, indicative efficiency 0.95, pf 0.85):

I_B = P / (√3 × U × pf × η) = 160 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.85 × 0.95) = 160 000 / 559 = 286 A ≈ 280–290 A

Use the nameplate/schedule value I_B = 280 A (indicative) for the sizing; motors are sized on FLC, not on today's pumping duty (§3).

Step B2 — Installation and derating chain. One 4-core (3P+PE handled separately — see step B5) multicore Cu/XLPE cable on perforated tray, run with five other loaded power circuits, 50 °C air:

k_ambient = √((90 − 50)/(90 − 30)) = 0.82 ; k_grouping = 0.80 (6 circuits, single layer, touching — indicative) k_total = 0.82 × 0.80 = 0.656 Required table rating: I_table ≥ I_B / k_total = 280 / 0.656 = 427 A

Step B3 — Select from the table. Indicative multicore Cu/XLPE tray ratings (method E, 30 °C): 150 mm² ≈ 380 A — too small; 185 mm² ≈ 435 A — passes (435 ≥ 427, margin 2 %); 240 mm² ≈ 510 A — comfortable. Tentatively select 185 mm² and let the other two checks confirm or overturn it. Note how brutal the chain is: a cable tabulated at 435 A is, in this trench-mate-crowded Saudi installation, really a 435 × 0.656 = 285 A cable — barely above the 280 A load. The "430 A cable for a 280 A load" of the failure story was never the 50 % margin it appeared to be.

Step B4 — Voltage drop at 60 Hz over 120 m. 185 mm² Cu/XLPE multicore (90 °C, indicative): R ≈ 0.128 Ω/km; X50 ≈ 0.075 Ω/km → X60 = 1.2 × 0.075 = 0.090 Ω/km. Running condition, pf 0.85:

ΔU = √3 × 280 × 0.120 × (0.128 × 0.85 + 0.090 × 0.527) ΔU = 1.732 × 280 × 0.120 × (0.1088 + 0.0474) = 58.2 × 0.1562 = 9.1 V %ΔU = 9.1 / 400 = 2.3 % ✓ (≤ 3 % motor steady-state target; ≤ 5 % absolute limit)

With the raw 60 Hz reactance the answer would have been 58.2 × (0.1088 + 0.0395) = 8.6 V (2.2 %) — only 0.1 point different here, because at 185 mm² resistance still dominates. The 60 Hz correction matters more as cables get bigger; the habit of applying it should be unconditional.

Step B4a — Starting voltage drop (sanity check). If this pump starts direct-on-line it draws ≈ 6 × 280 = 1 680 A at starting pf ≈ 0.2 (§3):

ΔU_start = 1.732 × 1 680 × 0.120 × (0.128 × 0.20 + 0.090 × 0.98) = 349 × 0.1138 = 39.7 V ≈ 9.9 %

Nearly 10 % drop in the cable alone, before the transformer's own dip is added. For a DOL start this would push the motor-terminal voltage during start toward the edge of the usual 80–85 % torque-safety band — one reason this size of pump on this plant is started by VFD or soft starter rather than DOL. The steady-state check passed; the starting check is what actually disciplines long motor feeders.

Step B5 — Adiabatic short-circuit check — and a trap. Bus fault level 38.5 kA. If the feeder MCCB clears instantaneously (< 10 ms, and current-limiting below its let-through threshold — §14, LV breaker catalogue), the energy let through is far below the cable's capability. But check the conservative case — clearing in 0.3 s, as if the fault were cleared by a delayed upstream stage:

S_min = I × √t / k = 38 500 × √0.3 / 143 = 147 mm² Selected 185 mm² ≥ 147 mm² ✓ — but only just (margin 26 %)

The trap: run the same numbers for a small cable. A 35 mm² feeder to a small panel on this same 38.5 kA bus, protected by a breaker that clears in 0.3 s, would need 147 mm² — it fails by a factor of four. Even at a fast relay-graded 0.1 s, S_min = 38 500 × 0.316 / 143 = 85 mm². Small cables near a stiff bus are governed by the adiabatic check, not by ampacity, and survive only because their protective device is current-limiting (its let-through I²t, not the bus fault level and relay time, is what the cable sees). Whenever you see a small cable on a big board, the question is never "can it carry the load?" — it is "what exactly limits the energy when it faults?"

Step B6 — Earth-fault loop and protective conductor. The plant is TN-S (§9): an earth fault returns through the dedicated PE conductor, and the loop impedance must be low enough that the feeder protection disconnects within the standard's time limit. Two notes close the design:

  • PE size. By the IEC 60364-5-54 table rule, for phase conductors above 35 mm² the PE may be half the phase size: 185 / 2 = 92.5 → 95 mm² Cu. Cross-check adiabatically with the earth-fault current and actual clearing time (e.g. 0.1 s at an indicative 20 kA earth-fault level: S_min = 20 000 × 0.316 / 143 = 44 mm² — 95 mm² passes comfortably).
  • Loop check. Estimate Zs = source + phase + PE impedances over the 120 m run and confirm the resulting earth-fault current sits above the breaker's magnetic/short-time pickup so disconnection is instantaneous. Long LV runs fail this check before they fail anything else; the fix is a larger PE, a parallel earth conductor, or earth-fault (G) protection on the feeder breaker (§14).

Verdict, Case B: 1 × 4c (or 3c + 95 mm² PE) 185 mm² Cu/XLPE, passing ampacity (just — the derating chain ate the apparent margin), voltage drop 2.3 % running, adiabatic 185 ≥ 147 mm², PE 95 mm². If the project later adds circuits to that tray, the grouping factor drops and this cable must be rechecked — a derating chain is a living calculation, not a one-time stamp.

Three checks, two cases, three different governors: ampacity sized the transformer link, the starting/steady voltage drop disciplines the long pump feeder, and the adiabatic equation silently governs every small cable on a 38.5 kA bus. Formula → substitution → result → sanity check, every time — and never trust a margin until the derating chain has been applied to it.

Equipment Settings — Making the Breaker Defend the Cable

Equipment settings

A cable has no settings of its own — it is a passive thermal device. Its "settings" live entirely in the protective device upstream, and the whole point of this sub-section is one sentence: the breaker must be set to protect the cable you actually installed, in the environment it actually lives in — the derated cable, not the catalogue cable. Three coordination rules follow, one per timescale.

Rule 1 — Overload pickup bracketed by load and derated ampacity. The long-time (thermal) pickup Ir of the feeder breaker must sit between the design current and the cable's derated capacity:

Setting window — overload pickup for our two cases

Condition: I_B ≤ I_r ≤ I_z(derated)

Case A, TX-1 incomer ACB (see §14 for the trip-unit anatomy):

2 309 A ≤ I_r ≤ 2 568 A → set I_r = 2 375 A (0.95 × the 2 500 A sensor — see the breakers chapter)

Case B, 160 kW pump feeder MCCB:

280 A ≤ I_r ≤ 285 A → set I_r = 285 A (0.71 × a 400 A frame — indicative)

Sanity check: the Case B window is only 5 A wide — that is what a derating chain eating the margin looks like at the settings stage. If the window closes completely (I_z < I_B), no setting exists: the cable is wrong, and the fix is one size up, never a higher pickup. A pickup set above I_z is the failure story of this section written into the trip unit.

Rule 2 — Let-through energy below the cable's adiabatic limit. The adiabatic capability of a cable is the constant k²S² (in A²s), and the breaker's job is to clear any fault before it delivers more I²t than that. Compare the worst-case let-through with the limit:

Mini-check — is 38.5 kA for 0.3 s acceptable for the chosen sizes?

Energy delivered if a 38.5 kA bus fault persists for a 0.3 s short-time delay (a typical incomer ACB STD setting — §14):

I²t = 38 500² × 0.3 = 1.482 × 10⁹ × 0.3 = 4.45 × 10⁸ A²s

Cable capability, k = 143 (Cu/XLPE):

185 mm²: k²S² = 143² × 185² = 20 449 × 34 225 = 7.00 × 10⁸ A²s ≥ 4.45 × 10⁸ ✓ (margin 1.57×) 630 mm² (one run): 143² × 630² = 8.12 × 10⁹ A²s ✓ (margin 18×) 95 mm² PE: 143² × 95² = 1.85 × 10⁸ A²s < 4.45 × 10⁸ ✗ at full bus fault for 0.3 s

Reading the result: the phase conductors are fine — this is the same 147 mm² answer as the worked calculation, expressed in energy instead of cross-section. The 95 mm² PE "failure" is the instructive line: it never sees the full 38.5 kA three-phase level (earth-fault current is lower) and earth faults on a TN-S feeder are cleared instantaneously or by the G stage, not after a 0.3 s delay — but the arithmetic shows why earth-fault clearing time must be fast, and why a PE conductor is never protected by hope. Verify with the actual earth-fault level and the actual G-stage time, per §9.

For small cables the same rule works in reverse: a 35 mm² cable (k²S² = 2.5 × 10⁷ A²s) on the 38.5 kA bus survives only because a current-limiting MCCB lets through perhaps 10⁶–10⁷ A²s — read the let-through curve from the manufacturer data (LV breaker catalogue) and check I²t_let-through ≤ k²S² explicitly. Never assume; the curve is in every catalogue precisely for this comparison.

Rule 3 — Overload protection must ride through the motor start. Case B starts at ≈ 1 680 A for several seconds (§3). The feeder's long-time curve, set at Ir = 285 A, must not trip during a healthy start; the instantaneous pickup must sit above the asymmetrical starting peak (≈ 2–2.5× the starting current, so set Ii ≥ ~4 200 A indicatively) but below the minimum fault current at the motor terminals. The cable is comfortable: a 6× start for 10 s delivers 1 680² × 10 = 2.8 × 10⁷ A²s, 4 % of the 185 mm² limit. Cables shrug off motor starts; it is the settings that must be taught not to flinch.

Three settings rules defend a cable: I_B ≤ I_r ≤ I_z(derated) for slow heat; let-through I²t ≤ k²S² for fast heat; and a curve that rides through motor starting without ever exceeding either. If a setting window will not close, change the cable, never the rule.

Equipment Technology — What a Cable Actually Is, Layer by Layer

Equipment technology

Cut a power cable square and you see a set of concentric answers to the questions this section has been asking. Working outward from the centre of a typical LV cable to IEC 60502-1:

  • Conductor — copper or aluminium, normally class 2 stranded (compacted strands: flexible enough to install, solid enough to terminate). Class 1 solid is for small fixed wiring; class 5 fine-stranded flexible is for moving equipment and needs ferrules at every termination.
  • Insulation — XLPE on this plant: 90 °C continuous, 250 °C short-circuit, k = 143 with copper. The cross-linking is the whole trick — ordinary polyethylene softens near 80 °C; chemically cross-linking the chains makes a thermoset that holds shape to far higher temperatures. This single material choice buys the 0.82 (not 0.71) ambient factor that makes Saudi sizing workable.
  • Screen (MV cables, and metallic-screened LV designs) — a copper tape or wire screen over each insulated core, carrying capacitive charging current and earth-fault current, and tying the electric field to a defined earthed boundary.
  • Bedding and armour — SWA (galvanised steel wire armour) on multicore cables gives mechanical protection and a low-impedance earth path. On single-core cables steel is forbidden — a magnetic tube around one phase is a transformer core and heats ferociously — so single-core armour is AWA (non-magnetic aluminium wire).
  • Outer sheath — PVC for general buried duty; LSZH (low smoke, zero halogen) wherever cables run through occupied or safety-critical buildings, because burning PVC releases dense smoke and corrosive HCl. Specify UV-stabilised (carbon-black loaded) sheath for anything outdoors here — Saudi sun destroys unstabilised polymer sheaths in a few years.

Copper vs aluminium. Aluminium has ~61 % of copper's conductivity, so an Al cable needs roughly 1.6× the cross-section (about two standard sizes up) for the same duty — but it weighs half as much and, per ampere, usually costs noticeably less. The price is termination discipline: aluminium creeps under pressure and grows an insulating oxide skin instantly, so it demands bimetallic lugs, joint compound, and torque-checked connections. Indicative plant practice: Al for large, long, rarely-disturbed feeders (where the cost saving is real and terminations are few), Cu for motor feeders, controls, and anything frequently terminated. The adiabatic k also drops (≈ 94 for Al/XLPE vs 143), so the short-circuit check tightens too.

Single-core vs multicore. Multicore (3- or 4-core) is the default up to roughly 300–400 mm²: one pull, balanced fields, armour usable as PE. Above that, cables become unmanageably stiff and heavy, and the design shifts to single-core in trefoil — exactly the Case A solution. Trefoil (three cables touching in a triangle) keeps the three phase fields cancelling, which minimises reactance and stray heating; flat formation runs slightly cooler but has higher and unequal reactances and needs transposition on long runs.

Screen and armour bonding — a genuinely 60 Hz topic. The metallic screen/armour of a single-core cable links the phase flux, so a voltage is induced along it, proportional to current and to frequency — about 20 % higher at 60 Hz than the 50 Hz literature value for the same geometry. The bonding choice decides what that voltage does:

  • Both-ends bonding (default for LV and short MV runs): the induced voltage drives a circulating current around the screen–earth loop. It is safe and simple, but the circulating current heats the screen and can eat 5–15 % of the cable's ampacity on large single-core runs — slightly worse at 60 Hz, and worth checking against the manufacturer's both-ends-bonded rating column, not the single-point one.
  • Single-point bonding (long, heavily loaded MV single-core runs): earth one end only — no loop, no circulating current, full ampacity — but the open end develops a standing voltage (volts to tens of volts per hundred metres at load, far more during faults), so it needs a sheath-voltage limiter and a warning label. Choose deliberately and document it; a "temporary" second earth quietly added later converts a single-point design into an undeclared both-ends one and the cable runs hotter than its calculation.

MV terminations and stress control. On the 13.8 kV feeders, cutting back the insulation screen creates the most electrically stressed few millimetres on the plant: the field that was uniform inside the cable concentrates at the screen edge. A stress cone or stress-control tubing (cold-shrink or heat-shrink) grades that field back to safe values. Termination kits are rated-and-tested assemblies to IEC 60502-4; they fail not by calculation error but by craftsmanship — a knife nick in the insulation, a fingerprint of conductive grime, a screen cut 5 mm short. Most MV cable failures are termination failures, which is why the monitoring section below aims its tests squarely at them.

Saudi installation notes. Outdoors: UV-stabilised black sheaths, metal (not plastic) tray covers as sunshades with an air gap, and glands rated for the enclosure's IP54+ dust rating. Buried: imported thermal backfill (stabilised sand–cement, resistivity ≤ ~1.2 K·m/W) around the cables — never native dune sand — with route markers and warning tape, because wind-relocated sand erases surface evidence of trench routes within months. Trenches and ducts attract rodents and nesting insects: seal duct ends, and use SWA/AWA rather than unarmoured cable for anything they can reach. Sand packing between cables in covered trenches destroys the convective cooling the ratings assume — specify spacing and inspect for drift ingress.

Control — Monitoring a Device That Has No Moving Parts

Control

A cable cannot be "controlled" in the way a breaker or a motor can — it has no coil to energise and no setting to change. What it has is a condition, and the control discipline for cables is condition monitoring plus ruthless data management. The failure story earlier in this section gave nine years of warning to anyone who looked; these are the ways of looking.

  • Insulation resistance (IR) testing — the basic LV health check: a DC test voltage (typically 500–1 000 V for LV cables) between conductors and between each conductor and earth, isolated and discharged first. New XLPE cable reads in the gigaohms; the absolute number matters less than the trend — a feeder that read 2 GΩ at commissioning and reads 50 MΩ this shutdown is telling a story, even though 50 MΩ still "passes". Record temperature and humidity with every reading; IR halves roughly every 10 °C, and an August reading compared raw against a January baseline will frighten you for nothing.
  • VLF withstand and partial-discharge (PD) testing — MV cables. The 13.8 kV feeders get more than a megger. A VLF (very low frequency, 0.1 Hz) AC withstand test stresses the insulation like power frequency without needing a building-sized test set; PD measurement listens for the tiny discharges inside voids, nicks and bad terminations — the precursor noise of every termination failure, detectable years before breakdown. Test at commissioning to fingerprint the system, then periodically or after any cable is disturbed. Never DC-pressure-test aged XLPE: DC can leave trapped space charge that damages exactly the insulation you were checking.
  • Thermography of terminations and routes. The hottest point of a healthy circuit is a clue; the hottest point of an unhealthy one is a prediction. An infrared survey of terminations, glands and accessible tray runs at peak load — summer afternoon, plant at full output — finds loose lugs, overloaded runs and sand-choked trench sections while they are still maintenance items. The practical technique, pass criteria and report format live in Part 5, the field-tools part of this manual.

The cable schedule is a control system. Every check in this section consumed data: route length, installation method, grouping, derating chain, breaker settings, bonding method, test history. The cable schedule is the single document that holds it, one row per cable, tags per the plant scheme. Its discipline rules: every cable on the plant appears in it; every change — an added tray circuit, a rerouted trench, a resized feeder — re-triggers the derating check of its neighbours (recall the grouping-factor erosion in the failure story); and test results are appended to the row, not filed in a separate binder that no designer will ever open. A perfect calculation with an unmaintained schedule decays into the failure story at the speed of plant modifications.

Cables are controlled by knowledge: trended IR for LV, VLF/PD for the 13.8 kV feeders, thermography at peak load for terminations and routes, and a living cable schedule that forces a derating re-check whenever anyone touches a tray or a trench. The plant gives years of warning — but only in the data.

Sizing Method — the Complete Procedure for the Rev 3 Basis

Sizing method

Everything above compresses into one repeatable procedure. Run it in order, write down every factor, and let the largest answer from the independent checks choose the size. All table values below are indicative; the SASO-adopted editions of IEC 60364-5-52 (LV ampacity and methods) and IEC 60502 (construction), applied with SBC 401 and the SEC Distribution Code, govern.

  1. Establish the design current IB. Transformer links: full nameplate FLC (Case A — 2 309 A). Motor feeders: motor FLC, not pumping duty (§2, §3). Distribution feeders: maximum demand after diversity. Add known future load now — recabling later costs ten times more than one size up today.
  2. Fix the route and installation method. Walk it or take it from the layout: length, in air or buried, tray/ladder/duct/trench, how many other loaded circuits share each segment, sun exposure. Identify the worst segment — the whole cable is rated by its hottest metre. Map to an IEC 60364-5-52 method letter (E multicore on tray, F single-core in air, D buried).
  3. Read the base table rating Itable for the method, candidate size and material, at the table's reference conditions (30 °C air / 20 °C soil).
  4. Apply the full derating chain — I_z = I_table × k_amb × k_group × k_soil × k_depth — using the Saudi factors below, and require Iz ≥ IB. The anchor factor is worth re-deriving from first principles whenever in doubt: k_amb = √((90 − 50)/(90 − 30)) = √0.667 ≈ 0.82 (XLPE, 50 °C air)
  5. Voltage-drop check at 60 Hz. ΔU = √3 × I × L × (R·cosφ + X·sinφ), with X ≈ 1.2 × the 60 Hz table value (X = 2πfL) and R at 90 °C (plus ~3 % skin allowance above ≈ 400 mm²). Indicative limits: 3 % steady-state to a motor, 5 % overall to any load, and check DOL starting drop on long motor feeders (Case B, step B4a). If it fails, go up a size — or question the route.
  6. Adiabatic check. S ≥ I × √t / k with k = 143 (Cu/XLPE), 94 (Al/XLPE), using the real fault level (§4) and the real clearing time — or compare the breaker's published let-through I²t against k²S² for current-limiting devices (§14, catalogue). This check, not ampacity, governs small cables on stiff buses.
  7. Earth-fault loop and PE conductor. Size the PE (half the phase above 35 mm², table rule, adiabatically cross-checked), estimate the TN-S loop impedance over the full run, and confirm the earth-fault current trips the protection within the disconnection time (§9). Long runs fail here first; fixes are a bigger PE or a G-stage on the breaker.
  8. Choose the next standard size ≥ every check's answer (… 95, 120, 150, 185, 240, 300, 400, 500, 630 mm²), or parallel runs above ~630 mm² (identical cables, symmetrical layout — Case A). Then close the loop: record the chain in the cable schedule and set the breaker per Equipment settings.

Worked micro-example — the chain at its worst

A buried feeder in unimproved dry sand, 35 °C soil, grouped with three other circuits:

k_total = 0.89 × 0.75 × 0.75 = 0.50

Half the table rating — a "400 A" cable carrying 200 A. Sanity check: this is why thermal backfill is specified (0.75 → ≈ 0.95 on the resistivity term, k_total back to ≈ 0.63) and why no Saudi buried run is ever sized from the bare table number.

The method is eight steps: load → route → base table → derating chain → 60 Hz voltage drop → adiabatic check → earth-fault loop → next standard size. The biggest answer wins, the schedule records the chain, and the breaker settings close the loop.
