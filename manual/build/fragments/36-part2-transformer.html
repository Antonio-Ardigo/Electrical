The Plant's Heart: Why 13.8 kV In and 400 V Out

Why this exists

If the switchboard is the plant's spine and the cables are its arteries, the transformer is its heart — every watt the plant ever uses passes through the windings of TX-1 or TX-2. It is also, by a comfortable margin, the most passive major item in the installation: no moving parts, no contacts, no electronics in the power path. Two sets of copper windings wound around a common steel core, doing one thing only — exchanging voltage for current at almost perfect efficiency. To understand why the plant needs that exchange, follow the power from the SEC network to a motor terminal box.

The plant draws about 2.0 MW at 0.85 power factor — roughly 2353 kVA. Power can be delivered as high voltage and low current or low voltage and high current; the product is the same. The cost of moving it is not. Cable heating goes as I²R — current squared times resistance — so the current you choose matters twice over.

Why distribute at MV — the I²R argument in one line

Same apparent power, two voltage choices:

At 400 V:   I = 2 353 000 / (1.732 × 400) ≈ 3 396 A At 13.8 kV: I = 2 353 000 / (1.732 × 13 800) ≈ 98 A

Voltage ratio 13.8 / 0.4 = 34.5, so the MV current is 34.5 times smaller. Cable loss in a given conductor goes as I²:

Loss ratio = 34.5² ≈ 1 190

For the same conductor, carrying the plant's power at 13.8 kV dissipates roughly 1 190 times less heat in the cable than carrying it at 400 V. That is the entire economic case for medium voltage in two numbers: 34.5 and 1 190.

So why not run the whole plant at 13.8 kV and skip the transformer? Because the trade-off cuts the other way at the point of use. Insulating, isolating and safely working on 13.8 kV equipment is expensive: creepage distances grow, every switching device becomes a certified MV apparatus, every termination needs stress control, and every task needs MV-authorised people. A 5.5 kW pump motor wound for 13.8 kV would be absurd — the insulation would occupy more of the slot than the copper. Utilisation equipment — motors below a few hundred kW, lighting, sockets, control gear — is cheapest, safest and most standardised at 400/230 V.

The transformer is the device that lets the plant have both: transport at 13.8 kV where current is the enemy, use at 400 V where insulation is the enemy. It performs the exchange through nothing but electromagnetic induction: the MV winding magnetises the steel core 60 times a second, and the alternating flux induces a voltage in the LV winding in exact proportion to its turns. No contact between the windings, no moving parts, efficiency around 99 %. It is arguably the most reliable machine humanity has ever mass-produced — and that reliability is precisely why it gets forgotten until the day it fails.

Why two transformers? Because the heart metaphor has a dark side: a single transformer is a single point of failure for the entire plant. A distribution transformer is not a stocked commodity — a failed 1600 kVA cast-resin unit is typically a 4–8 month factory lead item (longer in tight markets). No plant that earns money can accept a credible single failure with a half-year outage attached. The Rev 3 design therefore splits the load across two 1600 kVA units feeding two half-busbars of the LV-MSB, with a normally-open bus-tie. Lose either transformer and the tie closes, the other unit picks up the essential load, and the plant limps but lives. That is the N-1 criterion: the plant must survive the loss of any one major item. The worked calculation below proves the arithmetic.

The transformer exists because power is cheapest to move at high voltage (current down 34.5×, cable loss down ~1 190× at 13.8 kV vs 400 V) and cheapest to use at low voltage. There are two of them because one is a single point of failure with a 4–8 month replacement time. Everything else in this section — sizing, impedance, taps, cooling — is detail in service of those two facts.

A Failure Story: the Summer the Plant Went Dark

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional but assembled from real failure modes. It describes a plant much like ours — same load, same climate — but built to a cheaper single-transformer design. Read it once for the story, then again counting the decisions that were really made years before the failure.

The setup. The plant was built with one 2500 kVA oil-filled transformer feeding a single LV busbar. On paper it was generous: 2353 kVA of demand on a 2500 kVA nameplate, 94 % loading at peak. But the nameplate was an IEC rating at 40 °C maximum ambient, and the transformer bay faces west, unshaded, where August afternoons reach 50 °C. Over the years the plant also accumulated VFDs — about a third of the load now draws distorted, harmonic-rich current that heats the windings beyond what the fundamental amps suggest. Every summer afternoon for nine years, the hottest spot inside the HV winding ran 10–15 K above its design temperature. Insulation ageing is brutally exponential: as a rule of thumb each sustained 6–8 K above rated hot-spot temperature roughly halves insulation life. Nine summers of that arithmetic, silently.

The fault. A Thursday in August, 14:40, peak cooling load. Somewhere in the HV winding, paper insulation that should have lasted thirty years but has aged like ninety finally punctures between adjacent discs. A turn-to-turn fault — small at first, just a shorted loop acting like the secondary of a current transformer, cooking itself with hundreds of amps in a single turn. Gas bubbles through the oil. Within minutes the fault spreads across discs; the Buchholz relay finally trips the MV breaker. The transformer is dead. So, immediately and completely, is the plant: every pump, every compressor, the control room air conditioning, the lot. The standby generator carries the emergency loads only — it was never sized for production.

The aftermath is the real lesson.

Time after tripWhat happensCost driver

Total: a production outage measured in months, triggered by a component whose duplicate would have cost a low single-digit percentage of one month's production value. The post-incident review finds no villain — only a design decision made on a spreadsheet years earlier, where the second transformer looked like the easiest line to delete.

Contrast with the Rev 3 design. Same fault, our plant: TX-1 develops a winding fault and its protection clears it. Half the LV-MSB goes dark. The PMS sheds the non-essential load group, the bus-tie BT closes, and TX-2 picks up the essential ~1.4 MVA at about 87.5 % loading — proven in the worked calculation below. Production runs partial but runs. The failed unit is replaced on a planned programme while the plant earns. The entire difference between the two stories is one purchase order and one busbar partition, decided on day one.

The N-1 story only works if three things are true on the day of the fault: the healthy transformer is genuinely able to carry the essential load at the actual ambient; the bus-tie closes (auto or by drilled manual procedure); and load shedding actually sheds. A bus-tie that has not been exercised in years, or a shedding list that no longer matches the plant, silently converts the two-transformer design back into the single-transformer story above. Verify all three — see the site checklist at the end of this section.

Sixteen Kilowatts of Heat and a Hum at 120 Hz

Feel for the numbers

Before the formal calculation, build honest physical intuition for what a 1600 kVA transformer is: roughly two tonnes of steel and copper, the size of a large wardrobe, silently handling the power of about two thousand homes' worth of kettles. Three estimates anchor the scale.

Estimate 1 — "99 % efficient" still means a roomful of heaters

A good 1600 kVA cast-resin unit is about 99 % efficient at full load. The missing 1 % does not vanish — it appears as heat inside the enclosure:

P_loss ≈ no-load (core) loss + load (copper) loss ≈ 2.5 kW + 13.5 kW ≈ 16 kW

Sixteen kilowatts is about seven domestic kettles boiling continuously, 24 hours a day, inside one steel box. Per day that is:

E = 16 kW × 24 h ≈ 380 kWh/day

Now place that box in a 50 °C Saudi summer. The transformer can only cool itself by being hotter than its surroundings — and its surroundings are already at a temperature most machines consider an alarm condition. This is why ambient temperature is not a footnote in the spec but a headline: IEC ratings assume 40 °C maximum ambient, and every degree above that is subtracted directly from the insulation's thermal budget. Cooling design (room ventilation, fan boost, insulation class margin) is covered under Equipment technology below.

Sanity check: at the normal 73 % loading the copper loss falls with the square of load (13.5 × 0.73² ≈ 7.2 kW), so day-to-day dissipation is nearer 10 kW — still four kettles, still continuous.

Estimate 2 — Inrush: the transformer's one violent moment

Energising a transformer is the only time it behaves badly. At the instant the MV breaker closes, the core can be driven deep into saturation (worst case: switching at voltage zero with unfavourable residual flux), and for a few cycles the magnetising current is enormous:

I_inrush ≈ 8–12 × FLC = 8–12 × 66.9 A ≈ 540–800 A (MV side)

That is fault-sized current — yet it is completely healthy, decaying to the normal magnetising current (about 1–2 % of FLC, roughly 1 A) with a time constant such that the big peaks are over in roughly 0.1 s, with a long harmonic-rich tail. The protection must ride through it: an MV overcurrent element or fuse that trips on inrush makes the transformer impossible to energise. This is exactly why transformer protection curves are checked against the classic "12 × FLC at 0.1 s" inrush point, and why the 87T differential element uses second-harmonic restraint — see Equipment settings below and §10.

Sanity check: 540–800 A is 8–12× the 66.9 A nameplate current but still well below the ~25 kA-class faults the MV protection is set for — there is room to grade between "loud but healthy" and "fault", and the calculation in §10 uses it.

Estimate 3 — The hum is the core breathing at 120 Hz

Stand next to an energised transformer and you hear a low hum. It is not the windings and it is not 60 Hz. The steel core physically changes length very slightly every time it is magnetised — a property called magnetostriction. The core does not care which direction the flux points, only how strongly it is magnetised, so it contracts and relaxes twice per cycle:

f_hum = 2 × f_supply = 2 × 60 Hz = 120 Hz (Saudi grid)

On a 60 Hz system the same machine hums at 100 Hz — a useful party trick: you can tell a grid's frequency by ear. The practical value is diagnostic: the hum's loudness rises steeply with flux density, so a transformer that suddenly hums louder may be seeing overvoltage, a wrong tap position, or DC/geomagnetic bias pushing the core toward saturation. A change in the sound of a machine with no moving parts is always worth investigating.

Finally, hold on to the two currents that define this machine and that you will use constantly: 66.9 A on the 13.8 kV side, 2 309 A on the 400 V side — same power, 34.5× ratio. The MV cable feeding 1.6 MVA is a modest single cable; the LV connection carrying the same power is a busduct or a bundle of single-core cables as thick as your wrist (§ cables). Seeing both sides of one transformer is the fastest way to internalise why MV exists.

Worked Calculation (a): Sizing and Normal Loading

Worked calculation

Three calculations follow, and together they are the transformer's complete identity card: (a) how big it must be and what it carries normally and under N-1; (b) what its impedance means and what it does to fault level and voltage; (c) its full-load currents on both sides. Every step is shown; everything can be redone on a phone calculator.

Drawing basis note. This diagram was drawn for the original 13.8 kV / 400 V / 60 Hz reference design and has not been re-rendered. For this Saudi (SEC) Rev 3 edition read 13.8 kV as 13.8 kV, 400 V as 400 V and 60 Hz as 60 Hz. Topology, tags and protection philosophy are unchanged.

Step a-1 — From plant load to apparent power

The load study gives 2.0 MW of demand at an uncorrected power factor of 0.85. Transformers are rated in kVA, not kW, because the windings carry the full current regardless of power factor:

S = P / pf = 2 000 kW / 0.85 = 2 353 kVA

(After power-factor correction to 0.95 the apparent power drops to about 2 105 kVA, but the transformers must be sized for the worst credible case — PFC out of service — so we size on 2 353 kVA. See § PFC.)

Step a-2 — Choose the unit count and standard size

One 2500 kVA unit would carry the load (2 353 / 2 500 = 94 %) but is the single-point-of-failure design from the failure story. The Rev 3 choice is two units on a split bus. Each must carry half the load normally, with margin:

S_per tx = 2 353 / 2 = 1 176 kVA

The next standard IEC rating above 1 176 kVA is 1600 kVA (standard ladder: …1000, 1250, 1600, 2000, 2500…). 1250 kVA would also "fit" normally (94 % loading) but leaves no margin for growth, no headroom at 50 °C, and a hopeless N-1 case. So: 2 × 1600 kVA.

Loading_normal = 1 176 / 1 600 = 0.735 pu ≈ 73 %

Each transformer runs at about 73 % of nameplate in normal split-bus operation — a comfortable thermal regime that also extends insulation life (losses go as load²: 0.73² ≈ 53 % of full-load copper loss).

Worked Calculation (a, continued): the N-1 Check

Step a-3 — The N-1 check: one transformer lost

Worst case, no load shedding: the surviving unit would see the whole plant:

Loading_N-1,full = 2 353 / 1 600 = 1.47 pu (147 %)

Not acceptable continuously — at 50 °C ambient it would cook the insulation in short order. So the N-1 claim requires automatic load shedding: the PMS trips the pre-agreed non-essential group before or as the bus-tie closes.

With shedding: the essential fraction is about 55 % of plant load:

S_essential ≈ 0.55 × 2 353 ≈ 1 294 kVA

Design practice adds an allowance (≈ 8 %) on top of the studied essential load — for measurement uncertainty, motor restarting current after the transfer, and quiet load growth since the last load study. That sets the N-1 design check point at:

S_N-1,check ≈ 1 294 × 1.08 ≈ 1 400 kVA ≈ 1.4 MVA Loading_N-1 = 1 400 / 1 600 = 0.875 pu = 87.5 %

Margin math: remaining headroom on the surviving unit:

Margin = 1 600 − 1 400 = 200 kVA = 12.5 % of nameplate

87.5 % continuous loading is acceptable — below nameplate, with 200 kVA in hand for the unexpected. If the site later adds essential load, this margin is the first number to re-check. (With the AN/AF fan boost described later, short-term capability rises further — but never size relying on fans for the steady N-1 case.)

Sanity check: 73 % normal / 87.5 % N-1 / 147 % unshed forms a consistent ladder — shedding ~45 % of the load takes the surviving unit from an impossible 147 % to a comfortable 87.5 %. If your own numbers ever show N-1 loading below normal loading, you have made an error.

The sizing logic in one line: 2 353 kVA total → two units → 1 176 kVA each → next standard size 1600 kVA → 73 % normal loading → with shedding to ~1.4 MVA essential, the survivor runs at 87.5 % under N-1, leaving 200 kVA of margin. Every number is auditable on site against nameplates, ammeters and the shedding list.

Worked Calculation (b): What Z = 6 % Really Means

The nameplate says Z = 6 % (more formally uk, the short-circuit impedance voltage). This single number sets the plant's LV fault level, its voltage regulation, and its ability to share load in parallel — it is the most consequential number on the plate after the kVA. First, what it physically means:

The physical meaning. Z = 6 % says: if you short-circuit the LV terminals and slowly raise the MV voltage, full-load current flows when you reach just 6 % of rated MV voltage. That is literally how the factory measures it (the short-circuit test). Equivalently, the transformer behaves like an ideal transformer in series with a small internal impedance that absorbs 6 % of rated voltage at full-load current. The two consequences fall straight out:

  • Fault current: if 6 % of voltage drives 100 % of current, then 100 % of voltage (a bolted fault on the LV terminals with the grid behind) drives 100 / 6 ≈ 16.7 times full-load current.
  • Voltage regulation: at full load, up to ~6 % of the voltage is "used up" inside the transformer — how much actually appears as a drop depends on the power factor, calculated below.

Step b-1 — LV fault level from Z (per-unit method)

Base current is the LV full-load current (derived in part c below):

I_base = 2 309 A,  Z = 0.06 pu I_sc = I_base / Z = 2 309 / 0.06 = 38 480 A ≈ 38.5 kA

This is the fault current for a three-phase bolted fault at the LV terminals fed through one transformer, treating the 13.8 kV source as infinite — a slightly conservative and very useful simplification (including the real SEC source impedance shaves only a few percent off; §4 does the refined version, and § per-unit explains the method). It is why the LV-MSB is specified at 50–65 kA withstand.

Sanity check: 38 480 / 2 309 = 16.7 = 1/0.06. The fault level is exactly "full-load current divided by per-unit impedance" — if your answer is not ~16.7× FLC, recheck.

Tie-closed warning: with the bus-tie closed and both transformers in parallel, both feed the fault: ≈ 2 × 38.5 = 77 kA — above the switchboard rating. This is the calculational reason the tie is normally open (§4).

Step b-2 — Voltage regulation at full load, 0.85 pf

The 6 % impedance is mostly reactance. Split it into resistance and reactance: R comes from the load-loss: Pk ≈ 13.5 kW on 1600 kVA → R ≈ 13.5/1600 ≈ 0.85 %, round to R ≈ 1 %; then:

X = √(Z² − R²) = √(6² − 1²) = √35 ≈ 5.9 %

The standard approximate regulation formula (drop in % of rated voltage at full-load current, lagging pf):

ΔV ≈ R·cos φ + X·sin φ

At pf 0.85: cos φ = 0.85, sin φ = √(1 − 0.85²) = 0.527. Substitute:

ΔV ≈ (1.0 × 0.85) + (5.9 × 0.527) = 0.85 + 3.11 ≈ 3.96 % ≈ ~3.9–4 %

So at full nameplate load and 0.85 pf, the LV busbar sits about 4 % below its no-load value — on a 400 V base, roughly 16 V. Two readings of this number:

  • Why X dominates: the resistive term contributes only 0.85 %; over three-quarters of the drop is the reactive term X·sin φ. This is why power-factor correction (§ PFC) also recovers busbar voltage: at pf 0.95, sin φ = 0.31 and ΔV falls to ≈ 0.85 + 1.83 ≈ 2.7 %.
  • Why taps exist: a ±5 % off-circuit tap range comfortably brackets this ~4 % internal drop plus SEC supply-voltage variation — see Equipment settings.

Sanity check 1: the drop can never exceed Z itself; 3.96 % < 6 % ✓ (the theoretical maximum drop ≈ 6 % occurs at pf = sin φ → X/Z, i.e. pf ≈ 0.17 — almost purely reactive load). Sanity check 2: at unity pf, ΔV ≈ 1.0 × 1 + 5.9 × 0 ≈ 1 % — a resistive load barely dents the voltage, confirming the drop is a reactive phenomenon.

The impedance trade-off in one paragraph. The designer chooses Z. Lower Z (say 4 %): stiffer voltage (ΔV ≈ 2.7 % at 0.85 pf), but fault level jumps to 2 309/0.04 ≈ 58 kA — a more expensive switchboard. Higher Z (say 8 %): fault level drops to ≈ 29 kA (cheaper switchgear), but regulation worsens to ≈ 4 % + and motor-starting dips deepen. Z = 6 % is the classic distribution-class compromise, and both transformers must have equal Z if they are ever to share load in parallel (unequal Z splits load in inverse proportion to impedance).

Worked Calculation (c): Full-Load Current, Both Sides

Step c-1 — MV side (13.8 kV)

I_MV = S / (√3 × U_MV) = 1 600 000 / (1.732 × 13 800) I_MV = 1 600 000 / 23 902 = 66.9 A

This 66.9 A sets the MV cable size, the MV protection pickup (§10) and — multiplied by 8–12 — the inrush window (540–800 A) that the protection must ride through.

Step c-2 — LV side (400 V)

I_LV = S / (√3 × U_LV) = 1 600 000 / (1.732 × 400) I_LV = 1 600 000 / 692.8 = 2 309 A

This 2 309 A sets the LV incomer ACB frame, the LV busduct/cable group (§ cables), and the base current for the 38.5 kA fault calculation above.

Sanity check: the two currents must be in the inverse voltage ratio: 2 309 / 66.9 = 34.5 = 13 800 / 400 ✓. Power in = power out; only the voltage–current exchange rate changes. (At the plant level, both transformers together at full plant load carry 2 353 kVA → 3 396 A total on the LV side, matching the plant data card.)

Taps, Temperatures and the 87T Zone

Equipment settings

A transformer has no trip unit of its own, but four things are set on or for it: the tap position, the temperature thresholds, the fan-control points, and the protection elements that watch over it. All values below are indicative — the coordination study and the manufacturer's test report govern.

1. Off-circuit taps — ±2 × 2.5 %. The MV winding carries five tap positions: nominal, ±2.5 % and ±5 %, changing the effective MV turns and hence the LV voltage. They exist to compensate for two slow, steady offsets: the SEC supply voltage sitting persistently above or below 13.8 kV at this point of the network, and the transformer's own ~4 % full-load internal drop (worked calculation b-2). The logic is inverted from intuition: if the LV bus runs low, you select a minus tap (fewer MV turns → higher LV volts per MV volt).

Off-circuit means dead plant. These are off-circuit tap links, not an on-load tap changer: they may only be moved with the transformer de-energised, isolated and earthed — moving them live causes an internal flashover. Both transformers must always be on the same tap; unequal taps create a circulating current through any closed tie and unequal bus voltages. Change taps only on the evidence of a logged voltage trend over weeks (not one hot afternoon's reading), record the new position on the nameplate card, and expect to do it roughly never after commissioning.

2. Winding temperature — PT100 alarm and trip. Each cast-resin unit carries PT100 resistance sensors embedded in the LV winding ducts (one per limb, often plus a spare), wired to a temperature relay. Typical settings for a class F (155 °C) insulation system, indicative:

StageTypical settingAction
Fan start≈ 100 °CStart AF cooling fans
Alarm≈ 140 °CPMS/DCS alarm — investigate load, fans, ventilation
Trip≈ 150 °CTrip MV feeder breaker — insulation at limit

At 50 °C ambient the margin between "normal hot day" and "alarm" is thinner than the same machine enjoys in Europe — which is exactly why the alarm must go somewhere a human sees it (see Control) and why a fan failure in August is an operational event, not a maintenance note.

3. Fan boost — AN/AF +40 %. Cast-resin units are rated AN (air natural). Adding thermostatically-controlled fans (AF — air forced) typically lifts capability by about 40 %:

S_AF ≈ 1.4 × 1 600 = 2 240 kVA (indicative, per manufacturer test)

Treat the AF rating as contingency capability — riding through the N-1 period, a heatwave, or a short overload — never as the basis for normal sizing. Fans fail, filters clog with dust, and a plant that needs its fans every afternoon has no margin left. The N-1 case above (87.5 %) deliberately fits inside the plain AN rating.

4. Protection elements (set in the relays, not the transformer). The transformer is protected by the MV feeder relay and, classically, a differential element. The concept of 87T transformer differential: measure current into the MV side and out of the LV side, correct for the 34.5:1 ratio and the Dyn11 30° phase shift, and trip if the difference exceeds a restrained threshold — current that goes in and does not come out is feeding an internal fault. It is the only protection that is both fast and precisely bounded to the transformer zone. Two practical notes for a 1600 kVA unit:

  • Inrush discrimination: on energisation the MV side sees 540–800 A with nothing on the LV side — a perfect false differential. Modern 87T relays detect the characteristic second-harmonic content of inrush current and restrain.
  • Is 87T justified at this size? Dedicated differential is standard above roughly 5–10 MVA; at 1600 kVA many designs rely instead on MV overcurrent/earth-fault (graded above inrush and through-fault), LV restricted earth fault, and the temperature trip. The Rev 3 philosophy and the actual relay complement are set in §10 — Protection Coordination; the point here is to understand what each element can and cannot see.

Cast Resin at 50 °C, Dyn11, and the V/f Rule

Equipment technology

Cast-resin dry-type vs oil-filled. The two mainstream technologies at this rating: oil-immersed (ONAN — windings in mineral/ester oil, cooled by natural oil and air circulation) and cast-resin dry-type (windings encapsulated in epoxy, air-cooled, IEC 60076-11). The Rev 3 plant uses cast-resin indoors, and the choice is driven by the environment:

AspectOil ONANCast-resin AN/AF
Fire riskOil is fuel — needs bunding, separation or fire wallsFire-behaviour class F1 (self-extinguishing, low smoke/toxicity) — can sit next to the switchroom
MaintenanceOil sampling, gaskets, breather, BuchholzEssentially clean, dry, inspect & test
50 °C / dustRobust outdoors; oil cooling tolerant of heatNeeds a ventilated, dust-filtered enclosure; resin classes C2/E2/F1 cover condensation, pollution, fire
Overload behaviourLarge thermal mass — forgivingLess thermal mass — fans (AF) buy back capability
Typical sitingOutdoor bays, larger ratingsIndoor substations, ≤ ~3 MVA — the Rev 3 case

The 40 °C problem. IEC 60076 thermal ratings are referenced to a 40 °C maximum ambient (30 °C monthly / 20 °C yearly average). The Rev 3 design ambient is 50 °C. The insulation does not care where the heat comes from — ambient plus temperature rise must stay under the class limit — so a standard unit at 50 °C must either be derated (order of 10 % per 10 K above reference, per manufacturer data) or, better, specified up: a class F (155 °C) insulation system run at a class-B-like rise, leaving the missing 10 K as designed-in margin. Remember the ageing rule of thumb from the failure story — each sustained 6–8 K above rated hot-spot roughly halves insulation life. Specify the ambient explicitly on the data sheet; never let "1600 kVA" silently mean "1600 kVA in Frankfurt". Enclosure ingress protection follows the same climate logic: ventilated indoor enclosures around IP31 with sand-trap/filtered louvres as a minimum, IP54 for anything outdoors per the plant basis — accepting that tighter enclosures trap more heat, which the ventilation design must remove.

Dyn11 — reading the vector group. The code on the plate unpacks as:

  • D — MV winding in delta;
  • yn — LV winding in star ("wye") with the neutral brought out — which is what makes 400/230 V and the TN-S earthing system possible: 400 V between phases for motors, 230 V phase-to-neutral for lighting and small loads;
  • 11 — clock notation: the LV phasor sits at 11 o'clock relative to the MV at 12, i.e. the LV side leads by 30°.

The delta MV winding earns its keep twice. First, it gives the third-harmonic magnetising currents (which are co-phasal in all three phases) a closed loop to circulate in — trapping them inside the delta so they never reach the network, and keeping the LV voltage waveform clean. Second, it isolates zero-sequence: an LV earth fault drives current through the LV neutral and the delta, but produces no zero-sequence current on the MV network — which is precisely why LV and MV earth-fault protections can be set independently (§10). The 30° shift itself matters operationally in one way: never parallel transformers of different vector groups — a Dyn11 and a Dyn1 differ by 60° and would drive a ruinous circulating current through the tie.

K-factor — harmonics from the VFDs. A significant share of the Rev 3 load runs on variable-frequency drives, which draw harmonic-rich current. Winding eddy losses rise with the square of harmonic order, so distorted current heats the windings far more than its RMS amps suggest. Specify the harmonic duty: either a K-rated design (K-4 typical for mixed industrial load, K-13 for drive-dominated MCCs, per the UL/IEEE convention) or the IEC "factor K" calculation to EN 50541/IEC 61378 practice — or, equivalently, an agreed derating of a standard unit. A transformer sized on fundamental amps alone for a drive-heavy plant runs chronically hotter than its loading suggests; the detuned PFC design (§ PFC) addresses the same harmonics on the capacitor side.

50 vs 60 Hz — the V/f rule. Core flux is proportional to V / f (Faraday: the same voltage sustained over a longer cycle drives more flux). The Rev 3 grid is 60 Hz. A transformer designed only for 60 Hz, applied at the same voltage on a 60 Hz system, sees V/f rise by 60/50 = 1.2 — 20 % more core flux, driving the core into saturation: magnetising current soars, the core overheats, the hum turns into a roar, and the unit fails. Never apply a 60 Hz-only unit at 60 Hz. (The reverse — a 60 Hz design at 60 Hz, same voltage — lowers flux and is generally benign, subject to manufacturer confirmation.) This matters in a global spares market: check the nameplate frequency on any borrowed, rented or relocated unit before it is ever energised, and beware dual-rating claims without test evidence.

Watching a Machine with No Moving Parts

Control

A transformer cannot be "controlled" in the usual sense — once energised it simply follows the load. Control therefore means monitoring and automatic response, in four layers:

  • Temperature relay (local). The PT100 sensors land on a dedicated temperature monitoring relay in or near the transformer enclosure: it displays per-limb winding temperature, drives the fan-start / alarm / trip stages set out under Equipment settings, and supervises its own sensor circuits (an open or shorted PT100 raises a discrepancy alarm rather than a false trip).
  • Fan control. The AF fans start automatically from the temperature relay (≈ 100 °C stage), with a hand/off/auto selector for testing and a run-feedback contact to the PMS. In this climate, fan filters and fan-failure alarms deserve the same respect as protection relays — a clogged filter quietly deletes the +40 % contingency margin.
  • PMS load monitoring. The power management system trends each transformer's kVA loading (from the incomer metering), winding temperatures, fan status and tap position record. It is the PMS that holds the N-1 logic: on loss of one transformer it executes the load-shed list and (where so configured) the bus-tie auto-close — the sequence whose arithmetic was proven in worked calculation a-3. Alarms route to the DCS/operator and the maintenance system.
  • Condition monitoring (periodic). For cast-resin units: thermography of terminations, cable boxes and busbar joints under load (resistive hot-spots announce themselves years before they fail); partial-discharge (PD) measurement on the resin insulation at major outages — rising PD is the earliest warning of insulation degradation; insulation-resistance and ratio tests at shutdowns; and the humblest, most valuable check of all: clean, intact ventilation paths and the absence of dust blankets on the windings (a dust layer is a thermal insulation upgrade nobody ordered).
Trend, don't snapshot. A winding temperature of 120 °C means little in isolation; the same reading 15 K higher than the same load and ambient produced last summer means blocked cooling, harmonics growth or sensor drift — all worth finding early. The PMS history is the transformer's medical record; make sure it is actually being recorded.

Sizing Method — Step by Step

Sizing method

The procedure below generalises this section into a checklist you can apply to any distribution transformer purchase or verification. Steps 1–4 set the rating; steps 5–10 set the specification.

  1. Load study. Build the kW/kVA list from the load schedule: connected load × utilisation, at the realistic (uncorrected) power factor. Rev 3: 2.0 MW at 0.85 → 2 353 kVA.
  2. Apply diversity. Not everything runs at once — apply documented diversity/coincidence factors per load group, and resist the habit of stacking worst cases (that is how 2 MW plants buy 5 MVA of transformer).
  3. Set the redundancy policy. Decide N-1 scope explicitly: which loads are essential (Rev 3: ~55 % ≈ 1.3–1.4 MVA with allowance), what is shed, whether the tie closes automatically. This decision, not the load study, fixes the unit count.
  4. Choose the standard size. Per-unit normal loading ≤ ~75–80 % and N-1 loading ≤ ~90 % of nameplate, then round up the standard ladder (1000 / 1250 / 1600 / 2000 / 2500 kVA). Rev 3: 2 × 1600 kVA → 73 % / 87.5 %.
  5. Choose impedance. Z trades fault level against voltage regulation (worked calculation b): check Isc = IFLC/Z against the intended switchboard withstand, and ΔV ≈ R·cosφ + X·sinφ against the voltage budget, including motor starting. Rev 3: 6 % → 38.5 kA and ~4 %.
  6. Correct for cooling and ambient. State the real ambient (50 °C) on the data sheet; derate or upspec insulation class against the IEC 40 °C reference; decide the AN/AF fan policy (contingency only) and the enclosure IP/ventilation scheme for dust.
  7. Fix the vector group and earthing. Dyn11 for a TN-S 400/230 V LV system with the neutral brought out; identical group and Z for any units that may parallel.
  8. Specify taps. Off-circuit ±2 × 2.5 % covers supply variation plus internal drop; on-load tap changers are not justified at this rating.
  9. Address harmonics. Estimate the non-linear (VFD) load share and specify K-factor / factor-K or derating accordingly.
  10. Verify against protection. Confirm the inrush window (8–12 × FLC for ~0.1 s) sits below the MV protection curve and the fault level sits within switchgear ratings and relay reach — close the loop with §4 and §10 before the order is placed.

What to Check on Site — Self-Check

First the field verification items, then conceptual self-check questions. If you can answer the questions without rereading, this section has done its job.

  • Nameplates: confirm kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV ratio, Dyn11, Z %, frequency, tap range and the current tap position — and that both units match.
  • Loading: read incomer ammeters / PMS trend and compare against the 73 % normal and 87.5 % N-1 figures; investigate any drift since the last load study.
  • Bus-tie BT: confirmed normally open, auto-close scheme functional, last test date recorded.
  • Load-shedding list: documented, matches today's plant, relays/PMS logic active and last tested.
  • Cooling: fans run on test, filters clean, ventilation paths clear, no dust blanket on windings; temperature relay reads sensible per-limb values.
  • Self-check — Why does distributing at 13.8 kV cut cable loss by ~1 190× rather than 34.5×? (Because loss goes as current squared.)
  • Self-check — A colleague proposes running with the bus-tie closed "for flexibility". State the two calculations that forbid it. (Fault level ≈ 77 kA vs switchboard rating; and the N-1/selectivity philosophy in §10.)
  • Self-check — From Z = 6 % alone, reconstruct the 38.5 kA fault level and the ~16.7× FLC multiple without notes.
  • Self-check — Why is the voltage drop at 0.85 pf (~4 %) so much worse than at unity pf (~1 %)? (X·sin φ dominates; the impedance is mostly reactance.)
  • Self-check — A rental transformer arrives marked "1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, 60 Hz". May it be energised on the SEC 60 Hz grid? What about a 60 Hz unit on a 60 Hz grid? (50→60 Hz generally benign — flux falls, confirm with maker; 60 Hz-only at 60 Hz: never — V/f up 20 %, core saturates.)
  • Self-check — Why must taps only be changed with the plant dead, and why must both transformers be on the same tap?
Two 1600 kVA hearts, 73 % loaded, each able to carry the essential ~1.4 MVA at 87.5 % when the other dies — but only if the tie closes, the shedding sheds, and the cooling cools at 50 °C. The transformer itself almost never fails unprovoked; what fails is the margin around it. Verify the margin, not just the machine.
