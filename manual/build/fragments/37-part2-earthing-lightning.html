12 — Earthing & Earth-Grid Design Core

Goal: understand the three distinct jobs an earthing system does and why mixing them up causes designs that look safe but are not; verify the MV neutral earthing resistor (MV-NER, 7.97 kV, 400 A / 10 s → 19.9 Ω) from first principles; estimate the resistance of earth rods and grids in Saudi soil conditions; understand touch and step voltage per IEEE 80 at concept level; and follow the full sizing method from soil-resistivity survey to test certificate on the Rev 3 plant basis (SEC 13.8 kV, 60 Hz, 400/230 V TN-S, 50 °C design ambient, dry sandy soil).

Earthing connects to almost every other calculation in this manual: the earth-fault current it permits is set against the relay pickups in §10 — Protection Coordination; the fault magnitudes and durations come from §4 — Short-Circuit Current; the earth-conductor cross-sections use the same adiabatic equation as §6 — Cable Sizing; and the transformer winding connections in §2 — Transformer Sizing decide what kind of neutral exists to be earthed at all. Typical NER hardware is surveyed in the NER & auxiliaries catalogue.

Indicative data. Every numeric value in this section — resistances, soil resistivities, currents, durations, thresholds — is indicative and chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis. The plant's formal earthing study (soil survey, IEEE 80 grid model, protection coordination study) governs. Standards cited are the SASO-adopted editions of IEC 60364 (esp. parts 4-41 and 5-54), IEC 60479 (effects of current on the body) and IEEE 80 (substation grounding), applied together with SBC 401 and the SEC Distribution Code.

One Word, Three Jobs: What Earthing Actually Does

Why this exists

"Earthing" is the most overloaded word in electrical engineering. Engineers, drawings and even standards use the same word for three different jobs that share hardware but have different physics, different design rules and different failure consequences. Most earthing mistakes in real plants start as a confusion between them. Keep them separate in your head from the first day:

Job 1 — Safety (touch-voltage control). Every conductive thing a person can touch — motor frames, switchboard doors, cable trays, handrails near electrical rooms — must stay at (very nearly) the same electrical potential as the ground the person is standing on, even while a fault is flowing. This is protective earthing and equipotential bonding. Its job is not to "send electricity into the ground"; its job is to make sure that when something goes wrong, the voltage between a person's hand and their feet stays below the level that drives a dangerous current through their body. The design quantity is voltage across a human, not ohms to remote earth.

Job 2 — Fault-current return path (so protection can work). When insulation fails and a live conductor touches a frame, a current must flow back to the source neutral — large enough, and through a predictable enough path, that a relay or breaker recognises it and clears it fast. On the LV side this path is the dedicated PE conductor of the TN-S system; on the MV side it is the earth grid and the neutral earthing resistor. Here the design quantities are impedance of the loop and current magnitude versus relay pickup (see §10). A safety earth that cannot carry enough current to trip the protection is a decoration.

Job 3 — Reference / functional earth. Electronics, instrumentation, surge protective devices, screened cables and the lightning-protection system all need a stable zero-volt reference and a low-impedance sink for high-frequency and impulse currents. This job cares about things Jobs 1 and 2 barely notice: conductor inductance, loop area, single-point versus mesh bonding. It is why the lightning section (§16) keeps insisting on one integrated earth system rather than separate "clean" and "dirty" electrodes.

The classic conflation. "We measured 0.8 Ω to earth, so the plant is safe." No. A low electrode resistance helps Job 3 and parts of Job 2, but a person can still be electrocuted beside a 0.5 Ω grid if bonding is missing (Job 1 failure), and a relay can still fail to see a fault on a perfectly bonded plant if the NER is open-circuit (Job 2 failure). Each job must be verified on its own terms: bonding continuity for Job 1, loop impedance and relay reach for Job 2, electrode resistance and bonding topology for Job 3.

On the Rev 3 plant the three jobs are implemented like this. The 400/230 V system is TN-S: the transformer LV neutral is solidly earthed at one point, and a separate PE conductor runs with every circuit, so an LV earth fault is effectively a phase-to-neutral short — big, fast, easily detected. The 13.8 kV system is resistance-earthed through the NER, deliberately limiting an MV earth fault to about 400 A — small enough to limit damage and touch voltage, large enough for relays to see reliably. Both neutrals, all equipment frames, the cable armours, the lightning-protection down-conductors and the instrument earths all land on one buried earth grid under and around the substation. Why each of those choices was made is the rest of this section.

Earthing is three jobs wearing one name: keep touch voltages survivable (bonding), give fault current a path big and predictable enough to trip protection (return path), and give electronics and surges a stable reference (functional earth). Verify each job separately — a good number for one proves nothing about the others.

A Failure Story: the Broken Green-and-Yellow Wire

What goes wrong without it

The following narrative is fictional but assembled from real incident reports; this exact sequence has killed people in plants on every continent. Read it once for the story, then again following the physics.

A 30 kW cooling-water pump sits on a concrete plinth at the edge of the process area. Years of vibration, one careless forklift and a corroded gland plate have done their work: the PE conductor in the supply cable is broken inside the gland, and the bonding strap from the motor frame to the local earth bar was removed during a bearing change and never refitted. Nothing trips, nothing alarms — a broken earth is invisible in normal operation, because the earth conductor carries no current until the day it is needed. The pump runs perfectly for months.

Then the winding insulation, cooked by years of 50 °C ambient plus load heating, finally cracks where a phase conductor leaves the slot. The phase touches the frame. In a healthy installation this is a non-event: the TN-S earth-fault loop returns the current to the transformer neutral, thousands of amps flow, and the feeder breaker clears it in a tenth of a second (§4, §10). But this frame has no return path. Almost no current flows — and so no protection operates. The motor keeps running. The frame, the pump casing, the discharge pipework and the steel baseplate quietly rise to nearly full phase voltage: 230 V above the ground a person stands on. The plant has manufactured an electrified object and left it in a walkway.

A technician arrives to log the discharge pressure. He puts one hand on the pump casing to steady himself. Now do the arithmetic his body does involuntarily. Hand to feet, through the chest: the body presents very roughly 1 kΩ (IEC 60479 gives 1000–1500 Ω for most people at 230 V, hand to feet, dry; less if sweating — and in a Saudi summer everyone is sweating).

The current through the technician

I_body = U_touch / R_body = 230 V / 1000 Ω = 0.23 A = 230 mA

Compare against the IEC 60479 physiological landmarks, in plain words:

• ~1 mA — perception; a tingle.
• ~10 mA — muscles clamp; many people cannot let go of a gripped conductor.
• ~30 mA — breathing muscles affected; this is the conventional safety threshold and the rating of an RCD protecting people.
• ~50 mA and above (for more than a heartbeat or two) — ventricular fibrillation territory: the heart's pacing signals are scrambled, it quivers instead of pumping, and without defibrillation death follows in minutes.

230 mA is four to five times the fibrillation threshold. The grip reflex means he cannot release the casing. Duration does the rest: IEC 60479's curves show that what a heart survives for 50 ms can kill at 1 s. There is no breaker in this story — nothing is measuring the current through a man.

Now rewind and fix only the earthing. With the PE conductor intact, the instant the insulation failed, roughly 3–6 kA of earth-fault current would have returned through the PE to the neutral; the feeder MCCB would have cleared in well under 0.1 s; the frame would have risen above true earth only briefly, and the bonding would have held the frame and the surrounding steel at nearly the same potential anyway — small touch voltage, short duration, both inside the tolerable envelope. The technician would have found a tripped breaker and written a work order. That is the entire difference earthing makes: a fatality becomes a maintenance item.

The same story has an MV variant. If the NER connection corrodes open and an MV cable fault occurs, the 13.8 kV system becomes unearthed: the earth-fault relays see (almost) nothing, the fault arcs and restrikes, healthy-phase voltages rise toward line-to-line values stressing every metre of cable insulation in the plant, and the fault burns until it becomes a phase-to-phase short. That is why NER continuity monitoring exists.

The cruel asymmetry of earthing faults. Every other protection defect announces itself eventually through nuisance trips or alarms. A broken earth announces itself only at the exact moment it is needed, through the body of whoever is touching the equipment. This is why earthing is verified by test and inspection on a schedule, never by "it has been fine so far".

Feel for the Numbers: 50 V, 32 MJ, and Saudi Sand

Feel for the numbers

Why 50 V is the magic touch-voltage number. Take the two physiological numbers from the failure story — body resistance roughly 1 kΩ, dangerous current starting around 50 mA — and multiply:

The origin of the 50 V limit

U_limit ≈ R_body × I_threshold = 1000 Ω × 0.05 A = 50 V

That is the entire derivation. The conventional touch-voltage limit of 50 V AC (IEC 60364-4-41) is simply the voltage that drives roughly the fibrillation-threshold current through a typical body. It is reduced to 25 V for wet or conductive locations because wet skin halves the resistance, and it can be relaxed upward when the exposure is guaranteed brief — which is why IEEE 80 tolerable touch voltages depend on fault clearing time (worked calculation (c) below). 50 V indefinitely, more only if it is gone quickly: that one sentence summarises both standards.

How much energy the NER swallows. The NER's job is to stand between 7.97 kV and earth and dissipate the full limited fault current for the rated 10 s without melting. Feel the size of that duty:

NER thermal duty — honest comparison

E = I² × R × t = 400² × 19.9 × 10 ≈ 31.8 MJ ≈ 32 MJ

32 MJ is about 7 kg of TNT equivalent (TNT releases ≈4.6 MJ/kg) — but be honest about the comparison: TNT releases its energy in microseconds as a blast wave, while the NER releases the same energy in 10 s as heat in a steel-grid resistor stack roughly the size of a wardrobe. Nothing explodes — instead about three tonnes of resistor elements rise several hundred kelvin. The same energy would take a 3 kW domestic kettle nearly three hours to deliver; the NER absorbs it in ten seconds. That is why an NER is a serious piece of plant with mass and ventilation, not a component in a box — and why its time rating (10 s) matters as much as its ohms: the protection must clear the fault well inside that window (§10).

Saudi sand: the core local problem. Earth electrodes work by pushing current into the surrounding soil, so soil resistivity ρ sets everything. Feel the range:

Soil (indicative)Resistivity ρRelative difficulty
Wet loam / clay (N. Europe)~100 Ω·m1× (easy)
Moist sandy soil~300 Ω·m3×
Dry desert sand / gravel (typical KSA site)1 000 Ω·m and up10× and worse
Rock / dry wadi gravel3 000+ Ω·m30×+

Electrode resistance scales almost linearly with ρ, so the same rod that gives 6.5 Ω in Dutch clay gives 65 Ω in moist Saudi sand and several hundred ohms in dry dune sand. Worse, the top metre of desert soil is bone dry and its resistivity swings wildly between a rare rainy week and August. This is the central fact of earthing design in the Kingdom: you cannot buy your way to a low-resistance earth with one heroic rod. You get there with area (a buried grid), depth (rods or drilled earth wells reaching the permanently damp strata, often 10–30 m down), and chemistry (bentonite or conductive backfill around each electrode). The worked calculation below shows why with numbers.

One more intuition: ohms are not the goal. A common spec says "earth grid ≤ 1 Ω" — useful as a benchmark, but remember what actually protects people is touch voltage versus clearing time. A 5 Ω grid on a 400 A resistance-earthed system produces a 2 kV ground-potential rise; whether that is dangerous depends entirely on how the grid spreads that potential under people's feet and how fast the relays clear. Chasing ohms while ignoring grid geometry is Job-3 thinking applied to a Job-1 problem.

Three numbers to carry: 50 V (≈1 kΩ body × 50 mA — the conventional touch limit), 32 MJ (the NER's 10 s thermal duty — 7 kg-of-TNT heat released slowly in a wardrobe-sized resistor), and 1 000 Ω·m (dry Saudi sand, ten times worse than the wet loam most textbooks quietly assume).

Worked Calculation: NER, Earth Rod & Grid, Touch Voltage

Worked calculation

Three linked calculations, in the order a designer meets them: (a) size the neutral earthing resistor, (b) estimate electrode resistance and discover why one rod is never enough, (c) check the result against people, IEEE 80 style.

Worked example (a) — Sizing the MV neutral earthing resistor

Step 1 — the voltage the NER sees. During a solid earth fault on one phase, the transformer neutral is driven to (nearly) full phase-to-neutral voltage with respect to earth, and that voltage appears across the NER:

V_ln = V_LL / √3 = 13 800 / 1.732 = 7 967 V ≈ 7.97 kV

Step 2 — choose the limited current. The Rev 3 design limits MV earth-fault current to 400 A (a common choice for industrial 13.8 kV systems; SEC practice and the plant earthing study govern). Why limit at all, and why not lower?

• Damage: burn damage at the fault point scales with I²t — cutting a prospective several-kA earth fault to 400 A cuts core-burning damage in a motor or cable by orders of magnitude.
• Touch voltage: ground-potential rise = I_f × R_grid, so less current means proportionally less voltage under people's feet (worked example (c)).
• But not too low: the earth-fault relays need a signal that towers over CT errors, cable charging current and harmonics. With pickups of 40–80 A (10–20 % of the limit — see §10), 400 A gives a 5–10× margin between pickup and available current, so even a high-resistance fault at the far end of a feeder is seen.

Step 3 — the resistance.

R_NER = V_ln / I_ef = 7 967 / 400 = 19.9 Ω

Step 4 — the thermal (time) rating. The NER must survive full current for longer than the slowest earth-fault clearing stage. With MV earth-fault grading clearing in ≤1–2 s and a margin for breaker failure scenarios, the standard short-time rating is 10 s:

E = I² R t = 400² × 19.9 × 10 = 3.18×10&sup7; J ≈ 32 MJ P_10s = I² R = 400² × 19.9 = 3.18 MW (for 10 s)

Result — NER specification: 7.97 kV, 400 A, 10 s, 19.9 Ω (continuous current rating typically ~8 % of rated, i.e. ~32 A, for standing unbalance).

Sanity check: 7 967 × 400 / 1 000 = 3 187 kW ≈ 3.2 MW — consistent with the energy figure (3.2 MW × 10 s = 32 MJ). And 19.9 Ω is the same order as the seed design's 21 Ω at 11 kV / 300 A — resistance-earthed MV systems worldwide land in the tens of ohms. Values are indicative; the protection coordination study fixes the final figure.

Why the LV side gets no NER. The 400 V TN-S system is solidly earthed: an LV earth fault must look like a phase-to-neutral short (tens of kA) so that ordinary MCCBs clear it within their tripping curves — there are no dedicated sensitive earth-fault relays on every final circuit. At 230 V to earth, arc energy and touch-voltage rise are inherently bounded, so the MV trade-off (limit current to limit damage) is not worth losing the fast, simple overcurrent clearing. Different voltage, different trade-off, opposite answer — details under Equipment settings.

Worked example (b) — One earth rod in Saudi soil, and why it is never enough

The classical estimate for a single driven rod (length L, radius a, soil resistivity ρ) is:

R_rod ≈ ( ρ / (2 π L) ) × ( ln(4L / a) − 1 )

Take a standard 3 m × 19 mm copper-bonded rod (a = 0.0095 m) in moist sandy soil, ρ = 200 Ω·m — an optimistic Saudi case:

4L / a = (4 × 3) / 0.0095 = 1 263    ln(1 263) = 7.14 R_rod = ( 200 / (2 × 3.1416 × 3) ) × (7.14 − 1) = (200 / 18.85) × 6.14 = 10.6 × 6.14 ≈ 65 Ω

One rod: ~65 Ω. In dry sand at 1 000 Ω·m the same rod gives ~330 Ω. Against a target of a few ohms, a single rod is off by one to two orders of magnitude — one rod is never enough.

Sanity check: the formula says resistance is dominated by ρ/(2πL) — the soil within the first metre or two around the rod. Doubling rod length does not halve resistance (the log term grows too); and ten rods do not give one tenth, because closely spaced rods share the same hemisphere of soil (mutual resistance — effective only when spaced >1 rod-length apart).

How a real grid gets there (indicative):

• A buried mesh grid (e.g. 70/120 mm² bare copper at 0.5–0.8 m depth, 3–7 m mesh) under the substation: for a grid of area A, the driving term becomes R ≈ ρ/4 × √(π/A) — area, not length, does the work. A 30 m × 20 m grid (A = 600 m²) in 200 Ω·m soil gives R ≈ (200/4)×√(3.1416/600) = 50 × 0.072 ≈ 3.6 Ω before rods are even counted.
• Deep rods or drilled earth wells (10–30 m) reaching permanently damp strata — in desert sites these often contribute more than the whole surface grid, and they make the result stable across seasons.
• Soil treatment — bentonite or carbon-based conductive backfill in each rod hole and trench, dropping local contact resistance several-fold and buffering moisture.

Grid + wells + treatment in parallel plausibly reaches 1–3 Ω on a sandy site — confirmed only by measurement after installation.

Worked example (c) — Touch and step voltage, IEEE 80 at concept level

Step 1 — ground-potential rise (GPR). When the limited MV earth fault flows back through the grid, the whole grid rises above remote earth:

GPR = I_f × R_grid = 400 A × 2.5 Ω = 1 000 V

(using an achieved grid resistance of 2.5 Ω). One kilovolt under the plant — but GPR is not what a person experiences. A person bridges only the difference between two points: hand-to-feet (touch voltage, a fraction of GPR set by how fine the mesh is and whether the touched object is bonded) or foot-to-foot (step voltage, the gradient across a 1 m stride). A well-designed mesh keeps touch voltage to roughly 10–30 % of GPR — say ~200–300 V here.

Step 2 — what a person can tolerate (IEEE 80 body model). For a 50 kg person, tolerable touch voltage with fault duration t_s and a crushed-rock surface layer (resistivity ρ_s, derating factor C_s) is:

E_touch50 = ( 1000 + 1.5 × C_s × ρ_s ) × 0.116 / √t_s

With 100 mm of 3 000 Ω·m crushed rock (C_s ≈ 0.7) and an MV earth-fault clearing time t_s = 0.5 s:

E_touch50 = (1000 + 1.5 × 0.7 × 3000) × 0.116 / √0.5 = 4 150 × 0.164 ≈ 680 V

Step 3 — compare. Expected touch voltage ~200–300 V < tolerable ~680 V → pass, with margin. Note the three levers visible in the formula: less fault current (the NER), faster clearing (the relays, §10), and the humble gravel layer — the 3 150 V·A term from crushed rock more than quadruples what a person can tolerate, which is why substations are floored with gravel, not for drainage.

Sanity check: with no rock layer and a 1 s clearing time, E_touch50 = 1000 × 0.116 / 1 = 116 V — the same order as the 50 V conventional limit (stricter because indefinite exposure). The formula degrades gracefully toward the number we already trust. Real designs model the actual mesh (IEEE 80 full method, usually in software); this concept-level check tells you whether the answer is plausible.

Earth-Conductor Sizing (Adiabatic) — Carried Over from the Seed Assessment

The conductors that carry the fault current must survive it. The same adiabatic equation used for cable screens in §6 applies (IEC 60364-5-54):

Worked example — Minimum LV earth-conductor cross-section

A = I × √t / k

I = 38 500 A (LV fault from §4); t = 0.3 s (LV incomer short-time delay, §14); k = 143 (copper, XLPE/PVC per IEC 60364):

A = 38 500 × √0.3 / 143 = 38 500 × 0.548 / 143 ≈ 147 mm² Cu

Main earthing conductors at the LV-MSB must therefore be ≥ 150 mm² Cu (next standard size; or aluminium equivalent). On the MV side the duty is far gentler — 400 A for ≤10 s needs only A = 400 × √10 / 143 ≈ 9 mm² — but mechanical robustness and corrosion, not heat, govern: buried grid conductor is typically 70–120 mm² regardless. Sanity check: the LV case is current-driven (huge I, short t), the MV case is longevity-driven; both land in the same 120–150 mm² band for opposite reasons.

Equipment Settings: Earth-Fault Pickups, NER Monitoring, and the TN-S Link

Equipment settings

The earthing system has no settings of its own — it is copper and soil. What gets set is the protection that uses it, and the settings only make sense as fractions of the current the NER allows.

Relay / device (indicative)SettingRationale vs NER 400 A
MV feeder earth-fault (51N/51G, core-balance CT)Pickup 40 A (10 %), t ≈ 0.2–0.4 sWell below 400 A so resistive/far faults are seen; above CT error and cable charging current.
Incomer / transformer standby earth fault (51G in NER circuit)Pickup 60–80 A (15–20 %), graded slowerBacks up every feeder; must clear well inside the NER 10 s rating.
NER thermal protectionAlarm/trip per NER 10 s curveIf grading fails, the NER must not be the fuse.
LV feeder/incomer earth fault (MCCB/ACB ground-fault element)Per LV coordination studyTN-S solid earthing: faults are kA-scale; ordinary overcurrent elements clear them — see §10.

Two grading rules follow directly from the NER rating. First, every earth-fault stage, including breaker-failure backup, must clear in a fraction of 10 s — the resistor's survival is part of the coordination study. Second, the sum of pickup plus margin must stay far below 400 A: if a designer "improves security" by raising pickups to 200 A, a 2 000 Ω high-resistance fault (7 967 / (2 000 + 19.9) ≈ 3.9 A) was always invisible, but now even a 30 Ω fault path (≈160 A) hides too. Sensitivity is the whole point of resistance earthing.

NER monitoring is a setting that saves the scheme. Everything above assumes the NER is actually connected. An open-circuit NER (corroded bolted joint, cracked element, stolen copper tail — all real) silently converts the plant to an unearthed system: relays blind, healthy phases riding at line-to-line voltage during any fault. Fit and commission a neutral-circuit continuity monitor (typically a small injection device across the NER) with its alarm mapped to a permanently watched DCS/PMS point — details under Control and the NER catalogue.

Why MV is resistance-earthed but LV is solidly earthed — the same logic from both sides. At 13.8 kV, uncontrolled earth-fault current would be huge and destructive, touch voltages scale with it, and dedicated sensitive relays are affordable per feeder → limit the current, detect it with low-set relays. At 400 V, the priority reverses: hundreds of final circuits cannot each carry a sensitive earth-fault relay, so faults must be big enough for ordinary breakers to clear within the IEC 60364 disconnection times (0.4 s for 230 V final circuits) → solid earthing, TN-S, with the PE separated from N so protective and functional earths stay clean. One philosophy — "make the fault current match the protection you can afford" — two opposite implementations.

Equipment Technology: What the Hardware Actually Is

Equipment technology

The NER itself. A modern NER is a stack of punched stainless-steel grid elements (AISI 304/316 — chosen because its resistivity is high, stable with temperature, and corrosion-proof) bolted into open frames inside a ventilated outdoor enclosure (typically IP23 — it must breathe to shed 3.2 MW for 10 s; sand screens, not sealed doors). Mounted next to the transformer or as a free-standing unit, with: an MV bushing from the transformer neutral, a single-core neutral CT for the standby earth-fault relay, a bolted link for testing, and — strongly recommended — the continuity monitor. Old designs used liquid (brine) earthing resistors; they are obsolete precisely because evaporation in hot climates silently changed the resistance. Indicative product families in the NER & auxiliaries catalogue.

Electrodes: rods, strips, wells. The buried grid is bare stranded copper strip/cable; vertical electrodes are copper-bonded steel rods (steel core for driving strength, ≥250 μm molecularly bonded copper for corrosion life). Plain galvanized steel rods are cheaper but in aggressive soil — saline sabkha ground near the coasts, chemically treated backfill, chloride-rich desert soils — the zinc sacrifices itself in a few years and the rod follows; copper-bonded (or solid copper in the worst soils) is the default here. Never mix bare copper and galvanized steel in the same wet soil volume without thought: they form a galvanic couple and the steel becomes the anode.

Joints: exothermic welds vs clamps. Buried joints are made by exothermic welding (graphite mould, copper-oxide/aluminium charge — a molecular weld with the conductivity of the parent copper, zero contact resistance to drift, maintenance-free for the life of the plant). Bolted clamps are acceptable only where a joint must be openable — test links, equipment tails — and every buried clamp is a future corrosion failure waiting for its inspection pit. Rule of thumb: below grade, weld; above grade, bolt — and make it inspectable.

Equipotential bonding network. Main earthing terminal (MET) in the substation; main bonding conductors to switchgear frames, transformer tanks, the NER, cable trays, building steel and incoming metallic services; the LV PE bar bonded to the MET; instrument/functional earth bars bonded to the same MET (single system — see §16 for why separate "clean earths" are a myth that lightning punishes). Flexible braids across gasketed joints, hinged doors and pipe flanges complete the mesh.

Test pits. Concrete inspection pits over selected rod tops and grid corners, each with a disconnectable test link, so the buried system can be measured and inspected for the next forty years without excavation. They cost almost nothing on day one and are nearly impossible to retrofit honestly.

Control: Monitoring a System That Does Nothing All Day

Control

An earthing system is passive — there is nothing to operate. Control therefore means surveillance: proving, continuously or on a schedule, that the system is still there.

Continuous (to DCS/PMS): NER open-circuit / continuity monitor alarm (the single most important point — an open NER is a blind protection system); NER over-temperature alarm where fitted; standby earth-fault relay pickup alarm (a standing alarm at a few amps means a developing insulation problem or standing unbalance — investigate, don't normalise); SPD and arrester status contacts (shared with §16).

Sizing Method: From Soil Survey to Test Certificate

Sizing method

The full procedure, in the order the data dependencies force. Each step feeds the next; skipping step 1 invalidates everything after it.

Step 1 — Soil resistivity survey (Wenner four-pin method). Four equally spaced pins, spacing a; inject current through the outer pair, measure voltage across the inner pair; then ρ = 2πaR. Repeat at several spacings (a ≈ depth probed: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16 m) and along two perpendicular traverses to reveal layering — desert sites typically show a very resistive dry top layer over a damper, kinder stratum, which is exactly what decides between "more grid" and "deep wells". Survey in the dry season; record the date and recent rainfall.

Step 2 — Fault current and duration. From §4 and §10: MV earth-fault current (the NER limit, 400 A, plus any SEC-side contribution per the utility study) and the slowest realistic clearing time (use the backup stage, not the optimistic primary one).

Step 3 — Tolerable touch/step limits. IEEE 80 body model with the clearing time from step 2 and the surface layer you intend to install (worked example (c)).

Step 4 — Lay out the grid, rods and wells. Mesh under the substation and around equipment (denser mesh at the perimeter where gradients peak), rods at corners, deep wells per the layering found in step 1, conductor sized per the adiabatic check (above) and corrosion allowance.

Step 5 — Compute R_grid, GPR, mesh and step voltages. Concept formulas for feasibility (as in worked example (b)); IEEE 80 full method or software for the record copy. Iterate the layout until touch/step pass with margin in the worst-season ρ.

Step 6 — Specify the NER: voltage 7.97 kV, current 400 A, time 10 s, resistance 19.9 Ω, continuous current, enclosure IP, CT and monitor — worked example (a).

Step 7 — Verify relay coordination: pickups 10–20 % of the NER limit, every stage clearing inside the NER time rating, sensitivity to a defined high-resistance fault — close the loop with §10.

Step 8 — Test after installation, retest periodically. Fall-of-potential measurement of the completed grid before energisation (the design is a prediction; the certificate is the fact), bonding continuity sample, results into the earth register — then the periodic schedule under Control forever after.

  • Estimate a single 3 m rod's resistance in 200 Ω·m soil (~65 Ω) and explain the three measures (grid area, deep wells, soil treatment) that get a Saudi site to a few ohms.
  • Explain GPR vs touch voltage vs step voltage, and why a crushed-rock layer raises the tolerable touch voltage.
  • State typical MV earth-fault relay pickups as a fraction of the NER rating (10–20 % → 40–80 A) and why an open-circuit NER must be monitored continuously.
  • Explain why the LV neutral is solidly earthed (TN-S) while the MV neutral is resistance-earthed.
  • List the eight sizing-method steps in order, starting from the Wenner survey and ending with periodic retest.
The earthing system is sized backwards from people and relays: tolerable touch voltage and relay sensitivity fix the allowable fault current (the NER's 19.9 Ω), the soil survey fixes how much copper and depth it takes to spread that current safely, and the test certificate — renewed on schedule — is the only proof the buried system still exists. Verify with measurements, not drawings.

16 — Lightning & Surge Protection Core

Goal: understand why microsecond-scale overvoltages — lightning and switching surges — are a different enemy from everything else in Part 2; run the IEC 62305 risk method at concept level to decide whether the plant needs an external lightning-protection system; select and check a coordinated SPD cascade (Type 1/2/3) against equipment withstand; and decode surge-arrester ratings at the 13.8 kV intake — all on the Rev 3 plant basis (SEC 13.8 kV, 60 Hz, 17.5 kV-class switchgear, 400/230 V TN-S, desert environment).

This section leans on its neighbours: the insulation withstand it defends is the switchgear class data in §4; the earth grid every surge current ultimately returns through is designed in §12; and protection devices appear in the relay catalogue and NER & auxiliaries catalogue.

Indicative data. All numbers — flash densities, stroke parameters, withstand levels, SPD ratings — are indicative teaching values. Standards cited are the SASO-adopted editions of IEC 62305 (lightning protection), IEC 60099-4/-5 (surge arresters), IEC 61643 (SPDs) and IEC 60071 / IEC 62271 (insulation coordination), with SBC 401 and SEC requirements.

A Different Enemy: Microseconds Against Insulation

Why this exists

Everything else in Part 2 defends the plant against too much current at normal voltage. This section defends it against too much voltage for almost no time. Insulation — cable XLPE, switchgear clearances, motor winding enamel, the micrometres of oxide inside every electronic device — is a dam built for a known water level. A surge is a wave that overtops the dam for a few microseconds. The dam does not care how brief the wave was: once insulation punctures, the 60 Hz system voltage follows the carbonised track through the hole, and a microsecond event becomes a power-frequency fault with all the consequences of §4.

The overvoltages come from two directions. From the sky: a lightning flash to the plant, to the incoming SEC overhead line, or merely to ground nearby — the last injecting surges by induction into any loop of conductor, no direct hit required. From inside: switching surges — every vacuum breaker chop, capacitor-bank energisation or transformer de-energisation launches a transient of up to a few times system voltage. Switching surges are smaller but happen daily; lightning is rarer but can exceed the withstand of everything in the plant at once.

The Saudi reality deserves honesty. The Kingdom's isokeraunic level is low to moderate — many central-desert areas see only a handful of thunderstorm days a year, and ground-flash densities are a fraction of the tropics'. But "rare" is not "never": the south-west highlands (Asir) and Gulf-coast convection produce genuinely severe storms, and a plant designed for forty years will meet its storm. Two local additions: violent dust and sand storms generate static charge — blowing sand tribocharges pipework, tank roofs and antenna masts to tens of kilovolts, producing sparks and damaging electronics through the same coupling paths as lightning (and demanding the same bonding discipline); and the desert's dry, high-resistivity soil (§12) makes every lightning earth termination work harder. So the design answer here is rarely "maximum protection everywhere"; it is a risk calculation — which is exactly what IEC 62305-2 provides, worked below.

Surge protection exists because insulation has a voltage ceiling and the world — sky, switchgear, even blowing sand — occasionally throws voltages over it for microseconds. The defence is layered: catch the flash (air terminals), carry it to soil (down-conductors + the §12 earth grid), and clamp what leaks inside (arresters and a coordinated SPD cascade), with the protection level chosen by calculated risk, not folklore.

A Failure Story: the Strike That Never Touched the Plant

What goes wrong without it

Fictional, assembled from real loss reports. An unprotected sister plant — same equipment, no LPS, no SPDs, "it never storms here" — on a spring afternoon when a convective cell drifts off the escarpment.

The flash does not hit the plant. It hits the ground about 200 m away, near the incoming SEC 13.8 kV overhead line. Three things happen in the first 100 microseconds. First, a slice of the stroke current couples onto the line — resistively through the soil and inductively into the line loop — and a travelling wave of several tens of kilovolts runs both ways along the conductors. Second, the ground-potential wave radiates outward through the soil and arrives under the plant earth grid. Third, every long metallic loop in the plant — power cables, instrument multicores, the loop formed by a cable tray and the earth conductor below it — has a voltage induced in it proportional to the loop area and the current's rate of rise (~30 kA in a few microseconds → kilovolts per square metre of loop).

The travelling wave reaches the plant's MV intake. There is no surge arrester. The wave meets the transformer — a near-open-circuit at surge frequencies — and nearly doubles on reflection: the 17.5 kV-class switchgear and cable terminations, with a lightning impulse withstand (BIL) of 95 kV, see a front in the same neighbourhood. Insulation that has spent fifteen summers at 50 °C does not enjoy its margins anymore. A cable termination in the MV switchgear punctures — silently, a pinhole in the stress cone. The 60 Hz voltage finds the track. Two cycles later it is a phase-to-earth arc, then phase-to-phase; the upstream protection clears it, but the switchgear panel is burned and the plant is dark.

Meanwhile, downstream, the induced surges that rode the LV and signal cables have done quieter work. The UPS rectifier front-end, the DCS analogue input cards fed by long field multicores, two VFDs and the plant's telephone exchange all absorbed transients of a few kilovolts — some died instantly, others were merely wounded (punched-through junctions that will fail over the next months as infant mortality in reverse). The insurance report later lists the switchgear repair as the headline, but the longer outage driver was recommissioning the control system.

Now replay it with the defences in place. Arresters at the MV intake clamp the incoming wave to ~45 kV residual — half the 95 kV BIL, switchgear unhurt. The bonded earth grid keeps the whole plant's steel rising and falling together as the ground wave passes — no potential differences, no flashovers between systems. Type 1 SPDs at the LV intake dump the through-coupled energy; Type 2 at the MCC and Type 3 at the electronics shave what is left to below 1.5 kV; screened, properly-bonded instrument cables keep induction out of the DCS. Total cost of the event: a few SPD status flags and one arrester counter increment. The flash never needs to touch the plant to destroy it — and the defence never needs drama to work.

The most common real-world failure is half-done protection: an LPS on the building but no SPDs (the strike is caught, then re-enters through the cables), or SPDs installed on a poor earth with long leads (the surge bypasses the protector because the lead inductance adds kilovolts). Lightning protection is a system — interception, conduction, earthing, bonding, clamping — and is only as good as its least-considered element.

Feel for the Numbers: Enormous Power, Tiny Energy

Feel for the numbers

A median negative lightning stroke delivers roughly 30 kA, rising in 1–10 μs, decaying within some hundreds of microseconds; severe positive flashes reach 100–200 kA. The channel briefly carries gigawatt-scale power — hotter than the sun's surface, hence thunder. The whole flash transfers some tens of coulombs and on the order of hundreds of megajoules to a few gigajoules in total — comparable to the NER's 32 MJ duty (§12) only it arrives a million times faster.

So why does a fist-sized SPD survive what melts a tree? Because energy is power × time, and the time is microseconds — and because the SPD never takes the full event. Feel it with one rough sum:

Energy actually deposited in an SPD (order of magnitude)

Suppose a Type 1 SPD diverts a 25 kA (10/350 μs) partial stroke current while clamping at roughly 2 kV. Effective duration of a 10/350 wave ≈ 350 μs:

E ≈ U_clamp × I × t = 2 000 V × 25 000 A × 0.000 350 s ≈ 17 500 J ≈ 18 kJ

18 kJ is the energy in a quarter of a cup of hot coffee — easily absorbed by a few hundred grams of metal-oxide varistor. The trick is the SPD's low clamping voltage: it takes huge current at small voltage, so the power and energy land in the soil and the source impedance, not in the device. A tree, by contrast, presents kilohms: the same current at megavolt drop deposits the megajoules in the wood, which explodes as steam. Sanity check: energy scales with time — this is also why a device rated for microsecond surges is instantly destroyed by sustained 60 Hz overvoltage (TOV), the failure mode behind most real SPD deaths.

The withstand side of the duel. Insulation ratings to remember (IEC 60071 / IEC 62271, indicative): the plant's 17.5 kV-class MV switchgear and cables have a lightning impulse withstand (BIL) of 95 kV (1.2/50 μs wave) and 38 kV power-frequency withstand. LV equipment per IEC 60664 impulse categories at 230/400 V: distribution boards 4 kV (category III), ordinary appliances 2.5 kV (category II), sensitive electronics 1.5 kV (category I). These numbers are the targets every arrester and SPD must clamp below — they are the entire design in five figures.

And the odds. Ground-flash density N_g in much of central Saudi Arabia is roughly 0.5–2 flashes/km²·year (versus 10+ in the tropics). A plant-sized footprint therefore collects a direct flash perhaps once a century — the worked risk calculation below makes this precise. The point of the calculation is to replace both kinds of folklore ("never storms here" and "must have LPL I everywhere") with an expected-events number you can defend.

Lightning is gigawatts for microseconds: total energy comparable to quantities the plant already handles, but compressed a million-fold in time. Protection works by voltage division, not absorption — clamp at kilovolts so the megajoules dissipate in the soil — and the pass/fail line is simply clamping voltage versus withstand: 95 kV BIL at MV, 4 / 2.5 / 1.5 kV categories at LV.

Worked Calculation: Strike Frequency and the SPD Cascade

Worked calculation

Two calculations carry this whole topic: (a) how often will lightning find the plant (IEC 62305-2 risk method, concept level), and (b) does the SPD cascade clamp below what the equipment withstands.

Worked example (a) — Expected direct flashes, N_d (IEC 62305-2 concept level)

Step 1 — Collection area A_d. A structure attracts flashes that would otherwise strike the ground within roughly three times its height. For a block L × W × H, IEC 62305 uses:

A_d = L·W + 2·(3H)·(L + W) + π·(3H)²

Take the plant's main building: L = 60 m, W = 30 m, H = 8 m → 3H = 24 m:

A_d = 60×30 + 2×24×(60+30) + 3.1416×24² = 1 800 + 4 320 + 1 810 = 7 930 m²

Step 2 — Ground flash density N_g. For this inland site take N_g = 1.0 flash/km²·year (indicative; use mapped/SEC data — Asir or coastal sites can be several times higher).

Step 3 — Expected direct strikes per year:

N_d = N_g × A_d × 10⁻⁶ = 1.0 × 7 930 × 10⁻⁶ = 7.9×10⁻³ per year

That is one direct flash about every 126 years. Sanity check: 7 930 m² is ~0.008 km², and at 1 flash/km²/yr you would indeed wait ~125 years — the formula is just "your share of the ground".

Step 4 — Decide. IEC 62305-2 compares the risk of each loss type against tolerable values (e.g. R_T = 10⁻⁵/yr for loss of human life) by multiplying N_d (plus the much larger nearby-strike and line-borne event counts) by probability-of-damage and loss factors. Concept-level reading of our numbers: with ~0.008 direct events/yr, ordinary occupancy, no flammable storage and a metal-clad structure, the human-life risk is typically tolerable without a Class I external LPS, and a Class III/IV LPS plus a full SPD scheme emerges as the proportionate answer — whereas the same arithmetic for a site with flammable inventory (loss factor up ~100×) or an Asir hilltop (N_g and position factor up) pushes firmly to LPL I/II. The full multi-factor calculation belongs in the formal study; the value of doing this estimate by hand is that you can smell a wrong answer in someone else's software.

Worked example (b) — SPD cascade selection and the Up-vs-withstand check

Step 1 — assign the cascade (IEC 61643 / IEC 62305-4 zones):

• Type 1 at the LV intake (LV-MSB), rated for partial lightning current I_imp = 25 kA (10/350 μs) per pole, U_p ≤ 2.5 kV — meets the direct-flash share that enters on the bonded services.
• Type 2 at the MCC / distribution boards, I_n = 20 kA (8/20 μs), U_p ≤ 1.5 kV.
• Type 3 at sensitive loads (UPS input, DCS cabinets), U_p ≤ 1.2 kV, installed within a few metres of the protected device.

Step 2 — check each stage against the local withstand (IEC 60664 categories from Feel for the numbers):

Intake: U_p 2.5 kV ≤ switchboard withstand 4 kV → OK (margin 1.5 kV) Boards: U_p 1.5 kV < appliance withstand 2.5 kV → OK (margin 1.0 kV, 40 %) Electronics: U_p 1.2 kV < 1.5 kV category I → OK (tight — keep leads short)

Step 3 — the lead-length correction that kills naive designs. The voltage the equipment sees is U_p plus the inductive drop along the SPD's connection leads (≈1 kV per metre at lightning rates of rise, taking L′ ≈ 1 μH/m and di/dt ≈ 1 kA/μs):

U_eq ≈ U_p + L′·ℓ·di/dt = 1 500 + (1×10⁻⁶ × 0.5 × 10⁹) = 1 500 + 500 = 2 000 V

0.5 m of total lead turns a 1.5 kV protector into a 2 kV one — still under 2.5 kV, but a sloppy 1.5 m loop (≈3 kV total) silently voids the design. Hence the universal rule: total SPD connection length ≤ 0.5 m, or use the V-(Kelvin-)wiring pattern.

Step 4 — coordination between stages: enough cable inductance (≥10 m, or a decoupling inductor) between Type 1 and Type 2 so the Type 1 takes the bulk energy before the Type 2 reaches its limit — per manufacturer coordination tables. Sanity check: each stage clamps lower than the one upstream and lower than its local withstand; energy capability decreases downstream as let-through decreases. A cascade where a Type 3 sits unprotected by upstream stages fails on energy, not voltage.

Equipment Settings: Decoding the Nameplates

Equipment settings

Surge devices have no adjustable settings — their "settings" are the ratings chosen at purchase. Choosing them is the setting exercise, so learn to read the four numbers on every LV SPD nameplate (IEC 61643-11):

RatingMeaningHow to choose (230/400 V TN-S, indicative)
U_c — max continuous operating voltageThe 60 Hz voltage the SPD tolerates forever without conducting≥ 1.1 × U_0 → ≥ 253 V; common choice 275 V (covers regulation + TOV per IEC 60364-5-53)

MV surge arresters at the 13.8 kV intake (IEC 60099-4, metal-oxide, gapless). Two voltage ratings matter and are routinely confused:

• MCOV / U_c — the continuous 60 Hz voltage the arrester blocks indefinitely. It must exceed the highest continuous phase-to-earth voltage. Here is the trap on our plant: because the 13.8 kV system is resistance-earthed (§12), during an earth fault the two healthy phases rise toward full line-to-line voltage (13.8 kV) above earth, and they stay there until the fault clears. The arrester must ride through that.
• Rated voltage U_r — the 10 s temporary-overvoltage capability, typically U_r ≈ 1.25 × MCOV.

Arrester selection at 13.8 kV, resistance-earthed (indicative)

Continuous: U_c ≥ 1.05 × 13.8 / √3 = 8.4 kV (normal) Earth-fault TOV: healthy phases → ≈ 13.8 kV to earth, duration up to relay clearing (≤ a few s) Choose: rated U_r = 18 kV, MCOV ≈ 15.3 kV → 15.3 kV > 13.8 kV → rides through ✓

A solidly-earthed system could use a ~12 kV-rated arrester that protects more deeply; the resistance earthing buys fault-energy limitation at the cost of a higher arrester rating. Typical residual voltage at 10 kA for an 18 kV-class arrester is ~45–55 kV — against the 95 kV BIL that is a ≥40 % margin (IEC 60099-5 recommends ≥20–25 %). Sanity check: margin exists at the arrester terminals; keep the arrester within a few metres of what it protects, or travelling-wave doubling eats the margin back.

One genuine "setting" remains: where devices sit. Arresters at the MV cable termination / transformer primary; Type 1 SPDs at the LV intake ahead of the main breaker per SEC/SBC practice; SPDs on the DC system (110 V DCDB) and on instrument/telecom lines entering from the field (IEC 61643-21); and every SPD's earth tail to the nearest bonding bar by the shortest possible route.

Equipment Technology: From Air Terminal to Varistor

Equipment technology

Interception — rolling sphere and air terminals. The design tool for placing air terminals is the rolling-sphere method (IEC 62305-3): imagine a sphere of radius r (20 m for LPL I up to 60 m for LPL IV) rolled over the plant in every direction; anywhere the sphere can touch, lightning can attach, so every touchable point must be a terminal or be shielded by one. Physically the terminals are plain rods or a roof mesh (e.g. 10–20 m grid for LPL III/IV) — passive Franklin hardware. So-called "early streamer emission" terminals claiming enlarged protection radii are not recognised by IEC 62305; specify conventional geometry.

Conduction — down-conductors. Multiple parallel paths (spacing 10–25 m by LPL class) of 50 mm²-class copper or aluminium, or — better and free — the building's structural steel columns used as "natural" down-conductors where continuity is verified. Multiple paths divide the current and lower the inductive voltage rise; each path gets a test joint above grade and lands on the §12 earth grid.

Earthing and bonding — one system, always. IEC 62305 type B ring earth or the substation grid itself; never a separate "lightning earth" electrode isolated from the power earth. Two separate earths at different surge potentials simply flash over through whatever path is handy — usually a cable screen or an instrument card. Single integrated grid, with bonding (direct or via SPD) for every metallic service at each zone boundary, is the entire IEC 62305-4 philosophy in one sentence.

Clamping devices — MOV vs spark gap. The metal-oxide varistor (MOV) is a ceramic of zinc-oxide grains; each grain boundary is a ~3 V junction, so a disc's thickness sets its voltage. Conduction is smooth and fast (nanoseconds), clamping is continuous — but MOVs age: each absorbed surge nudges leakage current upward, ending in thermal runaway (hence the thermal disconnector and status flag on every MOV SPD). The spark gap (triggered, in Type 1 service) is binary: it withstands until it fires, then drops to a near-short arc — huge I_imp capability, no ageing, but it must extinguish the 60 Hz follow current it invites. MV arresters are gapless MOV stacks in polymer housings (silicone sheds — good pollution performance in dust, no porcelain shrapnel mode). Hybrid Type 1 SPDs combine a gap in series/parallel with MOVs to get both virtues; product families in the catalogue.

Control: Knowing the Shield Is Still Up

Control

Surge protection shares earthing's cruel property: it does nothing for years, then must work perfectly for a millisecond — and several of its components are sacrificial. Control therefore means status surveillance plus disciplined post-event checks.

Continuous status (to DCS/PMS): every Type 1/2 SPD specified with a remote signalling contact wired to a monitored alarm — the window flag alone is invisible inside a closed board, and a failed-disconnected SPD protects nothing while looking installed. UPS and DCS incomer SPDs are the highest priority points. MV arresters get surge counters with leakage-current indication in the earth tail where specified — the leakage trend is the MOV ageing signal; a step change after a storm season means the arrester absorbed something serious.

After every significant storm (or plant-wide surge event such as a capacitor-switching incident): walk down SPD status flags and remote alarms; read and log arrester counters; thermography of arrester housings under service voltage (a warming arrester is a dying arrester); check for tripped SPD backup fuses/MCBs and replace failed modules promptly — most SPD families have pluggable cartridges precisely so this is a five-minute job.

Periodic (indicative): annual visual inspection of air terminals, roof mesh and down-conductors (corrosion, mechanical damage from sandstorms, loose or missing clamps, paint over bonding joints); continuity from selected air terminals to the earth grid via the test joints; full LPS inspection per IEC 62305-3 maintenance tables (every 1–2 years visual, 2–4 years complete, after any strike); earth-grid measurements shared with the §12 schedule. Keep an LPS/SPD register exactly like the earth register — model, location, install date, status history, storm log.

Sizing Method: From Risk Class to Maintenance Plan

Sizing method

Step 1 — Risk assessment → LPS class. IEC 62305-2: gather N_g, structure dimensions and surroundings, service lines, occupancy, contents (flammable?), consequence factors; compute the risks (worked example (a) is the kernel); compare with tolerable values; the outcome is "no LPS needed" or a required LPL I–IV, which fixes every later parameter (rolling-sphere radius, mesh size, down-conductor spacing, I_imp shares).

Step 2 — External LPS layout. Roll the sphere (radius per LPL) over the 3-D plant model; place air terminals/mesh so nothing unprotected is touchable; route down-conductors at the class spacing using structural steel where continuity is certified; terminate on the ring/grid earth. Check separation distances to internal metalwork per IEC 62305-3 (or bond where separation cannot be met).

Step 3 — Bonding at zone boundaries. Define lightning protection zones (LPZ 0 outside → LPZ 1 inside the structure → LPZ 2 inside cabinets); at every boundary, bond every metallic service directly (pipes, trays, armours) or through an SPD (live conductors). One earth system throughout — see Technology.

Step 4 — SPD cascade per zone. Type 1 at LPZ 0/1 boundaries (I_imp from the LPL and conductor count), Type 2 at LPZ 1/2, Type 3 at sensitive equipment; include DC, instrument and telecom lines; select U_c for the system earthing and TOV, I_n/I_imp for the exposure — worked example (b) and the settings table.

Step 5 — Verify U_p margins. For each protected withstand level: U_p + lead-length inductive adder < withstand, with ≥20 % margin; check stage-to-stage energy coordination per manufacturer data; check MV arrester residual vs 95 kV BIL and arrester distance to the protected terminals.

Step 6 — Maintenance plan. Status contacts mapped and alarmed, counters logged, post-storm walkdown procedure issued, IEC 62305-3 inspection intervals in the CMMS, spare SPD cartridges in stores. A surge protection scheme without a maintenance plan decays invisibly toward the unprotected case.
