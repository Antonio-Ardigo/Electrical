5 — Motor FLC, Starting Methods & LV/MV Feed Threshold Reference

Goal: understand why motor starting — not motor running — is the event the whole LV system must be designed around, calculate the full-load and direct-on-line starting current of a representative 160 kW pump motor, work the voltage dip that a DOL start imposes on a 1600 kVA transformer, and from that choose between DOL, star-delta, soft starter and VFD with open eyes.

This section leans on the full-load current method of §3 — Full-Load Currents, hands its answer onward to §6 — Cable Sizing and §10 — Protection Coordination, and borrows the transformer impedance from §2. The harmonic consequences of the VFDs it recommends are settled next door in §7 — Power-Factor Correction.

Indicative data. Every numeric value in this section is indicative and chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis: SEC 13.8 kV, 60 Hz supply; 2 × 1600 kVA, Z = 6 % transformers; 400/230 V TN-S LV system; 50 °C design ambient; 4-pole motors at 1800 rpm synchronous. The motor schedule, the plant's motor-starting study and the SASO-adopted IEC standards (IEC 60034 series, IEC 60947-4-1), read together with the SEC Distribution Code and SBC 401, govern.

Seventy Per Cent of the Plant Is Spinning

Why this exists

Add up the motor schedule of almost any process plant and you will find that motors take roughly 70 % of the electrical load. On the Rev 3 plant that is on the order of 1.4 MW of the 2.0 MW total: pumps, fans, compressors, agitators, conveyors. The transformers exist to feed them, the cables exist to reach them, and most of the protection in the MCCs exists to save them from themselves. If you understand motors, you understand most of the plant's electrical behaviour; if you do not, every other calculation in Part 2 is an abstraction.

Running motors are easy. A loaded induction motor draws a steady, predictable current at a reasonable power factor and turns electricity into torque with 93–96 % efficiency. The system barely notices it. The event that the whole LV design must tolerate is the start — and the start is violent. At the instant a direct-on-line (DOL) contactor closes, the stationary rotor looks to the network almost like a short-circuited transformer secondary: the motor draws roughly six times its full-load current, and it draws it at a power factor of about 0.2 — nearly pure reactive current, the kind that drags bus voltage down hardest (the X·sinφ effect met in §2). That current persists not for a cycle but for the whole acceleration — seconds for a pump, tens of seconds for a high-inertia fan.

So one event must be tolerated by everything at once: the cable must carry 6× current without tripping its protection (§10), the transformer must source the starting kVA without letting the bus voltage collapse, the bus voltage must stay high enough that other running motors, contactors and drives ride through, and the protection must tell this legitimate 6× surge apart from a genuine fault. The calculations in this section exist to prove, before the contactor ever closes, that all four will hold.

Motors are ~70 % of plant load, and the start is the design event: ~6× rated current at a power factor near 0.2, sustained for the whole run-up. Cables, transformers, voltage stability and protection settings are all sized around the start, not the run.

A Failure Story: One Pushbutton, Whole-Plant Trip

What goes wrong without it

The story is fictional; every mechanism in it is routine. A plant much like this one added a large transfer pump late in a project — 160 kW, the biggest single drive on site. The mechanical contractor wanted it running before commissioning week; the cheapest, fastest starter is a DOL contactor, and the MCC had a spare starter cubicle. Nobody ran a starting study, because "it's only one more pump".

What nobody checked was the state of the bus. The plant was running on one transformer that week — the other was out for an oil-free cast-resin retrofit — so the full plant load and the new start would share a single 1600 kVA unit, already at about 85 % load. When the operator pressed START, the pump demanded its ~1.2 MVA of starting kVA at power factor 0.2 from a transformer with 6 % impedance that was already working hard. The 400 V bus dipped about 12 % — to roughly 352 V — and stayed there for the six seconds the pump took to accelerate.

Twelve per cent does not sound apocalyptic. But contactor coils are electromagnets holding against a spring: most are only guaranteed to hold above about 85 % voltage, and at 80 % many let go within a couple of cycles. Across the plant, a scatter of small DOL starters dropped out — not tripped, just released — and their motors coasted. The VFDs were worse: their DC buses sagged, and one after another they tripped on undervoltage, exactly as their default settings told them to. The process did not see an electrical event; it saw cooling water stop, air flow die and a reactor feed pump vanish, all inside ten seconds. The interlocks did what interlocks do, and the plant tripped to a safe state — three hours of restart, one batch lost, from one pushbutton.

The post-incident fix cost a fraction of the lost batch: a soft starter for the new pump, a documented voltage-dip study for every motor above ~30 kW, and ride-through settings reviewed on every VFD. That is precisely the content of the rest of this section.

The victims of a bad start are rarely the starting motor or its feeder — they are everything else on the bus: contactor coils dropping out below ~80–85 % voltage, VFDs tripping on DC-bus undervoltage, instruments browning out. A starting study is plant-wide protection, not a single-feeder formality.

Feel for the Numbers: 280 A All Day, 1 680 A for Six Seconds

Feel for the numbers

Start with speed, because at 60 Hz it surprises engineers trained on 60 Hz plants. An induction motor's stator creates a magnetic field rotating at synchronous speed ns = 120·f / p (p = number of poles). On the SEC 60 Hz grid:

PolesSynchronous speed at 60 HzTypical full-load speed (with slip)Typical use
23 600 rpm≈ 3 550 rpmHigh-speed pumps, compressors
41 800 rpm≈ 1 780 rpmThe workhorse — most process pumps
61 200 rpm≈ 1 185 rpmFans, agitators, larger slow drives

Everything runs 20 % faster than the same machine on 60 Hz — which is why a 60 Hz pump curve cannot simply be reused here, and why direct-replacement spares must be 60 Hz machines.

Now the representative machine used through this section: a 160 kW, 4-pole pump motor at 400 V, pf 0.85, efficiency 0.95. Its full-load current, by the formula from §3:

Quick estimate — 160 kW motor FLC

I_fl = P / (√3 × V × pf × η) I_fl = 160 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.85 × 0.95) = 160 000 / 559.4 ≈ 286 A

Call it ≈ 280 A (nameplate values vary a few per cent with the actual pf and η of the machine offered; we carry the round 280 A through the starting study). Sanity check by the LV rule of thumb from §3 — roughly 1.7–1.8 A per kW at 400 V: 160 × 1.75 ≈ 280 A. ✓

DOL starting current (≈ 6 × FLC):

I_start ≈ 6 × 280 = 1 680 A at pf ≈ 0.2

Hold those two numbers side by side. 280 A is what the feeder carries for years. 1 680 A is what it carries every time someone presses START — nearly three-quarters of the entire transformer's 2 309 A full-load current (§2), demanded by one machine, at a power factor so poor that almost all of it is voltage-depressing reactive current.

Now the energy view, because it explains a rule that otherwise looks arbitrary. Accelerating the pump set means pouring kinetic energy into the rotating mass: E = ½·J·ω². For the 160 kW set (motor rotor plus impeller, J ≈ 4 kg·m²) at 1 800 rpm (ω ≈ 188 rad/s): E ≈ ½ × 4 × 188² ≈ 71 kJ — about the kinetic energy of a 1.5-tonne car at 35 km/h. Here is the brutal part of induction-machine physics: during a DOL start of an inertia-dominated load, the rotor cage dissipates as heat approximately the same energy that ends up as kinetic energy. Every start therefore dumps ~70 kJ into a few kilograms of aluminium bars in a handful of seconds — a 2 kW kettle's output for 35 seconds, concentrated into parts with no time to cool.

That is why motor datasheets carry a starts-per-hour limit (often 2–4 cold starts, fewer when hot, for machines this size): the limit is not bureaucratic, it is the time the rotor needs to shed ~70 kJ per start before the next one stacks more heat on top. An operator who jogs a big motor repeatedly to "clear" a process problem is performing slow rotor murder.

160 kW pump at 400 V: ~280 A running, ~1 680 A starting at pf 0.2. Each DOL start stores ~70 kJ in the spinning mass and burns roughly the same again as heat in the rotor cage — which is the real reason starts-per-hour limits exist.

Worked Calculation: DOL Voltage Dip on One 1600 kVA Transformer

Worked calculation

The flagship question: if the 160 kW pump is started DOL while the plant is on a single transformer, how far does the 400 V bus dip — and is that acceptable? We work it by hand with one stated approximation, exactly as a field engineer can redo it on a phone.

Step 1 — Starting apparent power

The motor draws 1 680 A at 400 V (the bus voltage will sag, which slightly reduces the current — we conservatively ignore that relief):

S_start = √3 × V × I_start = 1.732 × 400 × 1 680 ≈ 1 164 kVA

One machine, starting, demands ~1 164 kVA — nearly three-quarters of the transformer's 1 600 kVA rating.

Step 2 — Split it into watts and vars

At starting pf ≈ 0.2: cosφ = 0.20, so sinφ = √(1 − 0.2²) = 0.98.

P_start = 1 164 × 0.20 ≈ 233 kW Q_start = 1 164 × 0.98 ≈ 1 141 kvar

The start is reactive-dominant: 98 % of the starting current is vars. That licenses the simplification in Step 3.

Step 3 — Voltage dip across the transformer reactance

Stated approximation: because the start is almost purely reactive and the transformer impedance is almost purely reactive (Z = 6 %, X/R ≈ 5 → X ≈ 0.059 pu on 1 600 kVA — see §2), the dip is dominated by the X·Q term. The reactive-dominant per-unit estimate is:

ΔV ≈ X_tx × Q_start / S_tx ΔV ≈ 0.059 × (1 164 × 0.98) / 1 600 = 0.059 × 1 141 / 1 600 ≈ 0.042 → 4.2 %

The 400 V bus dips to roughly 0.958 × 400 ≈ 383 V for the duration of the start.

Step 4 — Add the source behind the transformer

The 13.8 kV SEC source is not infinitely stiff. At the 25 kA design fault level, Ssc ≈ √3 × 13.8 × 25 ≈ 597 MVA, so the grid reactance on the 1 600 kVA base is only 1.6 / 597 ≈ 0.003 pu — adding about 0.2 % of dip. Total ≈ 4.4 %. The transformer dominates, as it should on any utility-fed LV start.

Step 5 — Check against the limit

A common acceptance limit for motor-starting dip on a healthy bus is ≤ 10 % (project specifications vary; some allow 15 % at the starting motor's own terminals). 4.4 % < 10 % — acceptable on an unloaded transformer. But note what the failure story showed: the dip adds to the regulation already present from existing load. On a transformer already ~85 % loaded at 0.85 pf (~3.5 % regulation, §2), the bus is already low before the start, and the total excursion approaches the contactor drop-out region. Sanity check: dip scales with starting kVA over transformer kVA times X — (1 164/1 600) × 0.059 × 0.98 ≈ 4.2 %, consistent. ✓

Honest limits of the hand method. This is a first-cut screening calculation: it ignores cable impedance to the motor (which adds dip at the motor terminals but slightly relieves the bus), current reduction as voltage sags, and other motors' response (running motors briefly act as generators, then draw more current at lower voltage). The plant's formal motor-starting study models all of this. The hand method is for the field question: is this start obviously fine, obviously dangerous, or worth a study?

Worked Calculation (continued): Comparing the Four Starting Methods

The dip calculation makes the starting-method comparison quantitative instead of doctrinal. Each method trades starting current against starting torque — and torque is the part people forget: the motor still has to accelerate the pump.

Method Start current Start torque Bus dip (160 kW example) Character
DOL ≈ 6 × FLC (1 680 A) 100 % of motor's natural curve ≈ 4.2 % Brutal and simple; one contactor; full mechanical shock
Star-delta ≈ 2 × FLC (560 A) in star ≈ 33 % — may stall high-torque loads ≈ 1.4 % in star — but see transition spike Three contactors + timer; open-transition spike can approach DOL current for a few cycles
Soft starter ≈ 3 × FLC (840 A), settable Reduced ∝ V²; current-ramped ≈ 2.1 % Thyristor voltage ramp; smooth; bypassed when up to speed
VFD ≈ 1.0–1.1 × FLC (≤ ~310 A) Full rated torque from near-zero speed < 0.5 % (negligible) Full speed control, energy saving — and harmonics (§7)

Reworking the dip for a VFD start

A VFD never connects the stalled motor to the bus. Its rectifier draws from the network only the power being delivered (plus losses), ramping from zero; the violent magnetising surge happens on the drive's output side, managed by the inverter. Worst case during acceleration, the line current approaches the drive's current limit at a good displacement power factor (≈ 0.95 for a 6-pulse rectifier):

S_start,VFD ≤ √3 × 400 × 310 ≈ 215 kVA, mostly active power ΔV ≈ X×Q/S + R×P/S ≈ 0.059 × (215×0.31)/1600 + 0.012 × (215×0.95)/1600 ≈ 0.4 %

Under half a per cent — an order of magnitude below DOL. On a weak bus (single transformer, or the 1 250 kVA standby DG, where the generator's transient reactance of ~20 % makes DOL dips several times worse), the VFD or soft starter is not a luxury; it is what makes the start possible at all.

Star-delta's dirty secret: at the star-to-delta changeover the motor is briefly disconnected; it slips back, and re-closing in delta can draw a transient close to full DOL current — so the bus still sees a (short) DOL-class event, and the contactors take a beating. With 33 % torque it suits machines that start nearly unloaded. For pumps started against a closed valve it can work; for anything with real breakaway torque, prefer a soft starter or VFD.
DOL = 6× current, 100 % torque, ~4 % dip per 160 kW on one 1 600 kVA transformer. Star-delta trades torque away and bites back at transition. A soft starter halves the dip; a VFD removes the event entirely (< 0.5 %) and adds speed control — at the price of harmonics, which is §7's problem.

Settings: Telling a Start From a Fault From a Slow Death

Equipment settings

Motor protection has a uniquely awkward brief: it must allow a 6× current surge every legitimate start, yet trip for a 6× current that means the rotor is jammed, and also catch the slow 15 % overload that cooks the insulation over hours. The settings below (indicative; the coordination study and relay setting sheets govern — §10) are how those three duties are separated.

FunctionIndicative setting (160 kW pump)What it is for

Overload trip class Class 10 (normal starts); Class 20/30 for heavy/long starts Class number ≈ max seconds to trip at 7.2 × Ie (IEC 60947-4-1) — must exceed actual start time with margin

Instantaneous short-circuit (50) ≈ 10–12 × FLC (above asymmetric inrush peak) Genuine faults only; must not see the first-cycle inrush asymmetry

For VFD-fed motors, the protection moves substantially into the drive's parameters, and three of them matter most:

  • Acceleration ramp — typically 10–30 s for pumps. Too short and the drive sits at its current limit (re-creating a mild start event); too long on a high-friction load and the motor crawls at high current and low cooling.
  • Current limit — typically 110–150 % of motor FLC. This is the parameter that guarantees the negligible bus dip calculated above: the drive physically cannot draw the 6× surge.
  • Undervoltage ride-through — DC-bus trip level and ride-through strategy (kinetic back-up / controlled deceleration). Left at factory defaults, drives are often the first equipment to trip in the 4–12 % dips this section is about. Set deliberately, they ride through everything short of a real outage.
Field check worth gold: compare the overload class with the measured start time on the longest start (often the first start after maintenance, with a flooded line). A Class 10 relay on a 12-second start trips randomly "for no reason"; the fix is Class 20, not a bigger pickup — raising the pickup destroys the running-overload protection.

Technology: Slip, Heat at 50 °C, and What a VFD Really Is

Equipment technology

The induction motor in plain words. The stator windings, fed at 60 Hz, create a magnetic field rotating at synchronous speed (1 800 rpm for 4 poles). The rotor — a cage of aluminium or copper bars — is dragged around by that field, but it can only produce torque if the field moves relative to the bars (a moving field induces the rotor currents that make the torque). So the rotor always runs slightly slower than the field; the difference is the slip, about 1–2 % at full load (1 780 vs 1 800 rpm). At standstill the slip is 100 %: the field sweeps past the bars at full speed, inducing enormous currents — which is exactly the 6× starting current seen from the supply. The machine has no brushes, no permanent magnets, essentially one wearing part (bearings). That is why it owns the industrial world.

Efficiency classes and the 50 °C problem. IEC 60034-30-1 efficiency classes run IE1–IE4; IE3 is the practical minimum for new machines here, IE4 where the duty justifies it (a 160 kW IE3 machine is ~96 % efficient — its losses, ~6–7 kW, are a roomful of fan heaters running whenever the pump runs). Standard ratings assume 40 °C ambient; at the Rev 3 design ambient of 50 °C the permissible output derates typically to ~92–95 % of nameplate, or the motor must be specified with class H insulation / class B temperature-rise margin. A motor "exactly sized" from a 40 °C catalogue is undersized in a Saudi summer.

Enclosure and cooling. For the dust and sand environment: minimum IP55 (IP54 absolute floor outdoors), cooling IC411 — the standard totally-enclosed fan-cooled (TEFC) arrangement: an external shaft-driven fan blowing over a finned frame, no exchange of internal and external air. Two field consequences: a dust blanket on the fins is thermal insulation on a machine already derated for 50 °C; and below ~50 % speed on a VFD the shaft fan loses effectiveness — slow-running VFD duty needs either oversizing or a separately driven fan (IC416).

The starter electronics. A star-delta starter is pure electromechanics: three contactors and a timer reconnect the windings from star (each phase sees V/√3, current and torque drop to 1/3) to delta. A soft starter is two antiparallel thyristors per phase, firing later or earlier in each half-cycle to ramp the effective voltage — current control without frequency control, usually with a bypass contactor once at speed. A VFD is the complete power-electronic decoupling of motor from grid: rectifier → DC bus → inverter. The input rectifier (six diodes in the common 6-pulse drive) charges a DC-bus capacitor bank; the output inverter (six IGBTs) chops that DC into a PWM pattern — thousands of voltage pulses per second whose average is a sine wave of whatever voltage and frequency the motor needs. Constant V/f keeps the magnetic flux right; vector control adds torque control down to zero speed.

Three side effects of that elegance, all of which appear elsewhere in this manual:

  • Harmonics. The 6-pulse rectifier draws current in gulps, rich in 5th and 7th harmonics — 300 Hz and 420 Hz on the 60 Hz system. Multiply by a plant-full of drives and you have the harmonic environment that forces the detuned PFC bank design in §7.
  • dV/dt and motor cables. Each PWM edge is a steep voltage front; travelling down the motor cable it reflects at the motor terminals and can nearly double, stressing the first turns of the winding. Hence inverter-duty insulation (IEC 60034-25), screened symmetrical motor cables, kept short (typically < 50–100 m without filters), and dV/dt or sine filters on long runs — see §6 for the cable side.
  • Bearing currents. The PWM common-mode voltage couples capacitively through the airgap and discharges through the bearings, machining fluting patterns into the races. Standard mitigations above ~100 kW: insulated non-drive-end bearing and/or a shaft grounding ring, plus the 360° screen termination of the motor cable.
Indicative product families for starters, MCC components and drives live in the Part 6 catalogues — see MCC components and VFDs & soft starters.

Control: From Contactor-and-Overload to the Intelligent MCC

Control

Every LV motor on this plant lives in a motor control centre — a switchboard of standardised starter cubicles. Two control philosophies coexist, often in the same lineup:

Conventional starter: fuse or MCCB + contactor + thermal overload relay, with hard-wired START/STOP, a local/remote selector, and a few digital signals to the DCS (run, trip, available). Cheap, transparent, fixable with a screwdriver — and informationally blind: when it trips, all the control room knows is "trip".

Intelligent MCC: each cubicle carries an electronic motor protection/management relay instead of a bimetal overload. It measures the three phase currents (often voltage too), runs a genuine thermal model of the motor, provides all the protection functions in the settings table above, counts starts and operating hours, time-stamps every event, and talks to the DCS/PMS over a fieldbus (Profibus/Profinet/Modbus — and IEC 61850 at the switchboard level). The first trip now arrives with its cause, the current waveform and the thermal state attached. For troubleshooting a 3 a.m. trip, that difference is the difference between minutes and shifts.

Above the MCC, three control-system interactions matter for this section:

  • Start interlocks (DCS). Process permissives (suction valve open, level healthy) plus electrical permissives: the restart-inhibit timer from the starts-per-hour budget, and — on well-engineered plants — a PMS check that the bus can afford the start (e.g. blocking a DOL start of the largest motor while on single-transformer or DG supply, exactly the failure story's missing barrier).
  • Load shedding (PMS). Motors are the load-shedding currency: on loss of one transformer, the PMS sheds the non-essential motor list to bring the survivor below its N-1 limit (§2). The shed list and the motor schedule must be the same document; an out-of-date shed list is a latent plant trip.
  • VFDs on the bus. Drives take their setpoints (speed, torque limits) and give their diagnostics over the same fieldbus; their ride-through and auto-restart behaviour after dips must be coordinated plant-wide, not left per-drive — a plant where half the drives auto-restart and half latch out after the same dip is a process upset generator.
Quick assessment question for any MCC: "show me how you would find out why this motor tripped last Tuesday". If the answer is a paper logbook and a bimetal overload with no memory, the plant is running on folklore. If the answer is an event record with the thermal image at trip time, the control philosophy is doing its job.

Sizing Method: the Motor Feeder Design Chain

Sizing method

Sizing a motor feeder is a chain, and the order matters because each link's output is the next link's input:

  1. Motor rating and FLC. From the driven-load power (plus mechanical margin, typically 10–15 % for pumps), pick the next standard motor rating, confirmed for 50 °C ambient (derate or specify class H — see technology above). Compute FLC per §3; then use the nameplate FLC of the actual machine for everything downstream.
  2. Cable. Size per the four-step method of §6: design current = FLC, apply the Rev 3 derating set (air at 50 °C ≈ 0.82, grouping), check voltage drop run-length (≤ 5 % running, and at the motor terminals during start typically ≤ 10–15 %), and verify short-circuit withstand. For VFD feeders, use screened symmetrical motor cable and the drive maker's length limits.
  3. Protection and starter components. Per §10 and IEC 60947-4-1 type-2 coordination: the fuse/MCCB, contactor (AC-3 duty at motor FLC) and overload must be a proven combination that survives a fault without welding the contactor. Overload class from the measured/estimated start time.
  4. Starting method — by dip study, not habit. Run the § c-motors dip estimate for the start on the weakest credible source (single transformer; DG supply if the motor is on the essential bus). Indicative outcomes: ≤ ~30 kW DOL is invisible; 30–75 kW DOL usually fine on a healthy bus, check when on DG; 75–200 kW soft starter or VFD; speed-controllable loads (pumps, fans) — VFD on energy grounds alone (affinity laws: 20 % speed reduction ≈ 50 % power reduction).
  5. VFD sizing, where chosen. Rules: drive continuous current ≥ motor nameplate FLC (size by current, not kW — a low-pf motor fools the kW rating); overload duty matched to the load (110 %/60 s variable torque for pumps/fans, 150 %/60 s constant torque); derate for 50 °C per the maker's tables (drives are commonly rated at 40 °C; expect one frame size up or a stated current derate, plus switching- frequency derating); altitude derating is not applicable on this coastal site. Add input chokes or low-harmonic front ends per the harmonic budget in §7.
  6. Close the loop. Feed the final motor list back into the load estimate, the transformer N-1 shed list (§2) and the short-circuit study (§4 — running motors contribute fault current too).

The LV-to-MV Feed Threshold

The chain above eventually defeats itself: above roughly 150–200 kW per motor, LV currents (≈ 280–350 A running, ~2 000 A starting) drive cable, starter and switchboard costs up steeply, and the dip study starts failing. Such machines are more economically fed at MV (3.3 / 6.6 kV) from a dedicated MV starter — at which point they leave the 400 V MCC world entirely. On a ~2 MW plant the largest drives sit near this boundary, almost never beyond it: expect the answer here to be "soft starter or VFD at 400 V", but check the motor schedule rather than assuming.

What to Check on Site — Self-Check

  • Obtain the motor schedule; confirm motors > ~75 kW have soft start or VFD, and that any DOL machine above ~30 kW has a documented dip check (single-TX and DG cases).
  • Largest motor: read nameplate FLC, starting method, overload class; compare class against the actual measured start time.
  • Confirm 60 Hz nameplates throughout — a 60 Hz spare motor installed "because it fits" runs 20 % fast on its pump curve and overloads.
  • Check IC411 fans and fins for dust blanket; confirm 50 °C ambient was in the motor datasheet (temperature rise class vs insulation class margin).
  • VFDs: record current limit, ramp times, undervoltage ride-through settings and auto-restart philosophy; confirm screened motor cable, length within the maker's limit, and shaft-grounding/insulated bearing on large machines.
  • Confirm restart-inhibit (starts-per-hour) is actually configured, not just in the datasheet.
  • Self-check — Reconstruct without notes: 160 kW → ~280 A FLC → 1 680 A DOL → ~1 164 kVA → 4.2 % dip on one 1 600 kVA TX. Which single number would change if the plant ran tie-closed on two transformers? (Stx effectively doubles → dip roughly halves — but §4 forbids tie-closed operation for fault-level reasons.)
  • Self-check — Why does a star-delta starter still threaten the bus despite its 2× starting current? (Open-transition re-closing spike approaching DOL magnitude.)
  • Self-check — Why is each start "worth" ~70 kJ of rotor heat, and what rule on the relay enforces that budget? (Inertia-start rotor loss ≈ stored kinetic energy; starts-per-hour / restart-inhibit.)
The motor feeder design chain — FLC → cable → protection → starter by dip study → VFD sized by current and derated for 50 °C — exists because one pushbutton can otherwise trip a plant. Size for the start; the run takes care of itself.

Current That Does No Work but Heats Every Conductor

Why this exists

Every induction motor, transformer and reactor on the plant needs a magnetic field to function, and on AC that field must be built and collapsed 120 times a second (twice per 60 Hz cycle). The energy that builds the field is not consumed — it sloshes back to the source each half-cycle — but the current that carries it back and forth is real current in every conductor along the way. That oscillating, non-working flow is reactive power (Q, in var); the working flow is active power (P, in W); their vector total — what cables, transformers and generators must actually be sized for — is apparent power (S, in VA), with pf = P/S.

The popular analogy is the beer glass: the liquid is kW, the foam is kvar, and you pay for a full glass. It is memorable but it undersells the problem, because foam is weightless and harmless. The better picture: reactive current is a delivery truck that drives the route fully laden in both directions and never delivers anything. It burns fuel (I²R loss in every cable and winding), occupies road space (thermal capacity of cables and transformers), and wears out the fleet — while doing zero useful work. At pf 0.85, about 53 % extra "non-delivering" current is on the road compared with the same plant at unity; the conductors cannot tell the difference and heat up just the same.

Utilities can tell the difference, and bill for it. SEC penalises consumers whose power factor falls below 0.9 (indicative — the reactive charge mechanism and threshold are set by the SEC tariff in force and the connection agreement; verify the current figures). The economic logic is honest: the customer's kvar occupies SEC generator, line and transformer capacity exactly as kW does. Power-factor correction is therefore one of the very few electrical investments with a direct, recurring, line-item payback on the utility invoice — plus the hidden payback inside the fence: freed transformer capacity and lower cable losses, both quantified below.

The fix exploits a symmetry: an inductor borrows energy for a quarter-cycle and returns it; a capacitor does the same in antiphase. Put a capacitor next to an inductive load and the energy sloshes locally between them instead of travelling to the SEC grid and back. The supply sees only the working current. Nothing in the load changes — the trucks just stop driving the whole route.

Reactive power is current that delivers no energy yet heats and occupies every conductor between the source and the load. SEC bills for it below pf 0.9 (indicative). Capacitors fix it by closing the energy loop locally — the same sloshing energy, a much shorter route.

A Failure Story: "Full" Transformers Carrying 1.25 MW

What goes wrong without it

A plant much like this one ran for two years at a power factor of 0.78 — nobody had commissioned the capacitor bank properly after a controller fault, and the pf meter on the LV-MSB had been reading a failed CT phase for months. Three slow-burning consequences, none of them dramatic enough to force action on any given day:

First, the capacity illusion. Operations wanted to add a 250 kW crusher. The PMS trends said the 1 600 kVA transformer was at 100 % — "the plant is full". But 1 600 kVA at 0.78 pf is only 1 600 × 0.78 = 1 248 kW of actual work. The transformer was thermally full while delivering 1.25 MW; at 0.95 pf the same iron would have carried 1 520 kW. The plant was about to spend serious money on "reinforcement" to buy back capacity it already owned — the missing 270 kW was being spent hauling vars.

Second, the letters. SEC's metering integrates reactive energy whether or not the plant's own pf meter works. The monthly invoices carried power-factor penalty charges — individually modest, collectively the price of the entire capacitor bank every year or two. The letters were filed by accounts, who had no reason to know what a kvar was; the feedback loop from invoice to switchroom did not exist.

Third, the soft bus at the far end. Voltage drop in cables and transformers is driven hardest by reactive current through reactance (the X·sinφ term — the same physics as the motor-starting dip in §5). At 0.78 pf the running voltage at the most distant MCC sagged 2–3 % lower than it needed to be; motors there ran measurably hotter, and the marginal contactors that drop out during motor starts dropped out a little more often. Nobody connected the nuisance trips at MCC-D to the power factor; they blamed the contactor brand.

The audit that finally caught it took one afternoon: a clamp power meter on the incomer, an invoice, and the arithmetic in the next two sub-sections. The repaired and re-commissioned bank paid for its repair in under a year — and the crusher connected to the existing transformer.

Low power factor never announces itself. It hides as "full" transformers, unexplained penalty lines on invoices nobody technical reads, and a chronically soft bus at the far MCCs. If the plant pf meter and the SEC invoice disagree, believe the invoice.

Feel for the Numbers: 0.62 Mvar Riding Along with Every Megawatt

Feel for the numbers

Power factor numbers feel abstract until you convert them to "extra freight". The conversion is tanφ: for every megawatt of useful power, the plant also hauls P·tanφ of circulating reactive power.

Read the 0.85 row, because it is this plant: for every 1 MW of work, 0.62 Mvar rides along. The vars and the watts add vectorially, so the current is "only" 18 % higher than it needs to be — but cable heating goes as current squared, so the I²R losses in every feeder are 1.18² ≈ 1.39× what the same delivered power would cost at unity pf. The transformers, cables and busbars are utterly indifferent to pf as a concept; they see only the full kVA, and every one of them was bought, sized and derated (at 50 °C!) for that kVA.

Now the corrective hardware. A capacitor is a stored-energy device: E = ½CV². How much energy does the whole ~600 kvar bank actually hold? For an AC bank the stored energy at peak is Q/ω:

Quick estimate — energy stored in the full 600 kvar bank

E ≈ Q / ω = 600 000 / (2π × 60) = 600 000 / 377 ≈ 1.6 kJ

About the energy of a small battery — trivial as energy. The danger is the release time: a charged capacitor closed onto the bus (or onto another charged step) equalises in microseconds. 1.6 kJ in 100 µs is an instantaneous power of ~16 MW — hence inrush transients of kiloamps at kilohertz when steps switch carelessly. Every "boring" requirement in the settings section (discharge resistors, reclose delays, capacitor-duty contactors) exists to tame this microsecond physics.

One more intuition: the bank is roughly the reactive mirror of the motors. The ~1.4 MW of motors at pf ~0.85 generate most of the plant's ~1.24 Mvar demand (2 000 kW × 0.62); the bank hands back ~0.6 Mvar of it locally, leaving the pf at the SEC meter at 0.95. Capacitors do not make power — they make the motors' magnetising energy commute locally instead of to the SEC grid.

At 0.85 pf, every megawatt drags 0.62 Mvar with it; conductors see the full kVA and losses rise with current squared. The fix stores only ~1.6 kJ — but a capacitor releases its energy in microseconds, which is why switching it is an engineering discipline of its own.

Worked Calculation: Sizing the Bank from 0.85 to 0.95

Worked calculation

The sizing formula is pure trigonometry: the load's watts are fixed; correction only shrinks the var leg of the power triangle from P·tanφ1 to P·tanφ2. The capacitor must supply the difference.

Step 1 — Required compensation

Q_c = P × (tan φ1 − tan φ2)

pf1 = 0.85 → φ1 = 31.8° → tanφ1 = 0.620

pf2 = 0.95 → φ2 = 18.2° → tanφ2 = 0.329

Q_c = 2 000 kW × (0.620 − 0.329) = 2 000 × 0.291 ≈ 582 kvar

Step 2 — Choose a standard bank and its step structure

Select a 600 kvar automatic bank, switched in steps — e.g. 6 × 100 kvar or, better for a plant whose load swings, 12 × 50 kvar. Step size matters in both directions:

  • Steps too coarse: at light load (say weekend, 400 kW) a single 100 kvar step is 25 % of the load's entire var demand — the controller hunts, or worse, the last step pushes the plant leading (over-compensated). A leading plant raises its own bus voltage, stresses the capacitors, and can interact badly with the standby generator's AVR — DG sets tolerate leading pf very poorly.
  • Steps too fine: more contactors, fuses and reactors to buy and maintain. 50 kvar steps are a sensible LV compromise at this scale.

Step 3 — Transformer capacity released

Before: S1 = 2 000 / 0.85 = 2 353 kVA. After: S2 = 2 000 / 0.95 = 2 105 kVA.

ΔS = 2 353 − 2 105 = 248 kVA released

The same 2 × 1600 kVA transformers now carry the plant at 2 105 / 3 200 ≈ 66 % instead of 73.5 % — margin that improves the N-1 case in §2 and is, in effect, ~250 kVA of free transformer bought with capacitors.

Step 4 — Loss reduction in everything upstream of the bank

Conduction losses scale with current squared, and current scales with kVA at fixed voltage:

I2 / I1 = S2 / S1 = 2 105 / 2 353 = 0.894 Loss ratio = (I2 / I1)² = 0.894² ≈ 0.80

A 20 % cut in the I²R losses of the transformers and every cable upstream of the correction point — continuously, for the life of the plant. (Only the variable I²R losses scale this way; transformer iron loss is voltage-driven and unaffected.)

Step 5 — Sanity checks

(i) Result matches the Plant Data Card (≈ 580 kvar → 600 kvar standard). ✓ (ii) Rule of thumb: correcting 0.85 → 0.95 needs ≈ 0.29 kvar per kW; 0.29 × 2 000 = 580 kvar. ✓ (iii) The bank (600 kvar) is comfortably below the plant's total var demand (1 240 kvar at 0.85), so full-load over-compensation is impossible; the leading risk exists only at light load, which the step structure and controller manage. ✓

Why target 0.95 and not 1.00? The last vars are the most expensive (tanφ flattens near unity: 0.95 → 1.00 costs another 660 kvar — more than the whole bank so far, for a 5 % kVA gain), unity operation leaves zero margin before the plant swings leading on every load dip, and SEC's threshold is 0.9 (indicative) — 0.95 clears it with margin. Diminishing returns, increasing risk.

Detuning: 7 % Reactors and the 227 Hz Question

The single most important "setting" is chosen at purchase: the bank's detuning factor p — the series reactor's impedance as a fraction of the capacitor's. With a reactor in series, each step is no longer a pure capacitor but a series L-C circuit with its own resonant frequency:

Worked check — where does a 7 % detuned step resonate on a 60 Hz system?

n = 1 / √p = 1 / √0.07 ≈ 3.78 (resonance order, multiple of mains frequency) f_res = 60 Hz × 3.78 ≈ 227 Hz

Below 227 Hz the step behaves capacitively; above 227 Hz it behaves like an inductor — it cannot resonate with, or sink, any harmonic above that frequency. The plant's dominant VFD harmonics are the 5th and 7th — 300 Hz and 420 Hz at 60 Hz — both safely above 227 Hz. Sanity check: the familiar catalogue figure "189 Hz" for 7 % reactors is the 60 Hz-system value (50 × 3.78); on the SEC 60 Hz grid the same 7 % hardware tunes to 227 Hz. Same order, different hertz — quote the right one in specifications. ✓

Two consequences to specify, not discover: the reactor raises the fundamental voltage across the capacitor by ≈ 1/(1−p) = 1.075×, so detuned banks use capacitors rated ~480 V on the 400 V network; and the reactors dissipate real heat, which the cubicle ventilation at 50 °C ambient must be designed for. Why detuning matters at all — the resonance mechanism it defuses — is explained under Equipment technology below.

On site, the discharge interlock is testable without instruments: after the controller drops a step, time how long before it is allowed back. If steps re-close within seconds of dropping out, the reconnection delay has been "tuned out" by someone chasing pf responsiveness — and every re-close is a micro-lightning strike inside the panel.

Technology: Self-Healing Film, Brutal Contactors and the Resonance Trap

Equipment technology

The capacitor element. Modern LV power capacitors are MPP — metallised polypropylene: a polypropylene film a few micrometres thick carrying a vacuum-deposited metal layer thinner still, wound into cylindrical elements. The metallisation is so thin that a local dielectric breakdown vaporises the metal around the fault point and the capacitor self-heals — it loses a microscopic patch of capacitance instead of failing short. Elements are dry or resin-filled, grouped in cans with internal fuses and an overpressure disconnector: if end-of-life gassing swells the can, the expansion physically tears the internal connection before the can ruptures. A bulged capacitor can is therefore not "still working" — it is a fired safety device awaiting replacement.

Detuning reactors are iron-cored series inductors, one three-phase unit per step, sized to the p = 7 % rule above. Their iron must stay linear (no saturation) up to ~1.7–1.8× rated current, because a saturating reactor loses inductance exactly when harmonic current is highest — silently un-detuning the bank.

Switching the steps. An uncharged capacitor looks, for the first microseconds, like a short circuit; the §feel arithmetic (1.6 kJ in microseconds) becomes a multi-kA, multi-kHz inrush. Two technologies tame it:

  • Capacitor-duty contactors with pre-insertion resistors: auxiliary contacts close first through small resistors that take the sting out of the inrush, then the main contacts shunt them milliseconds later. Cheap, robust, the LV default — but electromechanical, so 30–60 s response.
  • Thyristor-switched steps: semiconductors close at the exact instant the bus voltage equals the capacitor's residual voltage — essentially zero transient, response within a cycle, no moving parts. Chosen where loads swing fast (welders, crushers, hoists) — at a price.

Beyond capacitors, for completeness: active filters are power-electronic converters that measure the harmonic and/or reactive current and inject the exact antiphase current, and a STATCOM is the transmission-class cousin providing continuously variable vars. On a plant this size they appear only where harmonics, flicker or very fast var swings defeat the conventional bank.

And the trap that makes detuning non-optional. Seen from the 400 V bus, a plain capacitor bank in parallel with the inductance of the supply (transformer + grid) forms a parallel resonant circuit. Near its resonant frequency, the L and C exchange current between themselves many times larger than the current that excites them — a tuning fork that any harmonic near its note will ring. The resonant order is estimated simply:

Worked check — where would a plain (un-detuned) 600 kvar bank resonate?

n_res ≈ √(S_sc / Q_c)

Ssc at the LV bus, one transformer ≈ √3 × 400 × 38 500 ≈ 26.7 MVA (§4):

n_res ≈ √(26 700 / 600) = √44.5 ≈ 6.7 → f_res ≈ 6.7 × 60 ≈ 400 Hz

400 Hz sits between the 5th (300 Hz) and 7th (420 Hz) — dangerously close to the 7th, and the resonant point moves with every step switched and every change of source impedance (single-TX operation, DG supply). Somewhere in the bank's operating range it will land on a harmonic the VFDs generate. The result is amplified harmonic current circulating through the capacitors (which overheat, bulge and blow fuses) and amplified harmonic voltage distorting the whole bus. The 7 % reactor breaks the trap by making every step inductive above 227 Hz: there is simply no capacitive branch left for the 5th or 7th to resonate with. ✓

A plain capacitor bank on a VFD-heavy plant is a resonance experiment running in production. The classic post-mortem sequence — blown step fuses, bulged cans, "the bank never lasts a summer" — is parallel resonance, not bad luck. With the plant's VFD share, only detuned (or thyristor-switched detuned) banks are acceptable; see also the drive-side mitigation in the VFD catalogue.

Control: the Controller Is Only as Honest as Its CT

Control

The automatic power-factor controller is a small relay on the bank's door: it measures the bus voltage and one current, computes the var error against the target pf, and switches steps in and out, respecting the C/k threshold, the switching delay and the discharge interlock from the settings table. Modern units also rotate duty among equal steps ("wear levelling", so one contactor does not die first), count operations per step, measure THD, and expose everything over Modbus to the PMS.

The classic commissioning error is the CT location, and it is worth a diagram in words. The controller's CT must measure the total plant current including the capacitor bank — i.e. it belongs on the incomer, upstream of the point where the bank tees off. Put the CT downstream of that point (seeing load only) and the controller can never observe the effect of its own switching: it sees a low pf, switches steps in, sees no change, switches more in, and parks the whole bank connected — over-compensating the real plant while reporting failure. The mirror error (CT seeing only the bank) is even more decorative. After any switchboard modification near the incomer, re-verify the CT position and polarity before trusting the pf reading.

Monitoring through the PMS closes the loop the failure story lacked:

  • Trend the pf at the SEC metering point and compare monthly with the SEC invoice's reactive charges — the invoice is the ground truth.
  • Alarm on step failure: the controller's step-current deviation alarm, or simply "demanded steps ≠ achieved kvar". A bank typically dies one step at a time, invisibly, over years.
  • Alarm on sustained leading pf at light load — the signature of a stuck contactor or a mis-set target.

Capacitor protection is layered: per-element internal fuses and the overpressure disconnector (inside the can), per-step fuses or MCCBs rated ≈ 1.35–1.5 × step current (to live with harmonics and tolerance, per IEC 60831), bank overcurrent at the feeder, and — on larger or MV banks built as double-star — an unbalance relay measuring the current in the star-point bridge: healthy halves balance to zero, and a few failed elements anywhere tip the bridge measurably long before anything smokes. On this LV bank, the step-monitoring alarm plays that early-warning role.

Five-minute field test of the whole control chain: note the pf and connected steps, start a large motor (vars jump), and watch the controller respond after its delay; then remove the load and confirm steps drop out and respect the 60 s reconnection lockout. One observed cycle verifies CT, controller, contactors and interlocks together.

Sizing Method: from Measured Profile to Verified Bank

Sizing method

  1. Measure, do not assume. Log P, Q, pf and THD at the incomer for at least a representative week (PMS historian or a portable power logger). The design case is the var profile, not a single pf number: note the minimum load (sets the smallest useful step) and the maximum var demand (sets the bank total). Cross-check against twelve months of SEC invoices.
  2. Fix the target: 0.95 at the SEC metering point — comfortably above the 0.9 penalty threshold (indicative), comfortably below the leading region.
  3. Compute the kvar: Qc = P × (tanφ1 − tanφ2) at the maximum-demand case — here 2 000 × 0.291 ≈ 582 kvar → 600 kvar standard.
  4. Harmonic survey before topology. If the measured THD and the drive inventory show significant non-linear load (as on this plant: VFD share well above the ~15–20 % rule-of-thumb threshold), the bank must be detuned — 7 % standard, tuned at 227 Hz on 60 Hz; choose 14 % (n ≈ 2.7) only where 3rd-harmonic problems exist. Plain banks are acceptable only on demonstrably linear plants.
  5. Choose the step granularity from the load profile: smallest step ≲ the var swing of normal load variation (12 × 50 kvar here); thyristor switching only if vars swing faster than a contactor bank's 30–60 s response.
  6. Locate the bank. Central at the LV-MSB (one bank, one controller, easy maintenance — the usual answer, and this plant's) or distributed at MCCs / fixed at large motors (also relieves the feeder cables, but multiplies equipment and risks self-excitation if a fixed capacitor rides on a motor feeder that gets disconnected with the motor). Never downstream of a VFD — the drive's rectifier sees it as a fault.
  7. Verify three numbers before ordering:
    • Resonance: nres = √(Ssc/Qc) for the plain-bank check (≈ 6.7 here — fails → detune), and the detuned 227 Hz placement below the 5th.
    • Voltage rise: ΔV ≈ Q/Ssc. Full bank on one transformer: 0.6 / 26.7 ≈ 2.2 % bus rise at no-load; per 50 kvar step ≈ 0.2 % — comfortable. The same check on the 1 250 kVA DG (much higher source impedance) is one reason the bank is normally off on generator supply.
    • Protection and cabling: step and bank protection at 1.35–1.5 × rated current, cables likewise (§6), and the feeder breaker coordinated per §10.
  8. Commission honestly: CT location/polarity proven, target and delays set, step currents measured and recorded as the baseline for the step-failure alarm.

What to Record on Site — Self-Check

  • Bank nameplate: total kvar (≈ 600), step structure, capacitor voltage rating (≈ 480 V for 7 % detuned on 400 V), reactor detuning factor.
  • Detuning reactors physically present on every step (banks get extended; extensions get ordered plain), and no bulged cans, blown step fuses or browned terminations behind the door.
  • pf at the LV-MSB ≥ 0.93 with the bank in auto; compare with the SEC invoice's reactive charges for the last 12 months.
  • Controller: target pf, switching delay, reconnection (discharge) lockout ≥ 60 s observed working; step duty-rotation enabled; step-failure alarm configured and routed to the PMS, not a local LED.
  • Controller CT upstream of the bank tee-off, polarity verified after any incomer modification.
  • Cubicle ventilation working — reactors run hot, the room is 50 °C, and capacitor life halves every ~7 °C.
  • Bank interlock/practice on DG supply documented (normally off on the 1 250 kVA generator).
  • Self-check — Reconstruct without notes: 2 000 kW, 0.85 → 0.95 ⇒ Qc = 2 000 × (0.620 − 0.329) ≈ 582 kvar; S falls 2 353 → 2 105 kVA (248 kVA freed); upstream I²R losses fall to 0.894² ≈ 80 %.
  • Self-check — Why 227 Hz and not 189 Hz on this plant? (n = 1/√0.07 ≈ 3.78 is a multiple of mains frequency: 60 × 3.78 ≈ 227 Hz; 189 Hz is the 60 Hz-system figure.)
  • Self-check — A colleague proposes correcting to unity "while we're at it". Give the three reasons to refuse. (660 extra kvar for 5 % kVA gain; zero margin before leading at every load dip; leading pf risks with capacitors, voltage and the DG's AVR.)
  • Self-check — Explain parallel resonance to an operator in two sentences, using the plain-bank number n ≈ 6.7 (≈ 400 Hz, between the 5th at 300 Hz and 7th at 420 Hz).
600 kvar of detuned capacitors turn a 0.85 plant into a 0.95 plant: ~250 kVA of transformer bought back, 20 % of upstream I²R losses removed, SEC penalties gone. The bank's enemies are microseconds (switching transients) and hertz (resonance at 227 vs 300 Hz); respect both and it is the quietest, highest-yield cabinet in the switchroom.
