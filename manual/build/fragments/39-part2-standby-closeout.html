8 — Standby Diesel Generator — Sizing, Step Loading & Fuel Core

Goal: size the DG standby set on an essential-load basis (Rev 3: 1 250 kVA ESP at 50 °C), design the staged restart so the set is never block-loaded beyond its step capability, estimate the voltage/frequency dip per load step from first principles, and verify fuel autonomy — then read an ISO 8528 nameplate without being fooled by the rating fine print.

This section is one third of the standby-power triangle. The other two corners are the UPS (§16), which keeps the no-break loads alive while the engine cranks, and the ATS (§17), which decides when to start the engine and when to move the essential bus. Read all three together: each one's sizing assumptions are the other two's inputs.

Indicative data. Every number here — ratings, fuel rates, reactances, timings — is indicative and chosen for teaching on the Rev 3 plant basis. The genset vendor's certified data sheet, the plant's load-acceptance study and the protection coordination study govern. Standards cited are ISO 8528 (generating sets), the SASO-adopted IEC suite (IEC 60034 rotating machines, IEC 61439 assemblies), SBC 401 and the SEC Distribution Code.

The Island Heart: What Must Survive a SEC Outage

Why this exists

For 99.9-something per cent of the year, this plant is a passenger. SEC's 13.8 kV network — backed by gigawatts of generation and a grid stiff enough to hold 60.00 Hz against anything the plant does — supplies every kilowatt. Then, perhaps two or three times a year, a feeder fault, a storm or a planned outage opens the intake breaker and the plant becomes, for a few hours, an electrical island. The standby diesel generator is the heart of that island: the only spinning machine on site capable of making voltage and frequency from nothing but fuel and air.

The first design decision is brutal and happens long before any kVA arithmetic: what deserves to survive? Nobody sizes a standby set for the full 2.0 MW / 2 353 kVA plant load — the capital cost, the fuel logistics and the rarity of outages do not justify it. Instead the LV distribution is split into two families:

  • Non-essential loads — the process itself, large utility motors, HVAC comfort cooling. When SEC dies, these die with it and wait. Production is lost; nothing is damaged; restart follows a procedure.
  • Essential loads — everything whose loss converts an outage from "lost production" into "damaged plant or unsafe plant": emergency lighting, the DCS and its marshalling cabinets, instrument air compressors, critical cooling and lube-oil pumps, fire-fighting systems, the UPS rectifier, the battery chargers for the DCDB 110 V DC system, communications, and the HVAC that keeps switchrooms and battery rooms below their design temperatures in a 50 °C summer.

The essential family lives on a dedicated essential bus (or essential MCC section) downstream of the ATS. In normal operation the ATS feeds that bus from the LV-MSB; on a confirmed SEC failure it starts the DG and transfers the essential bus to it. The split is therefore not just a list on paper — it is copper: separate switchboard sections, separate feeders, and a transfer switch whose rating and logic are calculations in their own right (§17).

Sizing the DG is then a four-question problem, and this section works all four:

  1. How big is the essential load? — steady-state kVA with diversity (the worked calculation below).
  2. How is it applied? — a generator that can carry 1 250 kVA cannot necessarily accept 1 250 kVA in one hit. Step-load capability, not nameplate, is usually the binding constraint.
  3. How long must it run? — fuel autonomy: day tank plus bulk storage against a stated policy (24–48 h is typical for a plant that can be refuelled by road).
  4. What does it do to everything else? — the DG is a weak source: fault levels collapse (§4), protection that relied on 38.5 kA may no longer see faults (§10, 51V below), and every motor start becomes a system event (§5).
The genset is not "backup power for the plant". It is the dedicated heart of a small, deliberately chosen island — the essential bus — and its size is set by what that island needs and the order in which the island asks for it. Define the essential-load list first; every other number in this section is derived from it.

A Failure Story: Sized on Paper, Crashed on Concrete

What goes wrong without it

The following story is fictional but every failure mode in it is routine in incident reports. The plant resembles ours; the mistakes are the two classics: sizing on nameplate kW only, and treating fuel as somebody else's problem.

The setup. At design time, someone added up the essential-load list: about 1 000 kW. A 1 250 kVA set was bought — correctly, as it happens. But the load-acceptance study was never done. The ATS was wired to transfer the whole essential bus in one operation, with every motor contactor left energised so that everything that was running before the outage tries to restart the instant the bus goes live. On the load list this is invisible: the kW column adds up. Electrically it is a monster: six motors starting simultaneously draw 6–7 times their full-load current at a power factor of ~0.2 (§5) — a starting demand of well over 2 000 kVA thrown at a 1 250 kVA machine.

The event. A July afternoon, 49 °C. A SEC feeder fault takes the intake out. The UPS carries the DCS; the engine cranks, fires, and reaches 400 V / 60 Hz in eleven seconds — flawless. The ATS transfers. Then:

Time after transferWhat is happening
0–0.3 s Every held-in contactor recloses. Starting kVA > 2 000 kVA hits the alternator. The subtransient/transient reactance of the machine drops the bus voltage by 25–30 % — far beyond any AVR's instantaneous correction.
0.3–2 s At 70 % voltage, motor torque (∝ V²) is half of normal: the motors accelerate slowly, holding their starting current for seconds instead of the usual one. The engine, suddenly asked for double its rated torque, bogs down: frequency dives toward 55 Hz. The turbocharger has no exhaust energy yet — the engine is smoke-limited and simply cannot make the power.
2–4 s Undervoltage (27) and underfrequency (81U) protection — set to protect the machine — give up first: the generator breaker trips. The essential bus goes dark again. The ATS, seeing a dead source, locks out on some designs, or cycles on worse ones.
+ minutes The control room is on UPS, watching screens, with no instrument air compressor and no critical cooling. The operators are now managing a plant shutdown on battery time.

The second insult. Two months later, after a staged-restart sequence has been hastily programmed, the next outage lasts longer. The set runs beautifully for five hours — then hunts, smokes and stops. The day tank shows half full. Cause: years of storage at 50 °C tank-skin temperatures have bred microbial sludge ("diesel bug") and water at the tank bottom; five hours of vibration stirred it into the suction line and blinded both filters. The fuel quantity was fine; the fuel quality had never been polished, sampled or drained. At desert temperatures fuel degrades faster, water condenses overnight in half-empty tanks, and filter plugging is a question of when, not if.

The lessons, in the order this section addresses them:

  • A genset is sized in kVA and torque transients, not kW totals. The starting sequence is part of the design, owned jointly with the PMS/ATS logic.
  • Step-load capability (~60 % of rating in one block for a typical turbocharged set) is a hard physical limit set by the turbocharger and the alternator reactance — no setting changes it.
  • Fuel autonomy is litres × quality × delivery. A tank volume calculation is necessary but nowhere near sufficient at 50 °C.
  • A standby set that has never been tested on real load is a hypothesis, not a backup (see Control, below).

How Big Is 1 250 kVA, Really?

Feel for the numbers

Before the formal sizing, calibrate your intuition on what a 1 250 kVA / 1 000 kW diesel set actually is — because the numbers decide everything from the slab to the fuel-truck schedule.

Estimate 1 — The engine

Electrical output 1 000 kW at alternator efficiency ≈ 0.95 means the engine must deliver:

P_mech = 1 000 / 0.95 ≈ 1 050 kW ≈ 1 410 hp

Engines in this class are catalogued around 1 400–1 700 hp to leave torque reserve. A heavy long-haul truck engine is roughly 450 hp — so the DG is three to four truck engines on one crankshaft: typically a V12 or V16 of 30–60 litres displacement, two tonnes of engine plus another two of alternator, radiator and base frame. It arrives on a low-loader, not in a van.

Estimate 2 — Fuel burn and tank life

A modern diesel genset burns roughly 0.27 litres per kWh generated (specific fuel consumption ≈ 210–220 g/kWh). At a typical essential-bus loading of 80 %:

P = 0.80 × 1 000 kW = 800 kW Fuel rate = 0.27 L/kWh × 800 kW ≈ 216 L/h

That is a full 200 L oil drum every 56 minutes. A 10 000 L day tank therefore lasts:

t = 10 000 L / 216 L/h ≈ 46 h

— call it two days at 80 % load. Sanity check: 216 L/h of diesel carries about 216 × 10 kWh/L ≈ 2.2 MW of chemical power, of which we extract 0.8 MW electrical — an overall efficiency of ~37 %, exactly where a good diesel sits. The other 1.4 MW leaves as exhaust and radiator heat, which is why the cooling system is a calculation of its own at 50 °C (Equipment technology, below).

Estimate 3 — What "step load" means

A turbocharged diesel can typically accept only ~60 % of its rating in a single block without excessive frequency dip (ISO 8528-5 performance class G2/G3 territory). For our set:

Max first block ≈ 0.60 × 1 250 kVA ≈ 750 kVA

The physics: below the load, the turbocharger is idling; torque beyond the naturally-aspirated capability of the engine must wait ~1–3 s for exhaust energy to spin the turbo up ("turbo lag"). Ask for more and the engine smoke-limits, the frequency dives, and the alternator's internal reactance simultaneously drops the voltage. Hence the staged restart designed in the worked calculation.

One more intuition: while islanded, the grid's inertia is gone. On SEC, switching a 200 kW motor moves the frequency by nothing measurable. On the island, the only rotating energy is the genset's own flywheel and rotor — a few megajoules. The same motor start is now a 16 % load step on the only machine holding 60 Hz. Everything the plant does while islanded, the engine governor feels personally.

Field shortcut: fuel L/h ≈ 0.27 × kW load, and autonomy h ≈ tank litres / (0.27 × kW). Two numbers from the logbook — tank dip and kW meter — give you autonomy on a phone calculator during any outage.

Worked Sizing Study — Rating, Steps, Dips and Fuel

Worked calculation

Four linked calculations, in the order a designer does them: steady rating → step-load sequence → transient dip per step → fuel autonomy.

Part A — Steady-state rating from the essential load

The audited essential-load list (after diversity — not every essential load runs simultaneously) totals ≈ 1.0 MW at pf 0.85:

S_ess = P / pf = 1 000 kW / 0.85 = 1 176 kVA

Select the next standard rating with margin:

S_DG = 1 250 kVA (ESP, quoted at 50 °C site ambient) ≥ 1 176 kVA ✓

Margin = 1 250 / 1 176 = 1.06 — only 6 %, which is acceptable only because the rating is already derated to site conditions (see Equipment settings) and the essential list is policed. Sanity check: 1 250 kVA at 400 V is I = 1 250 000 / (1.732 × 400) ≈ 1 804 A — this is the essential-bus FLC that also sizes the ATS (§17) and the generator breaker (§14).

Part B — Step-load (staged restart) design

Step capability ≈ 60 % of rating in one block:

S_step,max = 0.60 × 1 250 = 750 kVA

So the PMS/ATS restart sequence is designed so no stage exceeds 750 kVA of starting demand (motors counted at starting kVA, not running kW). Indicative sequence:

StageDelay after transferLoads picked up Starting demandRunning demand (cum.)

Note stage 0 transfers held loads instantly; all motor contactors are deliberately dropped out on loss of voltage and re-closed only by the sequence — exactly the discipline whose absence crashed the set in the failure story.

Part C — Transient voltage dip per step (concept formula)

During the first cycles of a load step, the alternator behaves like an EMF behind its subtransient reactance X″d. The fractional voltage dip is approximately the step's apparent power as a fraction of the machine base, scaled by that reactance:

dip ≈ X″_d × S_step / S_gen

With a typical X″d ≈ 0.12 pu for a low-voltage alternator, the worst stage (750 kVA starting block):

dip ≈ 0.12 × 750 / 1 250 = 0.072 → ≈ 7.2 %

Bus voltage momentarily ≈ 0.93 pu ≈ 371 V — recoverable by the AVR within ~1–2 s, and above the ~0.85 pu contactor drop-out danger zone ✓. For comparison, a single 300 kVA soft-started step gives 0.12 × 300/1 250 ≈ 2.9 % — invisible. This formula is deliberately crude (it ignores the step's power factor and the AVR forcing) but it is the right first filter: anything over ~15 % by this estimate needs the vendor's transient-performance curves and probably a bigger machine or a softer start. Frequency dips follow the same logic with the engine/governor instead of the reactance — vendors quote both against ISO 8528-5 performance classes.

Part D — Fuel autonomy

Policy: ≥ 24 h on site without refuelling at realistic load (here 850 kW average over a long outage):

Fuel rate = 0.27 L/kWh × 850 kW ≈ 230 L/h Day tank: 10 000 L / 230 L/h ≈ 43 h ≥ 24 h ✓

If the policy were 48 h at full essential load (1 000 kW → 270 L/h), required storage = 270 × 48 ≈ 13 000 L — the day tank alone is not enough and a bulk tank with automatic transfer pump is needed. Sanity check: 43 h × 230 L/h = 9 890 L ≈ tank volume ✓.

Anatomy of a Desert Genset

Equipment technology

Walk the machine end to end — every subsystem below has killed a "perfectly good" standby set somewhere, usually in summer.

  • Engine. Turbocharged, aftercooled V12/V16 diesel, 1 800 rpm for 60 Hz with a 4-pole alternator (rpm = 120 × f / poles = 120 × 60 / 4). The turbocharger is what makes 1 050 kW from 40-odd litres possible — and what imposes the 60 % step limit. The aftercooler matters doubly at 50 °C: charge air enters the engine hotter, so the same boost delivers less oxygen — part of the temperature derate.
  • Alternator. Brushless synchronous machine: a small exciter generator on the same shaft feeds the main rotor field through shaft-mounted rotating diodes — no slip rings, no brushes, nothing to wear in dusty air. The AVR controls the exciter field; a PMG (permanent magnet generator pilot) powers the AVR independently of the main output, so excitation survives the voltage collapse of a close-in fault and the machine can drive its 51V protection. Insulation class H, used at class F rise — the margin is the 50 °C ambient.
  • Cooling. A set radiator rejecting ~1.2 MW of heat must do it into 50 °C air with a top-tank limit around 100 °C — roughly half the temperature difference assumed by a European catalogue, so nearly double the core area/airflow. Specify the radiator for 50+ °C explicitly; many "tropicalised" sets stop at 46 °C. Dust screens on the inlet, and discharge ducting so hot air is not recirculated inside an enclosure.
  • Fuel system. Day tank (10 000 L here) feeding the engine by gravity/lift pump; bulk tank with transfer pumps for longer autonomy policies. Desert specifics: fuel polishing (recirculating filtration) or a scheduled sample/drain regime against water and microbial growth, tank shading or burial against 60+ °C skin temperatures, water-separator filters in duplex (changeable while running), and a low-level alarm wired to the PMS — the failure story's second act lives entirely in this bullet.
  • Starting system. Two independent 24 V DC starter motors and duplicated battery banks with individual chargers — the single most common cause of standby failure worldwide is a flat or sulphated start battery. Battery life at 50 °C is a fraction of catalogue life (same chemistry lesson as §16); some plants specify air-conditioned battery compartments or NiCd start batteries. Jacket-water heaters keep the block warm for fast starts — less critical in Saudi summers, still specified for winter nights.
  • Enclosure. Weatherproof, sand-trap louvred, min IP54-equivalent ingress philosophy for the electrical compartments, with acoustic attenuation (residential-rated canopies achieve ~75 dBA at 1 m; site limits govern). The enclosure is also a thermal system: its airflow design is part of the 50 °C rating, not cosmetic.
  • Generator breaker. An ACB or MCCB at the set, rated for the 1 804 A FLC and the generator's modest fault current — but coordinated with the 38.5 kA grid-side world through the ATS interlocks (§14, §17).

Who Starts It, Who Watches It

Control

The set is run by its own genset controller (engine management, AVR/governor interfaces, protection, alarms) with three operating modes:

  • AUTO — the normal standing state. The controller waits for the remote-start contact from the ATS (§17): contact closes → crank sequence → build voltage/frequency → signal "ready to load". Contact opens → cooldown timer → stop. A set found in MANUAL or OFF during an assessment is a P1 finding: it will not start at 03:00.
  • MANUAL — local start/stop for maintenance, with the operator owning the transfer decisions.
  • TEST — periodic exercise without (or with) transfer, scheduled from the controller or PMS.

Why there is no synchronizing here. This plant uses an open-transition ATS: the essential bus is always connected to either SEC or the DG, never both. The set therefore never runs in parallel with the grid, never needs a synchroniser, sync-check (25) relay, or SEC grid-code interconnection approval — at the price of a ~1 s dead-bus blink at each transfer, which the UPS and the staged restart absorb by design. Plants that cannot tolerate the blink buy closed-transition schemes and inherit the full synchronizing/protection/utility-approval workload (§17 discusses the trade).

Testing — the only proof that standby exists:

  • Weekly: auto-start test, off-load or briefly loaded — proves the start chain (batteries, fuel valves, controller).
  • Monthly: on-load run ≥ 30–60 min at ≥ 30 % load. Diesels run lightly loaded soot their cylinders and turbos ("wet stacking") — light-load testing slowly destroys the machine it is meant to prove. If the essential bus cannot be risked, use a load bank (resistive trailer or permanently installed) to load the set realistically.
  • Annually: full transfer test — actually fail the SEC supply and let ATS + DG + staged restart + retransfer run end to end, witnessed and logged.

The PMS/DCS supervises remotely: run/stop status, kW/kvar/V/Hz, fuel level, battery voltage, lube and coolant parameters, "fail to start" and "not in AUTO" alarms. The controller's event recorder is the equivalent of the breaker's trip log — read it after every real outage.

Sizing Method, Step by Step

Sizing method

  1. Write the essential-load list — load by load, with kW, pf, starting method and a defensible reason each item is essential. Resist scope creep: every kilowatt added is genset, ATS, cable and fuel money.
  2. Apply diversity to get the realistic simultaneous demand (here 1 000 kW / 1 176 kVA).
  3. Design the starting sequence — group loads into stages; compute each stage's starting kVA (motors at 6–7 × FLC if DOL, ~1.5–2.5 × if soft-started/VFD).
  4. Select the rating: S_DG ≥ running demand and 0.6 × S_DG ≥ largest stage's starting kVA. Take the larger machine the two rules give.
  5. Derate to site: demand the ESP rating at 50 °C and site altitude (−1 %/5 °C, −1 %/100 m as a screening rule); re-check rule 4 with the derated number.
  6. Choose the ISO 8528 rating class — ESP for pure standby (this plant); PRP if any sustained operation is foreseeable; never let a vendor quote ESP against a prime-duty need.
  7. Verify transients: dip ≈ X″_d × S_step/S_gen per stage (≤ ~15 % screening limit; check ISO 8528-5 class G2/G3 curves for the real commitment) and frequency dip/recovery from the vendor's load-acceptance data.
  8. Set the fuel autonomy policy (24–48 h typical) and size day + bulk tanks: litres = 0.27 × kW × hours; add the fuel-quality regime (polishing/sampling) to the maintenance plan.
  9. Verify the islanded fault world: generator fault contribution (~2.5–3 × FLC sustained) into the essential bus — confirm downstream protection still operates (§4, §10, 51V above) and the ATS/board ratings cover both source regimes (§17).
  10. Close the loop with ATS and UPS: the crank/start time budget feeds §17's timing diagram and §16's autonomy margin.

The Loads That Cannot Blink

Why this exists

Most of the plant tolerates interruption gracefully. A motor coasts for seconds; a heater doesn't notice; even the lighting can blink. But a small family of loads lives on a different timescale: digital electronics, whose working memory evaporates in milliseconds. The DCS controllers and their I/O, the ESD (emergency shutdown) logic solver, plant telecoms and network switches, analyser systems, and the operator stations. Their power supplies hold up for perhaps 10–20 ms — one 60 Hz cycle, 16.7 ms, is roughly the entire ride-through budget. Lose them and you do not just lose production: you lose visibility and control of the plant during the most abnormal event of the year, and the safety system reverts to its de-energised trip state, slamming the process down hard.

Now put that against the standby-power timeline of §8 and §17: a SEC outage means roughly 3 s of confirmation, 10 s of engine cranking and stabilising, and a transfer — the essential bus is dark for ~10–30 s. To electronics, 15 seconds is fifteen thousand lifetimes. Something must carry these loads continuously, with zero transfer time, across the gap — and for long enough afterwards to matter if the engine never starts at all.

That something is the UPS: a rectifier, a battery and an inverter arranged so the critical bus is always fed from the inverter, with the battery standing behind it. There is no "switchover" to fail because there is no switchover: when SEC dies, the only thing that changes is where the inverter's DC comes from — the rectifier stops pushing and the battery starts pulling, on the same DC busbar, seamlessly, in zero milliseconds. The UPS turns the worst 30 seconds of the plant's year into a non-event for the loads that think in microseconds.

The two sizing questions are independent and people regularly confuse them:

  • kVA rating — how much load the power electronics can carry continuously (here 100 kVA). This is an electronics thermal rating.
  • Autonomy — how long the battery can hold that load (here 30 min). This is an electrochemistry rating, and it is the one that silently decays with age and temperature.
The UPS exists because the DG needs ~10–30 seconds and the DCS has ~10 milliseconds. The kVA rating sizes the electronics; the autonomy sizes the battery; and at 50 °C the battery is a wasting asset whose real autonomy must be measured, not assumed from the nameplate.

A Failure Story: the Thirty Minutes That Were Four

What goes wrong without it

Fictional, assembled from utterly standard battery post-mortems. Note that in this story the UPS electronics never miss a beat — the failure is chemistry, and it happened years before the outage.

The setup. The plant's 100 kVA UPS was commissioned eight years ago with a VRLA (valve-regulated lead-acid) battery string: "10-year design life, 30 minutes autonomy" on the data sheet. The fine print nobody re-read: design life is quoted at 25 °C. The battery room's air-conditioner was downsized in a cost review, and for the last five summers the cells have lived at 35–40 °C — with weeks nearer 45 when the AC struggled. The rule of thumb for lead-acid is merciless: service life halves for every 10 °C above 25 °C. At an average of 37 °C, the "10-year" battery is a 4-year battery. It is now eight years old — twice dead. Nothing alarms: float voltage looks normal (float voltage measures the charger, not the battery), the UPS panel shows a green battery icon, and the annual "battery test" has quietly become a 30-second float-voltage reading because a real discharge test "risks the plant".

The event. 14:10, August, SEC outage. The UPS transfers to battery flawlessly. The control room sees "On battery — estimated runtime 30:00". The DG cranks — and fails to start: attempt one, attempt two, attempt three; "FAIL TO START" (the starting batteries live in the same hot room — same chemistry, same lie). Operators begin an orderly plant shutdown, budgeting on thirty minutes of DCS visibility.

The reality. The aged cells, internal resistance tripled by dried electrolyte and grid corrosion, sag immediately under the 90 kW load. The runtime estimate recalculates downward in lurches: 30:00, 19:00, 08:00. At minute four the string hits the low-DC disconnect and the inverter drops the critical bus. Every operator screen goes black mid-procedure. The ESD does its de-energise-to-trip duty and slams every valve in the plant simultaneously. Compressors trip on their own mechanical protections; the flare takes the inventory. The plant is now dark, blind, and hard-shutdown — the precise scenario the UPS was bought to prevent — with hours of recovery and a list of thermally-shocked equipment.

The audit afterwards finds the whole chain:

  • Battery room temperature never alarmed because no one set a battery-room temperature alarm.
  • No periodic capacity (discharge) test — the only test that measures autonomy — in eight years.
  • No battery monitoring system: per-block voltages and internal-resistance trends would have shown the decay years earlier.
  • Replacement was in the 10-year budget plan — calendar-based, not condition-based, and indexed to a 25 °C datasheet life the room never provided.
A UPS battery fails silently and gradually, then completely and instantly. Float voltage proves nothing about capacity. The only honest numbers are a measured discharge test and an internal-resistance trend — at 50 °C ambient country, treat the datasheet life as a fiction and the battery room's thermometer as a protection device.

Two Wardrobes of Lead, One Car Battery of Perspective

Feel for the numbers

Estimate 1 — How much energy is "100 kVA for 30 minutes"?

P ≈ 100 kVA × 0.9 pf = 90 kW; E ≈ 90 kW × 0.5 h ≈ 45–50 kWh drawn from the battery

Calibrate that: a healthy car battery stores ~0.7 kWh — so the UPS battery must deliver the useful content of ~70 car batteries in half an hour. In hardware it is a string of 240 two-volt cells, each the size of a large shoebox and ~30–50 kg, racked in tiers: roughly two wardrobes' worth of lead occupying its own ventilated room, several tonnes on the slab. Battery rooms are civil engineering, not a cupboard decision.

Estimate 2 — The discharge current

I ≈ 95 kW / 456 V ≈ 210 A — continuously, for 30 minutes

(The numbers are derived properly in the worked calculation.) 210 A DC is welding- cable current: this is why battery strings are connected with bolted bus links and why one corroded inter-cell connector — a few extra milliohms at 210 A — becomes a hot spot dissipating ~100 W, and the string's weakest link in both senses.

Estimate 3 — The inverter's party trick

The inverter takes raw DC and manufactures a 230/400 V, 60.00 Hz sine wave by switching IGBTs at several kilohertz — slicing the DC into thousands of pulse-width-modulated slivers per cycle and filtering them smooth. The output is typically cleaner than SEC's own waveform (THD < 2 % vs the 3–5 % of a working industrial bus). The critical bus is, ironically, the best power in the plant — synthesised ~80 times per millisecond from a row of batteries.

And the temperature intuition, since it dominates this whole topic: chemical reaction rates roughly double per 10 °C (Arrhenius). The same doubling that ages the plates also applies to self-discharge and grid corrosion. A battery at 45 °C is living roughly four times faster than its 25 °C datasheet — everything on the battery data sheet is a 25 °C number unless it says otherwise.

Worked Battery Autonomy Sizing — 100 kVA / 30 min

Worked calculation

Step 1 — Power the battery must deliver

Load: 100 kVA at pf 0.9 → P_ac = 90 kW. The inverter is ~94 % efficient, and the battery must supply its losses too:

P_dc = P_ac / η_inv = 90 / 0.94 ≈ 95.7 kW

Step 2 — The DC voltage during discharge (be explicit — this is where sizings go wrong)

DC bus: 240 lead-acid cells × 2.0 V nominal = 480 V. But the battery does not discharge at nominal. Three voltages matter:

  • Float (charged, standing): 2.25–2.27 V/cell → ~542 V
  • End-of-discharge (EOD) limit: 1.75 V/cell → 420 V — below this the inverter disconnects to save the cells;
  • Mean discharge voltage over the 30 min: ≈ 1.90 V/cell → 456 V — this is the honest number to divide by.

Step 3 — Mean discharge current

I_dc = P_dc / V_mean = 95 700 / 456 ≈ 210 A

(Cross-check at worst case: near EOD, 95 700 / 420 ≈ 228 A — cables, fuses and the battery breaker are sized for this end-of-discharge current, not the mean.)

Step 4 — Raw capacity at the 30-minute rate

C_raw = I × t = 210 A × 0.5 h = 105 Ah (at the 30-min discharge rate)

Step 5 — Apply the design factors

Standard practice (IEEE 485 philosophy): an aging factor 1.25 (battery is replaced when it falls to 80 % of rated capacity — sizing must work on the day before replacement), and a design margin ~1.1 for load growth and temperature/initial-capacity allowances:

C_design = 105 × 1.25 × 1.1 ≈ 144 Ah → select a ~150 Ah (30-min-rate) cell

Step 6 — The Peukert honesty clause

"150 Ah at the 30-minute rate" is not a catalogue "150 Ah" battery. Capacity is rate-dependent: a cell labelled 150 Ah at the 10-hour rate (15 A) might deliver only ~60–70 % of that at a 30-minute rate, because high currents don't give the acid time to diffuse into the plates (the Peukert effect). Vendors therefore publish discharge tables: amps (or watts) per cell vs time vs EOD voltage — real sizing means entering that table at 1.75 V/cell, 30 min, and 210 A × factors, and reading off the cell type. Our calculation predicts which row of the table we will land on; the table has the final word.

Step 7 — Sanity check in energy

E = 95.7 kW × 0.5 h ≈ 48 kWh delivered; string stores ≈ 456 V × 150 Ah ≈ 68 kWh nominal

We use ~70 % of nominal content — consistent with stopping at 1.75 V/cell and the rate penalty. If the arithmetic had demanded > 100 % of the string's energy, a step would be wrong. ✓

Step 8 — Recharge check

Rule: recover within ~10 × the discharge time. The rectifier must carry the full 95.7 kW load and recharge ~48 kWh (plus ~15 % charge inefficiency) in ≈ 5 h → ~11 kW of recharge power, i.e. ~24 A at 456 V — about 0.15 C, well within lead-acid acceptance. The rectifier is therefore sized ~110–115 % of the inverter's demand. ✓

Inside the Box, and the Chemistry Question

Equipment technology

Topologies — why industrial means double-conversion online (IEC 62040-3 class VFI):

  • Offline/standby — load on raw mains; inverter wakes on failure (~5–10 ms gap). Acceptable for a desktop PC, not for an ESD system.
  • Line-interactive — mains conditioned by an autotransformer, inverter assists; still a transfer instant, still passes mains frequency events through.
  • Double-conversion online — rectifier makes DC, inverter remakes AC, all the power, all the time. Zero transfer time, full isolation of the load from every mains sag/swell/frequency excursion. The 2–4 efficiency points it costs are the price of the only topology with no "switchover" failure mode. This is what "UPS 100 kVA" means in this manual.

The power path: an IGBT rectifier (PWM, near-unity input pf, low harmonic injection back into the LV-MSB — older thyristor six-pulse units were a harmonics source that mattered to the detuned PFC bank, §7); the DC link + battery; an IGBT inverter switching at several kHz; the static bypass switch — back-to-back thyristors that can pick the load up in under a quarter cycle; and the maintenance (wraparound) bypass — a manually operated external switch that carries the load on raw mains while the entire UPS is isolated, dead and worked on. No wraparound bypass = no safe UPS maintenance = a "critical" bus that must be killed to service its own protector. Check for it on every site assessment.

Watching the Watchman

Control

  • UPS HMI / controller: mimic of the power path (rectifier — battery — inverter — bypass), measured kVA/kW per phase, battery current and voltage, estimated runtime, event log. The event log outlives operator memory — read it on every assessment: repeated "on battery" entries reveal an upstream supply problem the plant has normalised.
  • Battery monitoring system (BMS for stationary strings): per-block (or per-cell) voltage, inter-cell connection resistance, internal impedance trend, and string temperature. Impedance creeping up ≈ capacity going down — the trend is the early-warning the failure story lacked. On Li-ion the BMS is mandatory and integral.
  • Alarms to DCS/PMS: on-battery, autonomy-low, on-bypass, battery breaker open, room temperature high, DC earth fault. "UPS on bypass" deserves the same operator urgency as "DG not in AUTO" — in both states the plant has standby equipment that will not perform.
  • EPO (emergency power off): the hard-wired mushroom that kills the critical bus for fire/rescue. It must be supervised, shrouded, and tested with ceremony — a momentary EPO operation is a full critical-bus outage, and accidental EPO operation is a well-known own-goal category.
  • Interaction with the standby chain: the UPS rectifier is itself an essential load on the ATS-fed bus (§17) — when the DG picks up, the rectifier restarts (walk-in current limit avoids a step on the genset) and begins recharging — which is exactly the ~11 kW recharge load counted in the essential list of §8.

Sizing Method, Step by Step

Sizing method

  1. List the true UPS loads — DCS/ESD, telecoms, analysers, operator stations — with measured (not nameplate) VA. Police scope creep ruthlessly: every printer and coffee machine that migrates onto the critical bus steals autonomy from the ESD system. A UPS load list is a privilege list.
  2. Set the kVA rating: measured load + 20–25 % growth margin, with inrush/crest-factor checks for any switched-mode-heavy load mix → here 100 kVA.
  3. Set the autonomy policy: either "bridge the DG start × a fat safety factor" (minutes) or "ride a full DG failure long enough for an orderly plant shutdown" (tens of minutes). This plant chose 30 min — the orderly-shutdown policy (see §17 for the honest margin discussion).
  4. Choose the chemistry for 50 °C reality (table above): VRLA + guaranteed HVAC, or NiCd, or engineered Li-ion. Decide on the whole-life cost including replacements at desert life, not purchase price.
  5. Fix the DC architecture: cells in string (240 × 2 V lead-acid → 480 V here), EOD voltage, then cell Ah from the vendor's rate-specific discharge table with aging 1.25 and design margin (worked calculation above → ~150 Ah class at the 30-min rate).
  6. Check the recharge: rectifier sized for load + recharge within ~10 × discharge time; confirm the recharge kW appears in the genset essential list.
  7. Engineer the room: floor loading, hydrogen ventilation (IEC 62485-2), HVAC with alarmed temperature, access for the biennial discharge test (load-bank connection point!).
  8. Close the protection loop: battery breaker/fuse rated for EOD current (~230 A here) and DC fault duty; downstream critical-board MCBs coordinated with the inverter's ~150 % current limit and the bypass's higher let-through (§14).

The nameplate says 30 minutes; the last capacity test report says what you actually have.

The Decision Nobody Is Awake to Make

Why this exists

Strip the standby system to its essence and there are four decisions:

  1. "SEC is really dead" — not a 200 ms voltage dip from a remote fault, not a single-phase disturbance, but a genuine outage worth waking a diesel for.
  2. "Start the engine — now."
  3. "The generator is ready; move the essential bus" — and never, under any combination of failures, connect both sources at once.
  4. "SEC is really back; go home" — not on the first flicker of recovery, but when the grid has proven itself stable; then cool the engine down and return to watch.

Every one of these is time-critical, safety-involved and dull beyond human endurance. An operator could make them — at 10:00 on a weekday, with coffee. The plant needs them made identically at 03:12 on the second day of Eid, in a sandstorm, in under fifteen seconds. That is the ATS: a voltage-watching brain bolted to a mechanically interlocked pair of contacts, whose entire personality is a handful of thresholds and timers. This section is about choosing those numbers, because every single one is a trade-off between speed (the loads want power back) and stability (the system must not be jerked around by a twitchy grid).

The ATS is also the keystone of the interlock philosophy: it is the one device whose construction physically guarantees the DG never backfeeds the SEC network (a lethal hazard to line crews and a Saudi Grid Code violation) and SEC never feeds into an unsynchronised generator (a destructive hazard to the machine). The brains decide; the mechanical interlock makes certain classes of wrong decision impossible.

The genset makes the power and the UPS hides the gap — but the ATS makes the decisions. Its dozen settings (pickup/dropout windows and five timers) encode the plant's entire outage philosophy. Read them on site and you can reconstruct what the designer feared; get them wrong and the failure story below happens.

A Failure Story: the Switch That Couldn't Wait

What goes wrong without it

Two short fictional case studies — one of bad timers, one of broken interlocks. Both are standard entries in transfer-switch incident folklore.

Case 1 — the four-transfer minute. A plant's ATS was commissioned with the retransfer delay set to 10 seconds ("why make the plant wait half an hour on diesel?" said someone at commissioning, and turned the dial down). One evening a SEC 13.8 kV feeder develops an intermittent fault — the classic dying-cable signature: trip, auto-reclose, hold for a few seconds, flash over again.

The plant's "essential" systems took more punishment from their own transfer switch than from the outage. A 30-minute retransfer stabilization timer exists precisely because real grid faults are often intermittent: the first reclose is a claim, not a promise. Diesel fuel for half an hour is the cheapest insurance the plant buys that night.

Case 2 — both sources closed. An older plant, a breaker-pair "ATS" assembled from two ordinary ACBs with only an electrical interlock — an auxiliary-contact wiring scheme, no mechanical linkage, never functionally tested. During a transfer test, a sticky auxiliary contact tells the controller the SEC breaker is open when it is not. The controller closes the DG breaker. For a few hundred milliseconds the running, unsynchronised generator is paralleled with the SEC network: the phase difference appears across the alternator's subtransient reactance and the resulting current is fault-magnitude with the full grid behind it. The alternator end-windings are mechanically wrecked, its shaft coupling is sheared, and the upstream protection trips the plant intake — converting a routine test into a total outage plus a six-figure rewind. This is why a real transfer switch has a mechanical interlock — a physical linkage that makes both-closed geometrically impossible — with the electrical interlock as the second layer, not the only one.

The two cardinal ATS sins: impatience (short retransfer timers that let an unstable grid batter the essential bus) and trust (electrical- only interlocks between sources that must never meet). Both are invisible in normal operation and devastating in the abnormal week the ATS exists for.

Fifteen Seconds, Budgeted Like Money

Feel for the numbers

The whole forward transfer lives inside about fifteen seconds, and every slice has an owner and a reason:

  • 1–3 s — detection and confirmation. Could the ATS react in 50 ms? Electrically yes — and it would then start the diesel for every remote 13.8 kV fault that SEC's own protection clears in 200 ms, every motor-start sag, every lightning-induced dip. The 3 s is deliberate deafness: "I only believe in outages that last longer than the grid's own self-healing."
  • ~10 s — engine start. Crank (2–4 s), fire, accelerate to 1 800 rpm, AVR builds 400 V, governor settles 60 Hz: ~8–10 s for a healthy warm set. NFPA-110-style emergency sets demonstrate 10 s; our spec says DG available ≤ 10 s from start signal.
  • < 1 s — the transfer itself. A contactor-type ATS moves in ~100 ms; with a deliberate open-transition pause (let motor flux decay before re-energising) call it 0.5–1 s.

Estimate — the dark window

t_dark ≈ 3 s (confirm) + 10 s (DG start & stabilise) + 0.5 s (transfer) ≈ 13.5 s

Call it 12–15 s of darkness for the essential bus — exactly the gap the UPS (§16) was bought to bridge, with its 30-minute autonomy standing behind a 13.5-second requirement. That ratio (~130:1) is not waste; it is the honest price of the question "and what if the engine doesn't start?" — examined in the worked calculation.

For scale at the other end: the retransfer stabilization timer is 30 minutes — over a hundred times longer than the entire forward transfer. Going to standby is urgent; coming home is optional. The asymmetry is the design philosophy in one sentence: fail toward the source you control.

Now the honest margin question: 13.5 s of need vs 30 min of UPS

Strict bridging requirement: 13.5 s. Even allowing the full crank program — three 10 s attempts with 10 s rests (§8) — the worst "engine eventually starts" case is:

t = 3 + (10+10) + (10+10) + 10 + 0.5 ≈ 54 s

So why 30 minutes? Because the autonomy policy is not sized for the success case. It is sized for "FAIL TO START" at 03:00: the time for an operator to acknowledge, try a manual start, accept defeat, and then execute an orderly, instrumented plant shutdown with the DCS and ESD alive — realistically 15–25 minutes. The 30-minute battery is not a 130× safety factor on a 13.5 s event; it is a 1.2–2× factor on the real worst case. State it that way in design reports, or the next cost review will "right-size" the battery to five minutes and quietly delete the plant's last line of defence.

Three Ways to Build a Transfer Switch

Equipment technology

  • Contactor-type (dedicated ATS). Two mechanically interlocked contactor mechanisms on a common frame, purpose-built to IEC 60947-6-1, driven by a solenoid/motor mechanism in ~100 ms. Compact, fast, the default at this size. Its fault credentials are withstand and close-on ratings (carry a fault until the upstream breaker clears it; close onto a faulted bus and survive) — it is not a circuit breaker and interrupts nothing (see Sizing).
  • Breaker-type ATS / dual-breaker schemes. Two ACBs (§14) with motor operators, plus both electrical and mechanical interlocks (key-exchange or linkage). Slower (~1–3 s) but each source path now has full protection and interrupting capability built in, and maintenance uses standard breaker racking. Common at switchboard scale — this is effectively how an essential-bus incomer pair is built when the "ATS" is part of the LV-MSB lineup. Failure story Case 2 is the warning: the mechanical interlock is not optional.
  • Transition types.
    • Open (break-before-make) — our plant: a deliberate dead-bus instant; simple, no paralleling, no grid-code entanglement; the UPS eats the blink.
    • Closed (make-before-break) — parallels DG and SEC for < 100 ms during retransfer: no blink, but requires synchronising, reverse-power protection (§8), utility approval under the Saudi Grid Code, and fault-level coexistence of both sources. Bought when the process truly cannot blink twice.
    • Delayed transition (center-off) — an enforced pause in a center position, sized for motor-flux decay; the standard answer for motor-heavy buses transferred live.
  • Bypass-isolation construction. A second, manually operated transfer path wrapped around the automatic one, so the ATS mechanism can be isolated, racked out and serviced while the essential bus stays fed. Without it, ATS maintenance means an essential-bus outage — which is why it never gets maintained, which is how 20-year-old ATS mechanisms come to fail on demand. Same wraparound philosophy as the UPS maintenance bypass (§16). Specify it for any bus that cannot take a planned outage.
  • 4-pole switching. On this TN-S system the neutral is switched too — see Sizing, step 4, for the earthing logic.

See the ATS catalogue section for indicative product families and ratings structure.

The Logic, the Wire, and the Fail-Safe

Control

  • The controller is a small dedicated logic unit (or a protection- grade relay in breaker-pair schemes) running exactly the state machine of the worked timing diagram: sense → confirm → start → accept → transfer → watch → retransfer → cooldown. Its settings are the table above; its event log is the plant's outage history, timestamped — photograph it on every assessment.
  • The engine-start wire is the most important two-core cable in the standby system, and it is wired fail-safe: the convention is a contact that closes to start, supervised, on a dedicated route — and the scheme must be reviewed for its failure mode. Ask of every failure (broken wire, dead ATS controller, lost DC): does the engine start or stay silent? Many schemes add a "start on loss of signal" fallback at the genset controller so a severed cable errs toward a running engine. Whatever the choice, it must be a documented decision, and the cable separated from the tray routes whose fire would create the very outage it serves.
  • Remote status to PMS/DCS: position (normal/emergency), source availabilities, "in AUTO", transfer in progress, fail-to-transfer, exerciser results. The two alarms that must wake somebody: "ATS not in AUTO" and "DG fail to start" — both mean the plant currently has no automatic standby and nobody would otherwise know.
  • Periodic auto-test: the controller's exerciser starts the DG weekly (off-load) and transfers monthly (on-load, §8 Control), at a scheduled hour with operations informed — an unannounced 1 s blink is a fine way to discover which "non-critical" loads were secretly critical.
  • Manual override: padlockable manual transfer handles and a legible procedure, for the day the controller itself is the casualty. Verify the handle is reachable, the procedure laminated, and at least one shift electrician has actually done it.

Sizing Method, Step by Step

Sizing method

  1. Rated current: ATS continuous rating ≥ essential-bus FLC. Here the bus is sized to the DG: 1 250 kVA at 400 V → 1 804 A → a 2 000 A class switch, with the 50 °C switchroom derating checked against the manufacturer's temperature table (same discipline as breakers, §14 step 7).
  2. Withstand and closing ratings vs the real fault world: on the SEC side the ATS sits downstream of the LV-MSB with prospective fault 38.5 kA (§4). The ATS does not interrupt — so it needs (a) a short-time withstand (Icw) or a certified conditional rating with the specific upstream breaker/fuse covering 38.5 kA for the upstream clearing time, and (b) a close-on rating for the worst asymmetric peak, because the ugliest duty is closing onto a faulted bus. Verify the certified combination, not two separate numbers on two data sheets.
  3. Number of poles — 4-pole for this TN-S system. The DG is a separately earthed source (its neutral-earth bond is at the set). If the neutral were not switched, the DG and SEC neutral-earth bonds would be permanently paralleled: earth-fault and neutral currents could return via the earthing system (defeating clean TN-S separation and any RCD/earth-fault measurement (§10)), and a "dead" plant during maintenance could carry live neutral potential from the other source. Switching all four poles (overlapping-neutral designs avoid floating the load neutral mid-transfer) keeps exactly one neutral-earth bond in service per state. 3-pole is correct only where the DG neutral is unearthed and bonded solely through the plant (one-bond schemes) — a deliberate earthing-design decision, never a cost shortcut.
  4. Transition type: open transition here (UPS bridges, motors restarted in stages); choose delayed transition for live motor-bus transfer; choose closed transition only with the full synchronising/protection/Grid-Code package costed in.
  5. Bypass-isolation: yes/no by outage policy — for this plant's essential bus, specified yes: the essential bus cannot take a planned dead-bus maintenance window every year.
  6. Close the loop: timer set per the worked timing diagram; engine-start wiring failure-mode review; UPS autonomy re-checked against the worst crank sequence; protection re-checked for the islanded (low fault level) regime (§8 step 9).

An ATS is a dozen numbers wrapped around one mechanical promise. Where the money is — and where the consequences are

The calculations that dominate capital cost are load/transformer sizing (§1–§3) and the fault-level pair (§4, §14): between them they fix ACB frames, breaking capacities, busbar bracing, cable cross-sections and the 1 250 kVA genset — the most expensive items on the plant. PFC (§7) and cable volt-drop (§6) mainly buy back operating cost and SEC reactive-power penalties. The standby chain (§8, §16, §17) is a different category again: moderate capital, extreme consequence asymmetry — a mis-timed ATS relay or a dead battery string costs little to fix and an entire plant shutdown to ignore. The studies (§9–§12, §15) are mostly engineering hours whose value is measured in avoided injuries and unplanned downtime.

Verify in this order: §1–§4 ratings first (if transformer/breaker/busbar numbers are wrong, everything downstream is at risk); then the life-safety pair §11–§12; then the standby chain §8/§16/§17 — the part of the plant that is only ever tested by its worst day. Everything else follows.

Interpretation questions

These test your ability to draw conclusions from numbers — the skill the PM will assess when reviewing your FMECA entries. Attempt each before opening the answer.

  1. The LV-MSB incomer ammeter reads 2 650 A on a 1 600 kVA transformer. The transformer FLC at 400 V is calculated as 2 309 A. What do you conclude, and what is your immediate action?

    Show answer

    The transformer is loaded at 2 650 / 2 309 = 1.15 pu (115 %) — significantly above rated FLC. This is an overload condition, made worse by the 50 °C ambient eating the thermal margins.

    Immediate FMECA implication: continuous overload accelerates insulation ageing. If this is normal operating load, the transformer is undersized for its duty. If the other transformer is lost and the bus-tie closes to back-feed from this overloaded unit, it will trip on overcurrent — the N-1 redundancy claim fails.

    Action: record the reading as an observation (OBS-xx in SF-04); open an FMECA row for TX overloading (S = 4 — loss of N-1 capability; O depends on whether the overload is sustained; D = 2 if transformer monitoring with alarms is live). Recommend revalidating the load list and considering demand reduction or uprating.

  2. The LV prospective short-circuit current at the LV-MSB is calculated at 38.5 kA for a single transformer. The bus-tie ACB nameplate shows a rated breaking capacity of 36 kA at 400 V. The two-transformer tie-closed fault level is 77 kA. Implications?

    Show answer

    Single-transformer case: 36 kA < 38.5 kA — even in the normal condition the bus-tie breaker is under-rated by ~7 %. A breaker must never be put in a position where it may be asked to interrupt more than Icu: potential P1 safety finding.

    Tie-closed parallel case: ~77 kA — more than double the rating. Operating with the bus-tie closed and no ACB upgrade is a life-safety hazard, and the board bracing (rated 50–65 kA) is also exceeded.

    FMECA row: S = 5 (catastrophic switchboard failure on through-fault); P1 regardless of RPN. Actions: confirm fault level with a detailed study using SEC source data (25–40 kA at 13.8 kV); enforce strict normally-open bus-tie operation with interlocks; specify replacement breakers with adequate Icu if paralleling is ever required (§14).

  3. The arc-flash label on the LV-MSB Bus A incomer cubicle shows 22 cal/cm², derived from a study conducted years ago. Since then, the second transformer (TX-2) has been connected, roughly doubling the available fault current to the bus. How do you treat this finding?

    Show answer

    The old study is Obsolete — log it as such. With the changed source, both the arcing current and (critically) the clearing times of every device have changed; the incident energy at the bus is unknown, and the PPE category printed on the label may be unsafe (§11).

    Immediate QHSE action: flag the out-of-date label; no energised work against the old label values until a restudy.

    FMECA row: "arc-flash label obsolete" — S = 5 (personnel injury); O = 2 (switching events occasional); D = 4 (error undetectable without a study). Escalate before the next live switching operation; commission an updated IEEE 1584 study and relabel.

Four problems covering the Rev 3 deep-dive topics. Fully worked answers below each.

  1. (Breaker LSIG choice.) A feeder ACB on the LV-MSB supplies an essential MCC. Feeder design current IB = 850 A; the largest downstream motor is 75 kW (FLC 136 A, DOL start 882 A); prospective fault at the MCC bus ≈ 20 kA; at the LV-MSB ≈ 38.5 kA.     Show answer

    Frame: next standard above 850 A → In = 1 000 A ACB/MCCB with Icu ≥ 38.5 kA at 400 V (it sits on the 38.5 kA board; use 50 kA class for margin), checked for 50 °C switchroom derating.

    L (long-time): Ir = 0.9 × 1 000 = 900 A ≥ 850 A ✓ — carries the load, protects the cable (cable Iz must be ≥ 900 A or Ir set to the cable, whichever is lower).

    S (short-time): pickup must ride the worst legitimate surge: motor start on top of remaining load ≈ 882 + (850 − 136) = 1 596 A. Set Isd = 4 × Ir = 3 600 A — margin 3 600 / 1 596 = 2.3× ✓.     I (instantaneous): must not operate for faults at the MCC bus (those belong to the MCC devices / the S element): with ~20 kA available there, set Ii = off, or ≈ 25–30 kA so only a fault close to the LV-MSB end of the feeder cable (where current approaches 38.5 kA) trips with zero delay.   2. (UPS battery autonomy.) A smaller UPS carries 80 kVA at pf 0.9 with inverter efficiency 0.93. The battery is 240 lead-acid cells (mean discharge voltage 1.90 V/cell, EOD 1.75 V/cell). Autonomy policy: 15 min. Aging factor 1.25, design margin 1.1. Find the required cell capacity at the 15-min rate, and sanity-check in kWh.

    Show answer

    Pac = 80 × 0.9 = 72 kW;  Pdc = 72 / 0.93 = 77.4 kW

    Vmean = 240 × 1.90 = 456 V → Idc = 77 400 / 456 = 170 A (EOD check: 77 400 / 420 = 184 A — rate the battery breaker for this).

    Raw capacity = 170 A × 0.25 h = 42.5 Ah;  factored = 42.5 × 1.25 × 1.1 = 58 Ah → ~60 Ah class at the 15-min rate (read the vendor's discharge table at 15 min / 1.75 V/cell — a "60 Ah" 10-h-rate catalogue cell will not do it; the rate penalty means a larger nominal cell).

    Sanity (energy): delivered = 77.4 kW × 0.25 h ≈ 19.4 kWh; string nominal ≈ 456 V × 60 Ah ≈ 27 kWh — using ~70 % of nominal, consistent with the EOD limit and high rate ✓.

  3. (ATS timing budget.) An ATS is set with: detection/confirm delay 2 s; DG crank-to-ready 8 s plus 3 s of voltage/frequency stabilisation; transfer (with open-transition pause) 0.5 s. (a) How long is the essential bus dark if the engine starts first crank? (b) If the first crank fails and the program is 10 s crank / 10 s rest before the second (successful) 8 s + 3 s start, what is the dark window? (c) What minimum UPS autonomy would a "bridge the DG start × 3" policy demand, and why does the plant carry 30 min anyway?

    Show answer

    (a) t = 2 + (8 + 3) + 0.5 = 14 s.

    (b) t = 2 + (10 + 10) + (8 + 3) + 0.5 = 33.5 s — a failed crank more than doubles the window; this, not the success case, is the bridging number to quote.

    (c) Policy minimum = 3 × 33.5 ≈ 100 s → ~2 min of battery. The plant nevertheless carries 30 min because the sizing case is "DG never starts": time for a manual start attempt plus an orderly, DCS-visible plant shutdown (~15–25 min). Against that case 30 min is a 1.2–2× margin, not a luxury (§17, §16).

  4. (Arc-flash energy at two clearing times.) An arc-flash study point at the LV-MSB gives E = 18 cal/cm² at the standard working distance with the incomer clearing on its short-time delay, t1 = 0.5 s. A maintenance-mode switch drops the clearing time to t2 = 0.1 s at the same arcing current. (a) Estimate the new incident energy. (b) What PPE reality does each number imply? (c) Estimate both arc-flash boundaries (D = 0.6 m, EB = 1.2 cal/cm²).

    Show answer

    (a) Same arcing current and distance → E ∝ t: E2 = 18 × (0.1 / 0.5) = 3.6 cal/cm².
