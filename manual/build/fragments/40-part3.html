What is a SPOF — and Why It Matters

A single point of failure (SPOF) is any one component or shared element whose failure, on its own, disables a function or de-energises a load that must stay energised. It does not matter how much redundancy exists downstream: if a common element upstream can black it all out, the function has a SPOF.

Why SPOFs matter in a process plant

Driver What happens when a SPOF trips Typical consequence
Availability / production loss Critical load loses supply without warning Process shutdown, spoiled batch, forced restart — hours to days of lost production
Safety Safety system, cooling pump, agitator or ventilation fan de-energises Hazardous condition: thermal runaway, toxic release, loss of fire suppression
Cost Emergency repair, overtime, expedited spares, regulatory notification Often 10–100× the cost of the redundancy that would have prevented it
A SPOF is a system property, not just a component property. Two pumps in parallel give no protection if they share a single supply cable. Always trace from source to load — the SPOF is wherever there is no alternative path.

The SPOF-Hunt Method

Work the single-line diagram systematically, starting at the point of supply and moving toward the loads. Use five repeating steps:

  1. Trace — follow every power path from the utility intake (MV-MET / MV-SWGR) through each transformer (TX-1, TX-2), along each bus section of LV-MSB, through every MCC and feeder, to each final load.
  2. Ask — at every element: "If this one item fails or is isolated for maintenance, what loses supply?"
  3. Flag — if the answer is "a load that must stay up" and there is no alternative path, mark it as a SPOF candidate.
  4. Check common elements — look especially at shared items that sit upstream of apparent redundancy: a single utility incomer, a single MV bus, an un-tied LV bus, a shared outgoing cable or MCC, a single ATS. Redundancy downstream is defeated if these are common.
  5. Mitigate — for each flagged item, propose an alternative path: N+1 equipment, dual feed, auto bus-tie, separate routing, DG/ATS standby, or UPS/DCDB.
Practical tip: Print the master SLD on a large sheet and use a highlighter to trace each path. Mark every element that is a common ancestor of two or more critical loads. Those are your prime SPOF candidates.

SPOF Library — Variants A to E

The five example SLDs below form a teaching library. SLDs A through D are flawed configurations — each isolates one type of SPOF. SLD E is the resilient reference. Study each in sequence: the flaw in A is corrected in B, B's flaw in C, and so on until E carries all the mitigations.

Variant A — Single Transformer, Single LV Bus

Configuration: One utility feed → TX-1 (1600 kVA indicative) → single un-split LV bus (LV-MSB) feeding all loads. No second source, no bus-tie.

SPOF(s) identified: The single transformer TX-1 and the single un-split LV bus are both whole-plant SPOFs. Also: the MV feeder to TX-1 and the LV incomer breaker.

Consequence: Loss of TX-1, its MV feeder, the LV incomer breaker, or any LV bus fault de-energises the entire plant. Total blackout. No load segregation limits the affected zone.

This configuration is common in small or legacy installations. Any single component failure produces a total plant blackout — there is no time to transfer loads because there is nowhere to transfer to.

Mitigation: Move to the master-SLD arrangement — add TX-2 on a split bus with auto-close bus-tie BT (N-1 capability), and provide DG + ATS for essential loads. This is exactly the reference design shown in Variant E.

Variant B — Two Transformers, Two Buses, No Bus-Tie

Configuration: TX-1 feeds Bus A, TX-2 feeds Bus B — two independent buses with no bus-tie. Appears redundant at first glance.

SPOF(s) identified: Each transformer is a SPOF for its own bus. The redundancy is illusory: the healthy transformer has no path to the dead bus.

Consequence: Loss of TX-1 drops all of Bus A; loss of TX-2 drops all of Bus B. Half the plant is lost on any single transformer or feeder failure — even though two transformers are installed.

The "two transformers but still not N-1" lesson: The number of transformers is irrelevant if there is no means to transfer load between buses. N-1 requires both the equipment and the interconnection.

Mitigation: Install a normally-open auto-close bus-tie (BT) between Bus A and Bus B. The bus-tie must close automatically on loss of either incomer. Verify that either transformer can carry the essential plant load in N-1 condition (see transformer loading calculations in Part 2).

Variant C — Single Utility Incomer / Single MV Breaker

Configuration: The LV side is well-arranged (TX-1, TX-2, bus-tie), but all power flows through a single utility feeder, single MV incomer breaker [52-I], and single MV bus.

SPOF(s) identified: Single utility feeder, single MV incomer CB, and the single MV busbar. The SPOF has moved upstream — it is now at the MV intake, not the LV distribution level.

Consequence: Loss of the utility supply, the incomer breaker, or an MV bus fault blacks out the whole plant regardless of LV redundancy. The LV bus-tie cannot help — both transformers lose their source simultaneously.

This is the most common "hidden SPOF" found in existing plants. Engineers who only examine the LV distribution miss it entirely. Always trace the full power path from the utility boundary.

Mitigation: Provide a second independent utility feeder with a closeable MV bus-section (as in Variant E). Where a second feeder is not feasible, rely on DG + ATS to ride through utility loss for all essential loads. Strengthen MV incomer protection (differential, IDMT O/C, earth fault) to minimise fault duration.

Variant D — Shared Outgoing Cable / Single MCC for Duty + Standby

Configuration: Sources are redundant upstream (TX-1, TX-2, bus-tie), but a single shared outgoing feeder and single MCC (MCC-1) supplies both a critical duty pump and its standby pump from the same bus section.

SPOF(s) identified: The shared outgoing cable, the MCC-1 bus, and the MCC-1 incomer breaker are all common elements serving both the duty and standby machines.

Consequence: One feeder cable fault, MCC bus fault, or MCC incomer trip takes both duty and standby pumps offline simultaneously. The standby machine provides no protection against the common supply element. The upstream redundancy is wasted.

Variant D demonstrates the general principle: redundancy of equipment is only as effective as the independence of its supply. Always check whether duty and standby loads share any cable, MCC, bus section, or protection device upstream.

Mitigation: Feed duty pump from MCC-1 on Bus A; feed standby pump from MCC-2 on Bus B. Route the two supply cables via physically separated routes. No single cable, MCC, or bus now disables the function.

Variant E — Resilient Reference Layout

Configuration: Dual independent utility incomers, closeable MV bus-section, two full-rated transformers on segregated LV buses with a closed/auto bus-tie, duty and standby pumps split across Bus A and Bus B with separately routed cables, DG + ATS for essential loads, and UPS + DCDB for control and protection supplies.

Why there is no dominant SPOF: Every element that was a SPOF in A–D now has an alternative path:

Element that was a SPOF in A–D Variant Alternative path in E
Single transformer + single un-split LV bus A Two full-rated transformers on segregated buses; N-1 load transfer
Two transformers but no bus-tie B Closed / auto bus-tie BT — fast automatic transfer on loss of either incomer

SPOF-Hardening Principles

The A–E library illustrates every mitigation type in practice. The seven underlying principles below apply to any MV/LV plant. Apply them in priority order: first eliminate the highest-consequence SPOFs, then work down the risk register.

N+1 Redundancy

Install at least one more capacity unit than the minimum required to carry the load. For transformers this means two transformers each rated to carry the essential load in N-1 condition (i.e. each ≥ essential kVA, not total kVA ÷ 2). For motors, one duty + one standby. N+1 is worthless without the means to transfer — see bus-tie below.

Segregation

Split the LV switchboard into Bus A and Bus B with separate incomer CBs and separate LV cable compartments. Physically separate cable routes (different trenches or cable ladders), separate protection panels, and — where practicable — separate room or bay allocations. Segregation limits the zone of any single fault: a bus fault, fire, or arc-flash event on Bus A cannot propagate to Bus B.

Bus-Tie / Auto-Transfer

A normally-open auto-close bus-tie (BT) between Bus A and Bus B lets the surviving transformer back up the dead bus within seconds (typical auto-transfer time: 100–500 ms, indicative). Without the bus-tie, N+1 transformers give no N-1 benefit (Variant B). The bus-tie scheme must be tested periodically (at minimum annually on-load or as defined by the site maintenance plan). The BT protection relay must co-ordinate with the transformer incomers to prevent mal-operation.

Dual Feed

Two independent utility incomers (physically separate cables from separate substations or ring points) with a closeable MV bus-section remove the upstream intake SPOF (Variant C). Where a second utility feeder is not economically or geographically feasible, the DG + ATS combination must cover all loads that cannot tolerate blackout. Document the gap explicitly in the risk register.

Separate Routing of Duty vs Standby

Run the supply cables for a duty load and its standby load via separate MCCs and physically separate cable routes — different cable trenches, ducts or ladder routes that cannot be simultaneously damaged by a single cable fire, flood, or excavation strike. The separation should extend from the LV bus section right back to the MCC incomer (Variant D lesson).

Standby Generation (DG)

A diesel generator (DG) sized for the essential load (typically 30–50 % of installed kVA, indicative) with an automatic transfer switch (ATS) provides a completely independent source independent of the utility supply. The DG covers: total utility failure, MV incomer fault, and transformer outage that would otherwise leave essential loads dark. Weekly no-load test and monthly on-load test are minimum recommended practice. Fuel, coolant, and battery condition are critical.

UPS for Control and Protection

The uninterruptible power supply (UPS) provides AC no-break power to DCS, PLC, and marshalling panels; the DC distribution board (DCDB) provides battery-backed DC (typically 110 V DC or 24 V DC, indicative) for protection relay trip coils, remote indication, and emergency lighting. Without these, a supply disturbance triggers a protection relay loss-of-supply condition, preventing controlled re-energisation. UPS battery condition, bypass functionality, and charger health must be routinely verified.

Proportionality: Every mitigation has a cost, complexity, and footprint trade-off. Apply redundancy where the consequence of failure justifies it. Use the FMECA risk ranking (Section FMECA) to prioritise spend. Essential and safety-critical loads always take precedence.

The checklist is generic and applies to any MV/LV plant of similar configuration; not every item will be present on every site.

Zone 1 — MV Intake / Utility Incomer (MV-MET)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Total plant blackout, including any downstream LV redundancy. Severity: Critical. Typical mitigation: dual independent incomers, DG + ATS for essential loads.
  • Confirm whether the site has ONE or TWO independent utility incomers (physically separate cables from separate utility substations or ring-main points).
  • If a single incomer: verify that DG + ATS coverage for essential loads is sized and commissioned — document the unprotected load gap.
  • Check the MV metering cubicle (MV-MET) — confirm protection relay is present, set, and test records are current.
  • Verify the incoming MV cable route: is the cable protected from physical damage (buried and marked, armoured, away from excavation zones)?
  • Confirm interlock/key scheme between utility incomer and any generator incomer — an accidental parallel condition is a SPOF for the protection system.

Zone 2 — MV Switchgear (MV-SWGR, MV-NER)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Total plant blackout or loss of earth fault detection. Severity: Critical to High. Typical mitigation: MV bus-section breaker, dual-incomer MV switchgear, proven NER scheme.
  • Is the MV switchgear a single bus or a split bus (with a bus-section breaker)? A single MV bus is a whole-plant SPOF.
  • Check that the MV incomer CB(s) have functional spring-charge / charged indicator and trip coil continuity — a failed trip coil is a hidden SPOF for fault clearance.
  • Verify the MV protection relay (IDMT O/C and earth fault) is active, in-service, and last tested within the maintenance interval.
  • Confirm the neutral earthing resistor (MV-NER) is connected, resistance value within tolerance, and has thermal overload monitoring.
  • Check arc-flash / pressure-relief arrangement on the MV switchgear — a single MV arc event that damages the bus will cause a prolonged outage unless the design contains it.
  • Confirm that MV switchgear control / trip power supply is from the UPS or DCDB — a supply disturbance must not disable protection.

Zone 3 — Transformers TX-1 / TX-2

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Loss of one transformer — half the plant if no bus-tie, or full plant if only one transformer installed. Severity: High. Typical mitigation: N+1 sizing, auto bus-tie, differential protection, oil/temperature monitoring.
  • Confirm two transformers are installed (TX-1 and TX-2). If only one transformer: the entire LV plant is a SPOF for that transformer — document and assess DG coverage.
  • Verify each transformer nameplate (kVA, voltage ratio, vector group Dyn11, impedance) matches the design basis; mismatched impedances affect load sharing after N-1 transfer.
  • Check that each transformer has independent MV and LV protection (IDMT O/C, earth fault, winding temperature, oil level / Buchholz gas — as applicable) — shared protection is a common-element SPOF.
  • Confirm the LV incomer CB of TX-1 and the LV incomer CB of TX-2 are separate circuit breakers — not a single shared LV incomer.
  • Verify transformer loading in N-1 condition: is either transformer rated to carry the full essential load if the other is out of service?
  • Check physical separation between TX-1 and TX-2 — are they in separate compartments or bays so a fire or catastrophic failure of one cannot damage the other?

Zone 4 — LV Main Switchboard & Bus-Tie (LV-MSB, BT)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Loss of bus-tie = loss of N-1 capability; single LV bus = whole-plant SPOF. Severity: High to Critical. Typical mitigation: split bus with functional auto-close BT, Form 4 compartmentalisation, arc detection.
  • Confirm the LV-MSB is a split-bus arrangement (Bus A and Bus B as separate bus sections with separate incomer CBs and separate busbars).
  • Verify the bus-tie CB (BT) is present and in service (not permanently open with no auto-transfer scheme).
  • Confirm the bus-tie auto-transfer scheme is commissioned: on loss of Bus A incomer, BT closes automatically to feed Bus A from Bus B (and vice versa). Test records?
  • Check the interlocking logic: both LV incomers and the bus-tie should not be able to close simultaneously (prevents dangerous parallel operation).
  • Verify the LV-MSB construction standard (Form 4 compartmentalisation preferred) — a low-form panel allows a fault to spread across bus sections, defeating the split.
  • Check for arc-flash detection (optical or pressure) on the LV-MSB — a bus arc event in a high-fault-level plant is a high-consequence SPOF for the LV distribution.
  • Confirm that Bus A and Bus B feeders to critical loads are balanced — a heavily unbalanced split means N-1 transfer may overload the surviving transformer.

Zone 5 — MCCs and Outgoing Feeders (MCC-1…n)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Loss of a critical duty and standby pair simultaneously (Variant D pattern). Severity: High. Typical mitigation: duty and standby on separate MCCs / bus sections, separately routed cables.
  • For every critical process function with a duty + standby machine: confirm the duty machine is fed from one MCC / bus section and the standby from a different MCC / bus section.
  • Trace the cable routes for duty and standby pairs — do they share a common cable tray, duct bank, or trench? If yes, a single cable fire defeats the redundancy.
  • Check each MCC incomer protection: is it set to clear faults within the cable rating and not to trip the whole MCC on a single outgoing feeder fault?
  • Confirm the MCC bus bar rating and construction (Form type) — a low-form or under-rated MCC bus is a common SPOF for all loads on that MCC.
  • Verify VFD bypass arrangements where provided — a VFD failure with no bypass on a critical load is a SPOF for the driven process function.
  • Check PFC (power factor correction) connection point — shunt PFC banks failing open can cause voltage/reactive power issues that trip loads indirectly.

Zone 6 — Standby / Essential Supply (DG, ATS)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Essential loads dead on utility failure; ATS or DG unable to start. Severity: Critical (for safety-critical loads). Typical mitigation: regular tested auto-start, redundant ATS control, essential bus clearly defined.
  • Confirm the DG rating is sufficient for the defined essential load — if the essential load list has grown since commissioning, the DG may now be undersized.
  • Verify the essential bus is clearly defined and segregated: only loads that must run on generator should be connected. Non-essential loads left on the essential bus overload the DG and cause it to trip — a SPOF for all essential functions.
  • Check ATS operation: is the transfer from mains to DG automatic on mains failure detection? What is the auto-start to load time (indicative: <15–30 s)?
  • Confirm ATS transfer logic — does a failed ATS cause the essential bus to stay dead? Is there a manual transfer bypass?
  • Verify DG auto-start test records (weekly no-load, monthly on-load indicative). A DG that has not been tested recently is a high-probability SPOF.
  • Check DG fuel level, fuel storage capacity (hours at full essential load), and fuel delivery contract — fuel exhaustion is a simple and avoidable SPOF.
  • Confirm DG battery (starter battery) condition and last replacement date — a failed battery is a very common reason for a DG not to start.

Zone 7 — Control / Protection Supplies (UPS, DCDB)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: DCS / PLC brownout, loss of protection relay trip power, inability to operate circuit breakers. Severity: High (cascades into all other systems). Typical mitigation: redundant UPS, battery monitoring, bypass capability.
  • Confirm a UPS is installed for DCS / PLC / relay panels. Check the rated hold-up time (battery autonomy at full load, indicative minimum 30 min) — is it current or degraded?
  • Verify the UPS battery has been tested (discharge test) within the maintenance interval and that battery string health is monitored (cell voltage, temperature).
  • Check UPS bypass path — can the UPS be bypassed for maintenance without a supply interruption to critical control panels? A UPS with no bypass is itself a SPOF during UPS maintenance or failure.
  • Confirm the DCDB (DC distribution board) is fed from the UPS or a dedicated battery charger, and that DC supplies reach all protection relay trip / close coils.
  • Verify DCDB battery charger AC supply — if it is fed from the same bus as the equipment it is meant to back up, a bus fault defeats the DC backup.
  • Check there is no single MCB or fuse in the DC distribution that, if it operates, cuts power to all relay trip coils simultaneously — that is a common-element SPOF for protection.

Zone 8 — Earthing and Neutral (MV-NER, LV neutral)

Typical consequence if SPOF present: Loss of earth fault protection selectivity, dangerous touch potentials, or undetected earth fault developing into a phase fault. Severity: High (safety). Typical mitigation: periodic resistance check, thermal monitoring, continuity testing of earth conductors.
  • Confirm MV-NER is connected and resistance value is within design tolerance (measured at last maintenance, with certificate).
  • Verify only one NER is connected at a time when the MV bus-section is closed — two parallel NERs alter the earth fault current and can defeat protection settings.
  • Check LV transformer neutral earthing (solidly earthed at one point per bus section) — multiple neutral earthing points or a broken neutral are both SPOFs for LV earth fault protection.
  • Confirm the main earth bar continuity: a broken earth conductor is invisible under normal conditions but becomes a dangerous SPOF during a fault event.
  • Verify LV earth fault relay settings match the NER impedance and cable capacitance — a mis-set relay that fails to detect an earth fault is a protection SPOF.
Complete all eight zones before closing the field inspection. Annotate findings directly on the SLD print. Assign a finding number to each SPOF identified; these numbers flow directly into the risk register and recommendations in Part 4.

Reliability Metrics

SPOF analysis answers where the weak points are. Reliability metrics answer how bad each weak point is, in quantitative terms. These four quantities are the common language of reliability engineering.

Quantity Symbol Formula Meaning
Failure rate λ failures / hour How often a component fails on average
Mean time between failures MTBF 1 / λ Average operating hours between successive failures
Mean time to repair / restore MTTR hours Average time from failure detection to restoration (includes spares, labour, access)
Reliability R(t) e−λt Probability of zero failures in operating time t
Availability A MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) Fraction of time the function is actually available
Unavailability U 1 − A Fraction of time down; multiply by 8760 h/yr for annual downtime estimate

Worked example — single transformer availability

Worked example — Single transformer (TX-1) availability (indicative)

Given indicative data: MTBF = 200,000 h (transformer major failure, oil-filled distribution type), MTTR = 168 h (one week, including spare logistics).

Step 1 — Availability:

A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) = 200,000 / (200,000 + 168) = 0.99916

Step 2 — Unavailability:

U = 1 − 0.99916 = 8.4 × 10⁻⁴

Step 3 — Estimated annual downtime:

Annual downtime = U × 8760 h/yr = 8.4 × 10⁻⁴ × 8760 ≈ 7.4 h/yr

Result: A single transformer is unavailable for approximately 7.4 hours per year on average (indicative). This sets the baseline for comparison with redundant configurations in the RBD section.

All values indicative — use project-specific or published reliability data for real studies.

Practical implication of MTTR: MTTR is strongly influenced by spares holding, access, and contractor response time — all factors within the plant operator's control. A critical spare transformer held on-site can cut MTTR from 168 h to perhaps 24–48 h (indicative), which roughly doubles the annual availability. Reducing MTTR is often the most cost-effective lever available.

Reliability Block Diagrams (RBD)

A reliability block diagram (RBD) models how individual component availabilities combine at the system level. Two fundamental topologies:

Series and Parallel Topologies

Topology Block representation Formula System availability vs components
Series (all must work) [A₁] — [A₂] — [A₃] — … Asys = A₁ × A₂ × A₃ × … Always lower than the worst individual component
Parallel (1-out-of-2) [A₁] in parallel with [A₂] Apar = 1 − (1 − A₁)(1 − A₂) Always higher than either component alone

Worked example — Essential path availability with and without bus-tie

Using the indicative transformer availability derived in Section Reliability Metrics: ATX = 0.99916 each.

Case 1 — Single transformer (SPOF — Variant A):

A = 0.99916 → estimated downtime ≈ 7.4 h/yr

Case 2 — Two transformers, no bus-tie (Variant B) — each bus still a SPOF:

Each bus: A = 0.99916 (unchanged — the two transformers are in series with their own buses, not in parallel with each other without a tie)

Case 3 — Two transformers with auto-close bus-tie (master SLD / Variant E):

With the bus-tie, TX-1 and TX-2 are effectively in a 1-out-of-2 parallel arrangement for the essential path:

A_par = 1 − (1 − 0.99916)² = 1 − (8.4 × 10⁻⁴)² = 1 − 7.1 × 10⁻⁷ ≈ 0.99999929 Annual downtime ≈ (1 − 0.99999929) × 8760 ≈ 0.006 h/yr (about 22 seconds per year — indicative)

Comparison: Adding the bus-tie reduces estimated annual downtime of the essential path from ~7.4 h/yr to ~0.006 h/yr — a factor of approximately 1 200× improvement (indicative), for the cost of one auto-transfer scheme.

Series elements on the essential path (the common-element catch):

The parallel TX benefit is capped by the series elements that both transformers share. For the essential path: MV-SWGR (series) → (TX-1 ‖ TX-2) → LV-MSB bus → BT (series) → MCC → load. Each series element reduces system availability:

A_essential ≈ A_MV-SWGR × A_par(TX) × A_LV-MSB × A_BT × A_MCC

Using indicative values (MV-SWGR: MTBF 500,000 h, MTTR 48 h → A ≈ 0.99990; LV-MSB busbar: MTBF 1,000,000 h, MTTR 72 h → A ≈ 0.99993; BT breaker: MTBF 200,000 h, MTTR 24 h → A ≈ 0.99988; MCC incomer: MTBF 300,000 h, MTTR 8 h → A ≈ 0.99997):

A_essential ≈ 0.99990 × 0.99999929 × 0.99993 × 0.99988 × 0.99997 ≈ 0.99968 → ~28 h/yr estimated downtime (dominated by series elements, not the transformers)

Lesson: The transformer redundancy is so effective that the system availability is now dominated by the series common elements — MV switchgear and bus-tie. This is exactly the Module 3 insight, now quantified. Improving MTTR (faster repair, on-site spares) on these series elements gives more benefit than trying to add further transformer redundancy.

All values indicative — use project failure data for real studies.

MTTR as a Design Lever

An automatic bus-tie (BT) or ATS reduces MTTR for the essential load from hours (manual switching, call-out, access) to seconds (automatic transfer). This single change typically raises availability far more cheaply than chasing a lower component failure rate:

Transfer method MTTR for essential load Estimated annual downtime (illustrative)
Manual switching (call-out, access, operate) ~4–8 h (indicative) ~3–7 h/yr per transformer outage event
Auto-close bus-tie or ATS (100–500 ms) <1 s (indicative) <0.01 h/yr equivalent (effectively zero for most processes)
Improving MTTR through automation (auto-transfer, ATS) is usually the most cost-effective reliability lever. It does not require new hardware on the supply side — only a correctly designed and tested control scheme.

FMECA — Failure Mode, Effects and Criticality Analysis

FMECA (IEC 60812) is the formal tool for converting a SPOF list into a scored, ranked risk register. For each component, you identify its failure modes, trace the local and system effects, and score criticality.

Scoring method — RPN

The most common scoring scheme is the Risk Priority Number (RPN):

Factor Symbol Scale Meaning (higher = worse)
Severity S 1–5 How bad is the worst system-level consequence of this failure mode? (5 = catastrophic / safety-critical)
Occurrence O 1–5 How likely / frequent is this failure mode? (5 = frequent / high failure rate)
Detection D 1–5 How difficult is it to detect before it becomes a system event? (5 = effectively undetectable)
RPN S × O × D 1–125 Overall risk priority — mitigate highest numbers first
Criticality (Crit) S × O 1–25 Severity × Occurrence, used alongside SPOF flag for ranking when detection data is uncertain
Scale guidance (1–5): S=5 = safety-critical or whole-plant blackout; S=4 = essential loads lost; S=3 = significant process impact; S=2 = minor/local disruption; S=1 = negligible. O=5 = frequent (annual or more often); O=1 = very rare (<once per 20 yr). D=5 = failure is latent / undetectable without testing; D=1 = continuously monitored with alarm.

Priority bands

After scoring, assign each row a priority band. These bands are consistent with the SF-03 priority rule in Part 5 — Post-Site Analysis:

Band Condition Action
P1 SPOF with S ≥ 4, or RPN ≥ 60 Immediate action — safety-critical or highest-risk; act before next scheduled outage
P2 RPN 36–59 Short-term — plan and implement within 6–12 months
P3 RPN 16–35 Medium-term — address in next planned maintenance window (12–24 months)
P4 RPN < 16 Monitor — review at next FMECA update; no immediate modification required
Detection (D) is the key lever. Adding condition monitoring (winding temperature, oil level, battery discharge test, relay self-test) lowers D and therefore RPN without changing the hardware or its failure rate. This is often the cheapest mitigation available.

Self-Check Questions

Work through these questions using the SPOF library and reliability sections above. Reveal the answer only after attempting each question.

Q1 — Identifying a whole-plant SPOF

In Variant A (single transformer, single bus), name two distinct elements that are each individually capable of causing a whole-plant blackout.

Show answer

Any two of the following are correct: TX-1 (the single transformer); the single un-split LV bus (LV-MSB); the MV feeder cable to TX-1; the LV incomer circuit breaker. Any single one of these failing removes supply from the entire plant — there is no alternative path and no load segregation.

Q2 — The bus-tie and N-1

Variant B installs two transformers, yet it is not N-1 secure. Explain why — and state exactly what single addition gives N-1 capability (subject to transformer loading).

Show answer

Without a bus-tie, the two transformers are electrically isolated — the healthy transformer has no path to the dead bus. Loss of TX-1 drops all of Bus A; loss of TX-2 drops all of Bus B. Half the plant is lost on any single transformer/feeder failure.

The single addition that restores N-1 capability is a normally-open auto-close bus-tie (BT) between Bus A and Bus B, with an automatic transfer scheme that closes BT within seconds on loss of either incomer — provided the surviving transformer is rated to carry the combined essential load in N-1 condition.

Q3 — Hidden SPOF upstream

Variant C has two transformers, a split bus, and a functional bus-tie. Why can a total plant blackout still occur?

Show answer

Because the SPOF is upstream of the transformers. All power flows through a single utility feeder and a single MV incomer CB on a single MV bus. If the utility supply is lost, the incomer CB fails, or an MV bus fault occurs, both transformers simultaneously lose their source. The LV bus-tie cannot help — it interconnects two dead buses. The lesson: always trace the full path from utility boundary to load, not just the LV distribution.

Q4 — Common element behind apparent redundancy

In Variant D, the upstream supply is fully redundant (two transformers, two buses, bus-tie). Where is the SPOF, and how do you remove it?

Show answer

The SPOF is the shared outgoing cable and single MCC-1 incomer that supply both the duty pump and its standby pump from the same bus section. One cable fault, MCC bus fault, or MCC incomer trip takes both pumps offline at once — the standby gives no protection against this common element.

Remove it by feeding the duty pump from MCC-1 on Bus A and the standby pump from MCC-2 on Bus B, with separately routed supply cables. No single cable, MCC, or bus section now disables both the duty and standby function.

Q5 — Why "two of everything" can still be wrong

A colleague says: "We have two transformers, two buses, and two pumps — we're N-1 secure." Using the lessons from Variants B and D, explain why this statement can still be wrong.

Show answer

Variant B lesson: Two transformers and two buses without a bus-tie means each bus still has only one source. Lose one transformer, lose half the plant. The "redundancy" of the second transformer is inaccessible to the dead bus.

Variant D lesson: Two pumps supplied by the same cable, same MCC, and same bus section — a common element — means a single cable or MCC fault disables both duty and standby simultaneously. The "redundancy" of the second pump is defeated by the shared supply.

Conclusion: N-1 security requires not just N+1 equipment but also that no single shared element can disable the function. Always check for common elements that bridge the apparent redundancy.

Q6 — Reliability metrics calculation

A component has MTBF = 150,000 h and MTTR = 120 h. Calculate its availability and estimated annual downtime.

Show answer

Availability:

A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) = 150,000 / (150,000 + 120) = 0.99920

Unavailability:

U = 1 − 0.99920 = 7.99 × 10⁻⁴

Estimated annual downtime:

U × 8760 h/yr = 7.99 × 10⁻⁴ × 8760 ≈ 7.0 h/yr (indicative)

Q7 — Series vs parallel availability

Two identical units, each with A = 0.99. Find the system availability in: (a) series; (b) 1-out-of-2 parallel. Comment on the difference.

Show answer

(a) Series:

A_ser = 0.99 × 0.99 = 0.9801 → ~174 h/yr downtime

(b) Parallel (1-out-of-2):

A_par = 1 − (1 − 0.99)² = 1 − (0.01)² = 0.9999 → ~0.9 h/yr downtime

Comment: The parallel arrangement is approximately 200× better than series for the same two components. This is the fundamental quantitative argument for N+1 with a transfer path (bus-tie). Series elements (such as the MV switchgear) cap the system availability; they must be addressed through MTTR reduction (faster repair, auto-transfer) rather than adding parallel components.

Q8 — FMECA scoring and detection

An FMECA entry for the ATS initially scores S = 9, O = 3, D = 4. (a) Calculate the RPN. (b) If monthly on-load testing improves detection to D = 2, what is the new RPN? (c) What does this illustrate?

Show answer

(a) Initial RPN:

RPN = S × O × D = 9 × 3 × 4 = 108

(b) After improving detection:

RPN = 9 × 3 × 2 = 54 (reduction of 50 %)

(c) Illustration: Improving detection through regular testing cuts the RPN by 50 % without any change to the hardware or its failure rate. This is often the lowest-cost mitigation available — a well-structured maintenance and testing programme is a direct reliability investment.
