Desk Study Procedure

Where you are in the workflow: Part 5 is your field task guide. Work through it in order for every plant. You are here — beginning the pre-site desk study, before you travel to the plant.

The desk study is the preparatory phase that takes place in the office, typically 3–7 days before the site visit. Its purpose is to understand the plant's electrical architecture before you arrive, identify gaps in the document package, and frame the questions you need to answer on site. A thorough desk study shortens the site visit and substantially improves the quality of your FMECA.

Documents to request

Issue a formal document request to the plant owner / O&M team as soon as the plant is assigned. The table below lists the categories to cover and the specific detail to ask for.

Category Documents to request Why you need it
Single-line diagrams Overall SLD (all voltage levels); MV switchgear SLD; LV main switchboard SLD; MCC & distribution SLD; standby (DG/ATS) schematic. Request as-built revision with date stamp. Foundation of every analysis: SPOF identification, load-flow model, protection review, SLD verification on site.
Utility (SEC) connection data SEC connection agreement / service order; declared point-of-supply voltage (13.8 kV is typical for plants of this size); SEC-advised fault level at the intake (typically quoted in the 25–40 kA range — confirm the site-specific letter, indicative); metering arrangement; any Saudi Grid Code / SEC Distribution Code compliance correspondence. The SEC fault-level letter is the source datum for the short-circuit study; without it the MV switchgear withstand check cannot be closed. SEC Distribution Code conditions (power factor, harmonics) also frame the PFC review.
Equipment schedules & load list Electrical load schedule (kW, pf, demand factor per feeder); equipment schedule (make, model, year, rating for transformers, switchgear, generators, UPS, batteries, VFDs); cable schedule. Input for transformer sizing, load-flow, short-circuit, and genset sizing calculations (see Part 2 — Calculations).
Protection coordination study Most recent protection coordination / relay grading study with relay setting records (incomer, feeders, bus-section, transformer differential). Request the live controlled revision — not the original EPC study if the plant has been modified since. Grading selectivity is a common SPOF class. An outdated study (predating expansions or modifications) is itself a risk finding.
Earthing design & test records Earthing system design study (grid resistance, touch/step potential); soil resistivity report; as-installed grid resistance test results; bonding schedule. Date of last test. Earthing is a latent safety SPOF: a degraded or untested earth grid impairs earth-fault protection and creates touch/step hazards.
Arc-flash study IEEE 1584 / IEC 61482-1 arc-flash / incident energy study; PPE category labels in use; safe-working-distance schedule. Check that the study post-dates any switchgear replacement or protection re-grading. Obsolete arc-flash studies are a QHSE SPOF. An out-of-date study gives incorrect PPE boundaries — a direct safety risk.
Transformer condition records Factory acceptance test (FAT) and site acceptance test (SAT) reports; dissolved gas analysis (DGA) / oil-sample trend reports; temperature monitoring logs; thermography records. Transformer internal faults are high-severity events. DGA trend is the primary condition indicator — missing DGA data raises the Detection (D) rating in the FMECA.
Standby generator records Genset O&M manual; auto-start / load test log (date, duration, outcome); maintenance history; fuel-delivery records. Genset fails-to-start is a hidden (latent) failure mode — high Detection rating if test logs are absent or intermittent.
DC / battery charger records Station battery and charger O&M manual; battery capacity-discharge test records; DC-bus voltage trend; alarm-annunciator history. DC supply is the trip power source for MV protection. Charger ageing with no capacity test on record is a top-priority latent SPOF.
Switchgear maintenance records Circuit breaker service records (racking operations count, contact-wear status, timing tests, primary injection dates); switchgear inspection reports. Ageing switchgear with no primary injection records has uncertain protection reliability. Identifies candidates for the FMECA switchgear rows.
Asset register Plant electrical asset register (ISO 14224 format if available); tag list. Maps tags to equipment for FMECA rows and document traceability.

How to review the received documents

Follow this sequence when documents arrive:

  1. Open the Document Inventory form (SF-01) immediately. Log every document as it arrives: title, revision, date, format, quality (Good / Legible-only / Poor), and status (Received / Partial / Missing / Obsolete). Do not wait until after the visit.
  2. Study the master SLD first. Trace the power flow from the utility point of supply through MV-MET → MV-SWGR → TX-1/TX-2 → LV-MSB → MCC and loads. Mark the bus-tie status (normally open or closed) and note any parallel-feed or alternative supply arrangements. Sketch a mental resilience map: what fails if component X is lost?
  3. Cross-check revision dates. Compare the SLD revision date to the protection study revision date. If the protection study predates plant modifications (equipment additions, transformer replacements, feeder changes), flag it as a gap in SF-01 and a potential FMECA finding.
  4. Extract equipment ratings for the calculation model. From the load schedule and equipment schedule, build the shell of your load-flow and short-circuit models. Fill in transformer ratings, impedances, and the SEC-advised utility fault-level data where available (if the SEC letter is missing, assume a 25 kA design value and state the SEC range 25–40 kA as the uncertainty — indicative); mark unknowns to be resolved on site.
  5. List the open questions. For each missing or poor-quality document, write a specific question to ask the O&M supervisor on site. Annotate these directly on a working (print) copy of the SLD.
Tip — start SF-01 at the document-request stage, not on arrival. When you send the document request, log the requested items in the Document Inventory with status "Requested". As documents arrive, change the status to "Received" or "Partial". By the time you travel to site, the inventory already reflects the pre-visit gap picture — which becomes your data-call agenda for the visit.

Starting SF-01 before the visit

Every row in SF-01 where status is Missing, Partial, or Obsolete at the end of the desk study becomes an action item in the SF-04 Site Visit Report. Prioritise these:

  • Missing DGA / oil-trend data → raises transformer FMECA Detection (D) rating.
  • Missing battery capacity-discharge test → latent DC SPOF.
  • Obsolete protection coordination study → grading selectivity SPOF.
  • Outdated or missing arc-flash study → QHSE SPOF (immediate priority).
  • No confirmed relay setting records → cannot validate relay grading.
Key takeaway: The desk study is not a bureaucratic step — it is where you build the mental model of the plant and identify the biggest risks before you travel. Arriving with an annotated working SLD, a part-completed SF-01, and a list of targeted questions will make your site time twice as productive.

Site-Visit Preparation

Where you are in the workflow: the desk study is complete; you are preparing to travel to the plant for the non-intrusive site survey.

QHSE and permit-to-work

Before travelling to any plant site, confirm the host organisation's QHSE requirements and the permit-to-work (PTW) procedure. You are a visitor operating under the host's safety management system. Key points:

  • Site induction: most plants require a formal site-safety induction on first entry. Allow time for this in Day 0 of your schedule. Request the induction materials in advance if possible.
  • Permit to work (PTW): entry to any switchroom or electrical plant room requires a valid PTW issued by the host's authorised person (AP). Arrange this in advance — do not assume you can enter on arrival.
  • Accompanying escort: the host O&M electrical supervisor must accompany you throughout the electrical areas. Confirm their availability and schedule before travel.
  • Emergency procedures: know the site's muster points, emergency contact numbers, and first-aid arrangements before entering the plant.
Warning — NON-INTRUSIVE surveys only. This campaign operates under a strictly non-intrusive rule. You must not open any energised electrical panel, cubicle, or enclosure; operate any switch, circuit breaker, or isolator; perform any live electrical test; or take any measurement that requires direct contact with live conductors. Your tools for the survey are eyes, a camera, a torch, a notepad, and a tape measure for clearances only.

Visual inspection, nameplate reading, photograph capture, and accessible (non-contact) meter readings are permitted. Where a panel is already open and de-energised under a live PTW issued for O&M work, you may observe but must not touch equipment or interfere with the O&M activity.

Be aware of LOTO (lock-out / tag-out) points. Never remove or interfere with a lock or tag, even if you believe it is incorrectly placed. If you find LOTO absent where it should be present, record it as an observation and raise it with the escort — do not act on it yourself.

Pre-entry checklist

Complete this checklist before entering any electrical plant area:

  • Site induction completed and card / sticker received.
  • PTW reviewed, understood, signed, and held on person.
  • Escort (plant electrical supervisor or authorised person) confirmed and present.
  • Personal protective equipment donned: arc-rated coveralls, hard hat, safety glasses, safety shoes, gloves (HV-rated if required by PTW).
  • Camera / phone cleared for site use by host security — confirm no confidential-data or photography restrictions apply.
  • Working copy of SLD and annotated question list in hand (printed).
  • Blank or pre-headed Site Visit Report (SF-04) and Document Inventory (SF-01) in hand.
  • Emergency muster-point location and site emergency contact number confirmed.
  • Heat plan agreed for the day: water supply, shaded/air-conditioned refuge identified, outdoor work scheduled around the midday restriction window if in season (see Working Safely in Saudi Conditions).
Tip — print the SLD at A3 or larger. A reduced A4 SLD is almost unreadable for red-lining in a switchroom. Print at A3 and laminate it if possible. Carry two copies: one for red-lining discrepancies, one to keep clean as a reference.
Key takeaway: Safety first, always. The PTW and escort must be in place before you enter any electrical room. The non-intrusive rule is absolute — do not open any live panel regardless of circumstances. If in doubt, stop and consult the escort.

Visual Inspection Checklist

Where you are in the workflow: you are on site, PTW in hand, escort present. Work through this checklist zone by zone.

This is the primary field inspection tool. Work through each zone in the order below, checking off items as you observe them. Record observations, nameplate data and discrepancies in SF-04 as you go. Where a finding seeds an FMECA row, note the preliminary FM-ID in the SF-04 observation table.

Warning — non-intrusive throughout. Every check in this list is visual, nameplate-based, or photographic only. Do not open any energised panel, cubicle, or door. Do not operate any device. Do not remove any label, lock, or tag. If a cubicle or panel is already open and de-energised under an O&M PTW, you may observe but must not touch.

Zone 1 — Switchroom / electrical building

  • Building access: entry under valid PTW; door interlock or warning notice present and legible.
  • Room condition: adequate lighting (emergency lighting present); no water ingress; no condensation on walls or ceiling; HVAC operating and room temperature reasonable (record the thermostat / room thermometer reading — in a 50 °C ambient climate the switchroom HVAC is reliability-critical for relays and breakers; see Part 3 — environment-driven failures).
  • HVAC redundancy and filtration: is there a second (standby) A/C unit, or does a single unit serve the room (SPOF candidate)? Sand/dust filters on air intakes present and visibly clean.
  • Housekeeping: no stored materials, rubbish, or non-electrical equipment inside the switchroom.
  • Fire protection: fire extinguisher(s) present, in-date, correct type (CO₂ or clean agent — never water in live electrical rooms); smoke/heat detector present.
  • Floor-plan organisation matches SLD layout (left-to-right / top-to-bottom incomer → bus → feeders); note any discrepancies.
  • Cable entries: sealed with fire-stop / intumescent material; no open penetrations.
  • Safety signage: arc-flash hazard warning labels and PPE requirement signs visible at room entry.
  • Earthing: main earth bar accessible and visible; connections not corroded or loose.

Zone 2 — MV switchgear (MV-SWGR, MV-MET, MV-NER)

  • MV-MET: SEC metering cubicle present; SEC seals intact (never break or disturb a utility seal); meter readable through window; confirm utility voltage level on nameplate (expect 13.8 kV, 60 Hz for an SEC distribution supply).
  • MV-SWGR: switchgear make, type, voltage class (expect 17.5 kV class for a 13.8 kV system), rated short-time withstand current (kA / s) and year legible on main nameplate; photograph. Compare the withstand rating to the SEC-advised fault level (25–40 kA range — site letter governs).
  • Switchgear type: RMU (ring-main unit), metal-clad drawout, or fixed — record and compare to SLD assumption.
  • Number of panel sections and their function (incomer, bus-section/tie, feeder) matches SLD; record any discrepancy.
  • Breaker/switch positions visible through window or on mimic diagram: incomer CB — CLOSED or OPEN; bus-tie CB — CLOSED or OPEN (mark on working SLD).
  • Relay front plates visible: manufacturer, type, and functional label (IDMT O/C, E/F) legible; compare relay type to protection study assumptions.
  • Arc-flash hazard label on each cubicle: present, legible, and carrying an incident-energy value or PPE category; record the study revision date if stated. If absent or clearly out of date, flag as an FMECA finding.
  • Indication lamps functioning (visible from outside — no cubicle entry); trip indication flags reset or showing.
  • MV-NER: neutral earthing resistor present in its housing; nameplate rating (ohms, amperes, time rating) legible — for the reference design expect the order of 19.9 Ω, 400 A / 10 s at 7.97 kV (13.8 kV ÷ √3), indicative; no visible damage or corrosion on resistor elements or bushing terminals; housing ventilation louvres free of sand and dust accumulation.
  • Cable sealing ends visible through inspection windows: no apparent tracking, surface discoloration, or visible damage.
  • Switchgear age: note year of manufacture; if ≥ 20 years, flag for FMECA.

Zone 3 — Transformers (TX-1, TX-2)

  • TX-1 and TX-2 both present and identified; nameplate data photographed: kVA, primary/secondary voltage (expect 13.8/0.4 kV for the reference design), vector group (expect Dyn11), impedance % (reference design Z = 6 % — record the exact nameplate value, it drives the short-circuit calculation), year of manufacture.
  • Cooling type confirmed from nameplate: ONAN (oil natural air natural) or ONAF (with fans); fans operating if ONAF. Check the nameplate reference ambient — a transformer rated for 40 °C ambient is effectively derated at a 50 °C site; record for the loading check.
  • Radiator condition: fins free of sand, dust, and wind-blown debris (desert sites foul radiators quickly); no bent or blanked fin sections; sun-shade over the tank present where specified.
  • Oil level visible in sight-glass: within normal (green) range; no oil staining on tank or bund.
  • Oil temperature indicator: temperature reading noted; no over-temperature alarm showing.
  • Buchholz relay (if ONAN oil-cooled): present; no gas-alarm flag showing.
  • Pressure-relief device: present; no evidence of prior operation (staining, displaced disc).
  • HV and LV cable / busbar connections: external connections clean, no surface tracking or discoloration visible (do not touch).
  • Bund / drip-tray: present; clean; drain valve closed; oil-absorbing material present if required.
  • Transformer bay segregation: both transformer bays accessible independently for maintenance (not shared bund or blocking arrangement).
  • Earthing: transformer tank earth connection clearly bonded to earth grid; connection visible and accessible for future testing.

Zone 4 — LV main switchboard (LV-MSB, bus-tie BT)

  • LV-MSB nameplate: voltage (expect 400/230 V, 60 Hz), form type (Form 4 or similar), busbar rating (A), rated short-circuit withstand (Icw kA / 1 s), year of manufacture. For the reference design the prospective fault is ≈ 38.5 kA from one transformer and ≈ 77 kA with the bus-tie closed — the nameplate Icw must cover the worst credible case (indicative; the site short-circuit study governs).
  • Split-bus arrangement confirmed: Bus A and Bus B sections physically identifiable; incomer A and incomer B breakers present.
  • Bus-tie (BT): bus-tie breaker clearly labelled; position confirmed: OPEN (N/O) or CLOSED — record on SLD. If closed under normal conditions, flag for FMECA (negates split-bus SPOF mitigation).
  • Incomer A and incomer B breakers: rated current, type, and manufacturer legible on front label or nameplate.
  • Auto-transfer / bus-coupling scheme: any automatic changeover relay visible on the bus-tie panel front? If so, note make and type. If absent, flag the manual-only bus-tie as a potential SPOF.
  • Metering and instrumentation: ammeter, voltmeter, power-factor meter, or energy meter present and readable (note readings if practical).
  • Feeder breakers: count and compare to SLD. Note any spare (vacant) ways. Record any breakers in tripped condition and confirm with escort whether this is normal.
  • Visible condition: no burn marks, scorch marks, or discoloration on busbar chambers or breaker faces (observe through front panels / viewing windows where present).
  • Cable entries: armoured cables properly glanded; segregation between phases maintained; no overheating discoloration on cable-entry areas.

Zone 5 — MCCs and VFDs

  • Each MCC panel (MCC-1, MCC-2, etc.) identified by tag; verify count against SLD.
  • MCC nameplate: voltage, busbar rating, form type, year of manufacture — photograph each.
  • Starter modules: starters present and labelled by motor load; DOL, star-delta, or soft-starter type visible from module face label.
  • Overload relay setting: record on-site if legible through the module window; compare to nameplate FLC of motor where known.
  • VFD: VFD enclosure present; make, model, kW/kVA rating, and AC supply voltage on nameplate; photograph.
  • VFD ventilation / cooling: air filter grille accessible; check for visible dust build-up or blocked vents (do not open enclosure). Note any over-temperature indicator on the VFD front panel.
  • VFD control mode: local or remote; note any trip-history indicators showing on front panel HMI.
  • MCCs fed from Bus A or Bus B: confirm from the LV-MSB SLD which bus feeds each MCC; verify any cross-bus feeding arrangement (if any, flag for SPOF review).

Zone 6 — PFC and distribution (PFC, DB, EDB)

  • PFC: power-factor correction panel present; nameplate: kVAr rating, number of steps, detuning reactor type (for harmonic detuning); check that capacitor bank is connected to the correct bus section.
  • PFC controller: power factor target setting visible on controller front panel (typically 0.95 or unity); note actual power factor on LV-MSB meter if readable.
  • DB / EDB: each distribution board labelled; compare to SLD. Note any boards not on SLD — add to red-line discrepancies.
  • EDB (essential distribution board): fed from ATS or UPS output (not directly from normal supply only); verify source with escort if unclear.
  • Spare way capacity: note boards with no available spare ways (capacity constraint).
  • Cable management: trays and conduit runs in good order; no unsupported sagging; no exposed conductors in non-hazardous areas.

Zone 7 — Standby plant (DG, ATS)

  • DG: diesel generator set present; nameplate photographed: kVA rating and rating class (ESP/PRP — note the ambient temperature at which the rating applies; a set rated at 25 °C delivers materially less at 50 °C site ambient), voltage (expect 400/230 V), frequency (60 Hz, 1800 rpm for a 4-pole set), make/model/year, power factor.
  • DG fuel level: fuel-level gauge readable; adequate fuel for a minimum 8-hour run (indicative; confirm the site minimum with the escort).
  • DG condition: coolant level visible; no oil or fuel pooling beneath unit; air-intake filters accessible and not visibly clogged.
  • DG battery (starting battery): battery connections clean, no corrosion; battery charger indicator lamp showing charged. Note last documented start-test date from logbook if accessible.
  • DG start-test log: request from O&M supervisor — is auto-start / load-test performed monthly? Note any gaps in the log.
  • ATS: automatic transfer switch panel identified; nameplate rating and source labels (Normal / Standby) legible; confirm OPEN / CLOSED position of each source contactor or breaker with escort.
  • ATS transfer time setting: note if visible on controller (typical: 10–15 s for non-critical loads, 3–5 s for essential loads — indicative).
  • Load scope of ATS: confirm with escort which loads are on the essential (ATS-backed) circuit and which are non-essential — record on working SLD.

Zone 8 — Control and DC supplies (UPS, DCDB)

  • UPS: UPS present; nameplate: kVA, battery autonomy at full load (the reference design carries 100 kVA / 30 min — indicative), AC input / output voltage (expect 400/230 V, 60 Hz); bypass switch position.
  • UPS battery condition: battery cabinet(s) present; no swollen or leaking cells visible through cabinet vents or viewing points (do not open cabinet). Confirm the battery room / cabinet is air-conditioned — VRLA battery life roughly halves for every 8–10 °C above 25 °C, so an un-cooled battery room in a 50 °C climate is itself a finding.
  • UPS front-panel status: mains-OK, battery-OK, fault-alarm indicators; note any active alarms.
  • DCDB: DC battery and charger panel identified; make, model, DC voltage (typically 110 V DC for MV switchgear trip circuits, or 24 V DC for control), charger rating.
  • DC charger condition: note age; any audible fan noise, vibration, or elevated surface temperature? Check for corrosion on terminals. Note last capacity-discharge test date from records if accessible.
  • DC battery: note battery type (VRLA / flooded); year of manufacture; any evidence of electrolyte leakage or cell swelling (visible without opening enclosure).
  • DC-fail alarm: confirm with escort that a DC-supply loss alarm is active and routed to SCADA or local annunciator.
  • Control panels: DCS / PLC / SCADA marshalling cabinets identified; physical condition (no open doors in normal service); UPS-backed supply confirmed.

Zone 9 — Visible earthing

  • Main earth bar: accessible, connections clean, no corrosion on the bonding strap or clamp; conductor cross-section approximately as designed (note mm² if labelled).
  • Earth test point / pit: earth test disconnect link accessible; test tag (date of last test) present and legible. Note last test date. If no test tag, or last test > 3 years, flag as FMECA finding.
  • Transformer tank bonds: each transformer tank earthed independently to the earth bar; bond visible and accessible.
  • Cable-armouring bonds: MV cable armouring bonded at both ends (check at gland plate — visual only).
  • Exposed metalwork bonded: switchgear frame, transformer bund, cable tray continuity bonds visible; no isolated metal sections.
  • MV-NER earth connection: NER body earthed to earth bar with a clearly identifiable conductor (this is separate from the neutral point conductor — note if both are visible).

Photolog — mandatory photographs

The following 12 photographs are mandatory for every site visit. Capture them in order; check each off as taken. Each photo must include a scale reference (hand, A4 sheet, or tape measure) or a legible nameplate in frame. Label images sequentially (e.g. P01-IMG-01) and index them in SF-04 Section 5.

  • PH-01 — Overall switchroom general view: taken from the room entrance looking in; shows the full panel line-up and housekeeping condition.
  • PH-02 — MV switchgear line-up and arc-flash labels: shows the incomer, bus-section, and feeder cubicles; arc-flash PPE-category labels visible (or their absence).
  • PH-03 — Bus-tie (BT / MV bus-section) breaker and position indicator: close-up of the bus-tie cubicle front showing the position (O or I / OPEN or CLOSED) indicator.
  • PH-04 — TX-1 nameplate: transformer 1 rating plate showing kVA, voltage ratio, vector group, impedance, year.
  • PH-05 — TX-2 nameplate: transformer 2 rating plate (same fields as PH-04).
  • PH-06 — LV-MSB incomer and bus-tie section front: shows incomer A, incomer B, and bus-tie breaker with their position indicators and labels.
  • PH-07 — DG nameplate and starting-battery condition: generator rating plate and battery / charger in same frame if possible.
  • PH-08 — ATS panel front: shows source labels (Normal/Standby) and transfer-switch position indicator.
  • PH-09 — DCDB / battery charger: charger nameplate, battery type label, and any front-panel fault/alarm indicators visible.
  • PH-10 — UPS front panel: nameplate and status indicators showing mains-OK, battery-OK, and any active alarms.
  • PH-11 — Main earth test point / pit: test disconnect link and test tag (showing last test date); earth bar bonding connections.
  • PH-12 — Any notable defect or discrepancy found: photograph the specific item (cable termination tracking, missing label, corrosion, apparent overheating discoloration, absent arc-flash label, etc.) that seeds an FMECA finding. Take one photograph per distinct finding.
Warning — do not photograph classified or sensitive information. Some plants have restrictions on photographing SCADA screens, relay setting displays, production data, or utility metering. Confirm with the escort before photographing anything other than physical equipment nameplates and general views.
Key takeaway: Work through this checklist zone by zone, checking off items as observed. The 12 mandatory photographs are non-negotiable — they provide the evidentiary basis for the site-visit report and support the FMECA Detection ratings. Everything is non-intrusive.

Drawing Availability & SLD Verification

Where you are in the workflow: alongside the visual inspection, you are verifying the single-line diagrams against the as-found plant and recording all discrepancies.

The SLD is the primary engineering document for the plant's electrical system. Verifying it against the as-found installation is a core deliverable of every site visit. Discrepancies between the SLD and the real plant are often the most direct route to identifying SPOFs: an unlabelled bus-tie that is closed in normal operation, a second transformer that has been added without updating the SLD, or a protection relay that has been replaced with a different type but the coordination study was never updated.

Drawing availability — what you need

Drawing Minimum acceptable Risk if missing
Overall master SLD (all voltage levels) Latest controlled revision, readable at A3 Cannot verify plant architecture; FMECA incomplete
MV switchgear SLD / internal arrangement Cubicle-by-cubicle layout, relay type per panel Cannot verify relay types against protection study; miss selectivity gaps
LV main switchboard SLD Bus A/B split, incomer and feeder labelling, BT position Cannot confirm bus-tie scheme; miss LV SPOF
Standby (DG/ATS) schematic ATS source selection, backed loads listed Cannot confirm essential-load scope; genset sizing FMECA row uncertain
Earthing system plan / layout Grid layout, test-point locations, bonding schedule Cannot identify earthing SPOF; cannot locate test points on site
Cable schedule (MV feeders and LV main cables) Cable ref, route, cross-section, length (MV feeders as minimum) Cannot run voltage-drop or cable-sizing calculations

The red-line technique

Bring a printed working copy of the master SLD to site (A3 or larger, at a scale at which individual labels are legible). As you walk down the plant:

  1. Check every item on the SLD against the physical installation. For each component — transformer, breaker, bus-section, relay, feeder — confirm it is present and correctly labelled. If confirmed, tick the item on your working SLD.
  2. Mark any discrepancy in red. Cross out items not present; annotate added items not on the SLD; circle mislabelled items; note changed relay types or equipment ratings.
  3. Mark the as-found switch/breaker positions. Use "C" (closed) or "O" (open) pencilled next to each breaker symbol. Pay particular attention to the bus-tie breaker and any normally-open-by-design devices.
  4. Transfer all red-line marks to SF-04 (observations and findings table) at the end of the visit. Each discrepancy becomes an observation with its tag and a note of what action is needed (e.g., SLD revision, FMECA row).

Common SLD discrepancies to look for

  • Unlabelled or wrong-status bus-tie: bus-tie BT shown as normally open on SLD but confirmed closed in normal operation by escort — negates N+1 redundancy; major SPOF finding.
  • Missing bus-section breaker: SLD shows a split bus, but physically a solid busbar link (bolted connection) has replaced the bus-tie CB; no fault-isolation possible between halves.
  • Obsolete single-transformer SLD: SLD shows only TX-1; plant actually has TX-1 and TX-2 (second transformer added in a later expansion but SLD not updated).
  • Nominal vs as-found protection settings: relay type on SLD (e.g. IDMT overcurrent relay) does not match the relay on the actual panel (a different make/model with different characteristic curves installed).
  • Added loads not on SLD: additional MCCs or distribution boards installed since SLD revision; not reflected in the load schedule used for calculations.
  • Changed transformer taps: transformer on-load or off-load tap-changer position differs from SLD assumption; affects voltage-drop and protection-relay voltage setting calculations.
  • Earthing system changes: additional earth electrodes driven or NER earthing arrangement changed without updating the earthing design document.
  • ATS source labelling error: on site, Normal and Standby supply labels on the ATS are reversed from the SLD, or the ATS has been replaced with a different panel type.

When no SLD is available

If no SLD exists or the available SLD is so outdated as to be unreliable (e.g., a hand-sketch with no revision date), proceed as follows:

  1. Record the gap in SF-01 as Missing or Obsolete, with a data-call action to the plant owner for a redraw or as-built survey.
  2. Construct a sketch SLD during the visit. Using a blank A3 pad, sketch the one-line from the incomer downwards as you walk the plant. Note every component you physically observe: tag, make, rating, and switch position. This becomes the red-line baseline.
  3. Note the absence of a controlled SLD as an FMECA finding in its own right: operating a plant without an up-to-date SLD raises the likelihood of switching errors and impairs protection-setting maintenance (Detection rating raised for affected FMECA rows).
  4. Flag in SF-04 that calculations are based on site-observed data only and that a formal SLD should be drawn before the study is finalised.
Tip — take a photo of every nameplate to supplement the SLD. Even when a good SLD exists, the nameplate photograph provides data that may not appear on the SLD: exact impedance %, serial number for warranty / age verification, precise kVA rating, and tap position. This photographic record is also the source for completing the calculation model back in the office.
Key takeaway: The red-line SLD verification is a simultaneous deliverable alongside the visual inspection — not a separate post-visit task. Discrepancies found on site are direct inputs to the FMECA and to the SLD update recommendation. A missing or obsolete SLD is itself an FMECA finding.

Post-Site Analysis

Where you are in the workflow: the site visit is complete; you are back in the office, turning your site data into the four deliverable forms.

Post-site analysis converts the raw data captured during the desk study and site visit into the completed FMECA, Recommendations Register, and finalised Document Inventory. The table below maps each form to its data sources and the relevant manual sections.

Calculations to run post-site

For each plant, complete the following calculation studies after the site visit using the as-found equipment data captured. The relevant manual sections are cross-linked.

  • Transformer sizing and loading check — see Part 2 — Transformer Calculations: confirm that the installed transformer kVA rating is adequate for the as-found load (with N+1 contingency). A transformer running at >80 % of rated kVA on normal load with no N+1 available is a SPOF candidate.
  • Full-load current (FLC) check — see Part 2 — FLC Calculations: verify that MCC starter and overload relay settings match the motor FLC. Significantly wrong settings (e.g., overload set above locked-rotor current) are a protection SPOF.
  • Short-circuit level — see Part 2 — Short-Circuit: calculate maximum prospective short-circuit current at the MV and LV busbars; confirm that switchgear rated fault withstand (kA) exceeds the calculated level. If not, this is a P1 safety SPOF.
  • Power factor correction sizing check — see Part 2 — PFC: verify that the installed PFC kVAr rating matches the current load's reactive demand. Oversized PFC on a lightly loaded bus can lead to over-voltage; under-sized PFC misses the utility penalty boundary.
  • Genset sizing check — see Part 2 — Genset: verify the standby generator kVA rating against the essential-load list. If the genset is sized to essential loads only (not full production), document this explicitly in the FMECA — it is a design limitation, not a fault, but it must be communicated.
  • Load flow (indicative) — see Part 2 — Load Flow: run a steady-state load flow to verify bus voltages and identify any heavily loaded feeders. Confirm voltage at the end of the longest cable run is within ±5 % of nominal (SASO-adopted IEC 60364 / SBC 401 — consistent with the acceptance limit stated in Part 2 — Load Flow). Remember the 50 °C-ambient derating (XLPE in air ≈ 0.82) when judging "heavily loaded" cables.
Tip — update the FMECA Detection (D) rating based on SF-01 gaps. Every SF-01 item with status Missing, Partial, or Obsolete directly affects the detectability of a related failure mode. For example: no DGA oil-trend record → transformer internal-fault D = 4 or 5 (hidden); no battery capacity-discharge test → DC charger failure D = 5 (hidden). Mechanically update these D ratings as you finalise SF-01 — do not leave them as conservative estimates.
Key takeaway: Post-site analysis is where the data turns into decisions. The FMECA rows are built from three sources: the red-lined SLD, the SF-04 observations, and the SF-01 document gaps. Every gap in SF-01 has a direct mechanical effect on the Detection rating of at least one FMECA row — follow the chain.

Writing the Site Visit Report

Where you are in the workflow: you are finalising SF-04 within 48 hours of the site visit.

The Site Visit Report (SF-04) is the contemporaneous factual record of the visit. It is not an analysis document — analysis lives in the FMECA and Recommendations Register. SF-04's function is to record what you saw, where, when, with whom, and what data-calls remain open. It is the evidence base that supports the FMECA findings and allows the PM to quality-check that your FMECA rows are grounded in real observations.

Report structure

Section Mandatory content Notes
1 · Visit metadata Date and time (start–finish); plant name and reference; weather / ambient conditions; escort name and title; access constraints (e.g. PTW number, energised panels not opened). Complete on the day of the visit. Do not reconstruct from memory later.
2 · Minutes of meeting (MOM) Attendees (name, organisation, role); agenda items discussed; key facts confirmed by the escort (e.g. bus-tie operating philosophy, genset essential-loads-only scope, date of last arc-flash study); decisions; action list (owner + due date). The MOM is the primary source for confirming verbal statements made on site. Include any discrepancies the escort explained versus the SLD.
3 · Survey scope walked List of all areas inspected and the equipment tags covered in each area. If an area was not inspected (access denied, PTW not obtained), record that explicitly.
4 · Observations and findings One row per observation: observation number; area; asset tag; what was observed; the FM-ID of the FMECA row it seeds. Each observation must be factual — describe what you saw, not an assessment of its risk (risk belongs in the FMECA). Transfer directly from your site notes. Every FMECA row should trace back to at least one SF-04 observation or a SF-01 document gap.
5 · Photo log Index of all photographs: photo ID, caption, asset tag, what it shows, finding reference. A minimum of the 12 mandatory photos from the Photolog checklist. Caption every photo at the time of indexing — do not rely on memory 48 hours later. If photos were not permitted, state why.
6 · Safety / access notes PTW conditions observed; any QHSE concerns identified on site (e.g. absent arc-flash labels, missing fire extinguisher, inadequate lighting); the non-intrusive rule adherence statement; heat-stress and other environmental controls applied (work-rest cycle used, hydration, midday restrictions if in season — see Working Safely in Saudi Conditions). The arc-flash label finding (if raised) is both a QHSE entry here and an FMECA row — cross-reference the FM-ID.
7 · Sign-off Prepared-by (engineer name, organisation, date); acknowledgement by the host escort (name, signature or email confirmation); PM review date (leave blank until PM signs). Obtain escort acknowledgement on the day, even if only by email confirmation.

Photo selection for the report

Include in SF-04 all 12 mandatory photos plus any additional photos that directly support an observation in the findings table. The following photos carry the most evidential weight and should always be included if captured:

  • Bus-tie breaker position indicator — confirms normal operating mode (open or closed).
  • Arc-flash label (or its absence) — supports the PPE / incident-energy FMECA finding.
  • DC / battery charger condition — supports the latent DC-supply SPOF finding.
  • Earth test point with date tag — confirms when the earthing system was last tested.
  • Any visible defect: tracking on cable terminations, corrosion on connections, dust-clogged VFD filters, missing fire-protection equipment — each defect photo must link to an observation row.

Tone and accuracy

  • Factual, not inferential. SF-04 records what you observed; the inference ("this is a SPOF because…") belongs in SF-02 FMECA. Example: Good SF-04 text: "Bus-tie CB confirmed by escort as manually operated only; no automatic transfer relay fitted." Not for SF-04: "The manual bus-tie is a critical single point of failure."
  • Attribute verbal information to the escort. If the plant supervisor tells you something that is not visible on a nameplate or SLD (e.g. "we last tested the battery three years ago"), attribute it: "Confirmed by [Escort Name] on [date]".
  • Report absences explicitly. "No arc-flash label observed on MV-SWGR incomer cubicle" is a valid finding. Do not omit negative observations.
Key takeaway: SF-04 is a factual record, not a risk assessment. Write it within 48 hours of the visit. Every FMECA row must trace to a SF-04 observation or a SF-01 gap. The photo log is mandatory — index all photos the same day they are taken.

Peer-review commentary — what an experienced engineer notices

What an experienced engineer notices when reviewing this study

The following observations are the kind of quality-check commentary that a senior reviewer or the PM would raise when reviewing the Jubail study. Use them as a self-check when you produce your own forms.

  • The highest RPN row (FM-006, DC charger, RPN = 60) is rated P1 immediately — not because the occurrence is high, but because the Detection rating is 5 (hidden). The DC supply feeds the protection and trip circuits; a failure that is undetected until a fault occurs is the most dangerous class of latent SPOF. The study correctly assigns "Immediate" urgency and cross-links to the missing battery capacity-discharge test in SF-01. On your own plants, always check whether the DC charger / battery-test record is in SF-01 before assigning a D rating — if it is missing, D = 5, and the row will likely be P1.
  • The protection-selectivity finding (FM-002, RPN = 36) exploits the document gap in SF-01 (P01-DOC-03 Partial, pre-dates expansion) to justify a high Detection = 4. This is the correct method: a protection study that predates a plant modification is not evidence that grading is correct — it is evidence that grading is unknown. The study appropriately flags this as Partial in SF-01 and elevates D accordingly. When you see a protection study dated before a major expansion on any of your plants, treat grading as unverified until proved otherwise.
  • Three SPOF=Y rows are identified: FM-001 (manual bus-tie), FM-005 (single service transformer), and FM-012 (common MV bus). Notice the difference in severity: FM-001 and FM-012 carry S = 4 (significant production impact), while FM-005 carries S = 3 (sub-area only, not on RO critical path). Priority is assigned accordingly (FM-001 and FM-012 → P1; FM-005 → P2). When you identify a SPOF on your plants, always ask: does loss of this single component cause a plant-wide outage (S = 4 or 5) or only a partial / sub-system outage (S = 2 or 3)? The answer sets the priority class.
  • The recommendation for the manual bus-tie (REC-01) specifies "add automatic bus-tie transfer scheme with interlocks" — not just "close the bus-tie". Simply closing the bus-tie permanently would eliminate the N+1 architectural benefit. The correct fix is an automatic transfer scheme that closes the tie on a monitored event (transformer loss or incomer undervoltage) and re-opens it once the fault is cleared. The study correctly notes that grading must be revalidated in parallel, because closing the bus-tie changes the fault-current contribution seen by each relay.
  • The earthing finding (FM-014) carries a moderate RPN (24) but a note that the last test was in 2013 — 13 years prior to the study date. The study correctly escalates the Detection rating on the basis that no re-test has been performed since the original commissioning. Corrosion in a coastal (saline) environment like Jubail is significantly faster than in an inland site, making the time-since-test argument even stronger. On coastal plants, any earthing test older than five years should be treated as Partial or Obsolete in SF-01.
  • Seven of the 16 recommendation rows are "monitor — no modification". This is disciplined use of the recommendations register: not every FMECA finding requires a capital modification. Items with low RPN and adequate existing safeguards are closed with a monitoring action and a cost band of L. A recommendations register that lists modifications for every single failure mode is a sign of under-calibrated FMECA scoring, not thoroughness.
  • The VFD cooling-filter dust loading (O-08 in SF-04) seeds FM-008, but the recommendation is to clean and replace filters rather than a capital modification. The study correctly distinguishes between an O&M action (P3, cost band L) and a capital modification. On your own plants, always separate the "do now — O&M" actions from the "invest in — capital" recommendations, and be explicit about which is which in SF-03.
  • SF-04 Observation O-04 (arc-flash study predates switchgear upgrade) appears in Section 6 (Safety notes) as well as in the findings table. This is correct practice: an unverified arc-flash boundary is both a FMECA finding (FM-004) and a QHSE concern that must be documented separately in the safety section of SF-04. On your own site visit reports, always copy any arc-flash label absence or outdated study finding into the QHSE section as well as the findings table.
  • The study's cost-band summary (L/M/H = 9/6/1) is stated explicitly in the SF-03 footer. This is useful for the client's budget planning. On your own plants, include this summary line in the SF-03 footer — it helps the PM aggregate across all 21 plants for the campaign summary report.
Key takeaway: Read the Jubail study as a worked example of the complete method — not as a template to copy blindly. The peer-review commentary above identifies the reasoning behind the scoring decisions. Apply that reasoning, not just the scores, on your own plants.

Part 5 — Self-Check

Where you are in the workflow: test your understanding of the traceability chain and the non-intrusive rule before going to site for the first time.

Q1 — The traceability chain

Name the four links in the traceability chain that connects a site observation to a capital modification recommendation, and state which deliverable form each link lives in.

Show answer

The four links are:

  1. Site observation (OBS-xx) — recorded in SF-04 (Site-Visit Report), Section 4. Each observation is factual and tagged to an asset; it seeds a FMECA row via the FM-ID field.
  2. FMECA row (FM-xx) — in SF-02 (FMECA Worksheet). The observation provides the evidence; the FMECA assigns S/O/D scores, computes RPN, flags SPOF=Y/N, and generates a recommendation reference (REC-ID).
  3. Recommendation row (REC-xx) — in SF-03 (Recommendations Register). Each FMECA row that warrants action produces one recommendation row; the REC-ID cross-references the FM-ID so the chain is auditable.
  4. Modifications catalogue — the concept modification packages (Part 4). Each REC row maps to one or more Mod IDs from the catalogue, which describe what to specify, verify and document.

A completed register in which every REC-xx traces back to a specific FM-xx, and every FM-xx traces back to an OBS-xx or a DOC-xx gap, is a fully traceable deliverable. This is what the PM quality-checks before approval.

Q2 — The non-intrusive rule

You arrive at the plant and notice that the MV switchgear front door is standing open. The O&M supervisor says: "Go ahead — it's been like that all morning, it's fine." What do you do, and why?

Show answer

You do not enter or touch the open panel. The non-intrusive rule is absolute: you must not open energised panels, operate any device, or interfere with any equipment — regardless of whether someone else has already left it open, and regardless of verbal permission from local staff. Only the host's authorised person (AP) under a valid permit-to-work can authorise access to live electrical equipment.

The correct actions are:

  • Record the open panel in SF-04 Section 4 as an observation: "MV-SWGR front door found open, no PTW in evidence" — with a photograph if permitted.
  • Raise it in SF-04 Section 6 (Safety / QHSE notes) as a concern: an open live MV panel with no PTW is a QHSE finding in its own right.
  • Inform the escort and request either a formal PTW to use the access for observation, or request the door be closed.
  • Do not cite the open door as an opportunity to make visual observations inside the live panel without PTW authorisation.

Q3 — Document gap → FMECA Detection rating

The SF-01 Document Inventory shows that the battery capacity-discharge test record (DOC-15 UPS/DCDB battery maintenance records) is listed as Missing. How does this gap affect the FMECA row for the DCDB charger failure mode, and what priority band will the row most likely reach?

Show answer

A missing battery capacity-discharge test means the true battery hold-up time is unknown. Without evidence of a recent discharge test, there is no way to know whether the battery will deliver its rated autonomy — it may have degraded to a fraction of nameplate capacity. This is the definition of a latent (hidden) failure: the failure is undetectable until the moment the battery is called upon.

Mechanical FMECA update: Set Detection D = 5 (effectively undetectable — hidden until a fault event). For DCDB charger failure with S = 4 (whole-bus protection loss, safety-critical) and a typical O = 3 (ageing charger, once per few years), RPN = 4 × 3 × 5 = 60. This meets the P1 threshold (RPN ≥ 60). The row becomes Immediate priority — exactly as illustrated in the Jubail FM-006 row.

Key takeaway: The traceability chain — SF-04 observation → SF-02 FMECA → SF-03 recommendation → concept modification — is the backbone of every deliverable. The non-intrusive rule is absolute: no observation is worth a safety violation. Document gaps in SF-01 mechanically raise Detection ratings in the FMECA — follow the chain, do not estimate.

First-Assignment Walk-Through

Where you are in the workflow: you have received your first plant assignment. Follow this day-by-day sequence from first notification to submission.

This walk-through narrates a typical plant cycle from Day −5 (desk study begins) to Day 4 (quality assurance and submission). The timeline is indicative — a simple plant may compress to five days total; a complex plant with many document gaps may expand to eight or ten. Adjust as needed, but keep the sequence.

Day −5 — Desk study begins

Morning — receive the plant package and issue the document request

  1. Open the four blank assessment forms from Appendix — Blank Forms. Fill in the header of each form: plant name, plant reference number, engineer name, date, and status ("In preparation"). Save as working copies with the plant reference in the filename.
  2. Issue the document request to the plant O&M contact using the standard request template. Request all ten document categories listed in the Desk Study section. Log each requested item in SF-01 with status "Requested".
  3. Obtain the logistics information: site address, visitor access procedure, site safety induction requirements, escort contact details, and whether a PTW must be pre-arranged. Block the site visit date in your calendar.

Afternoon — first document review

  1. As documents arrive, update SF-01 status (Received / Partial). If documents are already available in the campaign document store, retrieve them immediately.
  2. Study the master SLD first. Trace the power path: utility → MV-MET → MV-SWGR → TX-1/TX-2 → LV-MSB (Bus A/B with bus-tie BT) → MCC-1 … MCC-n, VFD, DG/ATS, UPS/DCDB. Annotate: how many transformers? Is the bus-tie N/O? Is there an auto-transfer scheme? Any single-feed sub-areas?
  3. On a printed copy of the SLD, mark with a red pencil every component whose failure you believe might cause a plant-wide or significant partial outage. These are your preliminary SPOF candidates — you will verify them on site and refine them in the FMECA.

Day −2 — Desk calculations and pre-visit preparation

Morning — desk calculations

With the equipment schedule and load list in hand, build the calculation model shell and run the following desk-based checks (see Part 2 for all methods):

  • Transformer sizing check (Part 2): total active load (kW) ÷ assumed power factor → apparent power (kVA); divide by number of transformers; compare to installed transformer kVA. If >80 % loaded in normal operation, flag as a SPOF candidate.
  • FLC check (Part 2): for the largest motor on each MCC, calculate rated FLC; compare to MCC overload relay setting from the schedule. Note any that appear set ≥ 115 % FLC.
  • Indicative short-circuit level (Part 2): using transformer impedance and the SEC-advised utility fault level (if known), estimate the maximum fault current at the LV main busbar — for the reference design (1600 kVA, Z = 6 %) this is ≈ 38.5 kA per transformer, ≈ 77 kA with the bus-tie closed, indicative. Compare to switchgear rated fault-withstand current. If the SEC fault-level letter is not yet available, note this as a data-call and assume 25 kA at the MV intake (SEC range 25–40 kA).
  • Genset sizing check (Part 2): sum the essential-load list; compare to DG rated kVA at the assumed power factor. Confirm whether the DG covers full production or essential loads only. If the load list has gaps, note as a site question.

Afternoon — pre-visit preparation

  1. Print two A3 copies of the master SLD. Label one "RED-LINE COPY" and one "REFERENCE COPY". On the red-line copy, mark all preliminary SPOF candidates with a pencilled circle.
  2. Print the Visual Inspection Checklist and the Drawing Availability and SLD Verification Checklist from this manual. These are your primary field tools.
  3. Print a blank SF-04 Site Visit Report header and MOM template. Fill in the plant name, date, and visit agenda.
  4. Confirm with the escort: PTW for switchroom entry (confirm time), induction requirements, photography restrictions, and any areas that will not be accessible.
  5. Check PPE: arc-rated coveralls, hard hat, safety glasses, safety shoes, and gloves (confirm PPE category required by the PTW if known in advance).

Day 0 — Arrival and QHSE induction

On arrival

  1. Report to the plant control room or administration office. Sign in. Confirm the escort is present and the site induction is ready.
  2. Complete the site QHSE induction: emergency muster points, emergency contact numbers, permit-to-work procedure, specific hazards (process plant hazards are in addition to electrical hazards — confirm chemical, pressure, and process risks with the escort).
  3. Collect the PTW for switchroom entry. Read it in full. Confirm the scope, validity period, and conditions. Sign it.
  4. Attend the kick-off meeting with the escort and any other O&M staff available. Confirm the visit agenda, areas to be walked, and documents not yet received. Record attendees in SF-04 Section 2 (MOM). Confirm in writing (meeting minutes or email) the key verbal facts: bus-tie operating philosophy, genset scope, last arc-flash study date.

Day 1 — Site visit

Tip — plan the day around the heat. In summer, start the outdoor portions (transformer bays, DG compound, earthing pits) at 07:00–09:00 while ambient is lowest, keep the air-conditioned switchroom zones for the middle of the day, and respect the midday outdoor-work restriction window where it applies (see Working Safely in Saudi Conditions). The zone order below may be re-sequenced for this — the checklist content does not change.

Morning — MV and transformer walk-down

  1. Enter the MV switchroom under the PTW with the escort. Work through Zones 1–3 of the Visual Inspection Checklist: switchroom general, MV-SWGR / MV-MET / MV-NER, then TX-1 and TX-2.
  2. Photograph PH-01 (switchroom general), PH-02 (MV switchgear line-up and arc-flash labels), PH-03 (bus-tie position indicator), PH-04 (TX-1 nameplate), PH-05 (TX-2 nameplate) from the mandatory photo list.
  3. On the red-line SLD, confirm every item visible in the switchroom. Mark confirmed items with a tick; mark discrepancies in red. Note the as-found breaker positions.
  4. Ask the escort specifically: When was the protection coordination study last updated? Has the plant been expanded since that date? When was the arc-flash study last performed? Note answers in SF-04 Section 2.

Working Safely in Saudi Conditions

Everything in this part assumes you can think clearly on site. In a Saudi summer that is not a given: switchyards, rooftops and un-conditioned electrical rooms routinely exceed the 50 °C design ambient, and the physiological load of heat degrades exactly the faculties an assessment depends on — attention, judgement and fine motor control. Treat heat as a site hazard with the same discipline you apply to stored energy and arc flash.

Heat-stress management

Plan the day around the heat, not through it. Field work in summer follows a simple pattern: outdoor and un-conditioned work in the early morning, desk work, document review and conditioned-room inspections in the middle of the day, and a short second outdoor window in the late afternoon if needed.

  • Midday rule. Saudi labour regulations prohibit outdoor work under the sun during the hottest midday hours (approximately 12:00–15:00) from mid-June to mid-September (indicative — confirm the current ministerial decision and site rules). Plan visits so that no outdoor task lands in that window.
  • Work–rest cycles. At wet-bulb globe temperatures typical of a Gulf summer, plan 15-minute breaks in shade or air conditioning at least every 45–60 minutes of moderate outdoor work, shortening as humidity rises (indicative; follow the site HSE plan).
  • Hydration. Drink before you are thirsty — roughly a cup of water every 15–20 minutes during outdoor work. Avoid starting a site day already dehydrated.
  • Buddy system. Never work alone in un-conditioned electrical rooms or outdoor switchyards in summer. Early heat exhaustion is easier to spot in a colleague than in yourself: confusion, irritability, clumsiness, stopping sweating.
  • Refuge. Identify the nearest air-conditioned refuge (control room, vehicle, welfare cabin) before starting outdoor work, and pre-position water there.
Heat stroke is a medical emergency with rapid onset. If a team member becomes confused, staggers, or stops sweating in the heat: stop work, move them to the refuge, cool aggressively (water on skin, airflow), and call the site emergency number. Do not "walk it off".

PPE in the heat

Arc-flash PPE and heat work against each other. A CAT 2 arc-rated coverall is hot; a CAT 3/4 suit with hood is a personal sauna. The practical consequences:

  • Schedule energized tasks early. Any task requiring arc-rated PPE beyond daily workwear (panel opening, racking, live testing) belongs in the coolest part of the day — first thing in the morning.
  • Shorten stints. In summer, halve the planned continuous working time in CAT 2+ PPE and rotate personnel. Plan the task so the suited time is minimised (preparation and briefing unsuited, suit up only for the energized step).
  • Cooling aids. Cooling vests and neck wraps under arc-rated outer layers are effective; verify they are non-melting and compatible with the arc rating.
  • Never trade protection for comfort. The arc-flash boundary and PPE category from the arc-flash study are not negotiable in summer. If the PPE cannot be tolerated, defer the task or de-energize.

Permits, rooms and thermography

  • Heat as a permit condition. Summer permits-to-work should state the work window, work–rest cycle and refuge location alongside the usual electrical isolations. If the site permit form has no heat section, write it into "additional precautions".
  • Electrical-room thermal limits. A switchgear room above ~35 °C is telling you the HVAC is failing — and protection relays, VFDs and battery chargers are suffering with you (see environmental failure modes). Record the room temperature on the inspection checklist; treat a hot room as a finding, not an inconvenience.
  • Summer thermography. Peak summer load is the best time for a thermographic round of busbar joints, cable terminations and transformer fans — and the worst time to linger. Plan camera work in short stints from open doorways where possible.
  • Vehicle and equipment staging. Test instruments left in a parked vehicle can exceed 70 °C — beyond most instruments' storage rating. Stage instruments in conditioned space and check calibration stickers after any heat soak.

Summer field-day checklist

  • Outdoor tasks scheduled outside the prohibited midday window (≈12:00–15:00, mid-June to mid-September).
  • Work–rest cycle and refuge location agreed at the toolbox talk; water pre-positioned.
  • Buddy assignments confirmed for all outdoor and un-conditioned-room work.
  • Energized / arc-rated-PPE tasks placed in the early-morning slot, with rotation planned.
  • Electrical-room temperatures recorded on the inspection checklist; HVAC alarms verified.
  • Cooling vests issued and checked compatible with the required arc rating.
  • Instruments staged in conditioned space; no instruments or batteries left in vehicles.
  • Site emergency number and heat-stroke first-aid steps reviewed at the briefing.
Key takeaway: in a Saudi summer, the heat plan is part of the electrical safety plan. A dehydrated engineer in a 55 °C switchgear room makes the kind of mistakes that arc-flash PPE exists to survive. Schedule the heat out of the job first; PPE is the last line, not the first.
