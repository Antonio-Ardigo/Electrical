Why a field engineer reads catalogues at all

Everything in Parts 1–5 of this manual deals in abstractions: a tag on a single-line diagram, a calculated current, a required breaking capacity. TX-1 is "a 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11 transformer with Z = 6 %" — but you cannot phone a factory and order "TX-1". At some point every abstraction has to be translated into a commercial product: a specific manufacturer's product family, in a specific frame size, with a specific options package, that a factory will actually build, test and ship. Commercial catalogues are the dictionary for that translation, and Part 6 teaches you to read them fluently.

Consider the journey of one plant tag. The calculations in Part 2 — Transformer sizing tell you the plant needs 2 × 1600 kVA at 13.8/0.4 kV. A transformer catalogue then tells you which standard ratings exist (1600 kVA is a standard IEC ladder step; 1700 kVA is not), whether the vendor offers cast-resin and oil-immersed construction at that rating, what impedance options are available around the 6 % you assumed, what the losses and noise levels typically are, and — critically for a Saudi site — what the unit can deliver continuously at 50 °C ambient rather than the IEC reference conditions the headline rating assumes. Catalogue, datasheet, type-test certificate, GA drawing — four different documents

Field engineers routinely use these four terms loosely, and vendors exploit the looseness. They are four different documents with four different levels of commitment:

  • Catalogue. A marketing and selection document covering an entire product family. It tells you what exists: frame sizes, the ratings ladder, the options matrix, typical dimensions. It is deliberately generic, frequently revised, and legally commits the vendor to almost nothing. Use it for shortlisting and feasibility — never for final design.
  • Datasheet. A document describing one specific configuration — either a generic "technical data" sheet for one model, or (better) a project datasheet filled in for your order. A signed, project-specific datasheet is a contractual document; a catalogue page is not. The skill of reading one critically is the subject of the next section.
  • Type-test certificate. Proof, issued after laboratory testing of a representative sample (often by an independent body such as a recognised short-circuit testing station), that the design meets its standard — e.g. that an LV assembly survived its rated short-circuit withstand, or that a breaker actually interrupted its claimed Icu. Type tests are done once per design, not per unit. The companion document, the routine test report, is done on your serial number before despatch. You need both; they prove different things.
  • GA (general arrangement) drawing. The dimensional truth: overall sizes, masses, cable entry positions, door swings, lifting points, foundation/plinth details. The GA is what the civil designer, the cable router and the rigger work from. A switchboard that meets every electrical number but does not fit through the switchroom door is still a failed procurement — check the GA against the room early.
Key takeaway: a catalogue tells you what could exist, a datasheet what you asked for, a type-test certificate what was proven, and a GA drawing what will physically arrive. A complete equipment dossier contains all four — accept nothing less at order and at FAT.

How vendors structure product families

Once you have read three or four catalogues, you notice that every major vendor organises its range the same way, because manufacturing economics force them to. Understanding the structure makes any catalogue navigable in minutes:

  • Frame sizes. The physical "chassis" comes in a small number of sizes, and each frame hosts a band of ratings. An air circuit breaker family might use three or four frames covering roughly 630 A up to 6300 A; a drive family might use eight frames from fractional kW to several hundred kW. Within one frame the cost, dimensions and spare parts are nearly identical — which is why stepping up within a frame is cheap, and stepping across a frame boundary is expensive. A 2500 A and a 3200 A breaker may share a frame; a 4000 A unit may not.
  • Ratings ladders. Ratings follow preferred-number ladders inherited from the IEC R10 series: 630 — 800 — 1000 — 1250 — 1600 — 2000 — 2500 — 3200 A, or 800 — 1000 — 1250 — 1600 — 2000 kVA. Your calculated requirement almost never lands exactly on a rung; you round up to the next rung, which is exactly how the plant's 2353 kVA demand became 2 × 1600 kVA transformers and the ~2309 A transformer LV full-load current became a 2500 A-class incomer (indicative — see Part 2).
  • Options matrices. The base unit is configured by an options matrix: trip unit grade, number of poles, fixed vs withdrawable, motorised charging, shunt trip and undervoltage releases, auxiliary contacts, communication protocols, terminal styles, coatings for harsh environments. The matrix is where datasheet surprises hide — two "identical" breakers can differ enormously in what their trip units can actually do. Always specify options explicitly; never let "standard configuration" decide for you.

Typical families you will meet in the following sections include LV air circuit breaker ranges such as ABB Emax 2, Schneider Electric Masterpact MTZ and Siemens 3WA, and MV switchgear platforms such as Siemens NXAIR or ABB UniGear (indicative examples only — availability and SASO/SEC status must be confirmed per project).

Prices and lead times — the part the catalogue does not say out loud

List price vs project price. Where catalogues quote prices at all, they quote list prices. Real projects buy at project pricing, negotiated per package, with discounts that vary widely by vendor, volume, region and how badly the local channel wants the job — differences of tens of percent against list are routine (indicative; commercial terms are outside this manual's scope). The practical consequences for the field engineer: never build a cost estimate from list prices without a factor; never assume two vendors' list prices compare like for like; and understand that a vendor's "free" options at quotation time may reappear as expensive spare parts later. The whole-of-life cost — spares, service, local support presence in the Kingdom — often matters more than the purchase delta.

Lead time is a design constraint, not a procurement detail. Long-lead electrical equipment regularly sits on the project critical path. Indicative manufacturing lead times (ex-works, excluding shipping, customs and SASO/SABER clearance, and highly market-dependent):

When schedule is tight, ask vendors early about (a) stock or short-cycle variants of standard ratings, (b) production slot reservations against a letter of intent, and (c) which options force engineered-to-order treatment. Sometimes choosing the next standard rung up the ladder — already in stock — beats waiting for the "exact" unit.

How to Read a Datasheet

The anatomy of a datasheet — read it like an auditor, not a customer

A datasheet is written to be believed; you should read it to be verified. The single most important habit is this: every number on a datasheet is true only under stated reference conditions, and the reference conditions are usually printed in small type, in a footnote, or in a separate "service conditions" clause. IEC reference conditions typically assume a 40 °C maximum ambient, ≤ 1000 m altitude, clean dry air and a stated frequency. The plant in this manual sits at 50 °C design ambient, in dust and sand, on a 60 Hz system — so almost every headline number needs checking against a derating table before you may use it.

Read any datasheet in this order:

  • Standards line first. Which IEC standards, which edition, and — the key question — tested to them or merely "designed in accordance with" them (see the red-flag list below).
  • Service conditions next. Reference ambient, altitude, pollution/humidity class. This calibrates every other number on the page.
  • Then the ratings table, translating each row into the language of your Part 2 calculations.
  • Finally the footnotes — read every one. Footnotes are where ratings die.

Decoding the ratings that bite hardest in Saudi service

Rated current — at which ambient?

IEC LV gear is conventionally rated at 40 °C ambient (some assemblies at a 35 °C daily average). A Saudi outdoor or poorly cooled indoor location is designed at 50 °C, so the question to ask of every current rating is: "at what ambient, and what is the value at 50 °C?" A good datasheet has a derating table; a poor one is silent. Typical derating from 40 °C to 50 °C runs around 4–10 % depending on the device and enclosure (indicative). And remember the chain: breaker derating, busbar derating, cable derating (XLPE in 50 °C air ≈ ×0.82 — see cable sizing) all stack on the same circuit. The weakest derated link sets the circuit rating.

Icu vs Ics vs Icw — three different promises

These three kA numbers answer three different questions: can it break the worst fault at all (Icu), can it break a fault and stay in service (Ics), and can it sit there carrying the fault without tripping while something downstream clears (Icw, always with a time — e.g. 1 s or 3 s). Selection against the plant's fault levels (~38.5 kA one transformer, ~77 kA tie-closed, indicative) is worked through in Part 2 — breaker selection. On a datasheet, the trap is comparing one vendor's Icu against another's Ics, or accepting any kA figure quoted at a voltage other than yours.

Ue / Ui / Uimp

Three voltage ratings, three jobs: Ue is the operational voltage at which the performance tables are valid; Ui is the insulation design voltage (≥ Ue); Uimp is the impulse withstand for surges. Breaking capacity generally falls as Ue rises — always read the kA column at 400/415 V for this plant, not the flattering value at 240 V.

50/60 Hz — usually dual, never assumed

The SEC system is 60 Hz. Most modern switchgear, breakers, transformers in this class, and electronic equipment are genuinely dual-rated 50/60 Hz — but "most" is not "all", and the dual rating must appear on the datasheet and the nameplate, not in a salesman's email. Watch for: CTs/VTs and meters specified per-frequency; capacitor banks and detuned reactors whose tuning is frequency-specific by definition; and above all rotating machines. Motors and generator sets are not trivially dual-frequency: a 4-pole motor runs 1500 rpm at 50 Hz and 1800 rpm at 60 Hz, with different V/Hz, different fan loads and different driven-machine power demands. A genset alternator is wound and its engine governed for one frequency. Treat any 50 Hz-referenced data for rotating plant as inapplicable until the vendor issues 60 Hz data.

IP and IK — dust is the design case

IP ratings (IEC 60529) code ingress protection: first digit solids/dust, second digit water. This plant's outdoor minimum is IP54 — "dust-protected" and splash-proof; IP65 buys "dust-tight". IK ratings (IEC 62262) code impact resistance, relevant for gear in traffic areas. Two cautions: the IP rating belongs to the enclosure as installed (every unsealed gland hole voids it), and higher IP means less ventilation — re-check thermal derating whenever you raise IP.

Altitude and the standards line

Air insulates and cools less well at altitude; standard ratings hold to 1000 m (MV gear) or 2000 m (much LV gear), with correction factors above. Saudi sites are mostly low, but confirm — some inland sites exceed correction thresholds. Finally, the standards line: insist on the current edition of the relevant IEC standard (60947 for LV breakers, 62271 for MV gear, 61439 for assemblies, 60076 for transformers), cited as SASO-adopted IEC for Saudi procurement, with type-test evidence behind every claimed clause.

Red-flag checklist — when a datasheet is hiding something

Run this list over every datasheet that crosses your desk. Any single hit means "ask the vendor in writing"; two or more hits mean "treat the whole document as unverified marketing".

  • No type-test certificate offered, or "certificate available on request" with nothing attached — request it before technical acceptance, and check it names the same product family, frame and rating you are buying.
  • Ratings quoted at 20 °C or 25 °C ambient — laboratory-flattering conditions; demand the 40 °C standard values and the 50 °C derated values for Saudi service.
  • "Designed to", "in accordance with", "complies with the intent of" a standard — weasel wording. Only "type-tested to IEC xxxxx" (with the certificate) is evidence.
  • kA ratings quoted without a time qualifier or without the test voltage — a short-time withstand without its duration (1 s vs 3 s) or a breaking capacity without its voltage is not a rating, it is a slogan.
  • Ics not stated, or quietly set at 50 % of Icu on a main incomer application.
  • No 60 Hz column, or all performance data referenced to 50 Hz only — unacceptable for SEC-connected plant; rotating machines especially must have native 60 Hz data.
  • No derating tables at all (temperature, altitude, enclosure) — the vendor is leaving you to discover the limits in service.
  • Catalogue page submitted where a project datasheet was required — generic family data is not a commitment to your configuration.
  • IP rating quoted for the device but not for the assembly/enclosure as it will actually be installed on site.
  • Footnote symbols in the ratings table that lead to no visible footnote — request the unabridged document; the missing footnote is rarely good news.
Key takeaway: a datasheet is a claim, not a fact. The reference conditions (ambient, voltage, frequency, time) are part of every rating — a number quoted without its conditions is incomplete, and in a 50 °C, 60 Hz Saudi plant, incomplete usually means optimistic.

SASO conformity and the SABER platform — the concept

Technical compliance with an IEC standard and legal importability into Saudi Arabia are two different hurdles. The second is administered by SASO (the Saudi Standards, Metrology and Quality Organization), which adopts international standards — largely IEC for electrical products — as Saudi technical regulations, and requires regulated imported products to carry conformity certificates before they clear customs. The administrative vehicle is SABER, SASO's online conformity platform: at concept level, the importer registers the product, an accredited conformity assessment body verifies compliance against the applicable technical regulation, a product certificate of conformity is issued, and each consignment then receives a shipment certificate of conformity that customs checks at the border. (The exact scope of regulated categories, the certificate types and the process details evolve — treat this paragraph as orientation, and confirm current requirements per shipment.)

Why a field engineer should care about what looks like a customs formality: equipment that arrives without its SABER paperwork can sit in port while your outage window, your crane hire and your commissioning team do not. A transformer with a 9-month manufacturing lead time (see above) that then loses further weeks at customs for a missing certificate is a wholly avoidable project wound. Conformity certification belongs on the purchase order as a named deliverable, with the importer-of-record responsibility stated, not discovered at the port.

SASO conformity tells you the product class meets Saudi technical regulations. It does not tell you the product suits your application — that is what the datasheet, type-test certificate and your Part 2 calculations are for. Both hurdles must be cleared independently.

SEC vendor approval and the Saudi Building Code

For equipment on or facing the utility side — metering panels, the incoming MV switchgear arrangement, protection schemes at the intake — the Saudi Electricity Company operates approved-vendor and approved-material lists, and connection works are accepted against SEC specifications and the SEC Distribution Code. At concept level: it is not enough that your 13.8 kV switchgear is type-tested to IEC 62271-200; for the utility interface, the make and type should appear on (or be accepted onto) the relevant SEC list, and the intake design follows SEC standard arrangements. Getting a technically excellent but unlisted product accepted is possible but slow — for utility-facing packages, start from the approved list rather than from the open catalogue, and engage SEC early through the connection-application process. (List contents and procedures change; verify current SEC requirements per project.)

In parallel, the Saudi Building Code — specifically SBC 401 for electrical works — governs the installation design itself: wiring methods, protection, earthing, safety provisions for buildings and facilities. SBC 401 is the Saudi adaptation of international electrical-code practice, and authorities having jurisdiction review designs and installations against it. Practically: cite SASO-adopted IEC standards for products, SBC 401 for the installation, and the SEC Distribution Code / Saudi Grid Code for the utility connection — the three cover different territory and a compliant project needs all three (see the standards approach used throughout this manual).

The approved-vendor culture — Aramco/SABIC spillover

Beyond the regulator and the utility, Saudi industrial procurement has been shaped for decades by the practices of the largest end users — Saudi Aramco and SABIC — whose engineering standards and approved-manufacturer regimes (such as Aramco's materials-system specifications and vendor qualification processes) define, for their own projects, exactly which manufacturers and which factories may supply each commodity class. This culture spills over: many private industrial clients, EPC contractors and PMC consultants in the Kingdom adopt Aramco- or SABIC-style approved-vendor lists wholesale, or write "shall be from an approved manufacturer regularly supplying the Saudi market" clauses into otherwise ordinary specifications (indicative — this varies widely by client and contract).

For the field engineer the consequence is simple: the real catalogue for your project is the intersection of the vendor's catalogue and the client's approved-vendor list. Establish that intersection before falling in love with a product family. A technically superior drive or relay that is not on the client's list is, for your project, a product that does not exist — or one that costs you a months-long qualification exercise. Conversely, list-qualified vendors usually carry the Saudi-specific documentation (SASO certificates, 50 °C derating data, 60 Hz test reports, local service presence) as a matter of routine, which de-risks everything in the previous two sections.

What this means at the FAT and in the field

Pulling the threads together, here is the practical discipline. When you attend a factory acceptance test — or receive equipment on site — the hardware is only half the inspection. Ask for, and actually open, three documents: the SASO/SABER conformity certificate (does it cover this product category and this consignment?), the type-test report (does it name this family, frame and rating, this Icu/Icw, this standard edition?), and the routine test report (does the serial number match the nameplate in front of you, and did every routine test pass?). Then put the project datasheet beside the nameplate and read them line against line: voltage, frequency (60 Hz!), rated current and its reference ambient, fault ratings with their times, IP rating, tap range. Ten minutes of comparison at the factory saves ten weeks of dispute at site.

  • Confirm the vendor is on the client's (and where applicable SEC's) approved list before the technical evaluation starts.
  • Confirm SASO/SABER certification responsibility is named on the purchase order, with the importer of record identified.
  • At FAT: sight the type-test certificate and check it covers the exact family, frame, rating and standard edition supplied.
  • At FAT: obtain the routine test report and match serial numbers against nameplates, unit by unit.
  • Verify nameplate data against the frozen project datasheet — voltage, 60 Hz, current at stated ambient, kA ratings with time, IP rating.
  • Check 50 °C derating data is included in the final documentation, not just the 40 °C reference values.
  • File the complete dossier (datasheet, certificates, GA, O&M) before energisation — it is also your baseline for every future modification.
Key takeaway: in Saudi practice a piece of equipment is only "real" when four proofs line up — the client's vendor approval, the SASO/SABER conformity certificate, the type-test certificate for the design, and the routine test report for your serial number. The field engineer who asks for all four at FAT, every time, almost never gets surprised at site.
