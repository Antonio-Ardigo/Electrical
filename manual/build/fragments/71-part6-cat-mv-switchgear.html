MV Switchgear Catalogue

Indicative catalogue data. Product families and typical parameters are listed for training and familiarisation only, are approximate, and change with manufacturer revisions. No part numbers are given; always obtain current certified data sheets and SASO/SEC acceptance status from the manufacturer.

Goal: learn how to read a medium-voltage switchgear catalogue the way a specifying engineer reads it — starting from what the plant actually needs (tag MV-SWGR, the 13.8 kV intake board), mapping each plant requirement to the catalogue parameter that answers it, and finishing with the specification card you would attach to a request for quotation. The vendor families named here (ABB UniGear, Siemens NXAIR, Schneider PIX and their relatives) are the ones you will most often meet on Saudi industrial sites, so the comparison doubles as field familiarisation. For the reasoning behind the ratings themselves, this section leans on the short-circuit study and the circuit-breaker tutorial; for how Part 6 catalogue sections are meant to be used, see the catalogue introduction.

What the Plant Needs — the Demand Side of the Catalogue

Before opening any catalogue, write down what you are shopping for. A catalogue is a menu of answers; if you do not arrive with the questions, the glossy photographs will choose for you. For this plant the questions are fixed by the Rev 3 plant basis and by the short-circuit study, and they define one piece of equipment: tag MV-SWGR, the medium-voltage switchboard that takes the SEC 13.8 kV supply and distributes it to the two transformer feeders (TX-1 and TX-2 — see the transformer catalogue section).

The shopping list, with the reason behind each line:

Requirement (indicative)Why this value
Rated voltage class 17.5 kV The system runs at 13.8 kV. IEC switchgear is built in standard voltage classes (7.2 / 12 / 17.5 / 24 / 36 kV); the class must be the next standard step at or above the highest system voltage, so a 13.8 kV system lands in the 17.5 kV class. The 12 kV class — the most common class worldwide, built for 11 kV systems — is not acceptable here; this is the single most common ordering error when a design is transplanted from an 11 kV country to a 13.8 kV SEC network.
Rated frequency 60 Hz The SEC grid is 60 Hz. Most catalogue type tests cover 50/60 Hz, but the rating must be declared at 60 Hz — it changes the peak withstand factor (2.6 instead of 2.5) and some instrument-transformer accuracy figures.
Busbar rating 630–1250 A The plant draws roughly 2 × 66.9 A at MV — trivially small. The bus rating is set by the smallest frames the product family offers (typically 630 A) and by any future-load margin, not by load current.
Short-time withstand 31.5 kA / 3 s The design utility fault level is 25 kA, but SEC states a network range of 25–40 kA. 31.5 kA is the next standard rating above the design duty; if SEC confirms a connection-point level above 31.5 kA in writing, the board class must rise to 40 kA. See the short-circuit section for the full reasoning.
Metal-clad, withdrawable vacuum breakers Earthed metal partitions between busbar, breaker and cable compartments limit fault spread and allow safe access; withdrawable trucks give visible isolation and fast breaker exchange. Vacuum is the dominant MV interruption technology (breaker tutorial).
Internal arc classification IAC AFLR 31.5 kA 1 s If an arc ever ignites inside the board, the enclosure must contain it for long enough for protection to clear, without burning or blowing panels into the operator aisle. Directly linked to the arc-flash study (§ arc-flash).
Service conditions 50 °C design ambient, dust Catalogue continuous ratings are referenced to 40 °C; Saudi rooms must either be cooled or the gear derated — covered in the Saudi-specific notes below.

Notice what is not on the list: load current barely features. A 2 MW plant on a 13.8 kV intake is a fault-level-driven purchase, not a load-driven one. The board you buy will spend its life carrying a few tens of amps and must nevertheless survive 31.5 kA for three seconds, because the withstand duty is set by the SEC network behind it, not by anything inside the fence. This inversion — tiny normal duty, enormous abnormal duty — is normal at the boundary between a utility and a small plant, and it is why the catalogue comparison below concentrates on withstand, arc classification and compartmentalization rather than ampacity.

Key takeaway: arrive at the catalogue with the demand side written down — 17.5 kV class, 60 Hz, 630–1250 A bus, 31.5 kA/3 s, metal-clad withdrawable vacuum, IAC AFLR — and treat every product page as a yes/no answer to that list. All values indicative; the formal studies and SEC's written fault-level confirmation govern.

The Vendor Families You Will Meet

Three air-insulated metal-clad families dominate the primary-distribution market in the Gulf, one from each of the big three manufacturers. They are functionally equivalent — all are withdrawable vacuum-breaker boards type-tested to IEC 62271-200 — and a specification written around the standard, not around a brand, will receive compliant offers from all three. Where ring main units fit — and why not here

Catalogues also offer a cheaper, smaller class of MV gear: the ring main unit (RMU) and its modular cousins — Schneider SM6 (modular, air- or gas-insulated functional units) and ABB SafeRing / SafePlus (sealed gas-insulated ring main blocks). These are secondary distribution switchgear: fixed-pattern (no withdrawable truck), mostly switch-fuse or small-breaker functions, designed to loop a distribution ring in and out and tee off one or two transformers. They are superb at that job — compact, sealed against dust, near-maintenance-free — and you will see thousands of them in Saudi utility kiosks.

They are the wrong product for MV-SWGR, for three reasons. First, withstand: typical RMU short-time ratings sit around 20–25 kA, below the 31.5 kA this intake demands. Second, protection: a utility-intake board needs full-function IED protection on withdrawable breakers with proper CTs and VTs (protection coordination), not fuse-switch combinations. Third, service continuity: a fixed-pattern sealed block cannot have a breaker racked out and exchanged in an hour. Know the RMU class so you can recognise when a vendor is quoting you one to win on price — and decline.

Gas-insulated primary gear and the SF6-free trend

For completeness, the same vendors offer gas-insulated primary switchgear — Siemens NXPLUS C (and the 8DJH family at secondary level), ABB ZX-series — where the busbars and breakers live in sealed, gas-filled stainless enclosures. The attraction is footprint (roughly half the width of air-insulated gear) and immunity to dust and humidity, which is genuinely tempting in the Saudi environment. The costs are price, less straightforward extension, and historically the gas itself: SF6 is the most potent greenhouse gas known and is being regulated out of new installations worldwide.

The industry's answer is the SF6-free generation: vacuum interrupters for breaking, with clean/dry air or proprietary low-GWP mixtures (ABB's AirPlus, Siemens' "Blue" portfolio, Schneider's air-insulated AirSeT take on SM6/RM) for insulation. Typical ratings already cover the 17.5 kV / 31.5 kA point this plant needs. For a new Saudi plant the practical guidance is: air-insulated metal-clad remains the default for a primary intake board in a conditioned room; if a gas-insulated board is chosen for footprint or environment, prefer the SF6-free variant and ask the vendor for its SASO/SEC acceptance status explicitly. Typical ranges only — no part numbers, no prices; tender data sheets govern.

Comparing offers — the classic traps

When three compliant-looking bids land on your desk, the differences hide in the fine print, and they recur so reliably they deserve a list:

  • The 12 kV substitution. A bid built on the vendor's high-volume 12 kV variant "suitable for 13.8 kV operation". Reject: operating a 12 kV-class board (28 kV/75 kV insulation ladder) at 13.8 kV erodes the insulation margin the type test promised. Demand the 17.5 kV type-test certificates by number.
  • IAC at a smaller current or shorter time. "Internal arc classified" with 25 kA/0.5 s in a footnote. The IAC figures must match the short-time rating and exceed the realistic clearing time.
  • IAC AFL, not AFLR. Cheaper, but commits the installation to a rear wall forever and complicates rear cable work.
  • "Metal-enclosed" sold as "metal-clad". Metal-enclosed is the umbrella term; only LSC2B-PM with earthed metal partitions is metal-clad in the sense this plant specifies. Check the LSC/partition codes, not the brochure adjective.
  • Ratings quoted at 40 °C only. Silent on 50 °C performance — see the Saudi notes; make the 50 °C statement a mandatory bid deliverable.
  • 50 Hz-only type tests. Rare from the big three, common from re-badgers. The 60 Hz declaration affects the peak factor and instrument transformer accuracy; ask for it explicitly.
A clean way to force comparability: send the specification card as a fill-in table and require each bidder to return it line by line with certificate references. A bidder who will not fill in a row is telling you something.

Anatomy of an MV Switchgear Catalogue Page

An MV switchgear catalogue is organised in two layers. The front pages describe the system — construction, compartments, arc classification, type tests. The back pages are the configurator — panel types, rating combinations, dimensions and weights per panel. Engineers who only read the back pages buy boards that meet the ratings but disappoint in service; the front pages are where the safety and maintainability are decided.

Panel types — the vocabulary of a line-up

A switchboard is ordered as a row of standard panels ("functional units"). The catalogue's panel-type table is the menu, and for this plant the line-up is short:

Compartments and loss-of-service-continuity

"Metal-clad" means the panel interior is divided by earthed metal partitions into (at least) four compartments: busbar, circuit-breaker, cable (with CTs and the earthing switch), and the low-voltage box on top holding relays, meters and wiring. The point of the partitions is brutally practical: a fault, a tool, or a hand in one compartment is kept out of the others. IEC 62271-200 codifies this in two ratings you should read off every catalogue page:

Partition class — PM (partitions of metal, earthed) versus PI (insulating partitions). Specify PM for a primary board. Loss of service continuity class — what must be de-energised to open a compartment. LSC2B, the metal-clad benchmark, means you can open the breaker or cable compartment of one panel while the busbar and the other panels' cables stay live. On a single-incomer plant board this is what lets you work on a transformer feeder breaker while the other transformer keeps the plant alive. Lesser classes (LSC1, LSC2A) belong to secondary gear and RMUs.

Withdrawable versus fixed

In a withdrawable (draw-out) design the breaker sits on a truck; racking it from service to test to removed position physically separates the primary contacts behind automatic metal shutters. You gain visible-break isolation, a built-in test position for secondary injection, and breaker exchange measured in minutes. Fixed-pattern boards weld the breaker into the circuit and rely on a separate disconnector — cheaper and more compact, and the normal choice only at secondary level or in sealed gas-insulated designs where the interrupter is maintenance-free for life. For MV-SWGR, specify withdrawable.

Internal arc classification — decoding IAC AFLR 31.5 kA 1 s

The internal arc rating is a type test in which a real arc is deliberately struck inside each compartment and the enclosure must protect indicator cloths standing in for people. Decode the string: IAC = internal arc classified; A = authorised-personnel access only; F L R = the tested sides — Front, Lateral, Rear (an installation against a wall may only need AFL — but specify AFLR so the board can stand free); 31.5 kA 1 s = the arc current and duration survived. The arc current should equal the short-time rating; the duration must exceed the slowest realistic clearing time at the bus (check against the grading study and the arc-flash calculation). Note the asymmetry: short-time withstand is 3 s (a through-fault duty), internal arc is 1 s (an in-board arc, which protection must kill far faster).

IP ratings and the ratings ladder

Catalogue enclosure protection is typically IP3X or IP4X overall with IP2X between compartments — designed for a clean indoor room, not a dust storm. The implications for a Saudi switchroom are handled in the Saudi notes. Finally, every catalogue states the IEC 62271-1 insulation ladder for the voltage class; for 17.5 kV expect a 1-minute power-frequency withstand of 38 kV rms and a lightning impulse withstand of 95 kV peak (indicative standard values). If a data sheet shows the 12 kV-class ladder (28 kV / 75 kV), the wrong class is being offered — stop and query it.

Protection, Instrument Transformers and Interlocks

A switchgear panel is only as good as the measurement and intelligence built into it. The catalogue chapters on instrument transformers, low-voltage compartments and interlocking are where you verify that the board can host the protection scheme designed in the protection coordination section — and they are the chapters most often skipped at tender stage, which is why CT ratio changes and interlock disputes are the classic switchgear variation orders.

Relays and the LV compartment

Each panel's LV box hosts the protection IED (numerical relay), the trip/close control switches, indication lamps and terminal rows. Catalogues state which relay families are pre-engineered for the panel — typically the vendor's own (ABB Relion, Siemens SIPROTEC, Schneider Easergy/MiCOM; see the relay catalogue section) — but any IEC-compliant IED can be accommodated if you say so at tender. Specify, do not assume: relay family and model class, 110 V DC auxiliary supply from the plant DCDB, trip-circuit supervision, and at least one spare programmable contact per function. For the incomer, ask for the SEC-side requirements (intertrip, metering accuracy) to be confirmed in writing before the LV box drawings are approved.

CT and VT options

The cable compartment carries the current transformers; the catalogue offers block (resin) or ring-core types, single- or multi-ratio. Order the cores by duty: protection cores typically class 5P20, metering cores class 0.5 with an instrument security factor, ratios sized to the feeder duty (a 100/1 or 100/5 class on a 66.9 A transformer feeder is a typical indicative starting point — the protection study fixes the final ratio and burden). Voltage transformers come fixed or withdrawable; specify withdrawable, fused VTs so a failed VT or blown fuse can be exchanged without de-energising the bus. All accuracy figures must be declared at 60 Hz.

Voltage indication and earthing

Every panel should carry a capacitive voltage detecting system (VDS/VPIS to IEC 62271-206): bushing-mounted capacitive dividers feeding front-plate indicators that show, per phase, whether the cable side is live. This is the first thing a field engineer checks before racking or earthing, and the catalogue option costs almost nothing at order time and a panel rebuild afterwards. Pair it with the cable earthing switch — fault-making rated (it must survive being closed onto a live cable by mistake), operated from the front, with a clear mechanical position indicator.

Interlocks — the safety logic in steel

Catalogues list the standard interlock set; verify all of these are included and add project-specific keys:

  • Breaker can only be racked between service and test positions when open; the truck mechanically blocks closing mid-travel.
  • Earthing switch can only close when the breaker truck is in test/removed position; the truck cannot rack to service while the earthing switch is closed.
  • Automatic metal shutters cover the live spouts whenever the truck is withdrawn — padlockable per compartment.
  • LV plug interlock: the truck cannot rack to service with the secondary (control) plug disconnected.
  • Key interlocks with the upstream SEC point of connection and (if fitted) any bus-tie scheme, per the operating philosophy; key schedules are a deliverable to review at drawing approval, not at site.
At factory acceptance, physically attempt every forbidden operation — racking with the breaker closed, closing the earthing switch in service position. A correct interlock refuses smoothly; a misbuilt one lets you start the movement and jams. The FAT checklist in the Saudi notes includes this.

Worked check — margin between SEC duty and board rating

Confirm the 31.5 kA class actually covers the design duty with sensible margin. Design utility fault level (Plant Data Card): 25 kA at 13.8 kV.

Withstand margin = 31.5 kA / 25 kA = 1.26 → 26 % above design duty

Peak (first-loop asymmetric) duty at the bus, using the standard 60 Hz factor of 2.6 (see short-circuit section for where 2.6 comes from):

i_p,duty = 2.6 × 25 kA = 65 kA vs I_p,rated = 2.6 × 31.5 kA ≈ 82 kA ✓

Thermal withstand: the board is tested for 31.5 kA for 3 s, an I²t capability of:

(31.5 kA)² × 3 s ≈ 2977 kA²·s vs duty (25 kA)² × 1 s = 625 kA²·s ✓ (≈ 4.8× margin)

where 1 s is a conservative upper bound on backup clearing time from the grading study. Sanity check: all three margins are comfortable at the 25 kA design level — but if SEC's written confirmation comes back at, say, 35 kA (inside their stated 25–40 kA range), the withstand margin becomes 31.5/35 = 0.9 — under-rated, not allowed — and the whole board moves to the 40 kA class. This is why the fault-level letter is the first document on the critical path, before the RFQ goes out. All figures indicative; the formal study governs.

Saudi-Specific Practice — Heat, Dust, SEC and SASO

The 50 °C question

Every continuous current rating in an IEC switchgear catalogue is referenced to the IEC 62271-1 "normal service conditions": maximum ambient 40 °C (24-hour average 35 °C). The plant's design ambient is 50 °C. A board run at its catalogue current in a 50 °C room exceeds its tested temperature-rise limits at the contacts and joints — silently, year after year, until a spout or bus joint anneals and fails. The fix is administrative, not heroic: ask the vendor, in the RFQ, for certified continuous ratings at 50 °C. Because this board is thermally loafing (66.9 A on 630 A frames — about 11 % utilisation), the derated figure will still clear the duty by a wide margin; the point is to have it in writing rather than discovered in service. The rule of thumb vendors apply is a few percent of current per kelvin of excess ambient — a 630 A panel typically certifies somewhere around 540–580 A at 50 °C (indicative; the vendor's thermal calculation governs). The same question must be asked of the relay/IED operating range and the space-heater sizing.

In practice most Saudi switchrooms are HVAC-conditioned to hold ≤40 °C, which restores the catalogue ratings — but then the HVAC becomes electrical infrastructure: specify room over-temperature alarms to the control system, and check what the board is rated for during an HVAC failure on a 50 °C day, because that is exactly when the plant will refuse to shed load.

Dust and the room

Air-insulated switchgear breathes the room's air. Desert dust is conductive when combined with morning humidity, and a dust film across insulation is the classic precursor to the internal arc the IAC rating exists for. Practice: positively pressurised switchroom with filtered HVAC intake, door-closer discipline, IP4X enclosures as a backstop (not a substitute), space heaters running whenever panels are de-energised, and insulation inspection/cleaning in the maintenance plan. If the room cannot be kept clean — remote yard locations, temporary power — that is the legitimate use-case for sealed gas-insulated (preferably SF6-free) gear from the families section.

SEC acceptance and SASO/SABER paperwork

Two regulatory tracks run in parallel and neither is optional. First, SEC: the intake board sits at the utility boundary, so SEC reviews and accepts the design — fault level confirmation in writing, metering and protection requirements at the point of connection, intertrip and earthing interface, and in many cases a preference list of previously-accepted switchgear types. Engage SEC at specification stage, not at energisation. Second, SASO conformity: imported electrical equipment enters the Kingdom through the SABER platform with certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC standards. Make the vendor responsible in the purchase order for supplying type-test certificates (IEC 62271-1/-100/-200, including the internal-arc and 60 Hz evidence), SABER/SASO certification, and country-of-origin documents — missing paperwork strands switchgear in port for months far more often than technical faults do.

Factory acceptance test — scope checklist

The FAT is your last cheap chance to find problems. Witness it in person for an intake board. Indicative scope:

  • Verify ratings plates: 17.5 kV class, 60 Hz, 31.5 kA/3 s, IAC AFLR 31.5 kA 1 s, against approved data sheets.
  • Review type-test certificates and routine-test reports; confirm SASO/SABER and SEC acceptance documents are in the dossier.
  • Power-frequency withstand (routine) on main and auxiliary circuits witnessed and recorded.
  • Measure main-circuit resistance (ductor) across each breaker and bus joint; compare to vendor limits.
  • Rack every breaker through service–test–removed; check shutter operation and padlocking.
  • Attempt every forbidden interlock operation (rack with breaker closed, earth switch in service position, close with LV plug off) — all must be mechanically refused.
  • Operate each earthing switch; verify position indication and key-interlock scheme against the approved key schedule.
  • Secondary injection of every IED: pickups, curves and trip mapping per the approved settings file (protection study).
  • Primary injection of CT cores for ratio and polarity; VT ratio and fuse supervision; VDS indication per phase.
  • Function-test anti-pumping, trip-circuit supervision, and all alarms to terminal level at 110 V DC.
  • Check space heaters, thermostats and panel lighting; verify IP sealing, gland plates and cable-box sizing against approved cable schedules.
  • Confirm 50 °C derating statement, spares list, lifting/transport drawings and O&M manuals are delivered before shipment release.

The catalogue's job is only to make you fluent for that conversation.
