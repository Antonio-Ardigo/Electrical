Distribution Transformers

Indicative catalogue data. Product families and typical parameters are listed for training and familiarisation only, are approximate, and change with manufacturer revisions. No part numbers are given; always obtain current certified data sheets and SASO/SEC acceptance status from the manufacturer.

Goal: learn to read a distribution-transformer catalogue the way a specifying engineer reads it — starting from what the plant actually needs (tags TX-1 and TX-2: 2 × 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z = 6 %), translating each nameplate line into the plant consequence it controls, and finishing with the specification card you would attach to a request for quotation. The families named here (Siemens GEAFOL, Schneider Trihal, the Hitachi Energy dry-type and EcoDry ranges, and the hermetic oil-immersed distribution families) are the ones most often met on Saudi industrial sites. For the electrical reasoning behind the ratings — why 1600 kVA, why Dyn11, why 6 % — see the transformer calculation tutorial; for how Part 6 catalogue sections are meant to be used, see the catalogue introduction.

What the Plant Needs — the Two 1600 kVA Workhorses

Everything between the SEC 13.8 kV intake and the plant's 400/230 V world passes through two boxes of copper and steel: TX-1 and TX-2, each 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z = 6 %, feeding the two halves of the split LV main switchboard with the bus-tie normally open. Each unit draws an indicative 66.9 A on its MV side at full rating and delivers 2309 A on its LV side. The plant's design demand is 2.0 MW at 0.85 power factor — 2353 kVA — so the pair runs at roughly 74 % in normal split operation and one unit carries about 87.5 % of its rating in the N−1 case with non-essential load shed. The impedance, 6 %, is the deliberate compromise that holds the LV short-circuit current to about 38.5 kA per transformer while keeping voltage regulation acceptable (the full reasoning lives in the transformer tutorial). All values indicative.

A transformer is the quietest purchase in the plant — no moving parts, no firmware, a forty-year design life — and that is exactly why catalogue mistakes hurt: an error ordered into a transformer is bolted into the plant for decades. The three decisions the catalogue forces on you are technology (cast resin or oil), losses class (you pay for losses every hour for forty years), and thermal basis (a 50 °C Saudi ambient is outside the IEC default assumptions and must be engineered, not hoped for).

Cast resin or oil-immersed — the first fork in the catalogue

Distribution transformers come in two basic technologies, and every vendor's catalogue splits at this fork on page one. In a cast-resin dry-type unit the MV windings are vacuum-cast in epoxy and the whole active part is cooled by the surrounding air (AN — air natural; optionally AF with fans). In an oil-immersed unit the core and windings sit in a tank of mineral or ester fluid that both insulates and carries heat to the tank wall and radiators (ONAN — oil natural, air natural; hermetically sealed in modern distribution sizes). Neither is "better"; they trade against each other:

For this plant the indicative baseline is cast-resin units indoors, in an air-conditioned or at least force-ventilated substation adjoining the LV switchroom: the units sit close to the LV-MSB (short 2309 A bus runs), the fire case is benign, and the AF fan option buys cheap N−1 margin. The oil-immersed alternative — two hermetic ONAN units on an outdoor pad — remains legitimate where the building cannot host them; the selection card in the selection section works for either.

Key takeaway: the plant's demand side is fixed before the catalogue opens — 2 × 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z = 6 %, 60 Hz, 50 °C ambient. The catalogue only decides how those numbers are delivered: resin or oil, which losses tier, and what thermal margin. All values indicative; the detailed studies govern.

Losses classes — the Ecodesign tiers as a common yardstick

Transformer efficiency is bought at order time, and the industry's clearest yardstick is the EU Ecodesign regulation (548/2014, "Tier 2" since 2021), which fixes maximum no-load (P0) and load (Pk) losses per size — paralleled by the IEC 60076 loss-class letters (e.g. A0Ak, the lowest-loss combination). Saudi Arabia does not apply the EU regulation as law — SASO efficiency requirements and SEC specifications govern — but quoting "Ecodesign Tier 2 compliant" or "A0Ak" in the RFQ is the cleanest way to force comparable loss figures from all bidders. Indicatively, a Tier-2-class 1600 kVA cast-resin unit shows a no-load loss of roughly 2.2–2.8 kW and a load loss of roughly 12–14 kW at 120 °C reference; the oil equivalent is lower on both counts. Remember the asymmetry: no-load loss is paid 8760 hours a year on both units the moment they are energised; load loss scales with the square of loading. At ~74 % split-bus loading both terms matter, which is why a cheap high-loss unit is the most expensive item in this catalogue over a 40-year life.

Altitude and ambient basis — read the small print

Every figure in the table above is referenced to the IEC 60076 "normal service conditions": altitude ≤ 1000 m and ambient ≤ 40 °C maximum (30 °C monthly / 20 °C yearly average). Altitude is rarely a problem on Saudi coastal and plateau sites below 1000 m — but check it for highland sites (Abha sits above 2000 m, where dielectric and cooling corrections both apply). Ambient, by contrast, is always a problem at a 50 °C design figure, and it is handled properly in the datasheet section below.

As with the switchgear families, the brand comparison is not won on headline ratings — all the families above deliver 1600 kVA at 13.8/0.4 kV without strain. It is won on certified losses, the written 50 °C / 60 Hz statements, local service and SASO/SEC paperwork, and delivery time.

How to Read a Transformer Datasheet

A transformer datasheet is a one-page contract: every line is a number the factory promises and a routine test will verify. Walk the nameplate top to bottom, and for each line know which plant problem it controls. The order below is the order the lines usually appear.

Rated power — and what "1600 kVA" actually promises

Rated power (IEC 60076-1) is the apparent power the unit can deliver continuously at rated voltage and frequency without exceeding the temperature-rise limits of its insulation class, at the standard ambient. It is a thermal promise, not an electrical limit — the copper does not know about kVA, only about heat. Two consequences: first, every kVA figure silently carries its ambient basis (40 °C max), which is why the Saudi correction below exists; second, short overloads are a matter of thermal time constants, not of the nameplate — cast resin tolerates cyclic overloads per IEC 60076-12 loading guides, oil per IEC 60076-7.

Vector group — Dyn11

Read Dyn11 as three facts: D — MV winding in delta; yn — LV winding in star with the neutral brought out (the plant TN-S system earths it solidly at the LV-MSB); 11 — the LV line voltage leads the MV by 330° (11 o'clock). The delta primary traps triplen harmonic currents and lets the star secondary serve unbalanced single-phase 230 V load; the clock number matters the day anyone parallels the two units through the bus-tie or adds a third source — paralleling units of different vector groups is a dead short. Both plant units must therefore be identical: Dyn11, same ratio, same impedance.

Impedance voltage — Z = 6 %

The impedance voltage is measured, not assumed: the routine short-circuit test applies whatever MV voltage drives rated current through a shorted LV winding, and expresses it as a percentage of rated voltage. Z fixes both ends of a see-saw: fault current down (LV Isc ≈ FLC / Z ≈ 2309 A / 0.06 ≈ 38.5 kA per unit — the number the entire LV switchboard withstand rating hangs on) and voltage regulation up. IEC tolerance on Z is ±10 % of the declared value: a unit delivered at 5.4 % pushes the LV fault duty toward ~42.8 kA, which still sits under a 50 kA board but eats margin — and units to be paralleled must match within the tolerance band or they will not share load. Check the test certificate value, not just the nameplate.

No-load and load losses

Two numbers, two physical origins, two bills. No-load loss P0 is the core (iron) loss — hysteresis and eddy currents in the laminations — present whenever the unit is energised, at any load, 8760 h/year. Load loss Pk is the winding (copper) I²R plus stray loss, quoted at rated current and a reference temperature (75 °C oil / 120 °C dry — check which, because comparing a 75 °C figure against a 120 °C figure flatters the wrong bidder). At the plant's ~74 % split-bus loading the load loss realised is Pk × 0.74² ≈ 0.55 Pk. Evaluate bids on capitalised losses (a typical owner formula prices each kW of P0 several times higher than each kW of Pk), not on purchase price.

Insulation class and temperature rise — the 50 °C correction

A dry-type insulation system is classed by the temperature it can endure for its design life: class F = 155 °C, class H = 180 °C. The datasheet pairs the class with a rated winding temperature rise — e.g. "class F, 100 K rise" — and the arithmetic only closes at the standard ambient: 40 °C ambient + 100 K rise + ~15 K hot-spot allowance ≈ 155 °C. Put the same unit in a 50 °C room and the hot-spot lands ~10 K above the class limit; insulation ageing roughly doubles for every 6–10 K of over-temperature, so a forty-year transformer quietly becomes a twenty-year one. IEC 60076-11 gives the corrections, and the buyer has two clean options:

  • Derate: accept a reduced continuous rating at 50 °C — indicatively around 7–10 % off (a "1600 kVA" unit certified at roughly 1440–1490 kVA), per the vendor's thermal calculation; or
  • Buy headroom (preferred here): order a class F system designed for a reduced rise (e.g. 80 K), or a class H system run at class-F rise, so the full 1600 kVA is certified at 50 °C ambient with the ageing budget intact. The cost premium is small against the asset life it protects.
The classic tender failure: the datasheet says "1600 kVA, class F" and nobody asks at what ambient. Make "rated power certified at 50 °C design ambient per IEC 60076-11" a mandatory line in the RFQ — and apply the same question to the AF fan rating and to oil units via IEC 60076-2.

Tappings — ±2 × 2.5 %

The MV winding carries an off-circuit tap changer with five positions: ±2 × 2.5 % around 13.8 kV. Its job is to correct a sustained deviation of the SEC supply voltage from nominal so the LV bus sits near 400 V — set once at commissioning after measuring the actual MV voltage, then revisited only if the network changes. "Off-circuit" means exactly that: the unit must be de-energised and isolated to move the selector; operating it on load destroys the contacts. Both plant units must always stand on the same tap, or the bus-tie paralleling case circulates current between them.

Inrush, sound and the remaining lines

Inrush: energising a 1600 kVA unit draws a magnetising inrush with a first peak typically 8–12 × rated current (indicatively 500–800 A on the 66.9 A MV feeder), decaying over tens of cycles. The MV feeder protection must ride through it — the relay's inrush (2nd-harmonic) restraint and the curve choice in the protection study depend on the vendor's declared inrush figure, so ask for it. Sound power level: per IEC 60076-10, a 1600 kVA dry unit typically declares around 65–75 dB(A) sound power — it matters for substations adjoining occupied rooms, and low-noise designs (reduced flux density) trade against no-load loss. Finally, check the insulation levels (for a 13.8 kV winding, the 17.5 kV-class ladder: 38 kV rms power-frequency / 95 kV peak lightning impulse, indicative) and the rated frequency line — which deserves its own discussion in the selection section.

Sanity check: 38.5 kA sits under the 50–65 kA LV-MSB rating with margin; the ~77 kA paralleled figure is exactly why the bus-tie is normally open and interlocked. If a vendor's unit arrives at the low end of the ±10 % impedance tolerance (5.4 %), the single-unit duty rises to ~42.8 kA — still inside the board rating, but the margin is consumed; this is why the test-certificate Z value is reviewed before energisation. The AN / AF decision — buying N−1 margin with fans

Cast-resin catalogues quote two ratings per frame: AN (natural air) and AF (forced air, add-on cross-flow fans), with AF typically +40 %. Two ways to use it on this plant, both indicative: order 1600 kVA AN units and treat the AF boost (~2240 kVA) as emergency headroom for the N−1 case — the survivor could then carry the full 2353 kVA demand without load shedding; or use AF to cover future load growth without changing frames. The discipline: the AF rating only exists while the fans, the fan supply and the fan controls work, so a design that depends on AF for the normal case has quietly created a new single point of failure — use AF as margin, not as the basis. Fans are triggered by winding temperature sensors (PT100) via the T-relay; test them at FAT.

The 60 Hz note — ordering for the SEC grid

The SEC grid is 60 Hz, and the unit must carry a 60 Hz nameplate. The physics is forgiving in this direction: core flux is proportional to V/f, so a transformer designed for 50 Hz and operated at the same voltage and 60 Hz runs at lower flux density — no saturation risk, slightly lower no-load loss. A 50 Hz-designed unit is therefore generally electromagnetically fine at 60 Hz (never the reverse — 60 Hz design at 50 Hz over-fluxes and saturates). But "generally fine" is not a procurement basis: cooling fans, motor drives in accessories, declared losses, sound level and the routine-test basis all shift with frequency, and SASO/SEC paperwork will ask for the 60 Hz declaration. The rule: always order a 60 Hz nameplate, with the routine tests performed and certified at 60 Hz; treat the 50-at-60 tolerance only as comfort that a misdeclared unit is not instantly dangerous, and verify fan and auxiliary ratings explicitly if any 50 Hz-basis component appears in the offer.

Saudi-Specific Practice — Heat, Dust, SASO and SEC

SASO certification and the SABER platform

Imported transformers clear Saudi customs through the SABER platform with certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC 60076 series — and SASO additionally maintains minimum energy-performance requirements for distribution transformers, so the losses figures are a regulatory matter, not just an economic one. Make the vendor contractually responsible for: SABER product and shipment certificates, type-test and routine-test reports (IEC 60076-1/-11 for dry, -1/-2 for oil, including the 60 Hz basis), and country-of-origin documents. Missing conformity paperwork strands transformers in port far more often than technical defects do. Note also that several of the families in the families table are manufactured inside the Kingdom under licence — local content (and the IKTVA programme) can be a tender criterion and usually shortens delivery.

SEC approved-vendor expectations

Where the transformer sits at or near the SEC boundary, expect SEC to apply its distribution-transformer specifications (the SEC standard transformer specs, e.g. the 51-SDMS series for distribution equipment) and, in practice, an approved-vendor expectation: makes and factories with an SEC acceptance history clear review faster. Even for plant-side units behind the metering point, aligning the data sheet with SEC's spec families (loss levels, fittings, rating-plate content) costs little and removes review friction. Engage SEC at specification stage and get the requirements — including the fault-level letter that drives the LV board rating — in writing before the RFQ goes out.

Heat, dust and the substation room

The 50 °C thermal answer was engineered in the datasheet section; the room has to deliver it. Cast-resin windings breathe the room air, and desert dust is the technology's one real weakness: a dust film on the resin surface, wetted by morning humidity, tracks — and a tracking flashover inside an unenclosed transformer is a plant-down event. Practice for this plant, all indicative: cast-resin units in IP31–IP44 ventilated enclosures (the enclosure derates the unit slightly — make the vendor certify the enclosed rating), in a force-ventilated or HVAC-conditioned room with filtered intake and over-temperature alarms to the control system; PT100 winding sensors wired to the T-relay with staged fan-start / alarm / trip; and scheduled de-energised cleaning of the windings written into the maintenance plan. Oil units outdoors dodge the dust problem (sealed tank) but inherit solar gain, sand abrasion of radiators and bunding requirements per SBC 401 and the fire code.

Seismic and transport

Saudi sites are mostly low-seismicity, but SBC requirements and (for industrial-city sites) the local authority may still ask for seismic qualification of anchorage — cast-resin units are top-heavy, and the anchor-bolt calculation is the vendor's to certify against the declared seismic class. Transport is the larger practical risk: a 1600 kVA unit is a ~3–5 t fragile load, and resin windings dislike shock. Specify impact recorders on each unit for the road journey, inspect on arrival before off-loading signature, and check the catalogue's transport dimensions and enclosure split early — door and corridor sizes have redesigned more substations than electrical calculations have.

At the factory acceptance test, witness the routine tests (ratio, vector group, impedance, losses, applied/induced voltage, partial discharge for resin units — PD is the cast-resin quality fingerprint, indicatively ≤10 pC), and read the measured impedance and losses against the capitalisation in the bid. The FAT certificate values, not the catalogue, become the plant's protection-study and energy-model inputs.

Procurement review checklist

  • Ratings line verified: 2 × 1600 kVA, 13.8/0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z = 6 %, 60 Hz nameplate — both units identical, impedances matched within tolerance.
  • Rated power certified in writing at 50 °C design ambient (IEC 60076-11 / -2), including the AF and enclosed ratings where applicable.
  • Losses (P0/Pk) declared at a stated reference temperature, evaluated on capitalised cost, and compliant with SASO efficiency requirements.
  • Insulation class/rise, fire-behaviour class (F1) and climatic/environmental classes (C2/E2) stated; IP enclosure rating and room ventilation design agreed.
  • Tappings ±2 × 2.5 % off-circuit confirmed; commissioning tap-setting procedure and same-tap rule for both units in the O&M documents.
  • Protection interfaces complete: PT100/T-relay schedule, declared inrush data to the relay study, marshalling to DCS/PMS defined.
  • Paperwork chain complete: SABER/SASO certificates, type- and routine-test reports at 60 Hz, SEC spec alignment and acceptance references, transport/impact-recorder and seismic anchorage documents.
