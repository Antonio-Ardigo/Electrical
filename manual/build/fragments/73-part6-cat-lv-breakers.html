Three Tiers of LV Breaker — Where Each One Lives in This Plant

Low-voltage breakers come in three constructional tiers, and the easiest way to keep them straight is to walk the plant's own LV system from the transformer down to the last socket. All three tiers are certified to the same standard family — IEC 60947-2 for industrial breakers (ACBs and MCCBs) and IEC 60898-1 for the household-grade miniature breakers in final distribution boards — but they differ enormously in frame size, breaking capacity, adjustability and price.

TierTypical framesWhere in this plantDefining traits
ACB — air circuit breaker 800–6300 A LV-MSB incomers from TX-1/TX-2 (FLC 2309 A each), the normally-open bus-tie, and the largest outgoing feeders. Open-construction interrupter in air; usually withdrawable; high short-time withstand (Icw) so it can hold on through a downstream fault while a smaller breaker clears it — the anchor of selectivity. Always an electronic trip unit.
MCCB — moulded-case circuit breaker 16–1600 A LV-MSB outgoing feeders to MCC-1..n, the PFC bank, DB/EDB sub-boards; MCC incomers and large motor feeders; the ATS/DG LV connections. Sealed moulded housing; mostly fixed-mounted (plug-in/withdrawable options exist); current-limiting designs with very high Icu; thermal-magnetic or electronic trip; little or no intentional short-time delay capability at the small end.
MCB — miniature circuit breaker 0.5–125 A Final circuits in DB/EDB and the UPS distribution: lighting, small power, sockets, instrument supplies at 230 V. DIN-rail, fixed characteristics (B/C/D curves), breaking capacity typically 6–10 kA (IEC 60898-1) — acceptable only because cable impedance has collapsed the fault level by the time it reaches the final board.

The plant's numbers explain why the tiers exist. At the LV-MSB bus the prospective short-circuit current is about 38.5 kA with one transformer, and roughly 77 kA in the abnormal case where the bus-tie is closed with both transformers in parallel — a duty only an ACB or a heavy MCCB class can face. Two floors of cable later, at a 230 V lighting board, the same network can only deliver a few kA, and a 6 kA MCB is honest equipment. Matching breaker tier to the local fault level, not the worst one in the plant, is what keeps the switchboard affordable — and getting that local level right is precisely the job of the short-circuit study.

The LV system basis for everything in this section: 400/230 V, 60 Hz, TN-S earthing, fed by 2 × 1600 kVA transformers (LV FLC 2309 A each, Z = 6 %) onto a split bus with a normally-open tie. All values indicative per the Rev 3 plant basis; the formal studies govern.
Key takeaway: ACBs guard the bus and survive faults long enough to let others trip first; MCCBs clear feeder and motor faults hard and fast; MCBs mop up final circuits where the network can no longer deliver a big fault. One plant, three tiers, one selectivity story.

Three ACB families dominate the Gulf market, one per major vendor, and they are close functional equivalents: all are IEC 60947-2 category B breakers with electronic trip units, available fixed or withdrawable, in three or four physical frame sizes that between them span roughly 800 to 6300 A. Withdrawable versus fixed

ACBs are offered in fixed and withdrawable (cassette) versions. The withdrawable version racks between connected, test and disconnected positions exactly like its MV cousin: you gain visible isolation, secondary-injection testing in the test position, and breaker exchange in minutes — which on a two-incomer board like LV-MSB means one transformer's incomer can be swapped while the other half of the bus keeps the plant alive. The price is a deeper panel and a few percent on cost. For the three big breakers in this plant (two incomers and the bus-tie), specify withdrawable; for large outgoing ACB feeders, fixed is acceptable if the maintenance philosophy tolerates a dead-board exchange.

What actually differentiates the families

  • Trip-unit ecosystem — metering accuracy, protection options (LSIG and beyond), communications and diagnostics; this is where the brands genuinely diverge (see the trip-unit section).
  • Icw at the frame you need — the short-time withstand that underwrites time-graded selectivity; compare at your frame and performance level, not the brochure maximum.
  • Service in the Kingdom — spare trip units, racking accessories, local retrofit programmes; an ACB lives 25–30 years, far longer than its electronics.
  • Selectivity and cascading tables — vendor-published, type-tested pairings with their own downstream MCCBs; mixing brands sacrifices these tables and pushes you back onto first-principles coordination.
Catalogue frame names rarely equal current ratings: a "2500 frame" houses rating plugs or sensor sizes from roughly 1000 to 2500 A. Specify the sensor / rating-plug current In and the frame Iu separately, and leave sensor headroom for the 50 °C derating discussed in the datasheet section.

Moulded-Case Circuit Breakers — Feeder and Motor Workhorses

If the ACB is the board's anchor, the MCCB is its workhorse: every feeder from LV-MSB to an MCC, every MCC incomer, every large motor and the PFC bank connection is an MCCB decision. Four families cover most of the Saudi market. Each is built as a short ladder of physical frames, and each frame is sold in several breaking-capacity tiers — the same box with progressively stronger interrupting internals, typically stepping ~25 / 36 / 50 / 70 / 100 kA (some to 150 kA+) at 400 V. Thermal-magnetic versus electronic

A thermal-magnetic MCCB trips on heat (a bimetal bends — long-time overload) and on magnetics (a solenoid snaps — instantaneous short-circuit). It is cheap, immune to electronics failure, and adjustable only coarsely (thermal pickup, sometimes the magnetic threshold). An electronic MCCB measures current with built-in sensors and a microprocessor: full LSIG adjustability, settable short-time delay for selectivity, metering and communications. The practical split for this plant: electronic trip units on the LV-MSB outgoing feeders and MCC incomers (where selectivity against the ACBs must be engineered — see the LSIG study), and thermal-magnetic is acceptable for small, deep-downstream feeders where an instantaneous trip is fine. Note one honest limitation: a thermal element calibrated at 40 °C reads the hot switchboard air as load — another reason the 50 °C question in the Saudi notes matters.

Current limitation — the MCCB's party trick

Many MCCBs are current-limiting: their contacts blow apart electrodynamically within the first quarter-cycle and the arc voltage chokes the fault before it ever reaches its prospective peak. A current-limiting MCCB facing a prospective 38.5 kA may let through the energy of only a fraction of that — which is what makes vendor cascading (back-up) tables possible: a downstream breaker of modest Icu certified, by type test, to be protected by the current-limiting breaker above it. Cascading saves real money in MCCs, but only within one vendor's published tables — it is a type-tested combination, never a calculation you may improvise.

Key takeaway: pick the MCCB frame for the load current, the breaking-capacity tier for the local fault level, and the trip technology for the selectivity job the breaker has to do. Frame, tier and trip unit are three separate decisions hiding in one catalogue number.

Electronic Trip Units — the Intelligence in the Breaker

On an MV board the breaker is dumb muscle and the protection lives in a separate relay. On an LV board the two are fused into one product: the electronic trip unit bolted to the breaker face is simultaneously the protection relay, the meter and the communications gateway. When you compare ACB or MCCB offers, you are mostly comparing trip units. Each vendor has one trip-unit language spanning its range:

LSIG — the four letters that define the unit

Every trip unit is described by which of the four classic protection elements it carries — L (long-time, the overload element), S (short-time, the delayed short-circuit element that buys selectivity), I (instantaneous) and G (earth fault). An "LSI" unit has no earth-fault element; an "LI" unit cannot be time-graded. The full theory — what each pickup and delay means, how the curves are set for this plant's breakers, and the worked grading between MCC, feeder MCCB, incomer ACB and the transformer's MV protection — lives in the circuit-breaker section of Part 2; the catalogue task is simply to buy units that have the elements the study needs. For this plant: incomers, bus-tie and LV-MSB outgoing feeders need full LSIG; on a 400/230 V TN-S system the G element is your low-level earth-fault detection, and it must be ordered — it cannot be retrofitted into a unit that lacks the sensing.

Zone-selective interlocking (ZSI)

Time grading has a cruel price: the breaker nearest the source — facing the biggest fault — waits longest, and every added tenth of a second multiplies the arc energy at the main board. ZSI is the cure: a hard-wired pilot pair between trip units. When a downstream unit sees the fault, it sends a restraint signal upstream ("I have it — hold to your set delay"); when the upstream unit sees a fault and no restraint arrives, the fault must be in its own zone and it trips at minimum time instead of its grading delay. You keep full selectivity and lose the waiting. All three ACB trip-unit families support ZSI on their S and G elements; specify the interlocking wiring between the two incomers, the bus-tie and the major outgoing feeders, and demand the scheme be tested at FAT.

Metering and communications

Because every major breaker already measures its own current and voltage, the trip units double as the LV system's metering layer: per-feeder kW, kvar, energy and basic power quality, reported over Modbus (native on all families) or IEC 61850 (native module or external gateway, by family) to the plant DCS/PMS. Practical specification points: state the metering accuracy class you need (energy metering to ~Class 1 is a typical ask for incomers), order the comms module with the breaker rather than retrofitting, and remember the trip unit's protection function is self-powered from its current sensors but its display, metering and comms need the external 24 V DC auxiliary supply — feed it from a supervised source, and alarm its failure.

Standardise one vendor's trip-unit language per switchboard. A board whose incomers speak Ekip, feeders speak Micrologic and motor breakers speak ETU needs three engineering tools, three spares stocks and three training courses — a real cost no catalogue page will show you.

How to Read a Breaker Datasheet

An LV breaker datasheet is a dense grid of IEC 60947-2 symbols, and three of them — Icu, Ics, Icw — look interchangeable and are not. Misreading them is the most common LV specification error after the frame/rating confusion. Decode the page line by line:

SymbolNameWhat it really meansWhat to check for this plant
IcuUltimate breaking capacity The biggest fault the breaker can interrupt once and survive in a legally defined but possibly degraded state (test sequence O–CO). It is a never-exceed number, not a service rating. ≥ the worst prospective fault at the breaker's terminals — 38.5 kA at the bus normally, ~77 kA if tie-closed operation is credible (selection below).
IcsService breaking capacity The fault level the breaker can interrupt and remain serviceable (sequence O–CO–CO). Quoted as a percentage of Icu: 50, 75 or 100 %. A breaker with Ics = 100 % Icu goes back to work after the fault; at 50 % it survives the test but should be inspected/replaced. Prefer Ics = 100 % Icu for incomers and critical feeders; never compare one vendor's Icu against another's Ics.
IcwShort-time withstand current The fault current the breaker can carry without tripping or welding for a stated time — 1 s or 3 s. This is what lets an ACB hold on through its short-time delay while a downstream MCCB clears. Only category B breakers have a meaningful Icw. Icw must exceed the bus fault current for longer than the slowest set S-delay; this plant targets Icw ≥ 65 kA/1 s on the main ACBs.
Category A / BUtilisation category B = rated for short-time withstand (designed to be time-graded; ACBs and large MCCBs). A = no rated Icw; trips without intentional delay (most MCCBs). Incomers and bus-tie must be category B; downstream MCCBs are normally category A and grade by current and energy instead of time.
In vs IuRating vs frame Iu is the frame's maximum continuous current; In is the installed sensor or rating plug. One physical frame covers many In values; trip settings are multiples of In. Quote both: e.g. "3200 A frame, In = 2500 A sensors" for the incomer leaves a clean upgrade path.
Ue / UimpRated operational / impulse withstand voltage Ue is the voltage the ratings are declared at — and breaking capacity falls as Ue rises, so a breaker is "65 kA at 400 V" but perhaps "35 kA at 690 V". Uimp (typically 8–12 kV) sets surge robustness and clearances. Read every kA figure in the 400 V column — quoting the 690 V column undersells the breaker, quoting a 240 V column oversells it.

The reference-temperature trap — derating at 50 °C

Continuous ratings are declared at a reference ambient — and "ambient" here means the air around the breaker inside the switchboard, which runs 10–15 K above the room. Catalogues publish derating tables per frame: as a typical indicative guide, expect a usable 0.8–0.9 factor on In by the time a 50 °C Saudi room plus internal switchboard heating is accounted for — but this varies with frame, connection style and enclosure, so always verify against the vendor's own derating table; never apply a generic factor to a real purchase. Thermal-magnetic MCCBs derate inherently (the bimetal feels the heat); electronic units are compensated but the copper and insulation behind them are not, so the tables still apply. The room HVAC question in the Saudi notes is the other half of this answer.

60 Hz applicability

IEC 60947-2 breakers from the major vendors are dual-rated 50/60 Hz and the 60 Hz point matters less than it does for MV gear — but it is not nothing: a few ratings shift slightly at 60 Hz (iron losses in sensors, marginal derating on some large frames), and the peak-withstand arithmetic upstream of the breaker uses the 60 Hz asymmetry factor. The discipline is the same as everywhere in this manual: require the datasheet and routine tests to state 60 Hz explicitly rather than assuming the 50 Hz figures carry over.

The classic datasheet con: a headline "100 kA breaker" that turns out to be 100 kA Icu at 240 V, with 50 % Ics, in the smallest frame only. Always read the kA number with its three qualifiers: at which voltage, Icu or Ics, and for which frame.

Selection for This Plant — From Plant Data Card to Breaker Schedule

Now apply the datasheet vocabulary to the Rev 3 plant numbers. The driving figures: each 1600 kVA transformer delivers an LV full-load current of 2309 A; the prospective short circuit at LV-MSB is about 38.5 kA from one transformer and roughly 77 kA if the bus-tie were closed with both transformers in parallel (see the short-circuit study for the derivation). All results indicative; the formal study and vendor derating tables govern.

Worked selection — LV-MSB incomer ACB

Step 1 — frame for the load. Transformer LV full-load current:

I_FLC = S / (√3 × U) = 1,600,000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) ≈ 2309 A

The next standard ACB sensor rating above 2309 A is 2500 A — a comfortable 8 % margin at catalogue conditions.

Step 2 — check the 50 °C derating. Apply a typical indicative in-board derating factor of 0.85 (vendor table governs):

I_usable = 0.85 × 2500 A ≈ 2125 A < 2309 A ✗ — fails hot

A 2500 A frame that is adequate at 40 °C can be inadequate in a hot Saudi switchboard. Move up one frame and re-check:

I_usable = 0.85 × 3200 A ≈ 2720 A > 2309 A ✓ (≈ 18 % margin)

So the incomer is specified as a 3200 A-frame ACB with In = 2500 A sensors (or the vendor's certified 50 °C solution on a 2500 A frame, if their table proves it — an HVAC-held room changes this arithmetic; get the vendor statement in writing either way).

Step 3 — breaking capacity. Normal duty is one transformer per bus section: 38.5 kA. But if operations can ever close the bus-tie with both transformers live — even briefly, for a no-break changeover — the bus sees:

I_sc,tie-closed ≈ 2 × 38.5 kA ≈ 77 kA

A 50 kA breaker is then under-rated the moment the tie closes. Select the 85–100 kA performance level (all three ACB families offer it), with Ics = 100 % Icu:

I_cu ≥ 85 kA > 77 kA ✓

Step 4 — short-time withstand for selectivity. The incomer must hold on through its S-delay while a downstream MCCB clears. Target:

I_cw ≥ 65 kA / 1 s > 38.5 kA single-TX duty ✓ (≈ 1.7× margin)

which also keeps honest margin for grading studies at the tie-closed level when ZSI is active. Note the constraint this creates: at tie-closed duty the prospective fault (≈ 77 kA) exceeds Icw 65 kA/1 s, so at that duty the breakers must clear via ZSI/instantaneous rather than ride out a full short-time delay — only the single-transformer 38.5 kA duty may be time-graded within Icw. Sanity check: 3200 A-frame ACBs at the high performance level typically carry Icw of 65–85 kA/1 s — the requirement sits inside every family's range, so this is a selection, not an exotic special.

Saudi-Specific Practice — Heat, Dust, Paperwork and Spares

SASO/SABER conformity

LV breakers are regulated imports: they enter the Kingdom through the SABER platform with certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC standards (IEC 60947-2 for ACBs/MCCBs, IEC 60898-1 for MCBs). Make the supplier contractually responsible for SABER product and shipment certificates, type-test reports from a recognised laboratory, and country-of-origin documents — and check that the certificate covers the exact frame, performance level and trip-unit combination being shipped, because "family" certificates with the high-kA variants excluded are a known trap. As elsewhere in this manual: missing paperwork strands switchboards in port far more often than technical faults do.

SEC acceptance and SBC 401

The LV breakers themselves are inside the plant fence, so SEC's interest is lighter than at the MV intake — but not zero. SEC and the Saudi Building Code (SBC 401, the Kingdom's adoption of the electrical code) shape the LV installation rules: TN-S earthing practice, discrimination requirements for safety-services supplies, and the acceptance of the metering arrangement where SEC tariff metering touches the LV system. Where the DG/ATS standby scheme interfaces the SEC supply, the changeover breakers and their interlocks are part of what the inspector walks down — keep the approved single-line and interlock schedule in the switchroom dossier.

Heat: the switchroom is part of the breaker

Everything in the derating discussion lands here. The workable Saudi pattern is an HVAC-conditioned LV switchroom held at or below 40 °C, which restores catalogue ratings — with the derated 50 °C figures held in reserve as the documented answer to "what happens when the HVAC fails on an August afternoon". Specify: room over-temperature alarm to the DCS, the vendor's written derating statement for every frame at 50 °C, and temperature-rise type-test evidence for the assembled switchboard (IEC 61439-2), not just the loose breakers — the board manufacturer's form factor and busbar design move heat around in ways the breaker catalogue cannot see.

Dust and IP

Desert dust plus morning humidity is mildly conductive, and it settles on arc-chute louvres, trip-unit electronics and racking mechanisms. Indoors, specify switchboard enclosures at IP4X minimum (IP54 where the room quality is doubtful, noting the thermal price of sealing); any breaker outdoors or in unconditioned space lives in an IP54-minimum enclosure per the plant basis. Keep the switchroom positively pressurised with filtered intake, and put insulation-surface and arc-chute inspection on the maintenance plan — a dust film inside an ACB arc chute is the LV cousin of the MV internal-arc precursor.

Spares and vendor presence in the Kingdom

An ACB mechanism lives 25–30 years; its electronics will not. Plan accordingly:

  • Spare trip units — at least one of each LSIG type used, pre-loaded with the approved settings file; a failed trip unit otherwise immobilises an incomer for months of lead time.
  • One complete spare ACB of the common frame (or at minimum a spare breaker element for the withdrawable cassettes) — the classic justification for making incomers and bus-tie identical, as the schedule above does.
  • MCCB spares by tier — a small stock of each frame/tier/trip combination actually installed; resist "equivalent" substitutions that silently break the cascading and selectivity tables.
  • Local service — ABB, Schneider, Siemens and Eaton all maintain Saudi service organisations; weight the bid evaluation toward proven in-Kingdom retrofit and spares history, and ask for the named local service centre, not the regional one.
  • Settings custody — trip-unit setting files are protection settings; store them with the relay settings under the same change control, and verify them by secondary injection at FAT and at commissioning.
Key takeaway: in Saudi service the breaker's environment — room temperature, dust, the SABER dossier and the spares shelf — decides its real reliability at least as much as the kA numbers on the datasheet. Specify the environment and the paperwork with the same rigour as the ratings.

Breaker Procurement Review — Checklist

  • Confirm every breaking-capacity figure as Icu and Ics at 400 V, 60 Hz, for the exact frame offered — incomers/tie sized for the tie-closed duty (~77 kA → 85–100 kA class) or a documented dead-tie operating restriction in place.
  • Verify incomers and bus-tie are utilisation category B with Icw ≥ 65 kA/1 s, matching the grading delays in the approved LSIG study (Part 2).
  • Check frame and sensor sizing against the vendor's 50 °C / in-board derating table (typical 0.8–0.9 factor) — 2309 A incomer duty cleared with margin, in writing.
  • Confirm trip-unit scope: full LSIG where the study requires it, ZSI wiring between incomers/tie/major feeders, metering class and Modbus/IEC 61850 comms modules ordered with the breakers.
  • Verify mixed-brand coordination is avoided, or every cascading/selectivity claim is backed by the vendor's published type-tested tables for the exact pairings offered.
  • Check SABER/SASO certificates cover the exact frame, performance level and trip-unit variants shipped; type-test reports and 60 Hz declarations in the dossier.
  • Confirm the spares package: spare LSIG trip units, common spare ACB element, MCCB stock by frame/tier, named in-Kingdom service centre, and settings files under change control.
Closing the loop. Everything in this section is indicative training data. The binding chain for the real purchase is: formal short-circuit study (Part 2) and protection coordination study (LSIG settings) → approved breaker schedule and switchboard data sheets → vendor certified data sheets, derating tables and type tests → IEC 61439-2 assembly verification and FAT with secondary injection → site settings verification. The catalogue's job — here as throughout Part 6 — is only to make you fluent for that conversation.
