What the MCC Does in This Plant

A motor control centre is a switchboard that has specialised in one job: starting, protecting and stopping motors, dozens of them, packed into standardised compartments ("buckets") that a technician can exchange in minutes. Where the LV-MSB is a distribution board — a few large breakers feeding sub-boards — an MCC is a density product: a 2 m-wide column may hold ten or fifteen motor starters, each with its own short-circuit protection, contactor, overload device, control terminals and door-mounted controls, all plugged onto a common vertical bus.

In this plant the MCCs (tags MCC-1..n) hang off the two sections of the LV-MSB at 400/230 V, 60 Hz, TN-S. They carry the process motor population — pumps, fans, compressors, agitators — with three starter technologies side by side in the same line-up (the trade-offs between them are developed in the motors section):

Starter typeTypical use here (indicative)What sits in the bucket
DOL (direct-on-line) Small motors, roughly up to 15–22 kW, where the ~6× starting inrush is tolerable on the bus. MCCB or fuse-switch + contactor + overload relay (thermal or electronic).
Star-delta Mid-size motors on low-inertia loads where a reduced-voltage start is enough and speed control is not needed. Protection device + three contactors + timer; six motor leads instead of three.
VFD-fed Pumps and fans where flow control saves energy, and the largest machines (up to the plant's largest motor, ~160 kW typical) where a soft, current-limited start protects the bus and the process. Either the VFD itself in an oversized bucket, or — common for larger drives — a feeder bucket in the MCC supplying a wall- or floor-mounted drive (see the VFD catalogue section).

Intelligent versus conventional MCCs

A conventional MCC wires each starter's control circuit in copper: start/stop commands, run/trip status, maybe an analogue ammeter signal, all marshalled to terminal strips and multicore cables back to the control system. It is simple, field-repairable with a screwdriver, and every signal you did not cable at design time is a site modification later.

An intelligent MCC (iMCC) replaces the simple overload relay with a communicating motor-protection unit per bucket (Rockwell E300, ABB UMC100, Siemens SIMOCODE, Schneider TeSys T are the typical families) and replaces the multicore with a network drop — PROFINET, EtherNet/IP or Modbus TCP. Each bucket then reports current, thermal capacity, earth-fault leakage, starts-per-hour and trip diagnostics to the DCS/PMS, and accepts commands over the same link. The price is roughly 10–20 % on the MCC and a network engineering effort; the return is condition data that turns motor maintenance from calendar-based to evidence-based, and commissioning loops that take minutes instead of hours. For a plant of this size the practical recommendation is an iMCC for process-critical motors and conventional starters for trivial ones — but choose one philosophy per MCC; mixed boards confuse both technicians and the control system.

Hardwired trip rule. Even in a fully intelligent MCC, safety functions — emergency stops, process interlocks that protect people or machines — stay hardwired to the contactor coil circuit. The network carries information and convenience commands; it must never be the only path for a protective stop.
Key takeaway: an MCC is bought as a system (bus, form of separation, bucket standardisation, communications philosophy), not as a pile of starters. Decide intelligent vs conventional, starter technology bands, and spare-bucket policy before opening any vendor catalogue.

What "withdrawable bucket" buys you

A withdrawable starter unit plugs onto the vertical bus through shuttered contacts and carries its load and control connections on plugs. Racking it out isolates the unit with a visible, padlockable break — and a failed starter is exchanged for the shelf spare in minutes, live bus, no outage to the neighbours. Fixed and plug-in construction is cheaper and acceptable for feeders that rarely change, but for process motor starters the withdrawable pattern is what keeps a Saudi plant running between maintenance windows. Insist that bucket sizes are standardised so that one spare of each size covers the board, and order 10–20 % equipped spare buckets plus empty space for future ones.

When comparing offers, ask each bidder for the same three drawings: a typical DOL bucket schematic, the bucket-size table for your motor list, and the vertical-section arrangement showing forms of separation. Brochure adjectives differ; these three drawings make the products directly comparable.

How to Read MCC Catalogue Data — IEC 61439-2 Parameters

An MCC data sheet speaks IEC 61439-2. Six parameters carry almost all of the engineering content; learn to find them on the first page of any offer.

Rated current of the assembly (InA) and of each circuit (Inc)

The main horizontal bus rating (InA) and the rating of each vertical bus and outgoing unit (Inc) are verified values — proven by temperature-rise test on the assembled board, not by adding up component nameplates. Crucially, they are declared at a reference ambient (35 °C daily average, 40 °C peak, per the standard's normal service conditions). Every ampere figure in the catalogue silently assumes that room; see the derating discussion below.

Short-time withstand current (Icw) and peak (Ipk)

Icw is what the bus structure survives, braced and bolted, for the rated time (usually 1 s) while upstream protection clears a fault. It must exceed the prospective fault current at the MCC bus — calculated after the incoming cable impedance, which is why the MCC can legitimately carry a lower withstand class than the 38.5 kA (one transformer) duty at the LV-MSB. The peak rating Ipk covers the first asymmetric loop; the catalogue applies the standard factor (n = 2.1 for Icw > 50 kA, less below). Some buckets are alternatively protected by current-limiting devices and declared with a conditional rating (Icc) — acceptable, but then the protective device is part of the type test and cannot be substituted at will.

Form of separation — why this plant says Form 4b

IEC 61439-2 grades how thoroughly the assembly's interior is partitioned, from Form 1 (no internal separation) to Form 4 (busbars, each functional unit, and each unit's terminals all separated from one another). The sub-letter matters: Form 4b puts each unit's outgoing terminals in their own compartment, not in a shared cable alley. The practical meaning for a field technician: with Form 4b you can open one starter's terminal compartment to re-terminate a motor cable while every other circuit on the board stays energised behind earthed metal. On a process plant where the MCC can never be fully shut down, Form 4b is the standard specification; Form 3b is the cost-down compromise you will be offered, and the difference appears precisely at 02:00 when a motor cable must be changed live-board.

Internal arc — IEC TR 61641

Arc containment in LV assemblies is a technical report option, not a core type test like the MV IAC rating — so it must be explicitly requested. Ask for arc-fault containment tested to IEC TR 61641 at the board's fault level and protection clearing time, and state whether persons-protection (criteria 1–5) or also equipment-protection (criteria 6–7) is required. Active arc-quenching devices and arc-detection relays tripping the incomer are catalogue options that buy clearing times in tens of milliseconds.

IP rating for a dusty site

For an indoor, HVAC-conditioned Saudi switchroom specify IP42 as a practical minimum (protected against dripping water and >1 mm objects); IP54 if the room cannot be guaranteed clean — but beware: sealing a board to IP54 traps its own heat, and the catalogue will show a current derating of typically 5–15 % for the higher IP class. Dust is the enemy twice over — as a tracking film on insulation and as a thermal blanket on components — so filtered, positively pressurised rooms beat hermetic boards.

The 50 °C question, bucket by bucket

The components inside a bucket — contactors, overload relays, MCCBs, and above all VFDs — are themselves rated at 40 °C (many contactors at 55–60 °C, MCCBs at 40–50 °C with derating tables). The air inside a loaded MCC column runs 10–15 K above the room. At a 50 °C design ambient the in-board air can reach 60–65 °C, and component catalogues answer with derating curves: an MCCB that carries 100 A at 40 °C may be good for only ~85–90 A at 60 °C. The clean solutions, in order of preference: condition the room to ≤40 °C and treat the HVAC as electrical infrastructure; size starters one frame generously; and require the vendor to certify the whole assembly's ratings at 50 °C room ambient in writing. Never accept "components per manufacturer data" as the answer — the assembly temperature-rise verification is exactly the vendor's job under IEC 61439.

The classic Gulf MCC failure is thermal, not electrical: a fully-loaded DOL bucket in an unconditioned room trips on overload every afternoon in August and is "fixed" by winding the overload setting up — defeating the motor's protection. If afternoon nuisance trips appear, measure the in-board temperature before touching any setting (see motor protection).

Selection for This Plant — Worked Sizing

Take a representative board, MCC-1, fed by cable from one section of the LV-MSB. All figures indicative and to be confirmed by the load list and the formal short-circuit study.

Worked calculation — MCC bus rating with diversity

Assume MCC-1 carries a connected motor group totalling ~700 kW of mixed pumps and fans, including the plant's largest motor (~160 kW). Connected full-load current at 400 V, taking an aggregate pf × efficiency of ~0.80:

I_connected = P / (√3 × U × pf × η) = 700 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.80) ≈ 1263 A

Not all motors run at once, nor at full load. With a demand/diversity factor of 0.7 from the load list:

I_demand = 0.7 × 1263 A ≈ 884 A

Add a 20 % growth margin and allow for the 50 °C room (target ≤80 % utilisation of the verified rating):

I_bus ≥ 884 × 1.2 / 0.8 ≈ 1326 A → select 1600 A main bus

Sanity check: 1600 A at 400 V is ~1.1 MVA of bus capacity for a board demanding ~0.6 MVA — generous but normal for an MCC, where bucket count and future space, not amperes, usually fill the board first. The incomer from LV-MSB is then a 1600 A frame ACB or switch.

Worked calculation — fault level at the MCC bus after the cable

The Plant Data Card gives Isc ≈ 38.5 kA at the LV-MSB with one transformer (bus-tie normally open). Equivalent source impedance, treated as mostly reactive:

Z_source = U / (√3 × I_sc) = 400 / (1.732 × 38 500) ≈ 6.0 mΩ

Feeder to MCC-1: 2 parallel runs of 3×1c 240 mm² Cu XLPE, 40 m. Per run R ≈ 3.2 mΩ, X ≈ 3.2 mΩ; two in parallel:

Z_cable ≈ (1.6 + j1.6) mΩ Z_total ≈ |1.6 + j(6.0 + 1.6)| = |1.6 + j7.6| ≈ 7.8 mΩ I_sc,MCC = 400 / (1.732 × 0.0078) ≈ 29.6 kA → say ≈ 30 kA

Sanity check: 40 m of LV cable knocks the fault level from 38.5 kA to ~30 kA — cable impedance dominates fast at 400 V, which is exactly why the MCC may be specified one withstand class below the MSB. Specify Icw = 50 kA / 1 s anyway: it is a standard class in every family, gives ~65 % margin, and stays valid if the MCC is ever re-fed by a shorter cable. All values indicative; the formal study governs.

Why VFDs Here — Energy, Soft Starting and the Harmonics Bill

A VFD rectifies the 400 V, 60 Hz supply to DC and re-synthesises a variable-voltage, variable-frequency output, so the motor runs at exactly the speed the process needs. For the centrifugal pumps and fans that dominate this plant's load, the affinity laws make this lucrative: shaft power scales with the cube of speed, so running a pump at 80 % speed takes roughly half the power (0.8³ ≈ 0.51) that throttling the same flow with a valve would leave you paying. Across a motor population running thousands of hours a year, the drive typically repays itself in one to three years of energy alone.

The second benefit is electrical: a VFD starts a motor at a programmed current limit, typically 100–150 % of rated current, instead of the ~6× inrush of a DOL start. For the plant's largest motor (~160 kW, ~280 A full load), that is the difference between a ~1700 A inrush sagging the 400 V bus and a start the neighbours never notice. Mechanically, the soft ramp spares couplings, belts and the pump's check valves.

The price: harmonics — 6-pulse versus AFE

The standard, cheapest VFD front end is a 6-pulse diode rectifier. It draws current from the supply in two pulses per half-cycle, rich in 5th, 7th, 11th and 13th harmonics — at 60 Hz, that is 300, 420, 660 and 780 Hz currents circulating in the plant. A 6-pulse drive without any choke can show a total harmonic current distortion (THDi) of 80–100 %; with the DC-link or AC line chokes most reputable drives now build in, 30–45 % is typical. The cures, in rising order of cost: line/DC chokes (built in or added), passive harmonic filters, 12-pulse arrangements (phase-shifting transformer), and the active front end (AFE) — an IGBT rectifier that draws near-sinusoidal current (THDi ≈ 3–5 %), at unity or even controllable power factor, and can regenerate braking energy to the supply. AFE costs roughly 1.5–2× the 6-pulse drive and a few percent of efficiency; on this plant it is justified case-by-case, not by default.

Harmonics are also why the plant's PFC bank is detuned at 7 % (tuning frequency ≈ 227 Hz at 60 Hz — safely below the 5th harmonic at 300 Hz): plain capacitors on a VFD-loaded bus would resonate near the 5th/7th harmonics and cook themselves. The full story, including why 227 Hz, is in the PFC section — read it before buying either the drives or the capacitors, because the two purchases interact.

Where the VFD lives. Smaller drives mount in MCC buckets (one to two frame sizes larger than the equivalent DOL bucket, for heat); large drives such as the 160 kW machine's are floor- or wall-mounted in the switchroom and fed from an MCC feeder bucket — see the MCC selection sub-section.

How to Read a VFD Data Sheet — the Footnotes That Size the Drive

A VFD data sheet quotes one headline kilowatt number and then takes most of it back in footnotes. Five footnote families decide whether the drive actually fits the application; check all five, every time.

Normal duty versus heavy duty — the same drive, two ratings

Every industrial drive carries (at least) two current ratings. Normal duty (variable torque) assumes a pump/fan-type load and allows an overload of typically 110 % for 60 s every 10 minutes. Heavy duty (constant torque) covers conveyors, crushers, positive-displacement pumps, and allows typically 150 % for 60 s — at a continuous current some 15–25 % lower from the same hardware. A drive sold as "200 kW" is a 200 kW normal-duty drive and roughly a 160 kW heavy-duty one. Selecting a centrifugal pump drive on the heavy-duty column wastes money; selecting a crusher drive on the normal-duty column burns IGBTs. Decide the duty class first, then read only that column.

Switching frequency versus derating

The output IGBTs chop the DC link at a switching frequency of typically 2–4 kHz (default) up to 8–16 kHz (quiet). Every doubling of switching frequency roughly doubles the inverter's own switching losses, and the data sheet answers with a derating table — commonly 10–20 % current reduction at the higher settings. Motor acoustic noise drops as switching frequency rises, so commissioning engineers love to wind it up; the derating table is why the drive then trips on heatsink temperature in July. Size at the switching frequency you intend to run, and write that frequency on the data sheet.

Ambient temperature derating — the Saudi line

Drive ratings are referenced to 40 °C (some compact frames to 45 °C, with a 50 °C option). Above the reference, catalogues derate output current by typically 1–1.5 % per °C, usually up to an absolute ceiling of 50–55 °C. At this plant's 50 °C design ambient an unconditioned location costs 10–15 % of the drive's current — very often exactly the margin that pushes the selection into the next frame size, as the worked example below shows. Altitude derating (above 1000 m) stacks on top; most Saudi coastal and central sites are unaffected, but check.

Output side: dv/dt, motor cable length and bearing currents

The inverter output is not a sine wave but a train of steep-edged pulses. Each edge (high dv/dt) launches a reflection down the motor cable that can nearly double the voltage at the motor terminals when the cable is long. Data sheets answer with a maximum motor cable length — typically 100–150 m screened without remedy, extendable to ~300 m with output dv/dt or sine-wave filters — and with requirements for symmetrical screened motor cable, 360° gland earthing, and insulated non-drive-end bearings or shaft earthing rings on larger motors (typically ≥100 kW) to stop bearing-current pitting. Check the motor itself is declared per IEC 60034-25 for converter duty; on this plant the 160 kW pump motor must be (motors section).

EMC category and the residual checklist

IEC 61800-3 sorts built-in RFI filters into categories C1–C4; for an industrial plant on its own transformer, category C3 (second environment) is the normal specification — but only with the screened-cable and earthing discipline above. Finally, scan for: input voltage tolerance (±10 % of 400 V) and 60 Hz declaration; built-in chokes (or order them); STO (safe torque off) if the safety design uses it; and the protection ratings the drive gives its motor — electronic overload, stall, underload (a dry-running pump shows as underload), and motor thermistor input.

Key takeaway: a VFD is selected on rated output current at four declared conditions — duty class, switching frequency, ambient temperature, installation altitude — plus a motor-cable and EMC check. The headline kilowatts on the cover are the start of the conversation, not the answer.

Worked Selection — a 160 kW Pump Drive at 50 °C

Apply the data-sheet method to the plant's largest machine: a 160 kW centrifugal pump, 4-pole motor, base speed 1800 rpm at 60 Hz (synchronous; ~1780 rpm at full load with slip), installed in a switchroom with a 50 °C design ambient. All values indicative.

Worked calculation — frame selection with ambient derating

Step 1 — motor current. Nameplate full-load current of a typical 160 kW, 400 V, 4-pole motor (η ≈ 0.96, pf ≈ 0.86):

I_FLC = P / (√3 × U × pf × η) = 160 000 / (1.732 × 400 × 0.86 × 0.96) ≈ 280 A

Step 2 — duty class. Centrifugal pump → variable torque → normal duty column, 110 % / 60 s overload. Required continuous drive current: ≥ 280 A.

Step 3 — first guess. A "160 kW" 400 V drive frame typically offers ~300–310 A normal duty at 40 °C and default switching frequency. On paper: 300 A > 280 A ✓.

Step 4 — apply the 50 °C derating (typical 1.25 %/°C above 40 °C, i.e. 12.5 % for 10 K):

I_50°C = 300 A × (1 − 0.125) ≈ 262 A < 280 A ✗ — frame too small

Step 5 — next frame up. The "200 kW" frame typically offers ~370 A normal duty at 40 °C:

I_50°C = 370 A × 0.875 ≈ 324 A ≥ 280 A ✓ (margin ≈ 16 %)

Step 6 — overload check. Pump starting and transients within 110 % × 324 A ≈ 356 A for 60 s — ample for a current-limited pump ramp. Sanity check: one frame size (~25 %) bought back the 12.5 % ambient loss plus margin; if the room is instead HVAC-held at ≤40 °C, the 160 kW frame would suffice — but then the drive's availability hangs on the air-conditioning. For the plant's largest motor, buy the frame, not the HVAC promise. Indicative only; the vendor's selection tool at the declared conditions governs.

Input choke versus detuned PFC — make the two purchases talk

Specify the drive with its DC-link or AC input chokes (3–5 % impedance, built in on most of the families above). The chokes roughly halve the drive's harmonic current injection, which directly offloads the detuned PFC bank: the 7 % reactors (tuning ≈ 227 Hz at 60 Hz) keep the ~580 kvar bank out of resonance with the 5th harmonic at 300 Hz, but every ampere of 5th harmonic the chokes absorb at the drive is an ampere the reactors do not have to swallow as heat. The two specifications must state the same assumptions: if the harmonic study that sized the PFC assumed choked drives, an unchoked substitution at purchase quietly invalidates it. Conversely, never let a vendor "value-engineer" plain capacitors into this plant — on a VFD-loaded bus, detuned is mandatory.

Base speed bookkeeping at 60 Hz

A 4-pole motor here runs 1800 rpm synchronous at 60 Hz — not the 1500 rpm of 50 Hz countries. Pump curves, V/f settings and any "percent speed" scaling in the control system must all be built on the 60 Hz base: set the drive's motor data from the 60 Hz nameplate, field-weakening point at 60 Hz, and maximum frequency per the pump's mechanical limit. A 50 Hz parameter file imported from a sister plant abroad will run the pump 17 % slow and the "energy saving" will be an underperforming process.

Saudi-Specific Practice — SASO, Dust, Service and Harmonics Limits

SASO/SABER conformity and local service

Drives and MCCs imported into the Kingdom clear customs through the SABER platform with certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC standards (IEC 61800 series for drives, IEC 61439 for assemblies). Make the supplier contractually responsible for SABER certification, type-test and routine-test documentation, and country-of-origin papers — paperwork strands equipment in port far more often than technical defects do. Then look past the catalogue to the vendor's in-Kingdom organisation: certified service centre locations, spare-parts stock (fans and DC-link capacitors are the drive consumables), guaranteed response time, and Arabic/English commissioning support. A drive family with a service hub in Dammam or Jubail beats a marginally better one supported from Dubai.

Dust and heat at the installation

Drive heatsinks are forced-air cooled and desert dust blankets them within months. Practice for this plant: drives indoors in filtered, positively pressurised, HVAC-conditioned rooms wherever possible; replaceable intake dust filter mats on cabinet drives, on the maintenance plan with a dated change record; through-panel ("flange" or back-channel) mounting for bucket drives so the heatsink rejects heat outside the enclosure; and heatsink cleaning plus cooling-fan replacement intervals (typically 3–5 years at high ambient) in the CMMS from day one. If a drive must live outside conditioned space, specify IP54/IP55 variants and apply the full 50 °C derating of the worked selection — never both seal a drive and skip the derating.

Harmonics limits at the SEC connection

SEC connection requirements limit the distortion a customer may inject into the 13.8 kV network, assessed at the point of common coupling; IEEE 519 is the common reference framework (THDv typically ≤5 % at the PCC, with individual and total current-distortion limits scaled to the ratio of available fault current to load current). The compliance chain for this plant: drive count and front ends from the load list → harmonic study at the PCC → chokes/filters/AFE chosen to meet the limits → detuned PFC per the PFC section → measurement campaign at commissioning to close the loop. Keep the study with the SEC connection file; if SEC queries distortion later, the dated commissioning measurements are the evidence. All limits indicative — the SEC-stated values for the actual connection agreement govern.

Procurement checklist — MCC and VFD package

  • MCC verified to IEC 61439-2: 1600 A bus and bucket ratings certified at 50 °C room ambient (or conditioned room declared), Icw 50 kA/1 s, Form 4b, withdrawable buckets, arc containment per IEC TR 61641.
  • Intelligent-MCC philosophy fixed and consistent: communicating motor protection per bucket, one fieldbus to the DCS/PMS, safety stops hardwired.
  • Spares policy in the purchase order: ≥15 % equipped spare buckets, shelf spare per bucket size, drive spare fans/filters, and 20 % empty space per MCC.
  • Every VFD selected on current at declared conditions — duty class (normal vs heavy), switching frequency, 50 °C ambient derating (1–1.5 %/°C above 40) — with the 160 kW pump drive sized one frame up per the worked selection.
  • Drive input chokes (3–5 %) included and stated in the harmonic study; study results compatible with the 580 kvar detuned 7 % PFC bank (227 Hz at 60 Hz) and IEEE 519-based SEC limits at the PCC.
  • Output-side engineering closed: screened symmetrical motor cables within length limits, 360° glands, dv/dt or sine filters where needed, insulated bearings/shaft earthing on motors ≥ ~100 kW, motors converter-rated per IEC 60034-25.
  • SABER/SASO certificates, 60 Hz declarations, 50 °C derating statements and in-Kingdom service/spares commitments delivered with the bid — not after award.
