What the Plant Needs — Double-Conversion Online as the Standard

IEC 62040-3 recognises three UPS topologies, and a catalogue will happily sell you any of them. Offline (VFD class) passes raw mains to the load and switches to the inverter only when the mains fails — a transfer gap of several milliseconds that office PCs tolerate and process controllers may not. Line-interactive (VI class) adds voltage regulation but still exposes the load to mains frequency and most disturbances. Double-conversion online (VFI class) rectifies the incoming AC to DC and re-creates a new, clean AC waveform continuously: the load is always on the inverter, the battery sits on the DC link ready to take over with literally zero transfer time, and no mains disturbance short of total rectifier failure reaches the output.

For an industrial process plant the choice is not really a choice: double-conversion online (VFI) is the plant standard, and everything in this catalogue section assumes it. The plant's 100 kVA UPS carries the DCS/PMS controllers, marshalling and server cabinets, operator workstations, critical instrumentation and telecoms — loads whose value lies not in their kW but in what happens to the other 2 MW when they go dark. The 30-minute autonomy is not sized for riding out a long outage; it is sized to bridge the gap to the 1250 kVA diesel generator (ATS open transition ≤10 s — see the standby power section) with a wide margin, and, if the DG fails to start, to give operators time to drive the process to a safe, orderly stop instead of a crash stop.

Where the UPS sits. The UPS is an LV load like any other — fed at 400/230 V, 60 Hz from the essential side of the plant LV system (downstream of the DG/ATS arrangement), with its output distributed on a dedicated UPS distribution board. It is a separate system from the 110 V DC switchgear tripping supply (DCDB) covered in the DC systems catalogue section — do not let a vendor merge the two to save a battery bank.
Key takeaway: specify VFI (double-conversion online) to IEC 62040-3, 100 kVA, 30 min autonomy, 400/230 V 60 Hz — and treat every catalogue page as a yes/no answer to that line. All values indicative; the load schedule and formal studies govern.

Modular versus monolithic — what you are really buying

A monolithic UPS is one power path in one box: simple, cheap per kVA, but a power-stage failure means the whole machine drops to bypass until a service visit. A modular UPS builds the rating from hot-swappable power modules (e.g. 5 × 25 kVA for a 100 kVA N+1 system): a failed module is ejected and replaced in minutes with the load still on inverter, and N+1 redundancy costs one extra module rather than a whole second machine. The prices cross over surprisingly low; at 100 kVA a modular frame with N+1 is routinely competitive with two paralleled monolithic units, and far more competitive on floor area. The catch to check in the catalogue: a modular frame has shared elements (static switch, backplane, controls) — ask the vendor which of these are redundant, because a "fully redundant" brochure claim sometimes ends at the power modules.

Efficiency classes — VFI versus eco-mode

Every catalogue now headlines two efficiency numbers. The honest one is double-conversion (VFI) efficiency, typically 95–97 % at the 100 kVA class: the load is always on the inverter and the missing 3–5 % becomes heat in the UPS room (at 100 kVA that is 3–5 kW of continuous HVAC load — budget it). The headline 98–99 % figure is eco-mode (and its "advanced"/"dynamic" variants): the load runs on raw mains through the static bypass and the inverter wakes only when a disturbance is detected — which reintroduces exactly the transfer gap VFI was bought to eliminate. For an office or data hall with tolerant IT loads, eco-mode is a defensible energy play; for a process plant control system, run in full VFI and say so in the specification, and treat eco-mode as a commissioning setting to be locked out. Compare vendors on VFI efficiency at your load point, not on the brochure headline.

As with the MV board, the brand decision at 100 kVA is rarely won on electrical performance — all four families pass. It is won on service: local Saudi service centre and spares, guaranteed response time, battery supply chain, and the vendor's willingness to certify performance at elevated ambient (next sub-section).

How to Read a UPS Datasheet

A UPS datasheet is two pages of numbers, perhaps eight of which matter. Read them in this order.

1. The IEC 62040-3 classification string — VFI-SS-111

The single most information-dense line on the sheet. Decode VFI-SS-111:

  • VFI — output is independent of input Voltage and Frequency variations (true double conversion). The alternatives are VI (voltage independent) and VFD (voltage and frequency dependent) — reject both for this duty.
  • SS — output waveform is Sinusoidal both in normal operation and in battery operation. (Letters X or Y here flag non-sinusoidal behaviour under some conditions — fine for a cheap office unit, not here.)
  • 111 — dynamic output performance classes for three test conditions (mode change, linear step load, non-linear step load). Class 1 is the tightest tolerance band; 111 means the output stays inside it for all three. A "311" machine lets the voltage dip well outside limits on mode change — exactly the moment your controls care about most.

Specify VFI-SS-111 verbatim and ask for the type-test evidence. A vendor who quotes "online double conversion" without the classification string is asking you to take the dynamic performance on trust.

2. Efficiency at part load — where your UPS will actually live

Catalogue efficiency is usually quoted at 100 % load. Your UPS will never be there: a 100 kVA machine carrying a 50–60 kVA control-system load — and half of that in an N+1 or 2N arrangement — operates at 25–60 % load for its whole life. Modern transformerless machines hold efficiency well down to ~25 % load (typically within 1–2 points of peak), but older or cheaper designs fall off a cliff below 50 %. Demand the efficiency curve (25 / 50 / 75 / 100 % load points) rather than the single headline figure, and evaluate lifetime losses at your realistic operating point. Modular machines have a structural advantage here: some can put surplus modules to sleep and keep the active ones at their efficiency sweet spot.

3. Overload and the static bypass

The overload table tells you how the UPS behaves when downstream loads gang-start or a fault begins to develop. A typical inverter overload claim reads: 110 % for 60 min, 125 % for 10 min, 150 % for 1 min (indicative — every family differs). Beyond that, the static bypass takes over: it can typically carry 1000 % for a cycle or two — but only if the mains is present and healthy. On battery, with no mains behind the bypass, the overload capability is whatever the inverter alone can do. Read the overload table twice: once for "mains available" and once for "on battery", because the second column is the one that applies during the DG start window.

4. Inverter short-circuit capability — the coordination trap

This is the number engineers most often fail to look up, and it bites hardest. A 400 V utility supply feeds a downstream fault with tens of kiloamps, and miniature circuit breakers trip magnetically in 10 ms. An inverter is a current-limited electronic source: on battery, it can deliver only about 2–3 × In into a fault, and only for around 100 ms to a few hundred ms before it shuts down to protect its IGBTs. For the plant's 100 kVA UPS, In ≈ 144 A at 400 V, so the fault current available on battery is roughly 300–430 A — total, for the whole board.

Now follow the consequence: a B-curve MCB needs 3–5 × its own rating to trip magnetically, a C-curve 5–10 ×. A 32 A C-curve MCB on the UPS distribution board needs up to 320 A — most of the inverter's entire fault capability — to clear instantaneously. A fault on one branch circuit while on battery can therefore drag the whole UPS bus down (every healthy load sees the voltage collapse) until either the MCB trips thermally (seconds — far too slow) or the inverter trips on overload, killing every control system the UPS exists to protect. The fixes are design decisions, not settings: keep UPS final circuits small (10–20 A), use B-curve or fast electronic protection, consider fuses (which clear far faster at low fault multiples), and check every branch device against the vendor's declared fault-clearing figure. When the mains is present, the static bypass masks all this by feeding the fault from the mains — which is why the problem only ever reveals itself during a real outage.

Classic failure scenario. Power fails; the UPS rides through perfectly; ninety seconds later a borderline cable fault on one operator-station circuit flashes over. On any normal board it would be a one-MCB event. On the inverter, the 350 A available cannot fire the 32 A C-curve magnetically; the bus voltage collapses; every controller on the UPS reboots simultaneously — during a plant-wide blackout. Selectivity on a UPS board must be engineered against 2–3 × In, not against the mains fault level.

5. Input THDi and the generator handshake

The UPS rectifier is a non-linear load on whatever feeds it. Legacy 6-pulse thyristor rectifiers drew 25–30 % THDi; modern IGBT/PFC front ends draw <3–5 % THDi at near-unity input power factor. This matters twice in this plant: it pollutes the LV bus less (helping the PFC and other loads), and — critically — it is kind to the 1250 kVA DG. A high-THDi rectifier load on a standby generator distorts the alternator voltage and can confuse its AVR exactly when the UPS is recharging a depleted battery at maximum rectifier demand. Specify input THDi ≤5 % at full load, input pf ≥0.99, and a configurable battery-recharge current limit / walk-in so the UPS ramps its demand gently when the ATS lands it on the generator (standby power section).

6. Temperature rating — the 40 °C default versus the 50 °C site

Almost every UPS datasheet quotes full rating at 40 °C (some at 35 °C), with operation to 50 °C only at derated power — typically in the region of 10–20 % derate, vendor-specific. The plant design ambient is 50 °C. The standard answer is the same as for the switchgear: the UPS lives in an HVAC-conditioned room held at 20–25 °C — which the batteries demand anyway, far more urgently than the electronics (see the battery-room notes). But the specification must still ask two questions in writing: the certified output rating at 50 °C (the HVAC-failure case), and the electronics' maximum survival/alarm temperature. An over-temperature alarm to the control system is mandatory; a UPS that quietly derates or trips on a 50 °C afternoon during an HVAC fault is a single point of failure hiding behind a comfort statistic.

N or N+1?

A single 100 kVA machine (N) protects against supply failures but is itself a single point of failure: an inverter fault drops the controls onto raw bypass mains — which, during the very outage the UPS was bought for, is dead. The step up is N+1: either a modular frame with one redundant power module (e.g. 5 × 25 kVA for a 100 kVA duty) or two paralleled 100 kVA units. For a control system whose loss stops a 2 MW plant, the modular-N+1 route is the usual sweet spot: near-2N availability at far less than 2N cost and footprint. Whichever is chosen, the battery and the static bypass must be examined separately — an N+1 set of inverters hanging off one battery string and one bypass is N+1 in name only. Two battery strings (or a centre-tapped dual-string bank) and a per-unit bypass close those holes.

Worked selection — rating, battery handshake and bypass

Step 1 — confirm the rating. Indicative audited control-load schedule: DCS/PMS cabinets 28 kW, operator stations and servers 12 kW, critical instruments and analysers 8 kW, telecoms/network 5 kW, UPS distribution losses and margin 7 kW → ~60 kW. At a design power factor of 0.9:

S = P / pf = 60 kW / 0.9 ≈ 67 kVA

Apply a growth/diversity allowance of ~25 % and round to the standard frame:

67 kVA × 1.25 ≈ 84 kVA → next standard frame = 100 kVA ✓

The Plant Data Card's 100 kVA is confirmed, loaded at ~60–70 % — a healthy point for efficiency and overload headroom.

Step 2 — battery energy for 30 min. The energy the battery must deliver, taking inverter efficiency ~0.95:

E = P × t / η = 60 kW × 0.5 h / 0.95 ≈ 31.6 kWh

With design factors for aging (÷0.8, so a battery at its 80 % end-of-life capacity still delivers the duty) and temperature/design margin (÷0.9):

E_design = 31.6 / (0.8 × 0.9) ≈ 44 kWh

On a typical ~432 V DC link (36 × 12 V blocks):

Capacity ≈ 44 000 Wh / 432 V ≈ 102 Ah → specify ~100–120 Ah blocks (30-min rate)

Sanity check: 44 kWh is roughly the energy in four domestic EV-charger-hours — physically about two battery cabinets of 100 Ah VRLA blocks; plausible. The vendor sizes the real string from the cell maker's constant-power tables at the 30-minute rate, which differ from the 10-hour Ah rating — the full method, including why short-rate discharge punishes Ah capacity, is worked in the UPS battery sizing calculation. Note that 30 min ≫ the 10 s ATS transfer (standby section): the autonomy is sized for the DG failing, not for the DG starting.

Step 3 — bypass arrangement. Specify: internal static bypass (automatic, on inverter overload/failure), plus an external wrap-around maintenance bypass panel so the entire UPS — static switch included — can be isolated and replaced with the load fed from mains. Interlock it make-before-break, and feed the bypass line from the same essential (DG-backed) source as the rectifier so a transfer to bypass during an outage still lands on a live supply. All figures indicative; the load schedule and the vendor's certified battery calculation govern.

Key takeaway: the order is rating → topology (VFI-SS-111) → redundancy (N+1, including battery and bypass) → battery (30 min, aged, at temperature) → bypass (static + external maintenance) → generator handshake (THDi, walk-in). A UPS bought on kVA alone answers one line of eight.

What the Plant Needs — Why DC Security Outranks the UPS

Every circuit breaker in the MV switchgear, and the main LV breakers, trip and close from a 110 V DC battery system distributed through the DC distribution board (DCDB). The same system typically carries protection relay (IED) power supplies, trip-circuit supervision, switchgear indication, and often emergency lighting and SCADA/RTU loads. It is a battery + charger(s) + DCDB arrangement, deliberately independent of every AC supply in the plant — because its defining duty arrives precisely when the AC is in trouble.

Here is the ranking that surprises newcomers: the DC system matters more than the UPS. If the UPS dies, the control system goes dark and the plant trips — expensive, but the protection still works; the plant fails safe. If the DC system dies, the protection relays may still measure a fault perfectly and close their output contacts into a dead trip coil — and nothing trips. A fault then burns until some upstream device (perhaps SEC's own protection, seconds away) clears it, by which time a transformer or a busbar is destroyed and people have been exposed to an arc that should have lived for 100 ms. A dead battery does not announce itself the way a dead UPS does: the chargers keep the bus voltage up, every indication lamp glows, and the system looks healthy right up to the moment it is asked to deliver a 10–20 A trip-coil pulse from a battery with no capacity left. This is why DC systems get dual chargers, continuous battery monitoring, trip-circuit supervision and disciplined discharge testing — and why this catalogue section exists separately from the UPS one.

The silent failure mode. A failed battery on float looks identical to a healthy one — full voltage, no alarms on a basic charger. The failure is only discoverable by measurement: impedance/conductance trending, periodic capacity discharge tests, and a battery-monitoring option on the charger. Buy the monitoring with the system; retrofitting it after the first failure-to-trip investigation is the expensive way to learn.
110 V DC versus the UPS — division of labour. Switchgear tripping/closing, IED supplies and trip-circuit supervision live on the 110 V DC system; the DCS/PMS, operator stations and instrumentation live on the 230 V AC UPS (UPS catalogue section). Keep them separate: different voltages, different duty cycles (continuous IT load versus rare brutal pulses), different battery technologies, different failure consequences. Vendors occasionally propose a combined "one big battery" scheme — decline it.

Reading the table for a Saudi plant

VRLA AGM (valve-regulated lead-acid, absorbed glass mat) is the default catalogue offer because it is cheapest and compact. Its Achilles heel is the Arrhenius arithmetic in row two: every 8–10 °C above 20 °C halves its life. At Saudi ambients an unconditioned battery room sits at 35–45 °C for months — a nominal 10-year VRLA block becomes a 2-year consumable, and it fails towards open-circuit, the failure mode that kills a trip supply silently. VRLA is acceptable only with a reliably cooled room, and even then it earns its keep on the UPS, not on the trip battery.

Vented (flooded) NiCd is the heavy, expensive, ugly option that refuses to die. Pocket-plate NiCd tolerates heat, deep discharge, high ripple and abuse; its capacity loss is gradual and measurable; design lives of 20+ years are routine and achieved in practice. Its costs are real: topping-up maintenance, 2–3× the price, the largest footprint, and a lower cell voltage (1.2 V/cell, so ~92 cells for 110 V versus ~54 lead-acid cells) — more connections to torque and inspect. For switchgear tripping duty in the Gulf, vented NiCd is the preferred technology, and most regional utility and oil-and-gas specifications say so explicitly (Saudi notes).

Li-ion (LFP — lithium iron phosphate) has crossed from EVs into the stationary catalogues, mainly as a UPS battery: a quarter of the weight, half the footprint, 5–10× the cycle life of VRLA, integrated battery management (BMS) with cell-level monitoring as standard. For UPS autonomy duty it is genuinely attractive where floor space or cooling capacity is tight. For the 110 V switchgear battery it remains the cautious choice for now: certification trails lead-acid/NiCd (IEEE/IEC sizing practice is newer), the BMS adds an electronic dependency into the one system that must not have any, and fire-safety review (thermal runaway, room suppression) is extra engineering. Watch this space; specify it for the UPS if footprint demands, not yet for the trip battery.

Key takeaway: chemistry selection is a temperature decision before it is a cost decision. In this climate: NiCd for the 110 V DC trip battery; VRLA (or increasingly LFP) for the UPS battery in a conditioned room. Whole-life cost, not purchase price, is the comparison that matters — a NiCd bank outlives 4–6 generations of badly-housed VRLA. All values indicative.

Chargers and Rectifiers — the Other Half of the Catalogue

The battery makes the headlines; the charger does the work. In normal operation the charger (a controlled rectifier, thyristor or switch-mode) carries the entire standing DC load and float-charges the battery; the battery only ever discharges when the AC input is lost or a load pulse exceeds the charger's limit. Indicative vendor families you will meet on Gulf projects: AEG SVS (industrial thyristor chargers, a de-facto standard in oil and gas), Benning (Thyrotronic/Tebetron lines), Chloride/Exide (industrial chargers with a long regional install base), and GE/ABB industrial rectifier lines. All offer the same functional menu; the specification decides which options are ticked.

Float and boost charge

Two charging regimes, selected automatically or by key-switch. Float is the permanent state: constant voltage chosen so the battery stays full without gassing — for a 110 V nominal system, typically around 1.40–1.42 V/cell for NiCd or 2.23–2.27 V/cell for lead-acid (≈ 120–125 V at the bus for a ~54-cell lead-acid string; the 92-cell NiCd string in this plant floats at ≈ 130 V — hence the dropper diodes discussed below; indicative, per cell maker). Boost (equalise) is a temporary elevated voltage — ~1.45–1.55 V/cell NiCd — used to recharge quickly after a discharge or to recondition cells. Boost voltage can push the bus towards or beyond the upper limit of connected equipment (110 % of Un = 121 V, see the voltage window below), so the catalogue option to demand is either automatic boost with a bus-voltage limiter, or dropper diodes/a dual-bus arrangement that keeps the load bus inside its window while the battery sees the boost voltage. Verify this in the catalogue rather than discovering it when the IEDs alarm on overvoltage during commissioning.

Dual-charger redundancy

The standard high-integrity arrangement is two 100 % chargers on the one battery and DCDB — either both floating in load-share, or one duty and one auto-standby. The point: a charger failure then leaves the system fully functional with no time pressure, instead of starting a silent countdown defined by battery autonomy. For a plant this size a single charger plus a rigorous alarm scheme is the economy option; given that the whole DC system costs a fraction of one MV panel, dual chargers fed from different LV sources (one each side of the LV-MSB bus-tie, or one DG-backed) are cheap availability. Each charger needs: AC fail, charger fail, DC undervoltage/overvoltage alarms — all wired to the control system, not just to a lamp on the front of a panel in an unmanned room.

Battery monitoring and supervision

The catalogue options worth their price: battery monitoring (string current, voltage, temperature; per-block or per-cell modules on better systems; impedance/conductance trending on premium ones), battery circuit continuity supervision (detects an open fuse, corroded link or open cell — the silent killers), end-of-discharge protection (low-voltage disconnect, set to protect the battery without stranding the trip duty), and a discharge-test facility (a test load connection or charger current-walk-down mode so the periodic capacity test does not require dismantling the system). Temperature-compensated float charging is near-mandatory for VRLA and useful for NiCd in this climate.

DC earth-fault monitoring — the unearthed IT bus

Tripping DC systems are run unearthed (IT): neither pole is bonded to earth. The reason is availability — a single earth fault on an IT bus causes no current to flow and nothing trips; the system carries on working. But a second earth fault, on the other pole, short-circuits part of the system — and the classic catastrophic version is a positive-pole earth fault plus an earth fault on a trip-coil wire, which can trip breakers spuriously or, worse, hold trip circuits inoperative. The first fault is therefore an urgent maintenance alarm even though nothing has failed yet. Every charger catalogue offers an earth-fault monitoring relay (balanced-bridge or pulse-injection type) that continuously measures the insulation resistance of each pole to earth and alarms below a threshold (indicatively tens of kΩ). Specify it, alarm it to the control system, and make "find the first earth fault this week" a maintenance rule — the second one chooses its own time.

Charger sizing rule of thumb from the catalogues: rated DC current ≥ continuous standing load + battery recharge current (commonly recharge to 90 % in ≤10 h, i.e. roughly C10/7 to C10/5 for NiCd per the maker's tables). For the plant's system that lands at an indicative 25–40 A charger — small hardware, outsized consequences.

Selection for This Plant — a Worked 110 V DC Sizing Sketch

Battery sizing for a DC system is a duty-cycle exercise: list every load, when it occurs and for how long across the design backup period, then size the cell so the bus voltage never leaves its window even at the end of the period, aged, at the room temperature. The full formal method (IEEE 485 for lead-acid, IEEE 1115 for NiCd, period-by-period with the cell maker's published rate tables) belongs to the vendor and the detailed design; what follows is the engineer's sketch that checks the vendor's answer for sanity. Backup period: 8 hours — long enough to cover an extended grid outage with a failed DG, holding protection, emergency lighting and SCADA alive while the plant is made safe.

The voltage window first

The window is what actually sizes the battery. Connected equipment must work across the whole excursion of an unregulated battery bus: breaker shunt trip coils are typically rated 70–110 % of Un (77–121 V for 110 V), closing coils typically 85–110 %, IEDs commonly 80–120 %. The design convention is to end the discharge at ≥80 % Un = 88 V at the battery terminals, leaving the margin between 88 V and the 77 V trip-coil floor to cover cable voltage drop to the furthest trip coil at full pulse current — check that drop explicitly on long DCDB runs. The top of the window (121 V) is what constrains boost charging, as flagged in the charger sub-section.

Step 2 — ampere-hours, crude integration.

Ah = 8 A × 8 h + 10 A × 3 h + (50 A + 30 A) × (1/60) h ≈ 64 + 30 + 1.3 ≈ 95 Ah

Note where the energy lives: the dramatic 50 A trip burst contributes ~1 Ah; the boring 8 A standing load contributes 64 Ah. Capacity is bought for the long tail; the burst instead stresses voltage dip, which is where NiCd's flat, stiff discharge behaviour earns its price.

Step 3 — design factors. Aging factor 1.25 (system still delivers at 80 % end-of-life capacity), temperature/derating factor ~1.1 (room design temperature versus the 20–25 °C rating basis; cell-maker table value governs), design margin 1.1 (future breakers, load growth):

C ≈ 95 Ah × 1.25 × 1.1 × 1.1 ≈ 144 Ah → specify ~150–160 Ah (C10) cells

Step 4 — string. Vented NiCd at 1.2 V/cell nominal: ~92 cells gives 110 V nominal; float at ~1.41 V/cell ≈ 130 V battery voltage — confirm against the load bus upper limit and use dropper diodes or tap arrangement per the charger catalogue. End-of-discharge at 1.00–1.05 V/cell ≈ 92–97 V ≥ the 88 V floor ✓.

Sanity check: ~150 Ah × 110 V ≈ 16.5 kWh — about a third of the UPS battery's energy, in a bank that costs more; that is the price of 20-year heat-tolerant certainty. The real sizing is period-by-period to IEEE 1115 with the manufacturer's discharge tables, done and stamped by the vendor; this sketch exists so you can tell a sensible quotation from a wrong one. Saudi-Specific Practice — Heat, Hydrogen, SASO and the NiCd Habit

The battery room at 50 °C — HVAC as protection infrastructure

Batteries are the most temperature-sensitive equipment in the plant, sited in the country least inclined to indulge them. Cell-maker ratings are referenced to 20–25 °C; the site design ambient is 50 °C. For VRLA the Arrhenius rule (life halves per 8–10 °C above 20 °C) makes an uncooled room a battery shredder; even NiCd, though it survives, loses some capacity and life at sustained high temperature. The design conclusions: condition the battery/UPS rooms to a stable 20–25 °C; treat that HVAC as protection infrastructure, not comfort — alarm its failure to the control system and state in the design what happens during an HVAC outage on a 50 °C day (this is exactly the case where NiCd's tolerance buys you days of grace and VRLA's intolerance buys you an incident report). Record room temperature continuously; the battery's warranty and its real capacity both follow that log.

Ventilation and hydrogen

Charging batteries electrolyse water and evolve hydrogen — vented NiCd routinely (especially on boost), VRLA only slightly in normal float but significantly in fault/overcharge. Hydrogen is explosive from 4 % in air, and it pools at the ceiling. Practice per SASO-adopted IEC 62485-2: calculate the ventilation air flow from cell count and charge current; for a vented NiCd room provide dedicated mechanical extract (high-level), interlock or alarm the fans with charging, keep ignition sources and unsealed fittings out of the hydrogen zone, and fit eyewash facilities for the electrolyte. The HVAC therefore does two safety jobs at once — cooling and dilution — and its failure alarm matters twice.

SASO/SABER and SEC expectations

The same two regulatory tracks as the switchgear apply. Imported batteries, chargers and UPS equipment clear customs through the SABER platform with SASO certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC standards (IEC 62040 series for UPS, IEC 62259/60623 for NiCd cells, IEC 62485-2 for installation safety) — make the vendor contractually responsible for the certificates, because a battery bank stuck in port ages in the container. SEC acceptance touches the DC system indirectly but firmly: the intake protection that SEC reviews assumes a healthy 110 V DC supply, and SEC (and SBC 401) expectations for substation auxiliary supplies follow the same dual-charger, monitored-battery pattern described here. Align the spec card with the SEC interface requirements before the RFQ goes out.

Why the Gulf specifies NiCd — the rationale in one paragraph

Regional utility, ARAMCO-style and large-industrial specifications converge on vented NiCd for switchgear DC duties, and the logic is exactly this section's arithmetic: the trip battery is the one component whose failure converts a cleared fault into a destroyed substation; lead-acid life collapses exponentially with the temperature this region supplies for free; NiCd's failure mode is gradual and measurable rather than sudden and silent; and over a 25-year plant life one NiCd bank is cheaper than the 4–6 VRLA banks (plus one failure-to-trip investigation) it replaces. Buy VRLA where the room is reliably cold and the consequence of failure is a reboot; buy NiCd where the consequence is a fire. Indicative guidance — the project specification and reliability study govern.

Acceptance and routine-test checklist

  • Verify ratings plates and certificates: UPS VFI-SS-111 to IEC 62040-3 at 60 Hz; NiCd cells to SASO-adopted IEC 62259; SABER/SASO documents complete before shipment.
  • Witness UPS FAT: full-load heat run, transfer tests (mains fail, return, overload to bypass and back), efficiency at 25/50/75/100 % load, and the declared inverter fault-current test.
  • Witness an 8 h (DC) / 30 min (UPS) capacity discharge test on the actual battery strings, at declared temperature, to the end-of-discharge voltage — and file the curves as the baseline.
  • Confirm dual chargers load-share and that AC-fail, charger-fail, DC over/undervoltage, battery-continuity and earth-fault alarms each reach the control system individually.
  • Inject a deliberate single earth fault on each DC pole and verify the IT-bus earth-fault monitor alarms at the specified insulation threshold.
  • Check the voltage window end to end: boost charge with load bus ≤110 % Un; measured voltage at the furthest trip coil ≥70 % Un during a simulated multi-breaker trip burst.
  • Inspect battery room provisions against IEC 62485-2: ventilation flow calculation, high-level extract, HVAC failure alarm, temperature recording, eyewash and signage.
