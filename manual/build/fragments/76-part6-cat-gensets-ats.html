What the Plant Needs — the Standby Role Defined

Begin, as always in Part 6, on the demand side. The plant draws about 2.0 MW at 0.85 power factor — roughly 2353 kVA — from the SEC 13.8 kV intake. The standby diesel generator does not back up that whole plant. It backs the essential bus only: the loads whose loss creates a safety, asset-damage or restart problem — emergency lighting, instrument and UPS supplies, fire and gas systems, critical cooling and lube pumps, the control building HVAC that keeps the switchgear and servers alive, and whatever process loads the shutdown philosophy declares "must ride through a grid outage". Everything else stays dark until SEC returns.

That single decision — essential bus, not whole plant — sets the size class. The essential load list (built in the Part 2 tutorial) sums to an indicative ~900 kVA peak, and after motor-starting and 50 °C ambient checks the selected machine is a 1250 kVA standby-rated (ESP) set at 400/230 V, 60 Hz. On the 60 Hz SEC grid a 4-pole machine runs at 1800 rpm — a number worth internalising, because a 50 Hz catalogue page for the "same" engine quotes 1500 rpm and different powers; mixing the two columns is a classic transplant error, exactly like ordering 12 kV-class switchgear for a 13.8 kV system.

Why not back the whole plant? A set able to carry 2353 kVA continuously through a long outage would be a ~2500 kVA-class machine (or two paralleled units), with several times the capital cost, fuel storage, cooling airflow and exhaust treatment — bought to cover the rare hours per year when SEC is down, and running at part load (bad for diesels) whenever the outage load is modest. Standby engineering is the art of buying exactly the kVA the shutdown philosophy demands, and not one frame more. All figures indicative; the load schedule and detailed studies govern.

The set connects to the essential bus through the automatic transfer switch (tag ATS, open transition, ≤ 10 s total break — see the ATS catalogue section below and the ATS logic tutorial). Within that 10 s budget the engine must crank, fire, reach 1800 rpm and 400 V, and close onto a bus that then hits it with motor re-acceleration. Every catalogue parameter examined in this section — rating class, load-acceptance steps, transient voltage dip, fuel rate — exists to answer whether a given frame can survive exactly that sequence, year after year, at 50 °C.

The ISO 8528 Rating Ladder — ESP, PRP, COP

The single most consequential line on a genset data sheet is not the kVA number — it is the rating class printed next to it. The same physical engine-alternator pair carries two or three different kVA figures depending on how often and how hard you intend to run it, codified in ISO 8528-1. Reading "1250 kVA" without reading the class beside it is like reading a speed limit without noticing whether it is in km/h or mph.

ClassNameIntended duty (indicative)Load profileTypical use
ESPEmergency Standby Power Utility-outage backup only; typically ≤ 200 h/year, with a limited fraction (vendors often state ~25 h/year) at full nameplate. No overload capability — ESP is already the ceiling. Variable load; average over 24 h commonly limited to ~70 % of rating This plant's DG — grid is the normal source, the set runs only on SEC failure and on test.
PRPPrime Power Unlimited hours per year at variable load (24-h average typically ≤ 70 %), with 10 % overload available 1 h in 12. Variable Remote sites and rental fleets where the genset is the utility.
COPContinuous Power Unlimited hours at constant 100 % load, no overload. Constant base load Baseload/cogeneration duty; the lowest kVA figure of the three.

The three classes are rungs of one ladder for one machine. As a rule of thumb, ESP ≈ 110 % of PRP, and COP sits another 10–15 % below PRP — so a frame sold as "1250 kVA ESP" is typically about 1136 kVA PRP and roughly 1000 kVA COP (indicative; each vendor publishes its own exact ladder). The physics is thermal and tribological: the extra 10 % standby power is borrowed from the engine's wear and temperature margins on the argument that it will only ever be used a few dozen hours a year.

Why mis-applying ESP as prime destroys engines. Run a standby-rated set as the everyday power source and you are operating continuously inside the margin the manufacturer reserved for emergencies: higher exhaust and coolant temperatures, higher cylinder pressures, faster lube-oil degradation, and service intervals computed for 200 h/year arriving every few weeks instead. The failure pattern is depressingly repeatable — burned exhaust valves, cracked heads, turbocharger failures and bearing wear inside the first year or two — and the manufacturer's warranty is void from the first month, because the controller's hour counter tells the story. If a site genuinely needs daily generation (a construction phase before SEC connection is the common Saudi case), buy or rent a PRP-rated set for that phase; do not burn the plant's emergency machine.
Key takeaway: this plant's DG is specified as 1250 kVA ESP at 50 °C site conditions — an emergency-standby figure valid because the SEC grid, not the engine, is the normal source. Every comparison between catalogue pages must be made at the same rating class, the same site conditions and the same frequency, or it is not a comparison at all.

Controllers — native versus aftermarket

The controller is the genset's brain: it cranks, monitors, protects, synchronises and talks to the plant. The majors fit their own (Cat EMCP, Cummins PowerCommand, Kohler DEC/APM, MTU's line); the large packager market — firms that marry a bought-in engine and alternator on their own base frame, very common in the Gulf — fits aftermarket controllers, dominated by Deep Sea Electronics (DSE) and ComAp. These are perfectly capable devices; the specification points that matter are Modbus/Ethernet integration to the plant control system (ATS/PMS interface), remote start/stop and status contacts, protection functions per the study, and local support for spares and firmware. What to be wary of is not the controller brand but an undocumented packager: demand the ISO 8528 type-test evidence for the packaged set, not just the engine brochure.

Enclosures and silencers

At 1250 kVA the catalogue offers the set open-skid (for an equipment room), in a weatherproof enclosure, or in a containerised package (ISO 20/40 ft) with integral tank, attenuation and switchgear. For a Saudi outdoor installation the enclosure must be specified for dust (filtration on combustion and cooling air, minimum IP54 thinking on the electrical compartments per the plant basis) and for 50 °C cooling airflow — a sound-attenuated canopy restricts airflow, and the attenuation and the derating interact. Silencer grades run industrial (~−15 dB) / residential (~−25 dB) / critical (~−35 dB); typical attenuated canopies achieve around 75–85 dB(A) at 1 m (indicative). Specify the noise limit at the plant boundary and let the vendor pick the hardware.

Ask every bidder one question early: "state the set's standby rating at 50 °C ambient, with your dust filters and your canopy fitted, at 60 Hz / 1800 rpm". The brochure kVA almost never survives that sentence intact — see the data-sheet anatomy next.

Anatomy of a Genset Data Sheet

A generator-set data sheet is four documents stapled together: ratings (already covered), site derating, dynamic performance (load acceptance and transient response), and consumption and emissions. The brochure headline is written for a cool, low-altitude, unfiltered, open-skid machine; the Saudi answer lives in the footnotes.

Site derating — altitude, temperature, dust

Engine ratings are referenced to standard conditions (ISO 3046 / ISO 8528: 25 °C air, 100 kPa, 30 % humidity, with most vendors guaranteeing full power up to 40 °C and a stated altitude). Beyond the reference point the diesel loses breath: thinner or hotter air means less oxygen per stroke. Indicative rules of thumb — the vendor's curves govern:

  • Temperature: roughly 0.5–1 % of power per °C above 40 °C for a typical air-to-air aftercooled engine. At the plant's 50 °C design ambient that is an indicative 5–10 % off the brochure figure.
  • Altitude: typically ~1 % per 100 m above the rated altitude (often 1000 m for turbocharged engines). Most Saudi coastal and central sites are low enough for this to be minor — but check Riyadh-region sites (~600–700 m).
  • Dust filtration: heavy-duty combustion-air filters and canopy inlet attenuation/filtration add intake restriction — a further 1–3 % indicative, plus a maintenance obligation: a choked filter in a shamal re-derates the set in real time.
  • Alternator: the alternator derates too (class H insulation, typically rated at 40 °C); the packaged-set rating at 50 °C is the lower of the engine and alternator answers.

Load acceptance — ISO 8528-5 performance classes

A standby set is judged by what happens in the first seconds after the ATS closes, not by its steady-state nameplate. A diesel cannot swallow its full rating in one gulp: the turbocharger needs time to spool, so a naturally limited "first step" of load is all it can take without stalling or a deep frequency excursion. ISO 8528-5 packages this as performance classes — G1 (lighting-grade), G2 (general purpose: typical limits around 20 % transient voltage dip and recovery within a few seconds), G3 (tighter: ~15 % dip, faster recovery, for telecoms/UPS-heavy loads), and G4 (by agreement). The data sheet expresses the same physics as a load acceptance table: e.g. "first step 60 % of standby rating" with stated dip and recovery. For this plant — UPS rectifiers, contactor coils that drop out below ~70–80 % volts, and re-accelerating motors — specify class G2 as minimum, G3 preferred, and check the largest single load step against the table, not against the nameplate (worked in the selection below).

Transient dip and recovery

Two curves matter: voltage dip (alternator transient reactance X′d versus the kVA of the step — bigger alternator frame = smaller dip; vendors offer an "upsized alternator" option exactly for motor-heavy standby buses) and frequency dip (engine inertia and governor response). The data sheet states both for defined steps. Check them against what the essential bus tolerates: UPS input windows, contactor hold-in, VFD undervoltage trips. If the dip fails the check, the fix is usually one alternator frame size, far cheaper than one engine size.

Fuel consumption and sound

Fuel rate is tabulated at 50 / 75 / 100 % load. For a 1250 kVA (≈1000 kWe) class set, indicative figures are roughly 130 L/h at 50 %, 185 L/h at 75 %, 250 L/h at 100 % — i.e. a specific consumption around 0.20–0.27 L/kWh. These numbers size the day tank and the autonomy claim (worked below). Sound data is given as dB(A) at defined distances per enclosure option; specify the limit at the receptor (plant boundary, control-room wall), not at the canopy. Finally, check the small print on fuel specification — Saudi diesel grades and sulphur content versus the engine's emissions package — and on coolant for sustained 50 °C operation.

Key takeaway: read a genset data sheet in this order — rating class, then the 50 °C/dust derated figure, then the load-acceptance and dip tables, then fuel. A set that passes the first two lines but fails the dynamic check will black out the essential bus it was bought to save.

Selection for This Plant — from Load List to 1250 kVA ESP

This is the worked path from the plant's needs to the catalogue frame, on the Rev 3 Plant Data Card basis. The full engineering treatment is in the Part 2 standby generator tutorial; here it is compressed into the sequence a specifier actually follows on the catalogue page. All numbers indicative.

Worked selection — essential bus standby set

Step 1 — essential load. The essential load schedule (lighting/small power, UPS input, fire & safety, critical pumps and fans, control-building HVAC) sums to an indicative running peak of:

S_essential ≈ 900 kVA (≈ 765 kW at pf 0.85)

Step 2 — motor-start check. The largest single motor step on the essential bus is an indicative 75 kW pump (≈ 90 kVA running). Started direct-on-line at ~6 × FLC:

S_start ≈ 6 × 90 kVA ≈ 540 kVA (at ~0.2 pf, mostly reactive)

Against a 1250 kVA frame this step is ~43 % of rating — inside a typical G2/G3 load-acceptance first step, with a predicted transient dip well under 20 % on the standard alternator (vendor dip curve governs). The PMS sequences loads in steps (ATS/load-sequencing tutorial) so the set never sees the whole 900 kVA at once.

Step 3 — growth and pf margin. Add ~10 % schedule growth:

S_required ≈ 900 × 1.10 ≈ 990 kVA

Step 4 — site derating at 50 °C. Work backwards: a frame whose reference standby rating is ~1400 kVA, derated an indicative 1 %/°C for the 10 °C above 40 °C plus filter/canopy losses:

S_site ≈ 1400 kVA × (1 − 0.10) ≈ 1260 kVA → select 1250 kVA ESP at site conditions

Step 5 — utilisation sanity check.

990 kVA / 1250 kVA ≈ 0.79 → ~79 % loaded at peak ✓

Comfortably inside the ESP envelope, high enough to avoid chronic light-running (wet stacking) on test runs. Result: 1250 kVA ESP, 400/230 V, 60 Hz, 1800 rpm, class G3 preferred — matching tag DG on the Plant Data Card. Detailed load schedule and vendor transient data govern the final frame.

Worked sketch — fuel autonomy and day tank

Specify 8 h autonomy at an average outage load of ~75 % (the essential bus rarely sits at peak for a whole outage). From the data-sheet fuel table, ~185 L/h at 75 % for this class:

V_fuel = 185 L/h × 8 h = 1480 L → specify ≥ 1500 L usable; round to a 2000 L day tank

Sanity check: 2000 L gives ~10.8 h at 75 % load, or a full 8 h even at 100 % (250 L/h × 8 h = 2000 L). Add low-level alarm at ~4 h remaining, fill connection sized for a road tanker, and bunding per SBC/civil requirements. Longer autonomy belongs in a separate bulk tank with transfer pumps, not a bigger day tank at the engine.

The remaining specification lines come straight from the earlier sub-sections: ISO 8528 ESP rating declared at site conditions (ratings ladder), G2/G3 load acceptance with the 540 kVA step demonstrated (data-sheet anatomy), 60 Hz / 1800 rpm type evidence, dust-rated enclosure and silencer to the boundary noise limit, controller with remote start and Modbus to the plant control system, and the ATS interface contacts defined jointly with the ATS selection.

Transfer Schemes — Open, Delayed, Closed, Bypass

An ATS is conceptually simple — two sources in, one load out, an interlock that forbids both at once — but catalogues split into four scheme families, and choosing the scheme is the real engineering decision; the ampere rating is just shopping.

SchemeHow it transfersDead time (typ.)Use it when…
Open transition (break-before-make) Opens the connected source, then closes the other. The two sources are never paralleled. Tens of ms to a few s of switch dead time; total outage on grid failure is governed by engine start — ≤ 10 s end-to-end is this plant's standard. Default for standby duty. Loads ride on the UPS (100 kVA / 30 min) or simply tolerate the gap. No utility consent needed because the DG never touches the SEC network.
Delayed transition (open with centre-off pause) Opens, pauses deliberately in a centre-off position (programmable, often 1–5 s), then closes the other source. Seconds, on purpose. Motor and transformer-rich loads: the pause lets motor residual voltage decay so re-energisation does not slam an out-of-phase back-EMF — torque transients that snap shafts and trip breakers. The in-phase-monitor option is the faster alternative for the same problem.
Closed transition (make-before-break) Synchronises and parallels the two live sources for ≤ 100 ms, then opens the outgoing one. Used for return transfer and load tests; a dead grid still means an open transfer. Zero (when both sources are live). Seamless monthly testing and return-to-utility. Requires utility consent — paralleling with the SEC network engages the Saudi Grid Code / SEC Distribution Code interconnection rules and usually mandated protection (see Saudi notes).
Bypass-isolation Any of the above, built with a second, manually-operated switch path so the automatic mechanism can be isolated, drawn out and serviced while the load stays fed. n/a (a construction option, not a transition type) When the ATS itself must not be a maintenance single point of failure — hospitals, data centres, and a defensible option for this plant's essential bus.
Where the 10 seconds goes. For this plant's open transition the budget is roughly: undervoltage sensing and timers confirm the SEC loss (~1–3 s, to ride through dips without nuisance starts), engine crank and field build-up to 1800 rpm / 400 V (~5–8 s for a warm 1250 kVA-class set), then the transfer operation itself (< 1 s). The ATS controller owns the first and last items; the engine owns the middle. The full timing diagram is worked in the Part 2 ATS tutorial.

Construction classes hiding behind the brands

Underneath the branding, catalogues offer three mechanism constructions, and the difference matters more than the logo. Dedicated transfer mechanisms (ASCO 7000, OTPC class) are purpose-built double-throw devices, mechanically interlocked over-centre mechanisms type-tested specifically for transfer duty — the benchmark for endurance and withstand. Contactor-based switches are two mechanically and electrically interlocked contactors — economical at smaller sizes. Breaker-pair arrangements use two motor-operated circuit breakers with interlocks; they add integral overcurrent protection but transfer more slowly and their endurance is breaker-class, not contactor-class. For IEC procurement, all should be type-tested to IEC 60947-6-1 (transfer switching equipment); that certificate, at 400 V / 60 Hz, is the line to demand regardless of family. Utilisation category matters too: AC-33A/AC-33B (mixed/motor loads) rather than AC-31 (non-inductive) for this bus.

Decide early who supplies the ATS: with the genset (one responsibility for the start chain and timing) or with the LV switchboard package (one responsibility for busbar, withstand and arc containment). Both work; an ATS bought separately from both is how interface gaps are born. Whichever route is chosen, the DG controller, ATS controller and plant control system signal map is a drawing to approve before manufacture — see the selection.

Anatomy of an ATS Data Sheet

Rated current versus withstand — two different questions

The ampere number in the model name answers the easy question: how much load current the switch can carry continuously. The hard question is printed further down as the withstand and closing rating (WCR): how much fault current the switch can carry — and, worse, close onto — without welding or exploding, for long enough for an upstream protective device to clear. An ATS is not a breaker; it does not interrupt faults. It must survive them while something else does the interrupting. The WCR is therefore stated in combination with a protecting device: either "any breaker" for a stated time (e.g. X kA for 3 cycles / specific-breaker tables) or a short-time rating (e.g. X kA for 0.05–0.5 s) that lets it coordinate with an upstream breaker's short-time delay. The rule is absolute: WCR ≥ prospective fault current at the point of installation, verified against the actual upstream breaker or fuse type and its clearing time — not against a generic assumption. On this plant the essential-bus fault level is dominated by the LV network (one transformer ≈ 38.5 kA at the MSB; the level at the ATS depends on intervening cable and is fixed by the short-circuit study).

3-pole versus 4-pole — the neutral decision

On this plant's TN-S system the question "switch the neutral or not?" must be answered deliberately. A 3-pole ATS leaves the neutral solidly shared between SEC and DG sources; a 4-pole ATS transfers the neutral with the phases (overlapping or early-make/late-break neutral contacts in better designs, so the load neutral is never floated). The driver is earth-fault protection integrity: with both source neutrals permanently interconnected, parallel neutral return paths form, and residual/earth-fault relays on either source can be desensitised or made to nuisance-trip by circulating neutral current. Where the DG has its own neutral-earth bond and earth-fault protection — the normal arrangement for a packaged set, and the assumption here — specify 4-pole and switch the neutral. (The 3-pole alternative requires a single, carefully engineered neutral-earth bond and a protection review; it is a design decision, not a default.) The earthing logic is developed in the Part 2 earthing material.

Endurance, duty and environment

  • Endurance cycles: catalogues state mechanical and electrical operation counts (IEC 60947-6-1 classes; thousands of cycles for dedicated mechanisms). A standby ATS transfers rarely but is exercised monthly — 30+ years × 12 tests is only ~400 cycles, so endurance is rarely limiting; contact inspection intervals are.
  • Utilisation category: AC-33A/B for the mixed motor/UPS load of an essential bus, declared at 400 V, 60 Hz.
  • 50 °C derating: as with every catalogue in Part 6, the ampere rating is referenced to 40 °C (and often to free-air or a specific enclosure). Ask for the certified continuous rating at 50 °C, in the offered enclosure — an indicative 5–10 % reduction — plus IP54-thinking dust protection if outside the conditioned room.
  • Controller data: pickup/dropout voltage and frequency windows (set for 400 V / 60 Hz nominal), programmable timers (engine start delay, transfer, retransfer, cool-down), in-phase monitor or programmed-pause options, load-shed/elevator pre-signal contacts, engine exerciser, and comms protocol to the plant control system.
Key takeaway: an ATS data sheet is read in this order — transition type, then WCR against the real upstream device, then poles (4-pole for this TN-S plant), then the 50 °C ampere rating, and only then the controller features. A switch that merely matches the load current but not the fault duty is a bomb with a brand name.

Worked selection — ATS for tag ATS

Step 1 — frame current. The ATS must carry whatever the DG can deliver, because the genset breaker — not the ATS — is the limiting protection on the standby side. Full-load current of the 1250 kVA set at 400 V:

I = S / (√3 × U) = 1,250,000 VA / (1.732 × 400 V) ≈ 1804 A

Next standard frame up:

1804 A → 2000 A frame class

Sanity check: the essential load itself (~900 kVA ≈ 1300 A) fits with margin; sizing to the source rather than the load is the conservative convention and costs one frame size.

Step 2 — withstand and closing rating. Demand WCR ≥ the prospective short-circuit current at the ATS location from the short-circuit study (upper bound: one-transformer infeed ≈ 38.5 kA at the LV-MSB; the value at the essential switchboard is lower after cable impedance — the study fixes it). Verify the WCR against the actual upstream breaker type and clearing time, or specify a short-time-rated switch coordinated with the upstream breaker's short-time delay.

Step 3 — poles and transition. 4-pole (switched neutral, overlapping contacts) for the TN-S system with a neutral-earthed DG; open transition with programmable delayed transition / in-phase monitor for the motor loads; total transfer budget ≤ 10 s per the plant standard. Bypass-isolation construction is the recommended option so the essential bus never goes dark for ATS maintenance.

Result (indicative): 2000 A, 4-pole, open/delayed-transition bypass-isolation ATS, IEC 60947-6-1 class AC-33, 400/230 V 60 Hz, WCR per the short-circuit study, rated at 50 °C. The formal studies and vendor certified data govern.

Signal interfaces — the lines on the drawing that make it work

The ATS is a node in a control chain, and the interface list is part of the specification, not an afterthought:

  • To the DG controller: the hardwired, fail-safe engine-start contact (closed = run is the safe convention), plus generator-available status back to the ATS; remote stop and common alarm.
  • To the plant control / building management system: position indication (normal / emergency / centre-off), source-availability status, transfer-in-progress, fail-to-transfer alarm, exerciser status — via volt-free contacts and/or Modbus.
  • Load shed / staged reconnection: programmable contacts that drop or delay non-priority essential loads so the DG sees the staged load steps assumed in the genset selection.
  • UPS coordination: the 100 kVA UPS rides through the ≤10 s break; confirm its input window accepts the DG's voltage/frequency excursions on retransfer (timing tutorial).

Saudi-Specific Practice — SASO, SEC and the Desert

SASO / SABER conformity

Imported transfer switches and generator sets clear Saudi customs through the SABER platform with certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted standards (IEC 60947-6-1 for the ATS; ISO 8528 and the LV directives' IEC equivalents for the packaged genset). Make the vendor contractually responsible for the SABER/SASO certificates, type-test reports at 60 Hz, and country-of-origin documents at order stage — paperwork strands equipment in port far more often than technical faults do, exactly as noted for the MV switchgear.

SEC notification and the closed-transition question

An open-transition ATS never connects the DG to the SEC network, so no interconnection approval is engaged — this is a quiet but real advantage of the plant's chosen scheme. The moment closed transition (or any paralleling, however brief) is contemplated, the installation becomes an embedded generator paralleling with the distribution network: SEC must be notified and must consent, the Saudi Grid Code / SEC Distribution Code interconnection requirements apply, and protection (sync-check, reverse power, over/under voltage and frequency, and the mandated means of disconnection) must be agreed with SEC in writing. The ~100 ms overlap does not exempt it. If seamless retransfer is ever requested by operations, treat it as a project with an SEC workstream, not an ATS option tick-box.

Siting — heat and dust

Best practice is the ATS inside the conditioned electrical room with the essential switchboard, restoring the 40 °C catalogue basis. If it must live outside (e.g. in a containerised genset package), specify the enclosure for dust (minimum IP54 per the plant environmental basis), shaded installation, the certified 50 °C ampere rating, and anti-condensation heaters for coastal humidity swings. Verify the controller electronics' declared operating temperature — control boards, not copper, are usually the first thermal casualty.

Exercising under permit

A standby system that is never tested is a hope, not a system. Set the ATS exerciser for a monthly on-load test (engine start, transfer, 30–60 min on load to reach full operating temperature and avoid wet stacking, retransfer) — but on an operating plant this is a planned operation under the permit-to-work system, scheduled with operations, with the essential loads' tolerance of the two open-transition breaks confirmed in advance, and logged: start time, time-to-ready (verify ≤ 10 s), load reached, fuel state, alarms. Quarterly, add a genuine "pull-the-plug" black test of the whole chain — sensing, start, transfer, sequencing — because the exerciser's polite pre-arranged test exercises everything except the part that fails in real life: the loss-of-source detection.

  • Confirm DG rating is declared ESP per ISO 8528 at site conditions — 1250 kVA at 50 °C, with dust filters and enclosure fitted, at 400/230 V, 60 Hz, 1800 rpm (indicative; studies govern).
  • Check the largest essential motor-start step against the vendor's load-acceptance table and transient-dip curve (ISO 8528-5 class G2 minimum, G3 preferred).
  • Verify day-tank autonomy calculation against the data-sheet fuel rates (indicative 8 h → ~2000 L) with low-level alarm and bunding.
  • Verify ATS frame (2000 A class for 1804 A DG full-load current) and WCR ≥ prospective fault current with the actual upstream device per the short-circuit study.
  • Confirm 4-pole (switched neutral) construction for the TN-S system, with overlapping neutral contacts, and the open/delayed-transition scheme within the ≤ 10 s budget.
  • Approve the DG-controller / ATS / plant-control signal map (engine start, status, load shed, alarms) before manufacture; test every line at commissioning.
  • Collect SASO/SABER certificates and 60 Hz type-test reports with the bid; if closed transition is ever proposed, obtain SEC written consent first.
