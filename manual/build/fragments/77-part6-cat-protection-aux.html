What the Plant Needs — IEDs in This Protection Chain

A numerical IED is a small industrial computer that samples currents and voltages a few thousand times per second, runs protection algorithms in firmware, and closes a trip contact when an algorithm says so. One hardware box replaces a whole panel of the electromechanical relays it descends from — overcurrent, earth fault, thermal, under/overvoltage — and adds what no electromechanical relay could offer: event and disturbance records, self-supervision, communications, and settings you change from a laptop instead of a screwdriver. The protection philosophy for this plant is fixed in the protection coordination section; this section is about choosing the hardware that executes it.

Walk the single line from the SEC intake down and list every place a decision-making device sits. That list is the IED shopping list:

Where the boundary sits. In a plant this size the numerical-IED population is small — typically three to five devices on the MV board plus a handful of motor IEDs — because below the LV incomers the protection intelligence lives inside breaker trip units and motor starters. Do not let a vendor sell an IED for every LV feeder; the money is better spent on the few MV devices, where a wrong or slow trip takes out the entire plant.

One supporting actor appears throughout: the plant 110 V DC battery system (tag DCDB). Every IED and every breaker trip coil runs from it, precisely so that protection still works during the voltage collapse of a close-up fault. When reading relay catalogues, the auxiliary-supply range is therefore not a footnote — it is the second thing to check after the function list.

All are numerical, all are type-tested to the IEC 60255 series, all speak IEC 61850, and a specification written in ANSI-function and standards language will receive compliant offers from each. Read the table as four answers to the same question. Any family can protect this plant; the deciding factors are: (1) which family the MV-SWGR vendor pre-wires by default (ABB boards arrive with Relion, Siemens with SIPROTEC — fighting that costs engineering hours); (2) which engineering tool your maintenance team already knows — PCM600, DIGSI 5, AcSELerator or EnerVista are each a training investment; (3) spares and support presence in the Kingdom; and (4) whether you want arc-flash detection integrated in the same box, which all four offer but price differently. Mixing families across one small board is legal but unwise — two engineering tools, two spare-relay types, two training courses, for three panels.

Standardise on one family per plant and keep one tested spare of each hardware variant on the shelf. A numerical IED fails safe (its watchdog contact alarms) but a feeder without protection cannot stay in service; with a spare and an exported settings file, exchange-and-restore is under an hour.

How to Read an IED Data Sheet

An IED data sheet is long because the device does many jobs, but only six blocks of it decide selection. Read them in this order.

1 — The ANSI function list

The first page is a grid of ANSI/IEEE device numbers — the shorthand the whole protection world shares. For this plant the functions that must appear are:

ANSIFunctionWhy this plant needs it

50N / 51NInstantaneous / time earth fault On a resistance-earthed 13.8 kV system the earth-fault current is capped by the NER at 400 A — phase elements never see it, so dedicated, sensitive earth elements are mandatory and must clear well inside the NER's 10 s rating (earthing section).

2 — CT requirements

The relay is only as honest as its CTs. For plain overcurrent IEDs the catalogue asks for a protection-class core, typically 5P20 — at most 5 % composite error at 20 × rated current — with the rated burden matched to the wiring run. Differential protection (87T) is stricter: the data sheet specifies a minimum knee-point voltage (class PX/X or an equivalent 5P requirement) per winding, because the two CT sets must not saturate unequally during a heavy through-fault — unequal saturation manufactures a phantom differential current and trips a healthy transformer. If you choose 87T, the CT sizing calculation in the relay manual becomes a mandatory design deliverable, ordered with the switchgear: CT cores are the hardest thing to change after manufacture. Check also that the IED's rated input (1 A or 5 A, usually software-selectable) matches the CT secondaries, and that accuracy is declared at 60 Hz.

3 — Binary I/O count

Count what the scheme actually needs before reading the options table: trip and close outputs per breaker, breaker position (52a/52b) inputs, earth-switch position, trip-circuit supervision, gas/temperature inputs from the transformer (Buchholz-equivalent, winding temperature on the 1600 kVA units), SEC intertrip receive, watchdog out, and spare channels for the unforeseen. A base-model feeder IED ships with roughly 8–14 binary inputs and 5–10 output contacts; transformer and motor variants offer expansion modules. The classic error is ordering the base I/O and discovering at commissioning that trip-circuit supervision and the intertrip have consumed every spare — specify ~30 % spare I/O in the RFQ.

4 — Station bus: IEC 61850 and GOOSE

All four families support IEC 61850 — the substation communication standard — over an Ethernet station bus. Two services matter here. MMS reporting carries measurements, events and disturbance records up to the plant control system (DCS/PMS). GOOSE is the fast peer-to-peer service: a relay publishes a status change (typically in a few milliseconds) that other relays subscribe to. In this plant GOOSE (or hard wiring on a board this small) implements the blocking / zone-selective-interlocking scheme: a feeder IED that sees the fault tells the incomer IED "this one is mine — hold back", letting the incomer carry a fast setting for genuine bus faults without losing grading. The same mechanism carries arc-flash detection trips. Check the data sheet for: 61850 edition, GOOSE performance class, redundancy protocol on dual ports (PRP/HSR or RSTP), and whether the 61850 licence is included or a paid option — it is the most common hidden cost on the order.

5 — Auxiliary supply

Specify the plant standard 110 V DC from DCDB. Catalogue power-supply variants are wide-range (e.g. 48–250 V DC); make sure the variant ordered covers 110 V DC including the battery end-of-discharge voltage (around 88 V for a 110 V system), and that binary input thresholds are ordered for 110 V DC so a half-earthed trip wire cannot spuriously assert an input.

6 — Environment

Most IED data sheets state a continuous operating range of about −10 °C to +55 °C (storage wider). In this plant every IED lives in the conditioned MV/LV switchrooms, so the 50 °C site ambient is not a problem while the HVAC runs — but check the figure anyway, ask what is certified during an HVAC failure, and prefer conformally coated electronics (standard on some families, an option on others) as cheap insurance against dust and humidity ingress.

Key takeaway: select IEDs on six blocks — ANSI functions, CT requirements (5P20, knee-point for 87T), binary I/O with ~30 % spare, IEC 61850 GOOSE capability, 110 V DC auxiliary, and −10…+55 °C environment. Everything else in the data sheet is configuration, not selection.

The 87T question at 1600 kVA

Transformer differential protection (87T) compares current into the 13.8 kV winding with current out of the 0.4 kV winding; any imbalance beyond the bias characteristic means an internal fault, cleared in a few cycles with no grading delay. On large transformers it is non-negotiable. At 1600 kVA it is a judgement call, and the honest catalogue answer is that most plants this size do not fit it:

  • For 87T: fastest possible clearing of an internal fault limits tank damage and fire risk; with only two transformers, losing one for months of repair halves plant capacity (the bus-tie scenario in the SPOF study).
  • Against 87T: a dedicated transformer IED, a matched knee-point-rated CT set on both sides (the 2309 A LV side CT accommodation in the LV incomer is the awkward part), and engineering for inrush stabilisation — meaningful cost against a transformer this small. The conventional alternative — MV 50/51 with inrush-proof settings, 51N, plus the transformer's own devices (winding temperature, pressure relief, gas detection on sealed units) wired to trip — covers most internal failure modes a few hundred milliseconds slower.

Indicative recommendation for this plant: feeder IEDs with full transformer-device tripping, no 87T, unless the owner's insurer or reliability target says otherwise — and if 87T is chosen, order the CT cores with the switchgear and the LV board, not afterwards. Either way the decision must be written into the protection study, not left to the panel builder's default.

One scheme, one author. The incomer IED, feeder IEDs and LV trip units form a single grading ladder. If the MV relays are set by the switchgear vendor and the LV trip units by the switchboard vendor, nobody owns the gap between them — the classic result is an LV bus fault tripping the MV incomer first. One protection engineer must own the whole curve set from the SEC fuse-line down to the largest MCC starter (protection coordination).
Key takeaway: three to five well-chosen MV IEDs from one family, ZSI- capable LV trip units coordinated with them, motor IEDs only above ~75 kW, and a deliberate, documented yes/no on 87T — that is the entire relay purchase for a 2 MW plant. All picks indicative; the protection study governs.

Goal: learn to read the catalogues of the three "quiet" equipment classes that make the main equipment safe and efficient: the neutral earthing resistor (tag NER) that tames earth-fault current, the surge protective devices that tame lightning and switching transients, and the power-factor-correction bank (tag PFC) that tames the reactive power bill. None of them does anything on a good day — which is exactly why their data sheets are misread more often than switchgear data sheets. For the engineering behind each, see earthing, lightning and surge protection and power factor correction.

Neutral Earthing Resistors — Reading an NER Data Sheet

The NER is conceptually the simplest item in the plant — a resistor between the 13.8 kV system neutral and earth — and its data sheet is correspondingly short. That brevity is a trap: every line on it is load-bearing. The plant's NER duty, from the Plant Data Card and the earthing section: 7.97 kV, 400 A, 10 s → 19.9 Ω.

All serious NERs are stainless-steel grid resistors: punched plates or edge-wound strip in open frames, chosen because stainless alloy holds its resistance value and mechanical strength while being driven hundreds of kelvin upward in seconds. Cast iron and wire-wound alternatives exist at small ratings; for a 13.8 kV plant neutral, specify stainless grid and move on.

Rated let-through current400 A The deliberate cap on earth-fault current — high enough for relays to see (51N grading), low enough to limit burning damage at the fault point. The whole earthing design hangs on this number.

Protection must clear earth faults well inside this — typically ≤1 s with backup inside 10 s. Worked check — the data-sheet numbers agree with each other

Always close the triangle on an NER data sheet:

V_LN = 13.8 kV / √3 = 7.97 kV R = V_LN / I = 7967 V / 400 A = 19.9 Ω Energy per event ≈ I² × R × t = 400² × 19.9 × 10 s ≈ 31.8 MJ

Sanity check: 31.8 MJ is roughly the energy of 9 kWh — a domestic kettle running for four hours — dumped into a steel grid in ten seconds. That is why the enclosure is ventilated, why the time rating exists, and why the resistor must be allowed to cool before a second event. All figures indicative.

Enclosure, siting and monitoring

The NER lives outdoors next to the transformer or switchroom. Specify the enclosure for desert siting: hot-dip galvanised or stainless housing, IP23 minimum for a ventilated resistor (the elements must breathe; a sealed IP54 box defeats the cooling — instead use mesh-screened louvres, vermin- and sand-screened) and check the vendor's stance on wind-blown sand accumulation on the grids. Include an elevated plinth, padlockable access (the terminals sit at 7.97 kV during a fault) and a warning label stating the time rating.

Finally, the line most often missed: an NER can fail open-circuit — corroded joint, broken grid — and nothing visible happens until the next earth fault, when the system behaves as unearthed and healthy-phase voltages run wild. Specify an NGR monitoring relay (offered by the NER vendors themselves and as a function in some feeder IEDs): it injects a small sensing signal through the resistor continuously and alarms on open or shorted elements. Wire it to alarm at the DCS and, per the operating philosophy, to trip — see the earthing section for the system behaviour it protects against.

Surge Protection — MV Arresters and LV SPDs

Surge protection comes in two distinct catalogue worlds that must be read with two distinct vocabularies: metal-oxide surge arresters on the MV system (IEC 60099-4 language: Uc, Ur, energy class) and surge protective devices on the LV system (IEC 61643-11 language: Type 1/2/3, Iimp/In, Up). The coordination philosophy — why protection cascades from the service entrance inwards — is in the lightning and surge section; this is the catalogue view.

Three catalogue decisions, in order:

(1) Class. Distribution class arresters (lower energy, lighter duty) protect overhead distribution equipment; station class (higher energy capability, better protective level) protect substations and major plant. For the plant intake and the 1600 kVA transformers, common industrial practice on a cable-fed intake is a heavy-duty distribution-class or small station-class arrester at the overhead-to-cable transition and/or transformer terminals — driven by whether the SEC feed arrives overhead (higher lightning exposure: go station class) or fully in cable (lower exposure). Confirm the supply arrangement with SEC before specifying.

(2) Uc / MCOV — the line everyone gets wrong. The continuous operating voltage the arrester can hold forever must exceed the maximum phase-to-earth voltage it will ever see continuously — and on this resistance-earthed system, during an earth fault the two healthy phases rise toward the full line-to-line voltage, 13.8 kV, for up to the NER's 10 s rating. The arrester must ride through that temporary overvoltage. The data sheet's TOV curve answers it: indicatively, a 13.8 kV resistance-earthed system lands on a rated voltage Ur around 15–18 kV with Uc/MCOV around 12.7–15.3 kV — noticeably higher than the ≈8 kV phase-earth voltage would suggest, precisely because of the earthing method. An arrester chosen as if for a solidly-earthed system will cook during the first sustained earth fault.

(3) Energy class and protective level. Check the nominal discharge current (10 kA class typical here), the energy/charge classification, and the residual voltage Ures at nominal discharge — which must sit comfortably below the equipment lightning withstand (95 kV BIL for the 17.5 kV-class switchgear, with margin per IEC 60071 insulation coordination). Housing: silicone, with creepage specified for the desert pollution class; mount with disconnectors and counters where visible indication is wanted.

The LV catalogue logic is the Type 1 / 2 / 3 cascade (IEC 61643-11 test classes): Type 1 at the service entrance / LV-MSB, tested with the 10/350 µs impulse (Iimp, typically 12.5–25 kA per pole), absorbs partial lightning currents where the lightning protection system bonds to the power system; Type 2 at distribution boards (DB/EDB, MCC incomers), tested with 8/20 µs (In typically 20 kA), clips what passes the first stage; Type 3 at sensitive terminal equipment (UPS input, DCS cabinets), fine-clipping to a low protective voltage. Read off each data sheet: Uc ≥ 255 V for a 230 V phase-to-neutral TN-S system, the protective level Up (≤1.5 kV at terminal equipment is the usual target), the short-circuit withstand with the specified backup fuse, and the status contact for DCS alarming of a failed cartridge. Keep cable stubs short — the cascade only coordinates if the catalogue's separation-distance or energy-coordination rules between stages are followed (lightning section).

In TN-S, specify the connection scheme deliberately (4-pole, or "3+1" with a summary gap to PE) and order SPDs with remote-signalling contacts wired to the DCS — a surge arrester that died silently last thunderstorm protects nothing during the next one.

The detuned reactor — why 7 % and what it does to every other number

A plant full of VFDs (tag VFD) injects harmonic currents. A plain capacitor bank can resonate with the supply-transformer inductance near one of those harmonics, amplifying it until capacitors fail and fuses scatter. The fix is a series detuning reactor in each step, sized so that reactor + capacitor are series-resonant below the lowest significant harmonic — the branch then looks inductive to the 5th harmonic and above and cannot resonate with them. The detuning factor p = 7 % means the reactor's reactance is 7 % of the capacitor's at fundamental.

Worked check — tuning frequency and capacitor voltage at p = 7 %

Series tuning frequency on a 60 Hz system:

f_res = f_n / √p = 60 Hz / √0.07 = 60 / 0.2646 ≈ 227 Hz

Sanity check: 227 Hz sits below the 5th harmonic (300 Hz at 60 Hz) and clear of the 3rd (180 Hz) — the branch is inductive at every harmonic a 6-pulse VFD produces. The reactor also raises the fundamental voltage across the capacitor terminals:

U_cap = U_network / (1 − p) = 400 V / 0.93 ≈ 430 V

so the capacitors inside a 7 % detuned 400 V bank must be rated typically 440–480 V, not 400 V. A catalogue that offers 400 V-rated capacitors behind a 7 % reactor is offering early failures. All figures indicative.

kvar at rated voltage versus kvar at network voltage

The most common PFC ordering error follows directly: capacitor output scales with voltage squared,

Q_at_network = Q_rated × (U_network / U_rated)²

so a "100 kvar, 480 V" capacitor delivers only 100 × (400/480)² ≈ 69 kvar on a 400 V network before the reactor effect is added back. Reputable catalogues quote the effective kvar of the complete detuned step at network voltage — always confirm that the ≈580 kvar plant target is specified on that basis, in writing, and let the vendor pick element ratings to deliver it.

Saudi-Specific Practice — SASO/SABER, Spares and Support

Conformity paperwork

Everything in this fragment — IEDs, NER, arresters, SPDs, capacitor banks — is imported electrical equipment and therefore passes through the SASO conformity regime via the SABER platform: certificates of conformity against the SASO-adopted IEC standards (IEC 60255 series for relays, IEEE 32 / IEC practice for the NER, IEC 60099-4 for arresters, IEC 61643-11 for SPDs, IEC 60831/60931 for LV capacitors), plus SEC acceptance where the device sits at or interacts with the utility interface — notably the incomer IED functions and settings, which SEC reviews against the Distribution Code. Make the vendor responsible in the purchase order for SABER registration and certificate delivery; the auxiliary equipment is exactly the category that gets forgotten until it is sitting in customs while the switchgear it serves is already on its plinth. SBC 401 applies to the LV installation side (SPD provision and earthing practice in particular).

Spares philosophy

These are low-population, long-lead items; the spares logic is different from bulk consumables:

  • IEDs: one spare unit per hardware variant (not per installed relay), kept with current firmware and the plant settings files exported and version- controlled. A relay model is typically manufactured for a decade and supported for another; plan a mid-life fleet review rather than ad-hoc replacements.
  • NER: spare grid elements and joint hardware rather than a whole spare resistor; the enclosure and frame outlive the plant. Keep the NGR-monitor spare with the IED stock.
  • Arresters / SPDs: arresters are not repairable — hold one spare set per class; for LV SPDs hold plug-in cartridges per type, and expect to consume them: a cartridge that sacrificed itself in a storm did its job.
  • PFC: capacitor elements are wear parts at 50 °C — hold one or two step's worth of elements, step fuses and a spare switching contactor (contactor contacts wear fastest of all); record step capacitance annually and trend it, replacing elements that drift more than ~5 % down.

Vendor support in the Kingdom

All families named here are represented in Saudi Arabia, but with different depth: the switchgear-OEM relay families (ABB, Siemens, GE, Schneider) come with the OEM's Saudi service organisation; SEL runs regional protection support strong in the oil & gas corridor; NER, arrester and SPD specialists typically work through local agents — so name the local agent and the firmware/settings support arrangement in the bid evaluation, not after award. For IEDs, ask explicitly: who in-Kingdom can reconfigure the relays and run secondary injection, and within how many days? A protection scheme nobody local can maintain decays into "do not touch" folklore within five years.

  • One IED family selected for the MV board; settings files exported, version-controlled and one tested spare per hardware variant on the shelf.
  • Incomer IED 51N grading checked against the NER 400 A / 10 s let-through; clearing well inside 10 s (protection study).
  • 87T decision (fit / omit at 1600 kVA) documented in the protection study; if fitted, knee-point CT cores ordered with the switchgear and LV board.
  • NER data sheet triangle verified: 7.97 kV / 400 A / 19.9 Ω / 10 s; ventilated desert-spec enclosure; NGR monitoring relay wired to DCS.
  • MV arrester Uc/TOV checked against the 10 s healthy-phase rise of the resistance-earthed 13.8 kV system, not against phase-earth voltage alone.
  • LV SPD Type 1/2/3 cascade located per the zone plan with status contacts to DCS (lightning section).
  • PFC bank specified as ≈580 kvar effective at 400 V / 60 Hz, 7 % detuned (≈227 Hz), cubicle certified at 50 °C; SABER/SASO certificates for all items in the dossier before shipment.
