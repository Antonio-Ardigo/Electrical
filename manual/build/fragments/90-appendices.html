A — Glossary of Terms and Abbreviations

Alphabetical table of abbreviations and terms used across this manual. Expand from the course overview glossary and includes terms introduced in Parts 4 and 5. All voltage and frequency references follow the Saudi (SEC) Rev 3 basis of this manual (13.8 kV MV, 400/230 V LV, 60 Hz, 50 °C design ambient), with standards cited as SASO-adopted IEC plus the SEC Distribution Code / Saudi Grid Code and SBC 401.

Term / Abbreviation Meaning and context in this manual
ACBAir Circuit Breaker. Large LV breaker used for switchboard incomers and the bus-tie on LV-MSB. Ratings typically 1600–6300 A; short-time withstand up to 65–100 kA (1 s).
AFBArc-Flash Boundary. The working distance at which incident energy falls to 1.2 cal/cm² (the onset-of-a-second-degree-burn threshold). Determined per IEEE 1584-2018.
ANSIAmerican National Standards Institute. Device numbering scheme (e.g. 50/51, 87T) used on SLD relay/breaker labels as industry convention alongside IEC.
ATSAutomatic Transfer Switch. Monitors the utility supply and, on failure, automatically transfers the essential bus to the standby DG. A failed-to-transfer ATS is one of the highest-RPN items in a typical FMECA.
Availability (A)A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR). The fraction of calendar time a component or system is in its functioning state. Expressed as a decimal (e.g. 0.9992) or as annual downtime hours.
AVRAutomatic Voltage Regulator. Controls the excitation of the diesel generator alternator to maintain output voltage within limits during load changes.
BESSBattery Energy Storage System. Grid-connected or plant-connected battery system providing ride-through energy, inertia emulation, fast frequency / voltage support, or islanding capability.
BT (or B/T)Bus-Tie. The normally-open ACB (or contactor) connecting Bus A and Bus B of the LV-MSB split busbar. Closes automatically on loss of one incomer to allow the healthy transformer to back-feed the dead bus. A critical SPOF-mitigation element.
Cable ampacityThe continuous current-carrying capacity of a cable under defined installation conditions (temperature, grouping, burial). Determined from IEC 60364 / manufacturer tables after applying derating factors.
CT / VTCurrent Transformer / Voltage Transformer. Instrument transformers that step down MV/LV currents and voltages to safe measurement levels (e.g. 5 A / 110 V secondary). Feed protection relays, metering and SCADA.
DBDistribution Board. Final-circuit LV panel fed from the LV-MSB or an MCC; supplies lighting, small power and HVAC loads via MCBs/RCBOs.
DCDBDC Distribution Board (battery and charger system). Provides 110 V DC (typical) for MV switchgear trip/close coils and protection relay auxiliary supplies. A DCDB failure silences protection — very high severity in the FMECA.
DCSDistributed Control System. Plant-wide process control system with operator HMI. Receives electrical status (breaker position, voltages, currents) and issues high-level electrical commands.
Detuned filterA series reactor added to each PFC capacitor step so the branch is tuned below the lowest characteristic harmonic (7 % reactor → tuning frequency f/√0.07 ≈ 227 Hz on a 60 Hz system, safely below the 5th harmonic at 300 Hz). Prevents harmonic resonance between the capacitor bank and the supply impedance in VFD-rich plants. The plant PFC bank is 7 % detuned.
DGDiesel Generator set. Standby power source sized for the essential-load bus (1250 kVA indicative, ESP rating at 50 °C ambient). Includes AVR, governor, fuel day-tank, battery starting and acoustic enclosure.
DOLDirect-On-Line. Simplest motor starting method — motor connected straight to supply. Starting current typically 5–7 × FLC; suitable for motors up to ~7.5 kW or where the bus can absorb the inrush.
DVRDynamic Voltage Restorer. Series power-electronics device injecting a compensating voltage to protect plant loads against upstream voltage sags and swells.
EDBEssential Distribution Board. Fed via the ATS from the DG (or utility essential bus). Supplies safety-critical loads that must remain energised during a utility outage.
ESP / PRP / COPISO 8528-1 genset duty ratings. ESP — Emergency Standby Power (variable load, limited hours/yr, no sustained overload); PRP — Prime Rated Power (variable load, unlimited hours, 10 % overload 1 h in 12); COP — Continuous Operating Power (constant base load). The plant DG is rated 1250 kVA ESP at 50 °C (indicative). Always read the rating with its ambient — Saudi summer temperatures derate engine output, so a nameplate quoted at 25 °C is optimistic here.
FMECAFailure Mode, Effects and Criticality Analysis. Structured IEC 60812 method: list failure modes for each component, assess effects on the system, score Severity (S), Occurrence (O), Detectability (D), compute RPN = S × O × D, and rank for action. The primary SPOF quantification tool in this campaign.
FLCFull-Load Current. The rated continuous current at full load: I = S / (√3 · V) for 3-phase. Basis for breaker sizing, cable selection and protection relay pickup settings.
GOOSEGeneric Object-Oriented Substation Event. IEC 61850 fast peer-to-peer message over the station bus used for inter-IED tripping, bus-transfer logic and load-shedding. Latency typically <4 ms.
IACInternal Arc Classification per IEC 62271-200 (e.g. IAC AFLR 25 kA 1 s). States which sides of an MV switchgear panel (Front, Lateral, Rear) protect personnel against an internal arc of stated current and duration. Check the IAC rating plate on MV-SWGR during the site visit.
IDMTInverse Definite Minimum Time. Overcurrent relay characteristic (IEC 60255-151 curves: SI, VI, EI) where operating time decreases as fault current increases. The workhorse 51 element used for time-current grading between MV and LV devices in this plant.
IEDIntelligent Electronic Device. Numerical multifunction protection relay performing overcurrent (50/51), earth-fault (50N/51N), differential (87T), voltage (27/59) and other protection functions, plus measurement and control.
Incident energyThe thermal energy per unit area (cal/cm²) deposited on a worker at the working distance during an arc-flash event, computed per IEEE 1584-2018 from bolted fault current, clearing time, gap and enclosure geometry. Sets the arc-rated PPE category and the label on LV-MSB / MCC doors. 1.2 cal/cm² is the onset-of-second-degree-burn threshold.
IscShort-Circuit Current (symmetrical). The prospective fault current at a bus under bolted three-phase conditions. Used to specify the breaking capacity (Icu) of switchgear. Indicative this plant: ~38.5 kA per 1600 kVA, Z = 6 % transformer at 400 V; ~77 kA with the bus-tie closed (both TX paralleled — a condition the interlock must prevent); 25 kA design at 13.8 kV (SEC range 25–40 kA).
LPLLightning Protection Level. Classes I–IV per IEC 62305, determined by risk assessment. Sets air-termination geometry, down-conductor spacing and earth-electrode requirements.
LPSLightning Protection System. The complete system of air terminations, down conductors and earth electrodes protecting the plant structure and equipment against direct lightning strikes.
LVLow Voltage. Utilisation voltage; in this manual 400/230 V, 3-phase, 60 Hz per the SEC LV standard (IEC: ≤ 1 kV AC). Confirm per plant.
LVRT / UVRTLow / Under Voltage Ride-Through. The capability or protection settings that allow plant equipment (particularly VFD-driven loads) to remain connected through a defined voltage dip rather than tripping and exacerbating the disturbance.
MCCMotor Control Centre. LV assembly of motor starter bays (DOL, star-delta, soft-starter, VFD) feeding process motors. Tags MCC-1 … MCC-n per the plant scheme.
MCCBMoulded-Case Circuit Breaker. Compact LV protective device for outgoing feeders and motor circuits. Breaking capacity ratings from ~10 kA to 85 kA; motor-protection versions incorporate thermal/magnetic or electronic trip units.
MMSManufacturing Message Specification. IEC 61850 protocol for client–server monitoring, control and data exchange between IEDs and SCADA/EMS. Complements GOOSE for non-time-critical data.
MTBFMean Time Between Failures. MTBF = 1/λ (h). Average operating time between successive failures. Higher MTBF = lower failure rate = more reliable. See Appendix G for indicative values.
MTTRMean Time To Repair / Restore. Average time from failure occurrence to return to service (detection + logistics + repair + recommissioning). MTTR is the key lever that automation (auto bus-tie, ATS) acts on — reducing it from hours (manual) to seconds (auto-transfer) dramatically raises availability.
MVMedium Voltage. Higher distribution voltage; in this manual 13.8 kV — the standard SEC industrial distribution voltage (IEC: 1 kV < MV ≤ 36 kV; equipment is 17.5 kV class). Actual utility voltage must be confirmed per plant.
MV-METMV Utility Revenue Metering Cubicle. Contains utility-grade CTs/VTs and the tariff meter at the point of supply. Not a protection device — verify its presence and metering-point location during desk study.
MV-NERNeutral Earthing Resistor. Connected between the MV transformer neutral and earth to limit earth-fault current (400 A in this plant) while keeping faults detectable. R = 7.97 kV (13.8 kV / √3) ÷ 400 A ≈ 19.9 Ω, rated 10 s (indicative).
MV-SWGRMV Metal-Clad (or metal-enclosed) Switchgear. 13.8 kV bus (17.5 kV class) with vacuum circuit breakers; incomer, bus-section and transformer-feeder panels. Rated above the prospective fault level (indicative: 31.5 kA / 3 s withstand against a 25 kA design fault; SEC quotes 25–40 kA at the point of supply).
N+1 / N-1N+1: one more capacity unit than the minimum required — so one can be lost without losing the function. N-1: the condition after one unit has failed; the system must still serve the essential load. The 2 × 1600 kVA + bus-tie arrangement is N-1 secure for essential load (~55 % of total).
NERNeutral Earthing Resistor — see MV-NER. (US literature: NGR, Neutral Grounding Resistor.)
NER / NGR monitorA monitoring relay that continuously verifies the neutral earthing resistor path is intact (neither open-circuited nor shorted) by injecting a small sensing signal. An open NER is a latent failure: the system silently becomes unearthed and the first earth fault is then neither limited nor reliably detected. Common practice on resistance-earthed MV systems; a low-cost, high-value FMECA mitigation for MV-NER.
PCCPower Control Centre. The main LV switchboard that distributes power to MCCs and outgoing feeders. In this manual, LV-MSB is the PCC. Distinct from an MCC (which only controls motors).
PFCPower Factor Correction. Automatic capacitor bank (with 7 % detuned reactors to handle VFD harmonics) that raises plant power factor from ~0.85 to ≥ 0.95, reducing reactive demand and transformer / cable loading.
PMSPower Management System. Dedicated electrical controller responsible for: load shedding on generation failure, automatic bus transfer (bus-tie / ATS), generator synchronisation, and energy management. Interfaces with the DCS.
PPEPersonal Protective Equipment. Arc-rated PPE is required for work on or near energised electrical equipment; the required category is set by the arc-flash study (incident energy in cal/cm²). Non-intrusive site surveys in this campaign do not require arc-rated PPE if live-panel access is not needed — but confirm with the host's QHSE procedure.
QHSEQuality, Health, Safety and Environment. Management system framework (ISO 9001 / 14001 / 45001) applied to all site activities. All surveys in this campaign are non-intrusive; QHSE and permit-to-work rules of the host facility always take precedence.
RAMReliability, Availability, Maintainability. The quantitative study of system uptime: how often failures occur (R), what fraction of time the system works (A), and how quickly it is restored (M). See Part 3 — FMECA / RAM.
RBDReliability Block Diagram. Series / parallel model used to compute system availability from component data. Series elements degrade availability; parallel (redundant) paths multiply it up. See Part 3.
Ride-throughThe ability of equipment (VFDs, contactors, UPS-fed controls) to stay operational through a short supply disturbance — a voltage dip, phase jump or brief interruption — instead of tripping. Achieved by kinetic buffering in drives, DC-bus hold-up, delayed undervoltage settings, and UPS support. The Saudi Grid Code imposes fault ride-through duties on generating plant; inside the factory fence, ride-through settings determine whether a 200 ms utility dip becomes a full process trip.
ROCOFRate of Change of Frequency. Measure of frequency volatility on the grid (Hz/s). Increases as synchronous generation (with inertia) is displaced by inverter-based renewables. High ROCOF can trip plant protection (df/dt relays) prematurely.
RPNRisk Priority Number. FMECA criticality score = Severity (S) × Occurrence (O) × Detection (D), each scored 1–5 (higher = worse outcome / more frequent / harder to detect). RPN max = 125. Used to rank FMECA findings for action priority. Priority bands: P1 = SPOF with S ≥ 4 or RPN ≥ 60; P2 = RPN 36–59; P3 = RPN 16–35; P4 < 16. See Appendix C.
SABERSaudi Arabia's electronic conformity-assessment platform, operated under SASO. Regulated products (including much electrical equipment) require SABER product and shipment Certificates of Conformity before customs clearance. For the field engineer: missing SABER status is a procurement red flag, not a site finding. See Appendix H.
SASOSaudi Standards, Metrology and Quality Organization. The Kingdom's national standards body; adopts IEC standards (usually verbatim or with national deviations) as SASO IEC standards and operates the SABER conformity scheme. In this manual "SASO-adopted IEC" means: apply the IEC technical content, cited through its Saudi adoption. See Appendix H.
Saudi Grid CodeThe technical code governing connection to and operation of the Saudi transmission system (with the Distribution Code covering distribution-connected users such as this plant). Sets frequency/voltage operating ranges, fault ride-through, reactive and power-factor obligations, protection and metering requirements at the point of common coupling.
SBC 401Saudi Building Code — Electrical (SBC 401). The Kingdom's statutory electrical installation code (technically based on NFPA 70/NEC with Saudi amendments), enforced through municipal permitting. Coexists with SASO-adopted IEC design practice on industrial plants; where requirements differ, the contractually specified code governs — flag conflicts, do not silently pick one.
SCADASupervisory Control and Data Acquisition. Supervisory monitoring and control layer for the power system; aggregates IED data, trends energy, and provides alarm management to operators.
SECSaudi Electricity Company. The Kingdom's principal electricity utility and the supply authority assumed in this manual: 13.8 kV, 60 Hz point of supply, fault level 25–40 kA, connection per the SEC Distribution Code and SEC company standards (metering, protection interface, earthing at the intake). The utility intake arrangement (MV-MET, incomer protection) must be agreed with SEC.
SLDSingle-Line Diagram. One-line schematic showing the power system's main components and their electrical connections, using IEC 60617 symbols. The primary document for SLD verification on site and for SPOF hunting.
SPD (Type 1/2/3)Surge Protective Device per IEC 61643-11. Type 1 (tested with 10/350 µs impulse) at the service entrance / LV-MSB where lightning current can enter; Type 2 (8/20 µs) at distribution boards; Type 3 close to sensitive terminal equipment. Installed as a coordinated cascade with status contacts wired to the DCS. Essential in the Saudi coastal lightning environment.
SPOFSingle Point of Failure. A component or path whose failure alone disables a function or supply that must remain available. The central concept of this assessment campaign. See Part 3 and Appendix F.
STATCOM / SVCStatic Synchronous Compensator / Static VAr Compensator. Fast dynamic reactive-power devices used for voltage stabilisation and flicker / voltage-sag mitigation on weak or renewable-heavy grids.
TN-STN-S earthing system. IEC 60364 LV earthing arrangement where the neutral (N) and protective earth (PE) conductors are kept separate throughout. Used at 400 V LV in this plant.
TX-1 / TX-2MV/LV power transformers 1 and 2. Each indicatively 1600 kVA, 13.8 / 0.4 kV, Dyn11, Z ≈ 6 % (MV FLC 66.9 A, LV FLC 2309 A), feeding the split LV-MSB busbar. Both together provide N-1 redundancy via the bus-tie.
UPSUninterruptible Power Supply. Battery-backed, no-break clean AC supply for the DCS/PLC/SCADA, field instrumentation and critical communications. This plant: 100 kVA / 30 min autonomy, online double-conversion (VFI), indicative — confirm per plant.
VFD / VSDVariable Frequency Drive / Variable Speed Drive. Power-electronic converter providing continuously variable speed control of AC motors (pumps, fans, compressors). Reduces starting current to near FLC; generates harmonics requiring detuned PFC.
VFIVoltage and Frequency Independent — the IEC 62040-3 classification (VFI-SS-111) of a true online double-conversion UPS: output voltage and frequency are independent of input disturbances because power is always rectified and re-inverted. The plant UPS (100 kVA / 30 min indicative) is specified VFI. Contrast VI (line-interactive) and VFD-class (off-line) topologies, which pass some disturbances through.
WCRWithstand Current Rating (also rated short-time withstand, Icw). The RMS current a bus, switchboard or transfer switch can carry for a stated time (e.g. 65 kA / 1 s) without damage, while protection clears the fault. For the ATS, the WCR must be verified against the available fault current and the upstream device's clearing time — an ATS with a 3-cycle WCR behind a 0.3 s graded breaker is mis-applied.
ZSIZone-Selective Interlocking. Communication scheme between LV ACBs and MCCBs that allows the faulted zone's device to trip instantaneously while upstream devices remain on their normal graded delay — achieving both selectivity and fast arc-flash clearance.
λ (lambda)Failure rate (failures per hour). λ = 1/MTBF. The primary input to reliability calculations. See Appendix G for indicative values per equipment class.
Key takeaway: 61 terms defined, including the Rev 3 Saudi regulatory vocabulary (SEC, SASO, SABER, SBC 401, Saudi Grid Code) and genset/UPS rating language (ESP/PRP/COP, VFI, WCR). C — Formula Reference Card

Consolidated printable reference of every key electrical calculation formula used in this manual. For worked examples and derivations see Part 2 — Calculations. All quantities are SI; constants: √3 = 1.732, 1 pu = 100 %. All worked numbers are on the Rev 3 Saudi basis: 13.8 kV / 400-230 V, 60 Hz (plant 2.0 MW at pf 0.85 → 2353 kVA; 2 × 1600 kVA, Z = 6 %). Frequency-dependent quantities (reactance, motor speed, capacitor output) are evaluated at f = 60 Hz — do not reuse 60 Hz table values without correction. All values are indicative.

Quantity Formula Symbols Indicative example (this plant)
Apparent power S = P / pf S: apparent power (kVA/MVA); P: active power (kW/MW); pf: power factor. 2.0 MW / 0.85 = 2.35 MVA
3-phase current (from S) I = S / (√3 × V) I: line current (A); S: apparent power (VA); V: line-to-line voltage (V). 1600 kVA @ 400 V → 2309 A (TX LV FLC); 1600 kVA @ 13.8 kV → 66.9 A (TX MV FLC); 2353 kVA @ 13.8 kV → 98.4 A (plant total MV)
Motor full-load current I_fl = P / (√3 × V × pf × η) P: shaft power (W); V: line voltage (V); pf: motor pf; η: efficiency. 110 kW, 400 V, pf 0.86, η 0.95 → 194 A
DOL starting current I_start ≈ 6–7 × I_fl Use 6.5 × as indicative; confirm with motor manufacturer's data. 6.5 × 194 A = 1260 A
Motor synchronous speed N_s = 120 × f / p N_s: synchronous speed (rpm); f: frequency (Hz); p: number of poles. Actual speed = N_s − slip. At 60 Hz all speeds are 20 % higher than the 60 Hz equivalents. 4-pole @ 60 Hz → 1800 rpm (2-pole 3600, 6-pole 1200); same motor at 60 Hz would run 1500 rpm
Inductive reactance X = 2π × f × L X: reactance (Ω); f: frequency (Hz); L: inductance (H). At 60 Hz, reactances are 60/50 = 1.2 × the 60 Hz values — affects voltage drop, fault X/R and cable tables. L = 0.35 mH → X = 2π × 60 × 0.00035 = 0.132 Ω (vs 0.110 Ω at 60 Hz)
Transformer loading (pu) Loading = S_load / S_rated Values > 1.0 pu indicate overload; N-1 loading must be ≤ 1.0 pu (continuous). 1.175 / 1.600 = 0.73 pu (normal); 1.29 / 1.60 = 0.81 pu (N-1)
Short-circuit current I_sc = I_fl / Z(pu) I_fl: transformer LV FLC (A); Z(pu): transformer impedance as decimal (e.g. 0.06). Assumes infinite upstream source — conservative. 2309 / 0.06 ≈ 38.5 kA per TX; both TX in parallel (bus-tie closed) ≈ 77 kA — why the tie is interlocked open. MV design fault 25 kA (SEC range 25–40 kA)
Cable derating — required rating I_z(req) = I_B / (k_g × k_t) I_B: design (load) current (A); k_g: grouping factor; k_t: temperature factor. Select cable with tabulated I_z ≥ I_z(req). 194 / (0.80 × 0.94) = 258 A → 95 mm² Cu XLPE
Voltage drop (3-phase feeder) ΔV = √3 × I × (R·cosφ + X·sinφ) × L I: current (A); R, X: conductor resistance / reactance (Ω/km); L: length (km); cosφ/sinφ from load pf. Use 60 Hz reactance values (X is 1.2 × the 60 Hz table figure). 194 A, 120 m, 95 mm² Cu @ 60 Hz → ≈ 9.8 V ≈ 2.4 % (limit ≤ 5 %)
PFC reactive power required Q_c = P × (tanφ1 − tanφ2) P: active power (kW); φ1: present pf angle; φ2: target pf angle. 2000 × (0.620 − 0.329) = 582 kvar → ≈ 580 kvar bank, 7 % detuned (pf 0.85 → 0.95)
Capacitor output vs frequency Q = 2π × f × C × V² Q: reactive output (var); C: capacitance (F); V: voltage (V). Output is proportional to frequency: a capacitor delivers 60/50 = 1.2 × its 60 Hz kvar at 60 Hz. Always order capacitor steps and detuned reactors specified for 60 Hz; a 7 % reactor then tunes the branch to 60/√0.07 ≈ 227 Hz, below the 5th harmonic (300 Hz). A "50 kvar @ 400 V, 60 Hz" can → 60 kvar at 60 Hz (and its reactor detuning shifts) — never mix 60 Hz and 60 Hz PFC components
Generator sizing (essential load) S_DG ≥ (P_ess / pf) × k_step P_ess: essential active power (kW); pf: generator operating pf (0.8); k_step: step-load factor (~1.25). Quote the rating ESP at 50 °C ambient — high Saudi ambient derates the engine. (0.65 MW / 0.8) × 1.25 = 1.02 MVA → 1250 kVA DG (next standard size, with margin for 50 °C derating)
Transformer voltage regulation ΔV% ≈ load_pu × (R%·cosφ + X%·sinφ) load_pu: per-unit loading; R%, X%: transformer resistance and reactance in percent. 0.73 × (1.2×0.9 + 5.9×0.436) = 2.7 %
Protection grading margin t_up = t_down + Δt Δt: grading margin (typically 0.3 s) accounting for CB opening time, relay overshoot, CT error and safety margin. LV incomer 0.20 s → MV feeder relay 0.50 s
Arc-flash boundary AFB ≈ D × √(E / 1.2) D: working distance (m); E: incident energy at D (cal/cm²); 1.2 cal/cm² = onset-of-burn threshold. Simplified inverse-square scaling (full IEEE 1584 model required for labelling). 0.6 m × √(10/1.2) = 1.7 m
NER resistance R_NER = (V_LL / √3) / I_ef V_LL: MV line-to-line voltage (V); I_ef: target earth-fault current (A). Rate the NER for the fault duration (e.g. 10 s). (13,800 / 1.732) / 400 = 7970 / 400 ≈ 19.9 Ω (NER 7.97 kV, 400 A / 10 s)
Earth conductor (adiabatic) A = I × √t / k A: conductor cross-section (mm²); I: fault current (A); t: clearing time (s); k: material constant (143 for Cu/PVC). 38,500 × √0.2 / 143 = 120 mm² Cu
Availability A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) MTBF: mean time between failures (h); MTTR: mean time to repair (h). Annual downtime = (1 − A) × 8760 h. TX: 200,000 / (200,000 + 168) = 0.99916 → ~7.4 h/yr
Series availability (RBD) A_sys = A1 × A2 × … × An All elements must function; system availability is always lower than the worst individual element. 0.9992 × 0.9994 = 0.9986 (e.g. incomer + TX in series)
Parallel availability 1-of-2 (RBD) A_par = 1 − (1 − A1)(1 − A2) Function survives if at least one element works. Much higher than series for the same components. 1 − (1−0.99916)² = 0.99999929 → ~22 s/yr down
FMECA Risk Priority Number RPN = S × O × D S: Severity (1–5); O: Occurrence (1–5); D: Detection difficulty (1–5; 5 = hardest to detect). RPN max = 125. Higher RPN = higher risk. Priority bands: P1 = SPOF with S ≥ 4 or RPN ≥ 60; P2 = RPN 36–59; P3 = RPN 16–35; P4 < 16. Rank and act top-down. ATS: S=5, O=3, D=4 → RPN = 60 (P1 in example FMECA)
Reliability (survival function) R(t) = e(−λt) λ: failure rate (failures/h) = 1/MTBF; t: mission time (h). R(t) = probability no failure in time t. λ = 5×10-6/h, t = 8760 h → R = e−0.044 = 0.957
Tip: For arc-flash and short-circuit studies, always use recognised power-system software (ETAP, DIgSILENT, SKM or equivalent) with the full IEC 60909 / IEEE 1584 models. The simplified formulas above are for quick manual checks and teaching purposes only.

60 Hz ↔ 60 Hz conversion quick table

Much published reference data (cable tables, motor catalogues, capacitor cans) is tabulated at 60 Hz. Before reusing any such figure on this 60 Hz plant, apply the corrections below. Resistance, kVA, FLC and thermal ratings do not change with frequency; everything built on reactance or speed does.

Quantity 50 → 60 Hz behaviour Practical consequence on this plant
Inductive reactance XL× 1.2 (proportional to f)Cable and transformer X up 20 %: slightly higher reactive voltage drop, slightly lower X/R-driven peak factor differences — use 60 Hz impedance data in studies.
Capacitive reactance XC÷ 1.2 (inversely proportional)Capacitors draw 20 % more current and deliver 20 % more kvar at the same voltage — a 60 Hz-rated PFC can is overstressed at 60 Hz.
Motor synchronous speed× 1.2 (1500 → 1800 rpm, 4-pole)Centrifugal pump/fan power rises roughly with speed cubed — a 60 Hz motor-pump set moved to 60 Hz overloads unless re-rated or VFD-limited.
Transformer core flux (same V)÷ 1.2 (V/f lower)A 60 Hz-designed transformer runs less saturated at 60 Hz and the same voltage — benign; the reverse direction (60 Hz unit on 60 Hz) is the dangerous one.
Detuned-filter tuning frequency× 1.2 (7 % reactor: 189 → 227 Hz)Reactor-capacitor steps must be engineered as a 60 Hz set; mixing 60 Hz reactors with 60 Hz cans detunes the detuning.
Relay / trip-unit settings in secondsUnchangedTime grading margins are frequency-agnostic; settings in cycles, however, are 16.7 ms per cycle at 60 Hz, not 20 ms.
Key takeaway: 23 formulas on one page, all worked on the 13.8 kV / 400 V / 60 Hz Rev 3 basis. The three you will use most in the field are the FLC formula, the short-circuit formula (I_sc = I_fl / Z), and the availability formula (A = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)). Remember the three 60 Hz traps: reactance is 1.2 × the 60 Hz value, motor speeds are 1.2 × (1800 rpm for 4-pole), and capacitor kvar is 1.2 × nameplate-at-60 Hz. F — SPOF-Hardening Principles (One-Page Summary)

Distilled from Part 3 — SPOF Analysis and Module 3. Use this page as a rapid scan-reference when reviewing an SLD or writing recommendations. Every principle has a trade-off in cost, complexity and footprint — apply redundancy where the consequence of failure justifies it (critical / essential loads first). All values and configurations are indicative.

Principle What it means in practice Typical implementation (tags) SPOF it eliminates Cost / complexity
N+1 redundancy Provide at least one more capacity unit than the minimum. The surviving unit(s) must carry the essential load on loss of one unit. 2 × TX-1/TX-2 (each capable of carrying essential load at ~81 % loading on N-1); N+1 MCC feeders for critical motors. Single transformer / single source H capital; H benefit for critical loads
Bus segregation Split the LV busbar into independent sections (Bus A / Bus B) so a bus fault or maintenance on one section does not take the whole plant. LV-MSB split bus; Form 4b compartmentalisation of MCC assemblies. Single un-split LV bus (Diagram A) M capital; H benefit (contained fault zone)
Bus-tie with auto-transfer The normally-open bus-tie closes automatically within seconds on loss of one LV incomer, connecting the healthy transformer to the dead bus. N-1 loading must be verified (≤ 1.0 pu continuous). BT ACB with auto-transfer scheme via PMS or hard-wired interlock; loss-of-voltage / breaker-position logic. Periodic on-load test required. Two buses with no tie (Diagram B) M capital; very H benefit (MTTR reduced from hours to seconds)
Dual utility incomers Two independent utility feeds to the MV bus from different utility substations or circuits, with a closeable MV bus-section between them. Two incomer panels in MV-SWGR (52-I1, 52-I2); MV bus-section CB (52-BS). Single utility feeder / single MV incomer (Diagram C) H capital (utility connection); H benefit for essential-load continuity
Separate routing — duty vs standby Duty and standby motors / loads must be fed from different sources, MCCs and cable routes. A single cable, MCC bus or MCC incomer serving both duty and standby is a common-element SPOF. Duty pump on MCC-1 (Bus A), standby pump on MCC-2 (Bus B); cables in separate trays on opposite sides of plant. Shared cable / single MCC for duty + standby (Diagram D) M capital; H benefit (eliminates common-mode failure)
Standby generation (DG + ATS) A standby diesel generator, automatically started and connected via the ATS on utility loss, backs up the essential bus for safety-critical and process-critical loads. DG set (1250 kVA ESP at 50 °C, indicative) + ATS + EDB. ATS open-transition transfer typically ≤ 10 s (depends on DG run-up time). Auto-test on load weekly; fuel and battery monitoring continuous. Total loss of supply to essential loads on utility outage H capital per kVA; H benefit for safety loads
UPS for control / protection No-break, clean AC supply for the DCS/PLC/SCADA and instrumentation. Prevents a supply disturbance from crashing the control system and causing a process trip or safety incident. UPS (100 kVA, 30 min autonomy indicative) + DCDB (110 V DC for MV switchgear trip / close coils). Loss of control / protection on any supply disturbance M capital; H benefit for control continuity and protection dependability
Condition monitoring and testing Scheduled on-load testing (DG / ATS weekly; bus-tie transfer periodically) and continuous condition monitoring (transformer oil / temperature, battery impedance, UPS health) reduces Detection score (D) in the FMECA without changing failure rate. Monitored via PMS / DCS; oil/temperature sensors on TX-1/TX-2; battery monitoring on UPS and DCDB. Latent failures in standby equipment (DG fails to start; bus-tie fails to close) L–M capital; H benefit (lowers RPN through better detection)
Fault containment and selectivity Graded protection (time-current coordination) ensures the device closest to a fault trips first, isolating the smallest possible zone. Zone-selective interlocking (ZSI) maintains both selectivity and fast arc-flash clearance. Relay / trip-unit settings in MV-SWGR IEDs and LV-MSB ACBs; ZSI between ACB and outgoing MCCBs. Upstream breaker trips on a downstream fault (wider zone lost than necessary) L capital (settings only, unless extra relays); H benefit for zone containment
Earthing integrity A continuous, low-impedance earth system (grid + bonding + NER) ensures earth faults are reliably detected, current limited, and touch/step voltages kept safe. MV-NER (R ≈ 19.9 Ω, 400 A / 10 s indicative, with NER monitoring relay); LV TN-S; earth grid (≤ 1 Ω indicative, IEEE 80 touch/step check); 120 mm² Cu earth conductors (indicative); bonding to LV-MSB, MCC, cable armour. Undetected earth faults; personnel electrocution; equipment damage from high fault energy M capital; safety-critical
Environmental hardening (Rev 3 / Saudi) Design every rating and enclosure for the actual environment: 50 °C ambient, dust/sand, coastal salt. Heat is a slow common-mode failure mechanism — it ages every battery, capacitor and power module on site simultaneously. Min IP54 outdoor enclosures; switchroom/battery-room HVAC (itself on the essential bus); cable derating ≈ 0.82 for air at 50 °C; DG rated ESP at 50 °C; filter maintenance on VFD and UPS cooling paths. Common-mode thermal degradation of all electronics and batteries; HVAC loss silently shortening UPS/DCDB autonomy M capital; H benefit — and the HVAC itself must appear in the FMECA

SPOF-Hunt Checklist — Quick Scan of an SLD

  • Is there a single utility incomer / single MV bus? (Diagram C SPOF — check for dual incomers or DG/ATS coverage).
  • Is there only one transformer? (Diagram A SPOF — check for N+1 or bus-tie capability).
  • Is the LV bus split into sections A and B? If not, the whole LV plant is a single zone.
  • Is there a bus-tie (BT) between Bus A and Bus B? Is it normally open with auto-transfer?
  • Are duty and standby motors fed from different MCCs on different buses, via separately routed cables? (Diagram D SPOF).
  • Is the DG connected via an ATS? Is the ATS auto-transfer commissioned and tested regularly?
  • Is there a UPS for DCS/PLC/SCADA? Is there a DCDB for MV switchgear trip/close supplies?
  • Are transformer differential protection (87T) and earth-fault protection (50N/51N) commissioned on both TX-1 and TX-2?
  • Is the NER present and rated correctly? (Check neutral CT and earth-fault relay are connected).
  • Is protection coordination current — does the study reflect the as-found breaker / relay configuration?
  • Is the switchroom / battery room HVAC healthy and alarmed? (At 50 °C ambient, HVAC loss is a common-mode threat to UPS, DCDB and VFD electronics.)
  • Is the NER supervised by a monitoring relay? An unmonitored open NER silently unearths the 13.8 kV system.
Key takeaway: Eleven hardening principles; twelve SPOF-hunt checklist items. The five most common findings are: no bus-tie auto-transfer, duty and standby on the same MCC, single utility incomer with no DG coverage, ATS not commissioned, and outdated protection coordination study. Address these first.

H — SASO / SEC Standards Map

This appendix explains how standards work in Saudi Arabia for an electrical engineer used to plain-IEC projects, and then maps every major topic in this manual to its IEC/IEEE technical basis and its Saudi regulatory reference. Nothing in the engineering changes — Ohm's law is the same in Jubail as in Geneva — but the citation chain that makes a design contractually and legally acceptable in the Kingdom is different, and getting it wrong delays procurement, customs clearance and energisation approval. All references in this appendix are indicative; the project specification and the authority having jurisdiction define the governing editions.

How SASO adopts IEC standards

SASO (Saudi Standards, Metrology and Quality Organization) is the Kingdom's national standards body. Rather than writing electrical standards from scratch, SASO adopts international standards — overwhelmingly IEC for electrical equipment — and publishes them as Saudi standards, usually with the structure SASO IEC xxxxx. An adoption may be identical (the IEC text verbatim) or modified (with national deviations — the classic example being the LV supply voltage and the 60 Hz frequency). The practical consequences for you:

  • The technical content you learned from the IEC standard almost always applies. When this manual cites IEC 60909 for fault calculations or IEC 61439 for switchboard verification, the SASO adoption carries the same clauses.
  • Check the national deviations first. They cluster around voltage (400/230 V LV), frequency (60 Hz), ambient temperature (calibration of ratings to Saudi climate) and marking/language requirements.
  • Cite the Saudi adoption in deliverables. A datasheet that references "IEC 60076" is technically fine; a datasheet that references "SASO IEC 60076 (IEC 60076)" clears review faster. This manual uses the shorthand SASO-adopted IEC.
Note — the dual-code reality. Saudi industrial practice runs IEC-based equipment standards alongside SBC 401, the Saudi Building Code electrical chapter, which is technically derived from NFPA 70 (the US NEC). On a process plant you will routinely see IEC switchgear installed under an NEC-flavoured installation code, with IEEE 80 earthing and IEEE 1584 arc-flash studies on top. This mix is normal and workable — but it means you must always ask which code governs a given scope and flag conflicts (e.g. cable sizing tables, earthing terminology) rather than silently assuming one family.

SABER — conformity before the box ships

SABER is SASO's electronic conformity-assessment platform. For regulated products — which include a large share of LV electrical equipment — the importer must obtain a Product Certificate of Conformity (valid typically one year) and a per-shipment Shipment Certificate through SABER before customs clearance. Equipment families also fall under Saudi Technical Regulations (for example for LV electrical equipment and appliances), which define which standards the certificate must attest against.

  • For the desk study: SABER status is a procurement/QA document, not a site observation. Ask the supply chain for the certificates with the test reports.
  • For recommendations: when an FMECA action implies new equipment (e.g. adding an NER monitor or SPDs), note that delivery time must include SABER certification if the vendor has no current Saudi certificate.
  • Red flag: a vendor who cannot state their SABER/SASO position for the offered family. The major manufacturers in Appendix I maintain Saudi certification as a matter of course.

SEC company standards and the grid codes

The Saudi Electricity Company (SEC) is both the utility you connect to and a de-facto standards authority: its Distribution Materials Specifications and company standards govern everything at and upstream of the point of supply — the 13.8 kV connection, the revenue metering arrangement (MV-MET), the intake protection interface, and approved-vendor requirements for utility-facing equipment. Two codes sit above this:

  • Saudi Grid Code — connection to the transmission system and duties of large users/generators: operating frequency band around 60 Hz, voltage ranges, fault ride-through, reactive capability.
  • SEC Distribution Code — the distribution-level counterpart that applies to a 13.8 kV-connected plant like this one: supply standards, power-factor expectations (the ≈ 580 kvar PFC bank exists largely to live comfortably under this regime), harmonic emission limits, metering and protection coordination at the intake.
Tip — three questions to settle with SEC early. (1) The confirmed fault level at the point of supply (design value here: 25 kA, SEC range 25–40 kA — it sizes MV-SWGR). (2) The required intake protection and metering single-line (it fixes the MV-MET panel arrangement). (3) The earthing interface — whether the SEC network earthing and the plant MV-NER philosophy are compatible. All three are desk-study items; none can be verified by site inspection alone.

SBC 401 — the statutory installation code

The Saudi Building Code (SBC) series is the Kingdom's statutory construction code, enforced through municipal permitting; SBC 401 is its electrical volume, technically based on NFPA 70 (NEC) with Saudi amendments. On industrial plants its practical reach is mostly buildings, life safety and final-circuit installations (the DB/EDB world), while the process electrical system is specified to SASO-adopted IEC. Where both could apply, the contract's order of precedence governs — and where you find a genuine conflict (conductor sizing method, RCD vs GFCI language, earthing terminology), record it as a finding rather than improvising a hybrid.
